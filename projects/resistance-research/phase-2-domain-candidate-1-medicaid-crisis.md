## Executive Summary

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) is the most consequential contraction of American health coverage since before the ACA. The Congressional Budget Office projects **10.9 million additional uninsured people** by 2034, with **1.3 million losing coverage in 2026 alone**, escalating to **5.2 million by 2027** when mandatory work requirements take effect January 1, 2027. The $911 billion in federal Medicaid cuts over ten years fall hardest on the same populations already excluded from political power: Black Southerners in non-expansion states, Indigenous people in rural areas, low-income workers in tight labor markets, and the rural communities whose only hospitals are now closing.

**The democracy argument**: This is not primarily a healthcare policy dispute. It is a structural argument about citizenship infrastructure. Medicaid enrollment offices are legally required to offer voter registration under the National Voter Registration Act (52 U.S.C. § 20506). When those offices lose capacity, close, or shift to punitive compliance processing, voter registration in low-income majority-minority communities contracts. When people are disenrolled from Medicaid — 18,000 in Arkansas in six months without a single measurable employment gain — they lose their most reliable contact with government services and, with it, their most reliable voter registration touchpoint. The OBBBA does not reduce voter registration as a stated goal. But the mechanism operates as if it did.

**The live crisis**: Nebraska became the first state to implement work requirements on May 1, 2026 — eight months before the statutory deadline. Between 25,000 and 40,000 Nebraskans are expected to lose coverage. Montana follows July 1; Iowa December 1; Arkansas begins soft implementation July 1. The HHS interim final rule defining exceptions, exemption standards, and good-faith extension criteria is due June 1, 2026, and — critically — **the statute explicitly exempts it from public notice and comment**. The comment window that would normally exist does not exist for the rule. Advocacy must target the rule through channels other than the standard APA notice-and-comment process.

**Five core findings** (corresponding to the core research questions):

1. The OBBBA cuts $911 billion over ten years; FY2027 immediate impacts are a 5.2-million-person coverage loss spike driven by January 1, 2027 work requirement enforcement.
2. Work requirements operate as enrollment barriers, not employment incentives. Arkansas 2018 remains the definitive empirical record: 18,000 disenrolled, zero employment effect, blocked by courts.
3. 446 hospitals are at closure risk nationally. 196 are in Republican congressional districts. The rural closure geography tracks the non-expansion South — the region with the most to lose from Medicaid disenrollment and the fewest healthcare alternatives.
4. Medicaid disenrollment contracts voter registration infrastructure in exactly the communities where voter purges are highest and registration rates are lowest.
5. Litigation is active (Planned Parenthood/states AG coalition; state-directed payment challenges in 22 states) but the work requirement statute itself has not yet faced a direct APA challenge, partly because the interim final rule is not subject to notice-and-comment — a structural insulation from the most effective litigation vector.

## Section I: What the OBBBA Actually Cuts — The Mechanism Architecture

### The $911 Billion Figure: What It Means and What It Conceals

The Congressional Budget Office's official 10-year estimate for federal Medicaid and CHIP spending reductions under OBBBA is **$911 billion** (after accounting for interactions between provisions). This is the headline figure. It conceals a distribution that is backloaded and multi-mechanistic.

**Mechanism breakdown** (CBO estimates):
- Work and community engagement reporting requirements: **$326 billion** (largest single provision)
- Limits on state provider tax arrangements: **$191 billion**
- Restrictions on state-directed Medicaid payments (SDPs): **$149 billion**
- Elimination of enhanced federal matching for new Medicaid expansion states: remaining share
- Increased eligibility redetermination frequency (semi-annual vs. annual): contributing to 923,000 disenrollments

**Enrollment loss**: CBO projects the law eliminates at least **10.5 million people from Medicaid by 2034**, driving 7.8 million of the 10.9 million total uninsured increase (the remaining 3.1 million come from ACA exchange changes).

**The backload**: 76% of federal spending reductions occur in the final five years (2030–2034). This is not accidental — the reconciliation process requires a 10-year score, and provisions phased in later generate larger 10-year savings while limiting visible near-term political costs. The political architecture of the cuts is designed so that maximum impact arrives after the 2026 midterms and after the 2028 presidential election.

### FY2027: The Cliff Year

The coverage loss trajectory is not linear. CBO's phased estimate shows:

| Year | Additional Uninsured (cumulative increase) |
|------|--------------------------------------------|
| 2026 | 1.3 million |
| 2027 | 5.2 million |
| 2028 | 6.8 million |
| 2029 | 8.6 million |
| 2034 | 10.9 million |

The jump from 1.3 million (2026) to 5.2 million (2027) — a **fourfold acceleration in a single year** — is almost entirely attributable to January 1, 2027 work requirement enforcement beginning. This makes FY2027 the policy cliff, and it makes the June 2026 HHS interim final rule the last pre-cliff regulatory leverage point.

### Mechanism 1: Work Requirements (Effective January 1, 2027)

OBBBA Section 1905(y) requires all states to impose 80 hours/month of community engagement as a condition of Medicaid eligibility for expansion adults (non-elderly, non-disabled, non-caregiver), effective January 1, 2027. States may implement earlier through state plan amendments (Nebraska did; Montana, Iowa, Arkansas have announced early implementation).

**Who is subject**: Approximately 20 million adults in the ACA Medicaid expansion group. Exemptions exist for pregnant individuals, full-time students, individuals with documented incapacity, and designated caregivers — but exemption definitions in the statute are narrow, and operationalizing them requires documentation systems states are not yet equipped to run.

**The compliance requirement**: Enrollees must demonstrate 80 hours/month of qualifying activities (employment, job training, vocational education, community service, or combination) at initial application AND at renewal (minimum every 6 months). Reporting occurs through state-built portal systems that most states have not yet constructed. The statute allows but does not require states to use existing data sources to verify compliance — many states plan to require direct documentation from enrollees.

**Implementation funding gap**: Congress appropriated $200 million for FY2026 to assist states — $100 million distributed equally and $100 million by Medicaid population. That amounts to under $2 million per state for base allocation. Historical evidence: Georgia spent $54 million over four and a half years implementing a work requirement for a small portion of the expansion population — 88% going to administrative overhead, not coverage. The $200 million federal allocation does not fund national implementation at scale.

### Mechanism 2: Semi-Annual Eligibility Redetermination

Beginning with the January 2027 implementation, states must recertify Medicaid eligibility every 6 months (versus the prior 12-month baseline). This doubles the frequency of administrative contact required to maintain coverage.

**The documented pattern**: During the COVID-19 pandemic continuous enrollment unwinding (April 2023–June 2024), 27 million people were disenrolled from Medicaid after redeterminations resumed. **69% of those disenrollments were procedural** — not based on actual ineligibility, but because enrollees failed to respond to notices, had outdated addresses, or could not complete paperwork in time. Racial disparities in procedural disenrollment were pronounced: Black enrollees accounted for 22% of procedural disenrollments despite being 16% of the Medicaid population; Hispanic enrollees accounted for 34% of procedural disenrollments despite being 23% of enrollment. Doubling verification frequency mechanically doubles exposure to this procedural loss pattern.

### Mechanism 3: Provider Tax Phasedown and State-Directed Payment Caps

Provider taxes (levied by states on hospitals and managed care organizations, which generate state matching funds to draw down federal matching dollars) are frozen at July 4, 2025 levels and phase down from 6.0% to 3.5% beginning FY2028 for expansion states. State-directed payments (SDPs — supplemental payments above base Medicaid rates) are capped at 100% of Medicare rates for expansion states and 110% for non-expansion states, with grandfathered arrangements phased down 10% annually beginning 2028.

**State budget impact**: This mechanism drives the $664 billion reduction in state Medicaid budgets over the period. States that have built hospital financing models around provider taxes and SDPs — Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas — face the most severe long-run structural impacts from this mechanism, independent of work requirements. California projects $2.4 billion in additional state expenditure in FY2027 alone due to the expiration of existing supplemental payment arrangements.

### Mechanism 4: Planned Parenthood and Reproductive Health Provider Exclusion (Section 71113)

Section 71113 prohibits federal Medicaid funds from reaching any "prohibited entity" — defined as nonprofits primarily engaged in family planning or reproductive services that received $800,000+ in Medicaid funding in 2024 and provide abortions outside Hyde exceptions — for one year. This provision is under active litigation (22-state AG coalition) and has generated preliminary injunctions and circuit court reversals. It is addressed separately from the work requirement and FMAP provisions but contributes to the overall contraction of Medicaid-funded healthcare access.

## Section II: Work Requirements as Enrollment Barriers — The Arkansas Evidence and Its Applicability

### The Arkansas 2018 Natural Experiment

Arkansas implemented Medicaid work requirements under a Section 1115 waiver on June 1, 2018. The program applied to non-elderly, non-disabled adults ages 30–49 (later extended to 20–29 beginning January 1, 2019), requiring 80 hours/month of qualifying activities.

**Enrollment outcome**: **18,000 people were disenrolled** in the first six months. This represented approximately 20% of the non-elderly, non-disabled expansion population in the state. These individuals lost coverage not because they were ineligible by income — they remained income-eligible — but because they failed to navigate the new reporting system.

**Employment outcome**: Research published in the New England Journal of Medicine and reviewed by the D.C. Circuit court found **no measurable employment effect**. People who lost coverage were not more likely to be employed after disenrollment than before. The majority were either already working, had disabilities not yet formally documented in state systems, or were not working due to caregiving responsibilities that did not meet the narrow exemption criteria.

**The court's finding**: Judge James Boasberg (D.D.C.) vacated the waiver approval, holding that HHS "failed to consider adequately the impact of the proposed project on Medicaid coverage." The D.C. Circuit affirmed in *Gresham v. Azar*, 950 F.3d 615 (D.C. Cir. 2020). The court's analysis turned on whether work requirements "advance the statutory purpose of Medicaid" — finding they did not because they produced coverage loss without coverage gain or employment gain.

**The precedent's limited applicability to OBBBA**: The Arkansas ruling struck down a regulatory waiver (CMS approval of a Section 1115 demonstration). OBBBA work requirements are statutory — Congress itself mandated them in legislation. This is a different posture. Courts applying textualism post-*Loper Bright* must ask not whether the agency interpretation is reasonable, but whether the statute itself is permissible under constitutional limits and whether implementation guidance falls within the statutory grant. The *Gresham* precedent survives as persuasive authority on the empirical question (work requirements don't advance Medicaid purpose), but the legal posture for challenging statutory work requirements is different from challenging regulatory waivers.

### How Work Requirements Function as Administrative Barriers

The mechanism is not mysterious. Three structural features make work requirements operate as enrollment barriers regardless of whether the target population is employed:

**Documentation architecture**: Enrollees must affirmatively prove compliance — produce pay stubs, training certificates, time sheets for community service — to continue coverage. This documentation burden falls on people who are often in unstable housing, without reliable internet, working multiple jobs with irregular schedules, or navigating literacy barriers. The burden is not incidental to the requirement; it is the mechanism. States that implement work requirements without automated data matching (which requires state-to-state data sharing agreements that do not yet exist at scale) will rely on enrollee-submitted documentation — and documentation failure will drive disenrollment.

**Reporting-system failures**: States must build portals for compliance reporting from scratch (with 6–7 months between the June 2026 guidance and January 2027 implementation). Nebraska, the first state to implement, launched a portal that advocates immediately flagged as insufficient — with inadequate multilingual support, unclear exemption pathways, and no pre-enrollment guidance for newly affected populations.

**Exemption under-identification**: The statute exempts pregnant individuals, caregivers, students, and people with incapacity. But these exemptions require documentation — documentation that the state must affirmatively verify or that enrollees must prove. In Arkansas, people with disabilities who were not yet receiving SSI or SSDI (the administrative proxy for "documented incapacity") were disenrolled even though they were functionally unable to work. The exemption process creates a secondary documentation burden on the most vulnerable populations.

### Early Implementation as Policy Signal: Nebraska May 2026

Nebraska began enforcing work requirements May 1, 2026, eight months ahead of the statutory deadline. Governor Jim Pillen and HHS Secretary Dr. Oz announced the early implementation jointly, framing Nebraska as the national demonstration model.

**Population affected**: Approximately 72,000 Nebraskans are subject to the new requirements; 25,000 to 40,000 are projected to lose coverage (Urban Institute estimate: 25,000; advocacy estimate: up to 41,000). Nebraska has approximately 70,000 total expansion enrollees.

**Implementation concerns documented by advocates**:
- Insufficient advance notice to enrollees (shorter than the federally expected outreach window)
- No additional staffing hired to process compliance verifications
- Multilingual support gaps in the reporting portal
- Rural enrollees facing disproportionate documentation barriers (poor internet connectivity, limited transportation to workforce centers for in-person enrollment)
- Advocates stated that "a vast majority of Nebraskans are working or would meet an exemption — it's the red tape that causes people to lose coverage"

**States following**: Montana (July 1, 2026), Iowa (December 1, 2026), Arkansas (soft implementation July 1, 2026 — verification begins before enforcement). By January 1, 2027, all 40+ expansion states must be compliant.

## Section III: Hospital Closure Geography — The Congressional District Accountability Map

### The 446 Number and What It Means

Public Citizen's analysis of OBBBA impacts identified **446 hospitals at serious risk of closure or major service reduction**. These hospitals collectively hold **68,986 beds** and served approximately **6.6 million patients in 2024**. The figure encompasses hospitals that derive a critical share of revenue from Medicaid and whose operating margins cannot absorb the projected payment reductions without service cuts, unit closures, or full closure.

**The political geography**: The analysis found that **196 of the 446 at-risk hospitals are in Republican congressional districts** — representing the districts of House members who voted for OBBBA. Senate Republicans, all of whom voted for OBBBA, collectively represent states containing **146 at-risk hospitals**. This is not a Blue-district-only crisis. The rural hospital network depends on Medicaid funding regardless of the political composition of the district, and Republican-held rural districts in Ohio, Missouri, Iowa, West Virginia, and North Carolina contain a substantial portion of the at-risk facilities.

**Urban vs. rural**: Approximately 40% of the 446 are rural or critical-access hospitals; 60% are urban safety-net facilities serving majority-low-income and majority-minority urban populations. The rural figure is more politically legible; the urban safety-net figure is epidemiologically more significant.

### The Immediate Revenue Crisis (FY2026)

Independent rural hospitals face an estimated **$465 million in total revenue loss in 2026** — an average of **$630,665 per hospital** — according to Chartis Center for Rural Health analysis. This is not a projected future loss; it is the current-year impact of cuts already in effect (provider tax freezes, SDP caps, FMAP changes already operative).

**States with highest immediate rural hospital exposure**:
- Ohio: 5 additional hospitals at risk of negative net income in 2026
- Missouri: 4 hospitals
- Iowa: 2 hospitals
- North Carolina: 2 hospitals
- Maine: 2 hospitals
- West Virginia: 1 hospital

The concentration in the Midwest and Appalachian South is not coincidental. These are states where rural hospitals are the primary employer and the primary Medicaid office point-of-contact for the uninsured population.

**The total picture**: As of January 2026, the Center for Healthcare Quality and Payment Reform estimates **734 rural hospitals — one-third of all rural facilities in the nation — are at risk of closing**. The OBBBA does not create this crisis from nothing; it accelerates and deepens a rural hospital financial distress that predates it. But the margin that separates a viable rural hospital from a closing one is often the Medicaid revenue that OBBBA is cutting.

### Documented 2026 Closures and Service Reductions (May 2026 Update)

**Nebraska**:
- Chadron Hospital (independent, nonprofit): Closed dialysis services, citing H.R. 1 cuts
- Bluestem Health (Lincoln clinic): Projects loss of up to 15% of 21,000 patients; estimated $600,000 annual revenue loss

**New Hampshire**:
- Ammonoosuc Community Health Services: Closed clinic in Franconia in anticipation of OBBBA impacts

**Virginia**:
- Valley Health's Winchester hospital: Closing observation unit by July 1, 2026

**Oklahoma**:
- INTEGRIS Health: Planning multiple clinic closures in dermatology, pediatric, and mental health services; expects $130 million funding loss over OBBBA period

**Minnesota**:
- Astera Health: Recently opened cancer center threatened; system reassessing operations due to OBBBA

**Iowa**:
- Primary care clinic in Ottumwa closed February 2026 after one month's notice
- Iowa projects $1.5 billion in annual revenue reductions statewide

**Montana**:
- Big Sandy hospital's renovation and ongoing payment needs threatened despite receiving Rural Health Transformation Program allocation

**North Carolina**:
- 720,000 residents who gained Medicaid coverage under expansion at risk of loss
- Rural eastern North Carolina described by state officials as facing becoming "a medical services wasteland"

### The Rural Health Transformation Fund: A Structural Analysis

OBBBA includes a $50 billion Rural Health Transformation Fund distributed to states over the law's implementation period. The largest state allocations: Texas ($281 million in first-year allocation), Nebraska ($219 million), North Carolina ($213 million), Montana ($233 million).

Georgetown CCF analysis published May 1, 2026 concludes the fund "isn't doing much to soften the blow" because: (1) states are limited to using only 15% of rural health funds for direct patient care; (2) the fund targets "innovative delivery models" rather than sustaining existing infrastructure; (3) Texas's $281 million first-year allocation stands against a projected $4 billion decline in federal Medicaid spending in rural Texas over ten years — a 14:1 loss-to-mitigation ratio; (4) Pennsylvania faces a projected $20 billion loss in Medicaid funding between 2028 and 2038 against its fund allocation.

The fund is best understood as a political insulation mechanism, not a healthcare stabilization mechanism. It provides governors in high-loss states a pro forma response to constituent concerns while absorbing a fraction of the actual cut.

## Section IV: The Democracy-Health Nexus — Medicaid Disenrollment as Voter Suppression Infrastructure

### The Legal Architecture: NVRA Section 7

The National Voter Registration Act (NVRA, 52 U.S.C. § 20506) mandates that states offer voter registration opportunities at all public assistance agencies, including Medicaid offices, at the time of initial application, renewal, and change-of-address. This requirement is not optional; it is a condition of federal funding for public assistance programs.

**What NVRA Section 7 actually requires**: States must:
- Distribute voter registration applications with each Medicaid application
- Provide assistance completing applications on request
- Accept completed applications and transmit them to election officials
- Inform applicants of the opportunity to register and the non-connection between registration choice and benefit eligibility

**The gap between mandate and practice**: National data shows that public assistance agencies produce only 1.7 million voter registration forms per election cycle — 1.6% of total registrations nationally. This is not because Medicaid enrollees don't want to register; it is because overwhelmed offices provide paper forms without training, assistance, or follow-through. The NVRA mandate is real. The enforcement is weak. And the staffing capacity of Medicaid offices directly determines whether the mandate functions in practice.

### How Disenrollment Contracts Voter Registration Infrastructure

When Medicaid enrollment offices lose capacity — due to budget compression, staff reductions, or the administrative burden of compliance processing — the primary casualty is voter registration assistance. Compliance processing (work requirement verification, semi-annual redetermination) is mandatory; voter registration outreach is also mandatory but in practice lower-priority when offices are resource-constrained.

**Three compounding mechanisms**:

**Mechanism 1 — Direct contact reduction**: When 1.5 million people are disenrolled (projected 2027 work requirement effect, Year 1), each disenrollment eliminates a citizen's contact with a Medicaid office — and with it, a NVRA-mandated registration opportunity. In states without Automatic Voter Registration (AVR) linked to Medicaid — the majority of states — this is not recovered through other channels.

**Mechanism 2 — Capacity diversion**: Work requirement compliance processing requires significant administrative capacity. States must verify 80-hour/month compliance for up to 20 million expansion adults at application and semi-annually at renewal. This processing burden competes with voter registration assistance for the same staff hours. Nebraska advocates specifically documented that the state declined to hire additional staff for compliance processing — meaning existing staff must absorb both functions.

**Mechanism 3 — Enrollment office structural reduction**: The causal chain runs: OBBBA cuts → hospital closures → reduced state Medicaid revenue base → state Medicaid office budget compression → reduced enrollment office staffing → reduced voter registration capacity. This is a downstream effect, but it operates at the community level in rural areas where Medicaid offices are co-located with hospitals and other public services.

**The AVR gap**: Eight jurisdictions have implemented Medicaid Automatic Voter Registration (Colorado, Nevada, Massachusetts, D.C., New Mexico, Minnesota, Oregon, Michigan). In these states, the voter registration function is automated and decoupled from staff capacity — enrollment in Medicaid triggers automatic registration transmission unless the enrollee opts out. In the remaining 42 states, voter registration depends on staff capacity and paper forms. The 12 non-expansion states — concentrated in the South, with the highest proportions of Black and Indigenous Medicaid-eligible populations — have the lowest AVR adoption and the highest voter purge rates (Brennan Center data shows Southern states purge at twice the national average).

### The Racial Equity Dimension

The demographic distribution of OBBBA Medicaid impact is not random. The cuts fall most heavily on:

**Southern non-expansion states**: The 12 states that did not expand Medicaid by January 1, 2026 — Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Kansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas — are disproportionately concentrated in the South, where 3.6 million low-income adults (mostly in the South's coverage gap) have no pathway to coverage. The Medicaid-eligible populations in these states are disproportionately Black: Mississippi (37% of Medicaid-eligible population), Louisiana (32%), Alabama (27%).

**Work requirement barriers by race**: The employment barriers that make work requirements function as enrollment barriers are racially distributed. BLS 2026 data shows Black workers face 8.6% unemployment vs. 4.1% for white workers. AIAN (American Indian/Alaska Native) people on or near reservations face chronic unemployment of 23–45% depending on reservation. Hispanic workers in seasonal agricultural employment face documentation barriers — irregular work histories, employment in cash-basis seasonal work — that work requirement reporting systems are designed for formal, documented employment rather than the work patterns of low-income communities of color.

**The unwinding precedent**: During the COVID-19 unwinding (2023–2024), procedural disenrollment fell along racial lines with documented statistical regularity. Black individuals made up 22% of procedural disenrollments despite being 16% of the Medicaid population. Hispanic individuals made up 34% of procedural disenrollments despite being 23% of enrollment. The mechanism that produced these disparities — documentation burden, address verification failures, language barriers — is identical to the mechanism that work requirements will employ. The racial disparities are built into the administrative architecture, not into individual behavior.

**The political exclusion loop**: The geographic and demographic profile of OBBBA impact — Southern non-expansion states, majority-minority rural communities, low-income workers with documentation barriers — maps closely onto the communities that face the highest rates of voter roll purges, the lowest rates of voter registration, and the fewest healthcare alternatives. This is not coincidental. The policy mechanism produces healthcare access contraction and voter registration infrastructure contraction in the same populations, in the same locations, simultaneously.

## Section V: Active Litigation — What Is and Is Not Being Challenged

### The Statutory vs. Regulatory Posture Problem

All prior successful Medicaid work requirement litigation — *Gresham v. Azar*, *Stewart v. Azar*, *Philbrick v. Azar* — challenged regulatory waivers: CMS approvals of state Section 1115 demonstrations. Courts invalidated those waivers by finding that CMS failed to consider coverage loss when approving demonstrations against the Medicaid statute's purpose of "furnishing medical assistance."

OBBBA work requirements are statutory. Congress itself, not CMS, enacted the work requirement. This changes the litigation terrain significantly:

- Courts cannot invalidate a statutory work requirement for being inconsistent with the Medicaid statute's purpose — Congress can amend its own statute
- Post-*Loper Bright*, courts must give the statute its best meaning rather than deferring to agency interpretation — but the statute's text is relatively clear on the work requirement mandate
- The APA challenge that won in Arkansas (agency failed to consider coverage loss) is structurally weaker against a statutory provision because Congress's intent to reduce coverage is explicit in the CBO score Congress cited
- **The most promising litigation vector** is not APA challenge but Spending Clause / constitutional notice challenge — the argument that OBBBA imposes conditions on states in ways states could not have anticipated when joining Medicaid

### Active Litigation

**Planned Parenthood/AG Coalition (Section 71113)**:
- 22+ states filed suit against Section 71113 (Medicaid exclusion of abortion providers)
- Federal district court issued preliminary injunction in July 2025; circuit court reversed in December 2025; appellate posture currently unfavorable to challengers
- Coalition includes Washington, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, D.C., Hawaii, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, North Carolina, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, and Wisconsin
- This litigation is about provider exclusion, not work requirements — it does not directly address the coverage loss from Mechanism 1

**State-Directed Payment Challenges**:
- Multiple states and hospital systems have filed challenges to SDP caps (Section 71115)
- CMS released guidance in early 2026 on SDP restrictions; several states are contesting their state plan amendment denials
- Vermont AG: In March 2026, filed suit to enforce court order blocking HHS from sharing Medicaid data with ICE — a separate but adjacent challenge to HHS data-sharing authority

**Medicaid-ICE Data Sharing**:
- Vermont AG Clark (March 2026): Sought court enforcement of an order blocking HHS from sharing "large swaths of Medicaid data" with ICE
- This is an NVRA-adjacent issue: Medicaid data sharing with immigration enforcement chills enrollment among immigrant-headed households, reducing coverage AND reducing Medicaid office contact with NVRA-eligible populations

**The Work Requirement Litigation Gap**:
As of May 2026, no federal court challenge has been filed directly against the OBBBA work requirement provision itself. This gap exists for structural reasons:

1. The interim final rule is not yet issued (due June 1, 2026) — there is no rule to challenge
2. The statute itself exempts the IFR from notice and comment, removing the most common APA procedural challenge
3. The law does not take effect until January 1, 2027 — standing to challenge implementation before first enforcement is contested
4. Organizations planning litigation are waiting for the June 2026 IFR before filing, to challenge specific implementation guidance rather than the statute's face

**The IFR Notice-and-Comment Exemption as Structural Insulation**:
The OBBBA statute provides that the HHS interim final rule implementing work requirements "shall not be subject to the requirements of section 553" of the Administrative Procedure Act — the notice-and-comment rulemaking requirement. This is Congress's explicit elimination of public comment rights on the implementing rule. Legal scholars have identified this as a significant procedural anomaly: Congress rarely eliminates APA notice-and-comment for rules affecting millions of Americans. Potential legal argument: this exemption, if applied to a rule that makes significant substantive changes beyond the statute's text, may still face constitutional challenge as violating due process rights of affected individuals. However, this argument faces high bar at the current 6-3 SCOTUS.

**Post-*Loper Bright* Landscape for Future Work Requirement Litigation**:
When litigation does arrive — and it will, within months of January 2027 enforcement — courts will apply *Loper Bright*'s framework: What is the best meaning of the statute? This cuts in different directions:

- For challengers: Courts must independently determine whether OBBBA's work requirement definitions (incapacity, caregiver exemption, community engagement activities) are implemented consistently with the statute's text — not whether HHS's interpretation is "reasonable." Narrow implementation guidance could be struck as exceeding statutory text.
- For government: The work requirement provision is new statutory language, not ambiguous pre-existing regulation. Courts applying textualism to new clear statutory text are less likely to invalidate than under prior Chevron posture where "reasonable" interpretation was the standard.

**The NVRA Litigation Vector (Most Underdeveloped)**:
No organization has yet filed a challenge directly connecting Medicaid disenrollment to NVRA Section 7 voter registration obligations. The theory: If work requirement-driven disenrollment reduces state Medicaid office capacity below the level necessary to offer meaningful voter registration assistance, states are in NVRA violation. This argument requires:

1. Evidence that specific Medicaid offices have reduced voter registration output following OBBBA implementation
2. Causal connection between OBBBA and the capacity reduction
3. Standing for voter registration organizations to sue under NVRA Section 7

The evidentiary record for this argument will not exist until after January 2027 implementation. It is a medium-term litigation vector, not an immediate challenge.

## Section VI: Advocacy Architecture — The June 2026 Window

### The Closing Window

**June 1, 2026**: HHS must issue the interim final rule defining:
- Which populations qualify for exemptions (pregnant, caregiver, incapacitated, student)
- How "good faith effort" is defined for states seeking 2028 extension
- What data sources states must use for compliance verification
- What outreach requirements apply before first enforcement
- What appeal rights exist for disenrolled enrollees

**The critical insight**: Because the interim final rule is not subject to notice and comment, the standard advocacy strategy — submit public comments during 30–60 day comment period, build record for APA challenge — does not apply. The comment period does not exist. This means:

1. Advocacy must target HHS directly through channels other than formal comment (congressional testimony, media, direct agency engagement)
2. Post-rule litigation must focus on substantive legal defects in the rule itself rather than procedural notice failures
3. The June 1 deadline means advocacy pressure must be applied before the rule is issued, not during a post-publication comment window

### What the June 2026 Rule Can Still Do

Even without public comment, the rule's drafters face legal constraints. HHS cannot use the IFR to:
- Expand the work requirement to populations not covered by the statute (e.g., disabled individuals already excluded by statute)
- Define exemptions more narrowly than the statute's text permits
- Issue implementation standards that are facially arbitrary (post-*Loper Bright*, courts review agency interpretations de novo; arbitrary implementations are still challengeable)
- Eliminate the appeal rights that Medicaid's existing procedural architecture provides

Broad exemption definitions — particularly a generous "incapacity" standard and a broad caregiver exemption that includes extended family caregiving — are within HHS's authority and would reduce projected coverage loss by hundreds of thousands. This is where advocacy pressure before June 1 can affect outcomes.

### Congressional and State-Level Advocacy Leverage Points

**Congressional**: Repeal of the work requirement provision requires 50 Senate votes (budget reconciliation) — currently infeasible with Republican trifecta. But several Republican senators represent states with large at-risk hospital networks (Alaska: Murkowski, Collins; West Virginia: Capito; Maine: Collins). The 196 at-risk hospitals in Republican districts provide a constituent-interest pressure point. The political analysis: rural hospital closures are visible, attributable, and politically costly. This is the pressure point most likely to produce Republican defections.

**State-level**: 
- States can choose to implement broad exemption definitions before federal guidance requires them to tighten
- States can implement AVR for Medicaid enrollment before the voter registration infrastructure contracts further
- States facing large coverage loss projections — New Mexico, Oregon, West Virginia (20%+ enrollment reduction projected) — have their own political incentives to challenge implementation aggressively
- State legislatures in expansion states can pass Medicaid protection measures; ballot initiatives in Arizona, Colorado, Nevada, Montana, Ohio could enshrine Medicaid access protections independent of state legislatures

**Administrative comment post-rule**: Even though the statute exempts the IFR from notice-and-comment, HHS has historically accepted informal comments on interim final rules and incorporated feedback in final rules. The IFR is not the final rule — a final rule will follow, and that final rule may be subject to standard APA procedures. Organizations should develop substantive technical comments for the post-IFR final rule process.

## Section VII: The Long-Game Analysis — Health Disparities as Democratic Exclusion

### The Comparative Democracy Framework

No other developed democracy ties healthcare access to work requirements. The OECD health database documents zero peer nations that implement work-as-condition-of-healthcare across their covered populations. European systems respond to cost pressures through price controls and efficiency measures; work requirements are recognized internationally as producing coverage loss without health or employment improvement.

The relevant comparative precedent is not healthcare policy but democratic degradation. In Peru's Fujimori period (1990s), administrative capacity reductions in rural areas — affecting voting infrastructure, public service access, and identity documentation systems — operated as functional voter suppression without requiring explicit disenfranchisement law. Hungary's Orbán government has reduced funding for municipal services in opposition-majority areas to create administrative barriers. The mechanism — reducing state service capacity in specific communities — is a recognized autocratization vector documented by International IDEA and the V-Dem Institute.

The OBBBA mechanism is not the same as direct voter suppression. But it operates through the same administrative logic: reduce state capacity for services in targeted communities, and political exclusion follows as a structural consequence without requiring an explicit political intent.

### Recovery Precedent

South Korea's 1987 democratic recovery from Park Chung-hee's dictatorial successor regimes included rapid expansion of healthcare access to rural and working-class areas as a legitimating signal of democratic institutions. Uruguay's post-dictatorship recovery (1985–) prioritized healthcare access expansion as equity repair. The theory: democratic institutions must deliver tangible benefits to marginalized populations to earn legitimacy. Contracting those benefits during a period of democratic stress is not only harmful policy; it is counter-productive to democratic recovery.

The inverse follows: A democratic recovery program in the United States cannot focus solely on electoral mechanics (redistricting, voting rights, campaign finance) while the health infrastructure of low-income and majority-minority communities is actively contracting. The healthcare access and democratic participation crises are the same crisis, operating through the same communities, on the same timeline.
