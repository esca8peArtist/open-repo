## The Civil Servant's Position

This guide is different from the others in this series. The other guides address institutions that are outside government and can publicly oppose the administration's actions. Career civil servants are inside government — their legal obligations, their employment rights, and their professional ethics create a different set of constraints and a different set of opportunities.

The fundamental principle: career civil servants have legal rights and legal obligations that are separate from the political preferences of whatever administration is in power. The Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, the Hatch Act, the Whistleblower Protection Act, and the constitutional due process guarantee of the employment property interest all create specific protections that are legally defensible regardless of administration pressure.

The fundamental constraint: civil servants are not free agents. They have obligations to follow lawful directives from supervisors. The critical distinction — which this guide addresses directly — is between a lawful directive (which must be followed) and an unlawful directive (which the civil servant is legally obligated not to follow, and which carries whistleblower protection when reported).

**This guide is not legal advice for any individual civil servant's specific situation.** Before acting on any of the specific steps in this guide, consult with your union's legal representative, the agency's IG, or a private attorney who specializes in federal employment law.

## What This Crisis Looks Like From Inside Government

The Domain 26 analysis and the Domain 6 immigration court case study document what institutional capture looks like from within:

In immigration courts (Domain 6, Section 2.2): 100+ immigration judges appointed under Democratic administrations were fired; 600+ military lawyers deployed as temporary judges without immigration law experience; work requirements eliminated that had provided minimal process protections. Immigration judges who continued to apply the law as written were reassigned or removed. The courts were captured by changing the personnel, not the statutes.

In DOGE-targeted agencies (Domain 2): Career employees were presented with directives to access data systems outside their job scope, to share confidential information across agency lines without authorization, and to implement policies without the required rulemaking procedures. The April 2026 Fourth Circuit disclosure (AFSCME v. SSA) documented that DOJ admitted DOGE associates provided "patently false information to the district court" — meaning civil servants with knowledge of this had information that was directly relevant to ongoing contempt proceedings.

In DOJ (Domain 29): Career prosecutors have faced pressure to initiate or continue politically motivated investigations. The Nashville vindictive prosecution case (Judge Crenshaw's "realistic likelihood" finding — Domain 29) documented a case where a career prosecutor's assessment of the evidence was overridden by political appointees.

The pattern: institutional capture proceeds through personnel replacement, procedural bypass, and pressure on remaining career staff to either comply or leave. Civil servants who document what is happening, maintain their professional obligations, and use the legal protections available to them are the most effective internal defenders of institutional integrity.

## Crisis 4: Civil Service Targeting — The Primary Crisis for This Audience

### Crisis Scenario

A Schedule F-equivalent executive order reclassifies your position, or a mass firing targets your agency. Or: you are receiving directives that you believe are unlawful, and you face pressure to comply or be fired.

---

### 72-Hour Response: Individual Rights

**Step 1: Know your employment category (hours 0-2)**

Your legal protections depend on your employment category:

- **Competitive service** (most career civil servants): You have full Civil Service Reform Act protection, including the right to appeal adverse actions to the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB). Even under the current MSPB quorum problem (the Wilcox firing, Domain 6, Section 1.1, has created MSPB dysfunction), you retain the right to file an appeal and to pursue direct federal court action if MSPB fails to adjudicate your case within the statutory deadline.

- **Schedule F / Schedule Policy/Career reclassification**: If you have been reclassified, your first action is to consult an attorney. The reclassification itself is being challenged in litigation (AFGE v. OPM, various circuits). Your appeal rights depend on whether the reclassification was legally valid — and the litigation suggests it was not.

- **Excepted service**: Narrower appeal rights, but not zero. The specific agency's personnel rules and any applicable collective bargaining agreement govern your rights.

**Step 2: Contact your union within 2 hours of receiving any adverse action notice**

If you are in a union-covered position:
- AFGE (American Federation of Government Employees): afge.org | 202-737-8700 | Legal Defense Fund: 202-639-6424
- NTEU (National Treasury Employees Union): nteu.org | 202-572-5500
- NFFE-IAM (National Federation of Federal Employees): nffe.org
- NWSEO (National Weather Service Employees Organization): nwseo.org
- AFSCME (state and local government workers): afscme.org

Your union representative is your first call — before you respond to any supervisor, before you sign any document, and before you clear out your desk. The union's attorney has seen your situation before and knows the specific procedural steps that protect your rights.

**Step 3: Document everything before you lose access**

If you anticipate removal, create a personal record (on personal, not government, equipment or storage) of:

- Any directives you received that you believed were unlawful, with dates, names of the directing officials, and the specific content
- Any data access requests or cross-agency data sharing that occurred outside normal protocols
- Any pressure to violate established procedures, rulemaking requirements, or specific statutes
- Any instructions to destroy records, delete data, or remove documentation
- Your own performance record, commendations, and the absence of any performance-based justification for your removal

This documentation is the foundation of your legal case and potentially the whistleblower disclosure that constrains what the administration can do next.

**Do not remove classified information or government property.** Documents you can preserve: your own performance records, emails sent to or from your official account that you were a party to, and notes you took contemporaneously of meetings and directives. Consult your attorney before removing anything from the agency network.

**Step 4: Secure channels for sensitive communication**

If you are documenting unlawful government conduct, do not use government email or government devices to communicate with your attorney, union representative, or family about it. Use Signal (signal.org) for encrypted personal communications. Your government email is likely monitored and is discoverable in litigation.

### 72-Hour Response: Whistleblower Protection

**What qualifies for whistleblower protection**

The Whistleblower Protection Act (5 U.S.C. 2302) protects career civil servants who disclose:

1. A violation of law, rule, or regulation
2. Gross mismanagement
3. Gross waste of funds
4. Abuse of authority
5. A substantial and specific danger to public health or safety

The DOGE data-sharing cases (AFSCME v. SSA — litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 3.1) involve all five categories: violations of federal privacy law, gross mismanagement of sensitive data systems, waste of funds (unauthorized Cloudflare server use), abuse of authority (accessing SSA data without authorization), and substantial danger to public safety (immigration enforcement using tax data filed voluntarily).

**Who to report to**

Report to the following, in this order of preference:

1. **Inspector General**: Every major federal agency has an IG office. The IG is independent of agency management (though not perfectly — IG directors are presidential appointees who can be fired). The IG hotline is the most common whistleblower intake channel. IGs investigate and report to Congress. Find your agency's IG at ignet.gov.

2. **Office of Special Counsel (OSC)**: OSC investigates whistleblower retaliation and can order reinstatement of fired whistleblowers. Contact: osc.gov | 202-254-3600 | 800-872-9855. OSC's director is a presidential appointee; OSC's independence has been somewhat compromised, but the intake process is still legally protective.

3. **Congressional oversight committees**: Directly communicating with congressional committee staff is legally protected as a form of whistleblowing for federal employees, regardless of OSC or IG involvement. Senate and House Oversight Committee staff contact information: Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee (minority staff for opposition party members), House Oversight Committee (minority staff). You do not need to go through agency channels to contact Congress.

4. **Inspectors General Council (CIGIE)**: If your agency's IG is compromised or has been removed, report to CIGIE (ignet.gov). CIGIE can reassign investigations to another IG.

**The False Claims Act (qui tam) for financial fraud**

If the unlawful conduct involves financial fraud against the government — including misappropriation of federal funds, billing fraud in federal programs, or financial misrepresentation in contracts — the False Claims Act allows private citizens (including federal employees) to bring suit on behalf of the government and receive a percentage of any recovery. FCA qui tam suits are filed under seal by private attorneys. Contact: Taxpayers Against Fraud (taf.org) for qui tam attorney referrals.

### 30-Day Response: Sustaining While Litigation Proceeds

**MSPB appeal and federal court backup**

File your MSPB appeal within 30 days of an adverse action (this is the statutory deadline — missing it forfeits your right to MSPB review). The MSPB is currently dysfunctional due to the quorum problem (Domain 6, Section 1.1). If MSPB fails to adjudicate your appeal within 120 days, you have the right to proceed directly to federal district court. Your union attorney should file the MSPB appeal and simultaneously prepare the federal court complaint for filing at the 120-day mark.

**Reinstatement through TRO**

In cases of mass, politically motivated firing, unions and civil rights organizations have successfully obtained court orders reinstating fired employees pending litigation. The theory: the firing violated federal law, the employee has a property interest in continued employment under due process, and the balance of equities favors reinstatement. This is not available in every case, but for clearly unlawful Schedule F reclassifications, the preliminary injunction / TRO route is viable. The AFGE v. BOP case in D. Conn. (April 30, 2026 hearing — flagged in PROJECTS.md monitoring section) is a live example.

**Financial support while case proceeds**

A federal court case takes months to years. While it proceeds, you need financial support:
- Union legal services: Covered for union members in most circumstances
- Emergency assistance: Government Employee Solidarity Fund (through AFGE)
- GoFundMe: Public crowdfunding for political firing victims has been effective (several DOGE firing victims raised significant funds through advocacy organization amplification)
- Legal aid organizations: National Employment Law Project (nelp.org), Partnership for Civil Justice (pcjf.org) for politically motivated firings

### 90-Day Response: Institutional Strengthening

**Testifying in congressional proceedings**

If you are a fired civil servant with a documented record of politically motivated termination, congressional Democratic staff will want to hear from you. Minority members can invite witnesses to unofficial hearings, congressional forums, and member briefings. Testifying about your experience is legally protected (congressional privilege covers testimony before Congress), and it creates a public record that constrains future conduct.

Before testifying: consult your attorney about what you can and cannot say given any pending litigation, and confirm with your union that your testimony will not create complications for your employment case.

**Connecting with parallel institution networks**

The parallel institutions network (parallel-institutions.md) identifies organizations that are absorbing functions being lost in the federal government. If your expertise was in a specific technical area being gutted, consider whether a state agency, university research center, or civil society organization could use that expertise. Several former EPA scientists have joined state environmental agencies; former CFPB staff have joined state AG consumer protection divisions; former immigration judges dismissed from immigration courts have joined immigration legal aid organizations.

This is not abandoning the federal civil service — it is preserving institutional expertise while legal challenges to wrongful terminations proceed.

## The Unlawful Order: How to Recognize and Respond

The most common civil service crisis is not mass firing — it is the unlawful directive. A supervisor tells you to do something you believe is illegal. What are your obligations?

**The Nuremberg defense does not protect you, but neither does reflexive compliance.**

Federal law is clear: "Following orders" is not a defense to a criminal charge. If you implement a directive that violates federal law — a privacy law violation, a financial fraud, destruction of federal records — you can be personally prosecuted. This is the stick.

The carrot: refusing to implement an unlawful directive is protected by the Whistleblower Protection Act, provided you document the refusal, report it through appropriate channels, and follow proper procedures.

**The three-step procedure for an unlawful directive:**

1. **Consult**: Before refusing or complying, consult with your union representative and/or the agency's legal counsel. Confirm that the directive is in fact unlawful — not merely one you disagree with. There is a meaningful difference between a directive that violates a specific statute (unlawful) and a policy you think is bad (lawful to implement).

2. **Document**: Create a contemporaneous written record of the directive, who gave it, when, and in what context. This is done on personal equipment, not government systems.

3. **Report**: Report to your IG, your union, and — if the issue rises to the level of the Whistleblower Protection Act's covered disclosures — to OSC and Congress. Do not simply refuse in isolation without filing a protective report.

**The specific situations that are clearly unlawful:**

- Directive to share confidential taxpayer information (26 U.S.C. 6103) with another agency without the required authorization: Unlawful. The IRS-ICE data sharing litigation (litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 3.2) documented 42,695 adjudicated violations — each was the result of specific employees implementing a directive that violated federal law.

- Directive to destroy federal records: Unlawful under the Federal Records Act (44 U.S.C. 3101) and potentially obstruction of justice (18 U.S.C. 1519). Specific reporting channel: the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) has independent authority over federal records; report to NARA's IG.

- Directive to access data systems outside your authorized scope: Potentially unlawful under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. 1030) and the Privacy Act (5 U.S.C. 552a). Document the specific access requested, the specific system, and the stated purpose.

- Directive to implement a policy without required notice-and-comment rulemaking: Unlawful under the APA (5 U.S.C. 553). The appropriate response: formal comment to the agency's legal counsel in writing that the implementation requires rulemaking, and report to the IG if the directive proceeds over that objection.

## State Civil Servants: Cooperative Federalism Defense

State civil servants administering federal cooperative programs (Medicaid, SNAP, environmental enforcement, child protective services, workforce development) are in a different legal position from federal employees — but face analogous pressures.

**The cooperative federalism obligation**

State agencies that administer federal programs have independent legal obligations under the terms of the federal-state cooperative agreement. If the federal agency partner (HHS for Medicaid, EPA for environmental, USDA for SNAP) issues a directive that violates the terms of the cooperative agreement or that requires the state agency to violate state law, the state agency has independent legal grounds to refuse.

**Contact your state AG's office first**

If you are a state civil servant receiving a federal directive that you believe violates your cooperative agreement or state law, contact your state agency's general counsel and the state AG's office before complying. The state AG coalition (crisis-response-ag-coalition.md) is specifically organized to support state agencies resisting unlawful federal directives. Several AG offices (Massachusetts, New York, California, Maryland) have standing emergency protocols for exactly this situation.

**The anti-commandeering doctrine as protection**

*Printz v. United States* and *New York v. United States* established that the federal government cannot compel state officials to administer federal programs. A federal directive that requires state employees to implement a federal policy that violates state law is constitutionally unenforceable. Your state AG can provide a formal opinion confirming this in your specific situation — request it in writing.

**Document everything, as with federal employees**

The same documentation principles apply: personal equipment, contemporaneous records, union or association representative contact before responding to directives you believe are unlawful.

## The Long View: Why Civil Service Defense Matters

The Domain 6 immigration court case study is the most complete example of what happens when civil service defense fails. Immigration courts — Article I administrative courts within the executive branch — lost their independence in less than 12 months: 100+ judges fired, 600+ military lawyers deployed without immigration expertise, work requirements eliminated. The result is documented in asylum grant rates (declining), case backlogs (increasing), and legal error rates (rising). The human cost is borne by the people in immigration proceedings who depend on legally competent adjudication.

The same process in any major agency — EPA, CFPB, NLRB, FDA, DOJ career staff — would produce analogous results: institutional expertise replaced by political loyalty, legal standards subordinated to political outcomes, the people who depend on those institutions left without functional protection.

Career civil servants who maintain their professional obligations, document unlawful directives, use the whistleblower protection system, and support the legal challenges that unions and AGs are filing are doing the most consequential defensive work available to any institution in the current crisis. The legal infrastructure around them exists to protect that work — use it.
