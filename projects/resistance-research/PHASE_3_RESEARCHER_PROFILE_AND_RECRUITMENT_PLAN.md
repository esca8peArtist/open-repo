# Phase 3 Researcher Profile and Recruitment Plan

**Purpose**: This document is the operational guide for recruiting one or two researchers to execute Phase 3 research (Domain K and Domain H) beginning November 4, 2026. It is written for the user or coordinator executing outreach — all decisions are pre-made; execution is the only remaining work.

---

## Most Important Finding Up Front

The target researcher for Phase 3 is not a generalist academic writer. The work requires someone who can navigate dense primary legal source material (bill texts, court opinions, CRS reports), synthesize it at advocacy-ready quality, and produce 10,000–20,000-word deliverables on a defined weekly schedule. The strongest candidates are advanced-stage PhD students or recent PhD graduates in political science, constitutional law, or public law who have published in peer-reviewed venues and have prior exposure to democracy reform advocacy — not exclusively academic theory. The recruitment window is tight (8 working days of active outreach, September 15–23) and the decision is binary: hire by October 1 or execute the 2-person fallback.

## Section 1: Target Researcher Profile

### Core Credentials (Required — Non-Negotiable)

**Academic standing**: PhD candidate in final year (dissertation defended or under committee review) or PhD recipient within the last four years, in one of the following fields:
- Political science with constitutional law or judicial politics specialization
- Legal studies / law and society (JD-PhD or pure PhD tracks)
- Public law, comparative politics, or international relations with democratic governance focus
- History of constitutional development (as secondary option if legal expertise is strong)

A JD alone without a research track record is insufficient. The work requires research synthesis skills developed through academic training, not legal practice skills. A practicing attorney with a publications record in academic or policy venues is an exception to consider case by case.

**Publication record**: At least two peer-reviewed publications or one peer-reviewed publication plus two substantial policy reports (minimum 5,000 words, externally published) in the last three years. The publication requirement screens for demonstrated writing productivity under deadline. Unpublished dissertation chapters do not substitute for this unless they are under active journal review.

**Substantive domain knowledge**: Demonstrated familiarity with at least one of the following:
- Federal judiciary structure, Supreme Court reform, or judicial ethics literature
- Constitutional design, amendment theory, or structural democratic reform
- International democratic backsliding and comparative constitutional systems (Germany, Ireland, New Zealand models are directly relevant to Domain H)
- US democratic erosion literature (V-Dem, Polity, Freedom House frameworks are used throughout Domain H and K)

**Timeline availability**: Can commit 10–15 hours per week for 13 consecutive weeks (November 4 – January 31, 2027). This is a hard constraint. Researchers with teaching loads that cannot be reduced below 50% of normal, or with dissertation defenses, book manuscript deadlines, or job market commitments scheduled inside this window, are disqualifying — not because they are bad researchers but because the schedule will not hold.

### Preferred Credentials (Strongly Weighted)

**Advocacy-adjacent experience**: Has worked with or consulted for democracy reform organizations (Brennan Center, Fix the Court, ACS, Protect Democracy, Democracy Docket, Constitutional Accountability Center, ACLU, common cause chapters, state-level democratic reform coalitions). This experience indicates the researcher can write at the intersection of academic rigor and policy utility — the hybrid style Phase 3 requires.

**Familiarity with primary legal source navigation**: Comfortable working with Congress.gov bill texts, CRS reports, SCOTUSblog case archives, PACER court documents, and state legislative databases without instruction. This is a practical efficiency issue: a researcher who needs to be taught how to navigate these sources will lose 15–20 hours of the 120-hour total on orientation.

**Published on deadline**: Work history shows delivered writing to external clients, journals, or organizations on a defined schedule. Academic productivity on self-directed timelines is not equivalent. The Phase 3 schedule is a client-facing deadline structure — Week 1 deliverables are due on a specific date regardless of how the research is going.

**Geographic time zone**: Eastern or Central US time zones strongly preferred for synchronous check-in flexibility. Pacific and Mountain time zones are workable with schedule adjustment. International researchers are viable only if they have established US academic or advocacy networks and can reach key contacts during US business hours (9 AM–5 PM Eastern, Mon–Fri).

### Vetting Criteria for Interview

Candidates who pass the initial credentials screen are evaluated on four dimensions:

**Writing sample quality** (reviewed before interview): Ask for one published work (or a recent final draft) of at least 4,000 words on a constitutional, democratic governance, or legal topic. Evaluate: Does the writing lead with findings rather than methodology? Is primary source citation accurate? Does the argument build to a clear actionable conclusion? Is the prose readable by a non-specialist? Reject candidates whose samples are primarily methodological, exclusively theoretical, or whose citation practices are sloppy (check 3–5 citations against primary sources).

**Timeline stress test** (asked in interview): "Describe the most compressed writing project you've completed in the last two years — what was the deadline, how many words, and what did you produce?" Acceptable answer: a deliverable of 5,000+ words produced in under 3 weeks with defined external accountability. Red flag: only self-directed projects with flexible timelines.

**Domain orientation** (asked in interview): "What do you know about the current state of judicial reform legislation in Congress, and what are the main constitutional objections to statutory term limits?" The interview is not an examination — it evaluates whether the researcher has baseline orientation that allows them to enter the work at the level the domains require, or whether orientation week will consume 20–30 hours that are not in the budget.

**Collaboration style** (asked in interview): "How do you prefer to receive feedback on draft writing — in real time, in annotated form, or after a full draft is complete?" Phase 3 requires the researcher to receive substantive feedback on zone drafts and incorporate it before moving to the next zone. Researchers who struggle with editorial redirection or who frame feedback as a judgment of their work quality rather than a collaborative refinement tool create schedule risk. Look for researchers who describe iteration positively.

## Section 2: Capacity Allocation Options

Two configurations are viable. The decision between them should be made by October 1 based on which candidates are available, not on theoretical preference.

### Option A: Solo Researcher (1 Person, 160–200 Hours)

**Structure**: One researcher executes Domains K and H in sequence. Domain K (legislative inventory and feasibility analysis) runs November 4 – December 21 (7 weeks, approximately 90–105 hours). Domain H (constitutional resilience architecture research and distribution-prep) runs December 22 – January 31 (6 weeks, approximately 70–95 hours). Total: 160–200 hours at 12–15 hours per week.

**Confidence in timeline completion**: 85–90%. The solo configuration depends entirely on one person's output consistency. The main failure mode is a researcher who performs strongly in Week 1–3 and then encounters a competing obligation (a journal revision request, an unexpected teaching demand, a family situation) in Week 6–8. This is not a quality risk — it is a schedule risk.

**Advantages**: 
- Single researcher develops deep contextual continuity across both domains; Domain K and H are companion documents that share sources and framing, and a solo researcher carries that integration in their head rather than needing to coordinate it across two people
- No coordination overhead (no weekly sync between co-researchers, no overlap deconfliction)
- Faster orientation: one person is onboarded once rather than twice
- Lower total management burden on the user or coordinator

**Disadvantages**: 
- Single point of failure: if the researcher drops out after Week 6, recovery requires emergency recruitment into an already-active project
- No parallel-track execution: Domain K's Zone 1 (Legislative Inventory) and Zone 3 (International Precedent) can run simultaneously, but only if there are two researchers; solo execution is sequential

**Recommended when**: A strong candidate with a demonstrated completion record is identified by September 22 and expresses genuine commitment to the full 13-week window.

### Option B: Two-Person Team (2 People, 80–100 Hours Each)

**Structure**: Researcher A executes Domain K (zones 1–4, approximately 80–90 hours across 12 weeks). Researcher B executes Domain H (sections 1–6, approximately 80–90 hours across 10 weeks, beginning November 18 to allow the user to conduct initial onboarding sequentially). Total researcher-hours: 160–180. Total calendar time: November 4 – January 31.

**Confidence in timeline completion**: 88–92%. The 2-person configuration's higher confidence ceiling comes from redundancy: if one researcher encounters a schedule disruption, the other domain continues uninterrupted. The disrupted researcher's zone output can be partially recovered by compressing later zones or reassigning one zone to the other researcher.

**Advantages**:
- Schedule resilience: a Week 6 disruption to one researcher does not halt the project
- Parallel zone execution: Domain K Zone 1 (legislative inventory) and Domain H Section 1 (constitutional design analysis) can run simultaneously in Weeks 2–4, compressing the overall timeline
- Distributes the motivational burden — two researchers produce a social accountability structure neither solo researcher has

**Disadvantages**:
- Coordination overhead: requires a shared reference document, weekly sync to deconflict source use, and explicit domain boundary definitions so both researchers do not develop overlapping analyses of the same materials (Domain K and H share six primary sources including Levinson, the Presidential Commission, and the Germany Basic Law comparative materials)
- Higher onboarding cost: two separate orientation sessions, two separate writing standard reviews
- Integration risk: the final research outputs must read as companion documents with consistent voice and citation standards; two researchers writing in parallel require an explicit integration pass before distribution

**Recommended when**: The solo recruitment process produces an unresolved candidate situation by September 25 — meaning either no strong solo candidate has emerged or the leading solo candidate has given equivocal timeline commitment signals. The 2-person fallback preserves the November 4 launch regardless of individual researcher availability uncertainty.

### Recommendation

**Execute solo recruitment first, with the 2-person option as a standing fallback.** Begin September 15 outreach as if hiring one person. If by September 25 no candidate with strong credentials and firm timeline commitment has emerged, immediately activate the 2-person track. The 2-person track requires a second round of outreach to a partially different candidate pool (researchers who cannot commit 12–15 hours per week can commit 8–10 hours per week and are viable for the 2-person structure). Extend offers on October 1 regardless of which configuration is active.

## Section 3: Recruitment Channels

These ten channels are listed in priority order. The first three are the highest-yield per unit of outreach time. Channels 4–10 are backup and parallel-track channels for widening the candidate pool if the first wave does not produce three or more strong candidates by September 18.

### Channel 1: Direct Outreach to Existing Phase 2 Contacts with Research Staff

Several Phase 2 contacts have research staff, fellows, or affiliated academics who are known quantities in the democracy reform space. These contacts can either be directly recruited (if they have independent capacity) or can make warm introductions to candidates in their extended networks.

**Brennan Center for Justice** (judiciary program and democracy program): The Brennan Center employs research fellows, postdoctoral fellows, and senior research fellows who have academic backgrounds. Its judiciary program is directly focused on the exact subject matter of Domain K. Current or recently-departed Brennan Center research staff are the single most targeted candidate pool for this project. The Brennan Center has posted research fellow and postdoctoral fellow positions through 2026 (Research Fellow/Senior Research Fellow, Judiciary Program; Postdoctoral Research Fellow positions are known to cycle out after 2-year terms). Contact: Current Brennan Center judiciary program director; ask for referrals to fellows nearing end of term or recent alumni.

**American Constitution Society** (ACS): ACS employs research and policy staff and maintains relationships with constitutional law academics across its 250+ law school chapters. The ACS 2026 Next Generation Leaders cohort was announced in spring 2026 — these are early- to mid-career academics and advocates. Contact: ACS national office (acslaw.org); ask whether any Next Generation Leaders or recent ACS Issue Brief authors are available for consulting or research collaboration.

**Fix the Court**: Fix the Court is the primary Domain K advocacy organization and is directly positioned to know researchers working in the judicial reform space who might have independent research capacity. Contact: Fix the Court executive director Gabe Roth or deputy; frame the inquiry as a research collaboration rather than a job posting.

**Outreach script for Phase 2 contacts**: "We are launching Phase 3 research in November and are looking for a constitutional law researcher (PhD-level) with availability for 10–15 hours per week through January. Do you have any former fellows, research staff, or affiliated academics who you would recommend we speak with? We're doing targeted outreach rather than a public posting."

### Channel 2: Scholars Strategy Network (SSN)

SSN is a national membership organization of 2,000+ researchers across 47 states, founded by Theda Skocpol at Harvard, with an explicit focus on bridging academic research and democratic policy advocacy. SSN chapters are organized by state and by issue area; the democracy governance working group is the most relevant. SSN members are specifically self-selected for interest in public-facing research — which is the profile Phase 3 requires.

**Contact method**: Reach the SSN national office at scholars.org and ask to post to the democracy governance working group listserv. Frame the posting as a paid research consulting opportunity for an advanced PhD or postdoc with constitutional law or democratic governance expertise, 10–15 hours per week, November–January.

**Timing**: SSN listserv posts are processed within 3–5 business days. Post by September 15 for responses to begin arriving by September 18–20.

**Expected yield**: 5–12 inquiries from SSN's democracy governance constituency. Filter to 3–4 candidates with direct domain expertise for interview.

### Channel 3: APSA Constitutional Law and Jurisprudence Division

The APSA 2026 Annual Meeting is in Boston, September 3–6, 2026 — immediately preceding the recruitment window. The Constitutional Law and Jurisprudence Division organizes political scientists and legal scholars who work at the intersection of law and political science. APSA meeting attendance lists are not public, but the division's section officer listserv can distribute a one-page call for researcher interest.

**Contact method**: Email the APSA Constitutional Law and Jurisprudence section chair (chair contact is available through apsanet.org section directory). Ask to circulate a research opportunity notice to section members. The APSA general contact is apsa@apsanet.org for routing assistance.

**Best timing**: Post to the section listserv September 10–12 so interested attendees at the September 3–6 Annual Meeting can discuss the opportunity in person and follow up by September 15. This creates a pre-recruitment warm-up cycle.

**Secondary channel**: Post the same notice to the APSA Public Law section (distinct from Constitutional Law and Jurisprudence — different membership, broader).

### Channel 4: Harvard Ash Center Democracy Postdoctoral Fellowship Network

The Harvard Ash Center Democracy Postdoctoral Fellowships place postdoctoral scholars in two-year democracy research positions. Fellows whose terms are ending in Summer 2026 or beginning Fall 2026 are exactly the profile Phase 3 needs — advanced researchers with recent PhDs, democracy-focused research agendas, and typically some advocacy network exposure.

**Contact method**: Email the Ash Center program office (ash.harvard.edu/democracy-postdoctoral-fellowships) and ask if any current or recently-completed fellows are interested in supplementary research consulting work at 10–12 hours per week.

**Note on timing**: Ash Center fellows typically have principal employment commitments; this channel is best for researchers whose fellowship term is ending and who are in a transitional period before their next placement.

### Channel 5: NYU Law Global Fellows Program and Comparative Constitutional Law Network

NYU's Postdoctoral Global Fellows Program (which will open applications for 2027–2028 in October 2026) places researchers in comparative constitutional law, including the Mauro Cappelletti Global Fellow in Comparative Law track. Researchers who applied to the 2026–2027 cycle but were not selected are a strong target: they have demonstrated domain interest and have available time if they did not receive an institutional placement.

**Contact method**: Email NYU Law's Global and Comparative Law program office; ask if they can circulate a paid research opportunity notice to their applicant pool or prior fellows network. This is a less direct channel but covers comparative constitutional law researchers who are not visible through APSA.

### Channel 6: Kinder Institute on Constitutional Democracy (University of Missouri)

The Kinder Institute explicitly focuses on constitutional democracy and houses postdoctoral fellows. It is a smaller, more targeted network than APSA but directly on-domain.

**Contact method**: Email the Kinder Institute (democracy.missouri.edu) program coordinator; ask to post a research consulting opportunity notice.

### Channel 7: Democratic Erosion Collaborative

The Democratic Erosion Collaborative is an academic network with a course taught at 90+ universities, focused on pedagogy, data collection, and research synthesis on democratic erosion. Its faculty network includes exactly the kind of researchers Phase 3 needs — scholars who follow US democratic erosion empirically and who have public-facing academic communication experience.

**Contact method**: Contact through democratic-erosion.org; ask to post to their faculty and researcher network.

### Channel 8: UCLA Safeguarding Democracy Project

The UCLA Law Safeguarding Democracy Project promotes research, collaboration, and advocacy on free and fair elections. Its affiliated researchers are directly relevant to Phase 3 subject matter.

**Contact method**: Email the project director (law.ucla.edu/academics/centers/safeguarding-democracy-project); ask for referrals to affiliated researchers with capacity.

### Channel 9: Constitutional Accountability Center (CAC) Alumni

CAC's Douglas T. Kendall Fellowship is a one-year fellowship at $70,000 for recent law graduates. The 2026–2027 application cycle is now closed. Researchers who applied to the 2026–2027 Kendall Fellowship but were not selected — or researchers whose 2025–2026 Kendall Fellowship term is ending in Fall 2026 — are a specific target. CAC fellows work on constitutional text and history arguments with an advocacy orientation, which is close to Phase 3's Domain H requirements.

**Contact method**: Email CAC careers contact (theusconstitution.org/about-cac/careers); ask if they can informally circulate a research consulting inquiry to their recent Kendall Fellow network.

### Channel 10: Prior Phase 2 Coalition Contacts Who Expressed Research Interest

During Phase 2 distribution outreach, some contacts replied with substantive research engagement rather than adoption-pathway responses — academic affiliates of advocacy organizations, researchers at think tanks, or academics who followed up to discuss the domain content rather than ask about collaboration. These individuals are pre-screened for domain interest.

**Contact method**: Review Phase 2 email reply archives for contacts who mentioned their own research, asked substantive questions about the source methodology, or expressed interest in the research process. Email each directly with a personal note: "We are launching Phase 3 in November and are looking for a researcher with your background for a 13-week consulting engagement. Would you be available to discuss?"

**September 18–21**: First responses arrive; initial screening
- Review writing samples submitted with inquiries
- Conduct 15-minute phone screens with all candidates who pass writing sample review
- Identify 3–5 candidates to advance to full interview

**September 22–25**: Full interviews (45–60 minutes each)
- Timeline stress test, domain orientation questions, collaboration style assessment
- Request any additional writing samples if first sample was insufficient
- Check references informally (ask one question: "Would you recommend this researcher for a time-sensitive, high-volume research project?")

**September 26**: Decision meeting
- Rank candidates by credentials, timeline availability, and interview performance
- Confirm solo vs. 2-person configuration based on candidate pool
- If solo track: identify primary candidate and backup
- If 2-person track: identify both candidates and confirm domain assignments

**October 1**: Offers extended
- Send written offer to primary candidate(s) with rate, timeline, scope summary, and onboarding start date (November 4)
- Two-week decision window (October 1–15)
- If primary declines: immediately extend to backup candidate

**October 15**: Offer acceptance deadline
- All offers must be accepted by this date; no extensions (November 4 onboarding has no slack)
- If all primary candidates decline: extend to 2-person fallback configuration immediately; execute second-wave outreach to SSN and APSA with explicit 8–10 hours/week framing

**October 15 – November 3**: Pre-onboarding preparation
- Send researcher(s) the Phase 3 Onboarding Kit (PHASE_3_RESEARCHER_ONBOARDING_KIT.md) by October 18
- Researcher reads onboarding materials and submits 3 clarifying questions by October 25
- User answers questions by October 28
- Researcher confirms source access (JSTOR, ProQuest) by November 1
- Onboarding call (1 hour) on November 3 or 4: review zones, confirm Week 1 output targets

**November 4**: Research begins

## Section 6: Success Criteria for Recruitment

### Green Flags (Recruit Immediately)

- PhD conferred or under active committee review; dissertation in constitutional law, judicial politics, democratic governance, or comparative constitutional law
- Two or more peer-reviewed publications or published policy reports in the last three years, at least one of which is on a directly relevant topic (judiciary, constitutional design, democratic backsliding)
- Has produced a writing deliverable of 5,000+ words on a defined external deadline in the last two years
- Has prior working relationship with at least one Phase 2/3 contact organization (Brennan Center, Fix the Court, ACS, Democracy Docket, etc.) — indicates both domain knowledge and advocacy-writing fluency
- Timeline availability is unambiguous: no teaching load above 30% of normal during November–January, no pending dissertation defense, no journal manuscript deadlines in the window
- Writing sample leads with findings, uses primary sources accurately, and is readable by a non-specialist
- Interview demonstrates baseline familiarity with SCERT Act, term limits constitutional debate, or Domain H structural vulnerability analysis

### Red Flags (Eliminate or Require Documented Resolution)

- Only self-directed research history with no external accountability deadlines — this is not a disqualifier by itself but requires the interview to surface specific evidence of deadline-driven productivity
- Writing sample is primarily theoretical or methodological with no policy-facing analytical conclusions — Phase 3 requires hybrid writing and a purely academic writer will need significant redirection
- Equivocal timeline commitment ("I should be able to do it" rather than "I've confirmed I have the capacity") — accept only firm commitments; a candidate who is unsure should be moved to the 2-person track at lower hours/week
- No familiarity with primary legal source navigation — this is a training burden; it is not disqualifying if the researcher has strong domain knowledge and is willing to learn, but it must be factored into the Week 1 schedule as orientation time
- Previous work history shows a pattern of incomplete deliverables or scope expansion (a researcher who consistently produced more than requested but missed deadlines is a scope risk for a structured production timeline)
- No published work available for review — dissertation chapters in progress are insufficient; the writing standard cannot be assessed without an externally-reviewed product
