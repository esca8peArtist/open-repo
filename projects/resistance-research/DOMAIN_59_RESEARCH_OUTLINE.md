# Domain 59: Economic Precarity and Democratic Exclusion
## Research Outline — Staged for June 16 Production Handoff

*Pre-research pass: May 17, 2026. Full research window: June 16 – August 10, 2026.*

---

## Central Argument (One Paragraph)

Democratic participation is not a free activity, and 2024 proved it again. Among Americans earning under $50,000 annually, 42 percent did not vote in the 2024 presidential election; among those earning over $100,000, the non-voting rate was 16 percent. This 26-point gap is not an accident of preference or apathy: it is the systematic output of a political economy in which financial instability — wage garnishment from student loan defaults restarted January 2026, medical debt carried by 43 million Americans, housing costs that leave one in four renter households spending over half their income on rent, and childcare that consumes 32 percent of median household income — directly depletes the time, cognitive bandwidth, residential stability, and material resources that civic participation requires. The OBBBA (One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025) accelerated this exclusion: it simultaneously cut Medicaid for 10 million projected enrollees, reduced SNAP access for 22 million families, and ended childcare subsidies, stacking five concurrent reductions in civic capacity on the households already most constrained. Domain 59's contribution is naming this compounding mechanism: economic precarity as democratic design failure, not individual failure.

## Section 1: System Architecture — How Economic Precarity Is Structured as Exclusion

### 1.1 The Income-Participation Gap: Baseline Evidence

**The core empirical fact**: Among Americans earning under $50,000, 42 percent were non-voters in 2024 (PRRI 2024 data); among those earning over $100,000, non-voting was 16 percent. This 26-point gap has widened from the 2020 baseline and represents the largest income-based participation gap in any OECD democracy.

**The youth dimension**: 62 percent of non-voting young people (18-29) in 2024 reported sometimes or often finding it difficult to make ends meet (CIRCLE/Tufts 2024 survey). Financial insecurity is the strongest predictor of non-voting in the youngest cohort — stronger than political disinterest.

**International contrast**: Nordic democracies — Denmark, Sweden, Norway — maintain parliamentary election turnout above 80 percent paired with comprehensive welfare systems that eliminate the material barriers to participation. Sweden's turnout exceeds 87 percent; Denmark's 2024 European Parliament participation at 58 percent exceeded the EU average by 10 points despite a global decline in turnout. The mechanism: when basic material security is guaranteed, the opportunity cost of civic engagement drops dramatically.

**Research questions to answer in production**:
- What is the precise 2024 Census Bureau income-by-turnout breakdown (CPS Voting and Registration Supplement, November 2024)?
- How has the income-participation gap changed from 2020 to 2024 — widened, narrowed, or held?
- What does the OECD Better Life Index (2024–2025 edition) show about US civic engagement compared to Nordic peers?

### 1.2 Wage Stagnation as Structural Deprivation

The federal minimum wage has not increased since 2009 — 17 years of stagnation. As of 2025, EPI confirmed the federal minimum wage is officially a poverty wage; in the 20 states still at the federal floor, 11.8 million workers earn below $17 per hour. Meanwhile, the productivity-pay gap has widened continuously: if median household income had tracked productivity growth since 1979, it would be approximately $30,000 higher today (EPI, Wage Stagnation in Nine Charts).

Multi-job work has proliferated to compensate: 8.4 percent of the US workforce (approximately 13 million workers) holds two or more jobs, concentrated in households below $35,000 annual income. The American Time Use Survey shows that when paid work, commuting, and unpaid domestic labor are combined, low-income adults have fewer than 15 hours of genuinely discretionary time per week — insufficient for sustained civic engagement requiring 4-6 consolidated hours monthly.

**Research questions**:
- What is the current (2025-2026) BLS multi-job worker count and income distribution?
- How much discretionary time does the bottom income quintile have per week, controlling for caregiving obligations (ATUS data)?
- What is the EPI Nominal Wage Tracker showing for Q1 2026 at the 10th-percentile?

### 1.3 Housing Cost as Residential Instability and Registration Barrier

Nearly half of American renters are housing-cost-burdened (spending more than 30 percent of income on rent), and more than a quarter spend over 50 percent — the "severely cost-burdened" threshold. In major metros, cost-burdened renter households move every two to three years under financial pressure, with each move severing voter registration, organizational ties, and community social capital accumulated through years of local engagement.

Formal evictions are rising: cities including Nashville, Austin, and Greenville recorded 2025 filing rates 20-46 percent above their 2023-24 baselines. Beyond formal evictions, more than 14 million tenants report feeling pressured to move due to rent increases — "informal evictions" that may be five times more common than court filings.

The causal evidence is clear (Slee and Desmond, Politics and Society 2023): neighborhood eviction rates causally reduce voter turnout, with the effect concentrated in neighborhoods where eviction is less normalized (i.e., where it signals acute disruption). The PNAS 2024 housing mobility study found that induced residential mobility persistently depressed voting for nearly two decades among low-income Black and Hispanic adults who received housing vouchers to move — a finding that challenges the naive assumption that housing assistance automatically enables civic participation.

**Research questions**:
- What does the Urban Institute's 2026 housing affordability analysis (housingmatters.urban.org) show for key metros?
- What is the current National Low Income Housing Coalition data on affordable housing shortfall (units available per 100 extremely low-income renters)?
- What is the 2025-2026 Eviction Lab filing rate data for the highest-eviction jurisdictions?

### 1.4 Medical Debt and Bankruptcy as Civic Suppression

Medical debt is carried by 43 million Americans, totaling approximately $220 billion in outstanding obligations. Bankruptcy filings reached 591,850 in the 12 months ending March 31, 2026 — an 11.9 percent increase from 529,080 the prior period — with medical debt implicated in the majority of consumer bankruptcies. The OBBBA's Medicaid cuts (10 million projected enrollment losses by 2034, work requirements effective December 2026) will accelerate medical debt accumulation for populations whose coverage lapses.

The civic suppression mechanism operates through three channels: wage garnishment that further reduces disposable income (student loan default garnishment restarted January 2026, removing up to 15 percent of wages); cognitive load from debt management (8-15 hours monthly of phone negotiations, payment disputes, and documentation) that depletes the bandwidth available for political engagement; and credit destruction that forces geographic moves (loss of housing, inability to rent at preferred location) that break voter registration.

**Research questions**:
- What is the current CFPB data on medical debt collection practices and geographic distribution?
- What is the Urban Institute's estimate of post-OBBBA Medicaid lapse and resulting medical debt acceleration?
- What do the 591,850 bankruptcy filing data show about geographic concentration?

### 1.5 Student Loan Restart as 2026 Crisis Accelerant

The Trump administration restarted federal student loan collections in May 2025 and wage garnishment in January 2026. As of June 2025, approximately six million borrowers were 90+ days past due; the majority were projected to enter default by September 2025. Wage garnishment at 15 percent of wages, combined with existing income pressures, creates a sudden step-change increase in financial precarity for the households affected.

The Pressley-Booker-Warren bill (Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act of 2025) would suspend garnishment as a tool of student debt collection but has not passed — the January 2026 garnishment restart proceeded over congressional objection. This creates a direct, documented 2026 mechanism connecting federal policy to increased financial precarity.

**Research questions**:
- How many borrowers are currently in active garnishment as of May 2026 (DOE data)?
- What is the income distribution of borrowers entering default in 2025-2026?
- What is the estimated net income reduction per household from garnishment plus existing debt service?

## Section 2: Causal Pathways — How Precarity Suppresses Democratic Voice

This section must demonstrate causation, not merely correlation. The four primary pathways are supported by distinct empirical literatures; each should be anchored to at least one causal or quasi-experimental study.

### 2.1 The Time-Poverty Pathway

**Mechanism**: Wage stagnation + multi-job work + commute burden + childcare hours = insufficient discretionary time for sustained civic engagement.

**Causal anchor**: American Time Use Survey (ATUS) data establishes the time budget directly. The scarcity/bandwidth framework (Mullainathan and Shafir, Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much, 2013) provides the behavioral mechanism: financial precarity is not merely time-consuming in practical hours — it imposes a "bandwidth tax" that reduces cognitive capacity by an estimated 13-14 IQ point equivalent, affecting planning, self-control, and deliberative engagement. The tunneling effect directs attention exclusively toward the immediate financial threat, crowding out less urgent concerns — including civic participation.

**Research questions**:
- What does the ATUS show for average discretionary hours per week for the bottom income quintile, controlling for multi-job workers and caregiving obligations?
- How does the bandwidth tax (Mullainathan/Shafir) apply specifically to voter information processing and organizing capacity — are there any follow-on studies in political science?
- What does the CIRCLE 2024 survey show about financial insecurity as a predictor of youth non-voting?

### 2.2 The Housing-Instability-to-Registration-Lapse Pathway

**Mechanism**: Cost burden → frequent moves → voter registration lapse → organizational disconnection → community capital erosion → persistent non-participation.

**Causal anchors**: Slee and Desmond (Politics and Society 2023): eviction rates causally reduce turnout (quasi-experimental design using neighborhood variation). PNAS 2024 (housing voucher mobility study): induced moves caused persistent 20-year voting depression among low-income Black and Hispanic adults. Both studies are quasi-experimental with plausible causal identification.

**Key finding to develop**: Automatic voter registration (AVR) partially mitigates this pathway — states with AVR showed 49 percent average youth turnout versus 44 percent in states without. Same-day registration increases turnout among 18-24 year olds by 3.1-7.3 percentage points (Journal of Politics, 2022). The reform architecture is therefore directly responsive to the documented mechanism.

**Research questions**:
- What is the precise Slee/Desmond finding — effect size of eviction rate on turnout, and the identification strategy?
- How does the PNAS 2024 study characterize the 20-year persistence? Is it attributed to registration lapse alone or also to social capital disruption?
- What is Brennan Center's current count of states with AVR and their effectiveness data?

### 2.3 The Medical-Debt-Bandwidth Pathway

**Mechanism**: Medical crisis → debt accumulation → wage garnishment → cognitive load of debt management → reduced bandwidth for political engagement.

**Behavioral anchor**: Mullainathan/Shafir "tunneling" — a financial crisis of sufficient magnitude redirects attention so completely that civic engagement becomes cognitively inaccessible. This is not a preference or motivation failure; it is a resource allocation failure under genuine constraint.

**Research questions**:
- Is there direct survey evidence linking medical debt burden to reduced civic participation (Gallup, Pew, or PRRI)?
- What does the Commonwealth Fund show about medical debt's geographic concentration and its overlap with low-voter-turnout jurisdictions?

### 2.4 The Debt-Coercion Pathway

**Mechanism**: Wage garnishment + bank account seizure (student loans, payday loans) creates a surveillance-compliance dynamic in which financial institutions and government creditors have direct access to income streams, creating a chilling effect on organizing by workers who fear any destabilization of their already-precarious income.

**Specific 2026 development**: Student loan wage garnishment restarted January 2026 — up to 15 percent of wages — is a direct income surveillance mechanism. Payday loan lenders can garnish wages through court order, creating a private-sector dimension to the coercion. Workers in debt to both federal and private creditors face income uncertainty that makes sustained organizing — which requires time commitments that could affect job performance or income — materially dangerous.

**Research questions**:
- Is there sociological literature on wage garnishment as a social control mechanism (not just economic but behavioral/organizing)?
- What is the CFPB's 2025-2026 data on wage garnishment prevalence by income bracket?

### 2.5 The Stigma and Outsider-Status Pathway

**Mechanism**: Reliance on payday loans, SNAP, Medicaid, or eviction history creates social stigma that signals "outsider" status — reinforcing the perception that politics is for others, not for people in crisis. This is the least empirically developed pathway and should be treated carefully.

**Relevant literature**: Research on social trust and inequality (Uslaner and Brown, Russell Sage Foundation) documents that high inequality reduces interpersonal and institutional trust, which in turn reduces civic participation. The mechanism here is not just material but psychosocial: poverty signals exclusion from the polity, and that signal becomes self-fulfilling.

**Research questions**:
- What is the Scholars Strategy Network's framing of stigma as a barrier to working-class political engagement?
- Does the political science literature on political efficacy address financial distress as a predictor of low external efficacy?

## Section 3: 2026 Crisis Data — The Current Emergency

### 3.1 OBBBA as Compounding Accelerant

The OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025) made five simultaneous cuts to civic participation capacity that are multiplicative, not additive — each cut increases the impact of the others:

1. **Medicaid** (10 million projected enrollment losses by 2034; work requirements effective December 2026): Medicaid loss → medical debt → wage garnishment or asset loss → geographic instability → voter registration lapse
2. **SNAP** (22 million families lose some or all benefits; $106 billion cut over 10 years): SNAP reduction → food insecurity → increased multi-job work to compensate → time poverty acceleration
3. **Childcare subsidies** (reduction in CCDBG funding): childcare access loss → workforce exit for 4+ million women → paradoxically more time but loss of income and organizational networks
4. **Student loan protections** (SAVE plan eliminated): 6+ million borrowers enter default → garnishment pipeline
5. **Housing assistance** (OBBBA housing voucher reductions): housing instability acceleration → voter registration lapse

The compounding claim: a household affected by cuts 1 and 2 simultaneously faces medical debt AND food insecurity — they spend cognitive bandwidth managing both, reducing the tunneling threshold well below what either crisis alone would require. Add cut 4 (student loan default), and the household is now in three simultaneous financial emergencies. The civic participation capacity of this household is not reduced by 3x — it is reduced by a multiplier closer to 5-7x, because each crisis compounds the cognitive and temporal cost of the others.

**Research questions**:
- What does Urban Institute (2026) show about the overlap between Medicaid-dependent, SNAP-dependent, and student-loan-indebted households (i.e., how large is the multiply-burdened population)?
- What does CBPP's OBBBA distributional analysis show about the income concentration of these cuts?
- What does Georgetown CCF show about Medicaid work requirement projected implementation timeline and regional impact?

### 3.2 Housing Crisis 2026 Snapshot

- Nearly 50 percent of renters are cost-burdened
- More than 25 percent spend over half their income on housing
- Eviction filing rates 20-46 percent above baseline in key cities (Nashville +46%, Greenville +21%)
- 14 million tenants face informal eviction pressure from rent increases
- Affordable housing shortfall: the NLIHC "Gap" report shows fewer than 37 affordable units available per 100 extremely low-income renter households nationally

**Research questions**:
- What does the NLIHC 2025/2026 "Gap" report show as the current national affordable housing shortfall figure?
- What is the Eviction Lab's current data on filing rates for cities in the tracking network?

### 3.3 Student Loan Restart Crisis Timeline (2025-2026)

- April 2025: DOE announces resumption of student loan collections
- May 2025: Collections resume, including tax refund interception and Social Security benefit seizure
- June 2025: ~6 million borrowers 90+ days past due
- September 2025: Mass default projected for the majority of 90-day-past-due borrowers
- January 2026: Wage garnishment resumes (up to 15% of wages)
- Ongoing 2026: Garnishment continues for millions of defaulted borrowers, with no congressional resolution

**Research questions**:

- What is the geographic and income distribution of borrowers in default?

## Section 4: International Precedent — Economic Stabilization as Democratic Prerequisite

### 4.1 West Germany's Wirtschaftswunder and Democratic Consolidation

The most powerful comparative case is West Germany (1948–1960). When the Federal Republic was constituted in 1949, democratic consolidation was not just a political project — it was an economic one. Ludwig Erhard, as Economics Minister under Adenauer, implemented the Sozialmarktwirtschaft (social market economy): currency reform (June 1948), price control abolition, and a social safety net to protect households from the transition.

The argument for this domain is not that Germany's specific model should be replicated, but that democratic stabilization required economic stabilization as a prerequisite. Germans who were managing acute financial survival — hyperinflation, food scarcity, housing devastation — could not engage in democratic deliberation. The Wirtschaftswunder (economic miracle) that produced sustained GDP growth and rising living standards created the material conditions for democratic participation to become habitual. The CDU/SPD consensus on the social market economy endured through the 1960s precisely because it gave citizens a material stake in the democratic order.

The lesson for the US: economic precarity is not merely a policy failure — it is a democratic architecture failure. Restoring civic participation capacity requires addressing the material conditions that enable it.

**Research questions**:
- What does the Cambridge University Press (Abelshauser, "Rebuilding Germany") show about the causal relationship between Erhard's economic reforms and political stabilization?
- What does the Explaining History analysis ("Rising from the Ruins") show about the role of Wirtschaftswunder in identity reconstruction and democratic legitimacy?
- Are there other comparative cases? Nordic countries' post-WWII welfare state construction as democratic stabilization? FDR's New Deal as economic stabilization enabling democratic legitimacy?

### 4.2 Nordic Model: Economic Security as Civic Infrastructure

The Nordic countries (Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland) maintain parliamentary election turnout above 80 percent, far above the US average (65% in 2020, lower in 2024). The structural explanation: universal healthcare, free childcare, generous unemployment insurance, affordable housing, and free higher education eliminate most of the material barriers to civic participation documented in this domain. Citizens are not managing medical debt, student loan default, or housing insecurity — the state has absorbed these risks.

The Nordic model's democratic consequence: when economic survival is not at stake in every month, citizens have the cognitive bandwidth and time to engage with political questions. Turnout is high and relatively stable regardless of income bracket. In Sweden, the income-participation gap — the equivalent of the US's 26-point gap — is approximately 5-10 points.

**Research questions**:
- What does the IDEA Voter Turnout Database show for Nordic national parliamentary elections 2020-2024?
- What does the OECD Better Life Index show for US vs. Nordic civic engagement indicators?
- Is there academic literature explicitly making the economic security → civic participation causal link for Nordic countries? (Cambridge Core, comparative politics journals)

### 4.3 FDR New Deal Precedent (US Domestic)

The New Deal's most important democratic consequence was not its specific programs but what they signaled: that the state had a responsibility to protect citizens from economic catastrophe. The Social Security Act (1935), the National Labor Relations Act (1935), and the Fair Labor Standards Act (1938) created floor conditions for economic participation that translated, over the following decades, into expanded civic participation.

The 26th Amendment (1971) extended voting rights to 18-year-olds; the Civil Rights Act (1965) formally extended voting rights to Black Americans. But the material conditions for exercising those rights were built, in part, by the New Deal's floor conditions. This is the domestic precedent for the argument that economic policy is democratic architecture.

**Research questions**:
- Is there political science literature explicitly connecting New Deal economic reforms to civic participation expansion?
- What does the historical record show about voter turnout trends following the implementation of Social Security and labor rights?

## Section 5: Reform Architecture — What Would Reverse This

### 5.1 Participatory Infrastructure Reforms (Voting Access)

**Automatic Voter Registration (AVR)**: 24 states and DC have enacted AVR as of November 2025; the remaining 26 states maintain opt-in registration that is acutely burdensome for geographically mobile low-income households. AVR's democratic case: it decouples voter registration from residential stability, ensuring that moving — itself caused by economic precarity — does not automatically result in civic disenfranchisement.

**Same-Day Registration (SDR)**: SDR increases youth turnout by 3.1-7.3 percentage points (Journal of Politics 2022) and is most impactful for the populations most subject to geographic mobility.

**Voting Time Off**: Several states have enacted paid or unpaid voting leave, but federal law does not require it. For multi-job workers and hourly workers — who cannot absorb lost income — voting leave is a material access requirement, not a courtesy.

**Research questions**:
- What does the NCSL survey show about which states have enacted voting time off and what the requirements are?
- What is the Brennan Center's assessment of AVR's effect on turnout for low-income voters specifically (not just overall)?
- Is there any evidence of what percentage of registered-voter dropouts are attributable to moving vs. other causes?

### 5.2 Economic Floor Reforms (Upstream Interventions)

**Federal minimum wage**: Federal minimum wage has been $7.25/hour since 2009. The EPI documented in 2025 that it is now officially a poverty wage. A $17-20/hour floor would directly reduce the multi-job burden that drives time poverty.

**Medicaid expansion (post-OBBBA)**: States that have not expanded Medicaid under the ACA leave 2.5 million Americans in the coverage gap. Post-OBBBA, the priority is holding existing Medicaid against the work requirement implementation and challenging it in court.

**Childcare**: The Build Back Better childcare provisions expired; the OBBBA reduced CCDBG funding further. Universal or subsidized childcare would reduce the 32-percent-of-income burden that drives workforce exit for 4+ million women.

**Student loan reform**: The Pressley-Booker-Warren bill to suspend wage garnishment; broader restructuring toward income-driven repayment with a genuine economic hardship exemption.

**Research questions**:
- What is the CBPP's most recent analysis of minimum wage effects on poverty and labor market outcomes?
- What is the current status of the Ending Administrative Wage Garnishment Act in Congress?
- What does ITEP show about the distributional effects of CTC extension on household income for the bottom two quintiles?

### 5.3 Automatic Voter Registration for Public Benefit Recipients (Bridge Reform)

One underexplored reform connects economic policy directly to civic infrastructure: automatic voter registration for individuals enrolled in public benefit programs (SNAP, Medicaid, housing assistance). When someone enrolls in SNAP, they interact with a government agency; if that interaction included automatic voter registration (as in AVR's motor voter extension), enrollment would simultaneously trigger civic registration. This reform targets precisely the population most subject to geographic instability — benefit recipients who move frequently.

**Research questions**:
- Does any state currently implement AVR through public benefit program enrollment (not just DMV)?
- What does the Brennan Center or NCSL show about legal barriers to this approach?
- Domain 50 cross-check: does domain-50-healthcare-democracy-nexus-obbba-nvra.md address NVRA's application to benefit program enrollment?
