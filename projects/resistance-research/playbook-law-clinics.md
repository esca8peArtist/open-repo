# Playbook 3: Law School Clinics
## Sector-Specific Implementation Guide — Distributed Legal Capacity and Academic Infrastructure

**Status**: Full Playbook (Session 996, May 2026)  
**Outline source**: `phase-3-institutional-playbooks-outline.md` — Constituency 6  
**Cross-reference**: `institutional-adoption-playbooks.md` (existing university-sector playbook), `litigation-tracker-2026.md`

## Executive Summary for Clinic Directors and Coalition Coordinators

Law school clinics are the connective tissue of the democratic defense coalition. They accept cases that commercial firms cannot (pro bono is institutional purpose), partner with civil rights organizations without conflicts that constrain BigLaw, produce law review articles simultaneously with litigation, and create a self-replicating pipeline of democratic defense attorneys. No other constituency combines legal capacity, academic record production, and professional training in a single institutional package.

The 2025–2026 pressure on law school clinics is precisely because they occupy this position. The administration's targeting of Harvard, Yale, Columbia, and other elite law schools (funding threats, executive orders restricting federal contracts) is not primarily about ideology — it is about disabling the institutions that produce the academic record underlying democratic defense litigation.

This playbook addresses three distinct functions: (1) rapid-response academic record production (what law school clinics do that no other organization does); (2) distributed legal capacity (clinic cases across 30+ law schools create geographic and organizational redundancy that no single litigation shop can match); (3) professional pipeline (the 10,000+ students in clinical programs annually are the next generation of democratic defense attorneys).

The AALS 2026 Clinical Conference theme — "Holding the Line: Clinical Education in Defense of Democracy" (Portland, May 1–5, 2026) — represents the field's recognition that it is in a defensive posture. This playbook converts that defensive awareness into offensive capacity.

## Constituency Profile

**Organizational base**:

Law school clinics are distributed across approximately 200 ABA-accredited law schools. The coordination infrastructure:

- **Association of American Law Schools (AALS)**: The professional association coordinating law school clinical education. The AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse (launched 2025–2026 in partnership with ABA Task Force for American Democracy, co-chaired by Jeh Johnson and J. Michael Luttig) provides the coordination infrastructure for democracy-related clinic work. aals.org/rule-of-law-clearinghouse.

- **American Bar Association Task Force for American Democracy**: Co-chairs Johnson (former DHS Secretary) and Luttig (former 4th Circuit Judge). Provides institutional legitimacy and coordination with bar association resources. abaforam.org.

- **Clinic-specific democracy/rule of law programs** (highest capacity):
  - Harvard Law School — Democracy and the Rule of Law Clinic (Protect Democracy partnership); Election Law Clinic; Administrative Law Clinic
  - Yale Law School — Peter Gruber Rule of Law Clinic; Supreme Court Advocacy Clinic
  - NYU School of Law — Constitutional Litigation Clinic; Legislative and Regulatory Process Clinic
  - Georgetown Law — Civil Rights Clinic; Institute for Constitutional Advocacy and Protection (ICAP)
  - Fordham Law — Rule of Law Clinic
  - Columbia Law — Civil Rights Clinic; Immigrants' Rights Clinic
  - Michigan Law — Civil Rights Litigation Initiative
  - UCLA Law — Civil Rights Litigation Clinic; Rule of Law Initiative

**Structural constraints**:

- *Federal funding threats*: Executive orders targeting Harvard, Columbia, and other schools with research funding restrictions create financial pressure that can restrict clinical capacity. Any clinic that receives federal funding (through grants, contracts, or student loan program participation) is potentially vulnerable.
- *Independent clinic threat*: The Just Security analysis documents that the administration's assault on law school clinics is "an attack on democracy itself" — recognizing that clinics are primary targets because they are the academic record production infrastructure.
- *Academic independence norm*: Clinics must maintain genuine client representation and academic integrity; they cannot become pure advocacy organizations without compromising their core function. The playbook asks them to do what they already do — represent clients, produce scholarship — more strategically and in better coordination.
- *Student supervision requirements*: Clinical legal work is pedagogically constrained; supervisory ratios (typically 6:1 or 8:1 student-to-supervisor) limit output per clinic. Coordination across clinics multiplies output without violating individual supervision requirements.

**Current coordination gap**: Despite the AALS infrastructure, there is no standing mechanism for clinic directors to assign work to one another, share litigation strategy across institutions, or produce joint academic outputs on a coordinated schedule. The AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse is the emerging infrastructure for this; it needs activation, not creation.

## Domain Targeting: Five Highest-Leverage Areas

### Domain 6: Judicial Independence — Empirical Documentation Project

**The gap**: The most important thing a law school clinic can do for Domain 6 that no other organization can do is produce a rigorous empirical record. Not advocacy documents; empirical datasets that become the factual record underlying briefs, legislation, and academic consensus.

**Project specification**: Systematic coding project on the SCOTUS shadow docket, 2022–present:

*Dataset 1 — Shadow docket emergency orders*: Every emergency stay, injunction, and emergency application since 2022. Coded variables: (a) whether the emergency application involved a lower court injunction against executive action; (b) whether the SCOTUS order was issued within 48 hours; (c) whether the order reversed a lower court decision on the merits without full briefing; (d) the partisan composition of the deciding SCOTUS panel; (e) the subject matter (immigration, election law, civil service, tariffs, etc.). This is the empirical documentation of shadow docket abuse.

*Dataset 2 — Universal injunction history*: Every nationwide injunction issued by a district court against an executive action, 2017–2026. Coded for: scope of injunction, circuit, subject matter, ultimate SCOTUS disposition. This is the empirical baseline for *Trump v. CASA* analysis — the argument that universal injunctions were an accepted and useful practice before the 2024–2025 restriction.

*Publication vehicle*: Yale Law Journal, Harvard Law Review, or University of Chicago Law Review — the three journals that SCOTUS clerks read systematically. Articles from these journals are disproportionately cited in cert-stage briefing.

*Timeline*: Dataset 1 can be coded by 12–15 law students in a single semester with proper supervision. Dataset 2 requires two semesters. Initial article publishable in 12–14 months.

*Coordinating clinic*: Yale Rule of Law Clinic is the natural home (proximity to Yale Law Journal; faculty expertise); Harvard Election Law Clinic as co-author institution.

**Measurement milestone (Month 12)**: Dataset 1 complete; article submitted to Yale Law Journal or Harvard Law Review.

### Domain 29: Prosecutorial Weaponization — Retaliatory Prosecution Dataset

**The gap**: The 22-case retaliatory prosecution dataset (documented in litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 4) is the starting point for a rigorous empirical analysis that can serve as the factual predicate for both SPLC amicus briefing and for the Retaliatory Prosecution Transparency Act (proposed legislation requiring DOJ to report on prosecution decisions affecting political opponents or civil society organizations).

**Project specification**:

*Dataset construction*: For each of the 22 identified retaliatory prosecution cases, code: (a) the organization or individual targeted; (b) the stated legal basis for prosecution; (c) the factual basis (what conduct, exactly, is alleged); (d) the timing relative to the organization's adverse-to-administration activities; (e) any contemporaneous statements by administration officials about the target organization; (f) the disposition.

*Comparative analysis*: For each case, identify three comparable non-retaliatory cases (similar alleged conduct, similar organization type, similar factual pattern) that were not prosecuted during the same period. The comparison is the evidence of selective prosecution — the administration prosecuted Organization X but not Organizations Y, Z, and W, which did the same thing. This is the pretext analysis that makes selective prosecution claims legally viable.

*Academic output*: Journal article documenting the dataset. Primary placement: University of Chicago Law Review (peer review; prosecutorial discretion and separation of powers is a Chicago specialty) or Stanford Law Review.

*Brief output*: The dataset provides the factual predicate for an amicus brief in the SPLC case arguing that the indictment pattern is consistent with selective prosecution as a constitutional violation. The amicus is filed by the law school clinics that produced the dataset — their credibility as academic institutions, not advocacy organizations, is part of the amicus's persuasive value.

*Coordinating clinic*: Georgetown ICAP or NYU Constitutional Litigation Clinic, with clinical students doing the case coding under faculty supervision.

**Measurement milestone (Month 8)**: Dataset construction complete; article draft complete; amicus brief filed in SPLC case.

### Domain 36: AI Governance — CMS Medicare Denial Records Litigation

**The gap**: The Electronic Frontier Foundation's FOIA litigation against CMS for WISeR Medicare AI denial records is the model for clinic partnership. EFF filed FOIA requests for the algorithmic model CMS uses to make Medicare Advantage coverage denial decisions (the WISeR model). CMS has resisted disclosure. The clinic partnership model: a law school clinic takes on the FOIA litigation under student supervision, adding clinical labor capacity to EFF's institutional capacity.

**Project specification**:

*FOIA litigation partnership*: Identify clinic willing to take the CMS WISeR FOIA litigation as a clinical case. The case is well-developed (EFF has already filed and briefed the initial proceedings); the clinic adds brief-writing capacity and broadens the litigation coalition. Suitable clinics: Michigan Law (Administrative Law emphasis), Harvard (Administrative Law Clinic), Georgetown (Technology Law and Policy Clinic).

*Parallel FOIA project*: Social Security Administration's AI-assisted disability determination system (identified in research-notes.md). File parallel FOIA requests for the SSA's algorithmic model documentation. Clinical case development: three to four students per case over one semester.

*Data produced*: If successful, the WISeR model documentation reveals (a) the denial rate algorithm; (b) the training data; (c) the validation methodology; (d) whether the model was audited for bias. This is the factual foundation for Domain 36 AI accountability legislation.

*Legislative record*: The clinic produces a report summarizing its FOIA findings for submission to Senate HELP Committee and House Energy and Commerce Committee as evidence for AI Accountability in Health Care Act drafting.

**Measurement milestone (Month 9)**: At least two clinical programs have joined the CMS/SSA AI FOIA litigation track; first round of FOIA documents received and coded.

### Domain 2: Civil Service — Academic Record for *Loper Bright* Aftermath

**The gap**: *Loper Bright* (June 28, 2024) eliminated Chevron deference. This dramatically changes the Schedule Policy/Career litigation because courts no longer defer to OPM's interpretation of 5 U.S.C. § 3302. The post-*Loper Bright* academic record on § 3302 is thin. Law school clinics need to produce the law review articles that fill this gap — articles that SCOTUS clerks will read when AFGE v. OPM reaches the Court.

**Project specification**:

*Article 1 — "The Statutory Basis of Merit: § 3302 After Loper Bright"*: Full statutory analysis of 5 U.S.C. § 3302, the OPM implementing regulations, the history of excepted service placements since 1883, and the argument that "necessary" means genuinely necessary — not merely convenient. Author: administrative law faculty with clinical collaboration. Target: Michigan Law Review or Virginia Law Review (administrative law emphasis).

*Article 2 — "Schedule Policy/Career and the Take Care Clause"*: The constitutional argument that merit-based civil service is constitutionally grounded in the Take Care Clause — the President must faithfully execute the laws, and politicizing the executors of the law undermines faithful execution. Author: constitutional law faculty. Target: Harvard Law Review or Yale Law Journal.

*Article 3 — "The Original Understanding of Civil Service Independence"*: Historical analysis of the Pendleton Act debates, the Progressive Era civil service reforms, and the original understanding that merit-based appointment was a structural feature of the constitutional order. Author: legal history faculty. Target: Chicago Law Review or Stanford Law Review.

*Timeline*: All three articles should be submitted for publication within 12 months; all three should be in print before SCOTUS argument in AFGE v. OPM or any related case.

*Coordinating clinics*: Harvard Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic coordinates Article 1 (administrative law emphasis); Yale Rule of Law Clinic coordinates Article 2 (constitutional theory); NYU or Georgetown coordinates Article 3 (historical analysis).

**Measurement milestone (Month 12)**: All three articles submitted for publication; at least one accepted.

### Domain 7: Democratic Participation — Voter Registration and Election Administration Legal Support

**The gap**: The SAVE Act creates technical voter registration compliance challenges that will disproportionately burden low-income, rural, and minority voters. Law school clinics — particularly those with election law focuses — can provide direct legal services to election administrators and voters facing SAVE Act compliance requirements.

**Project specification**:

*Legal services to election administrators*: State and county election administrators face new legal compliance requirements under the SAVE Act and under the executive orders attempting to change mail ballot procedures. Clinics can provide direct legal advice (through partnerships with state election official associations) on compliance obligations, available legal challenges, and procedures for referring disputes to the AG coalition.

*Voter registration legal services*: Clinics in states with large unregistered populations (Texas, Florida, Georgia, Arizona) can operate voter registration legal assistance clinics — helping would-be voters navigate documentary requirements, identity verification requirements, and registration deadlines. This is direct legal services work that produces both trained students and concrete democratic participation outcomes.

*SAVE Act litigation support*: The AG coalition's SAVE Act challenge needs academic record support: empirical documentation of the actual burden imposed by documentary proof-of-citizenship requirements (how many voters lack documentary proof? what are the documented cost and time burdens?). This is exactly the kind of empirical research project that a clinical team can execute: survey research in partnership with sociologists, archival research in state vital records, and legal analysis of the NVRA preemption argument.

*Coordinating clinic*: Harvard Election Law Clinic (hlselectionlaw.org) is the natural coordinator; Georgetown Election Law program and Michigan Election Law Clinic as partners.

**Measurement milestone (Month 6)**: At least three clinics have active voter registration legal services programs; SAVE Act empirical documentation project underway.

## 36-Month Action Plan

### Year 1, Phase 1: Rapid-Response Architecture and Active Project Initiation (Months 1–6)

**Month 1: AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse activation**

The AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse exists but needs activation as an operational coordination mechanism, not just a resource repository. Key activation steps:

*Coordination protocol*: Establish a standing biweekly call among clinic directors at 15–20 priority institutions. Call agenda: (a) incoming cases needing clinic capacity; (b) litigation milestones in the next 30 days requiring academic record support; (c) publication opportunities (cases reaching circuit courts that will need law review articles by argument time).

*Assignment mechanism*: When a case needs an amicus brief by a 30-day deadline, the Clearinghouse coordinator circulates to the standing clinic network. The first clinic to confirm capacity within 48 hours gets the case. This is modeled on the ACLU's amicus brief coordination mechanism.

*Documentation*: Maintain a shared secure document repository (not Google Drive — use the Clearinghouse's own infrastructure) where pre-written legal templates, standing memos, and brief outlines are stored and updated.

Action: Clearinghouse coordinator identified (this may be a full-time staff position at AALS); first coordination call scheduled; 15–20 clinic directors confirmed as active participants.

**Month 2: Post-*Slaughter* rapid-response pre-staging**

Following the Harvard Law School *Loper Bright* rapid-response model (four analyses within 72 hours of the decision), pre-stage the law school clinic network for *Trump v. Slaughter* (expected June 2026):

*Pre-assigned faculty teams*: Each of the three expected ruling scenarios (full overruling, functional narrowing, prospective-only) should have a pre-assigned faculty author at a different institution. Scenario 1 (full overruling) — Harvard; Scenario 2 (functional narrowing) — Yale; Scenario 3 (prospective only) — Georgetown.

*Pre-written templates*: For each scenario, produce a pre-written analysis template that requires only facts inserted after the ruling. Analysis framework: (a) what was decided and what remains open; (b) the immediate implications for independent agencies (EPA, NLRB, FCC, CFPB, MSPB); (c) the available challenge strategies for each affected agency; (d) the constitutional argument for preserving at least functional independence even after *Slaughter*.

*Publication commitments*: Secure pre-commitments from Just Security and Lawfare to publish the *Slaughter* analyses within 72 hours of the ruling. These publications should be fully drafted (with blanks) before the ruling comes down.

Action: Three faculty teams assigned; templates drafted; Just Security and Lawfare commitments secured.

**Month 3: Shadow docket empirical project kickoff**

Launch Dataset 1 (shadow docket emergency orders) in a coordinating clinic. Recruit 12–15 students for a clinical project; establish coding manual; begin data collection.

Action: Student team assembled; coding manual complete; first semester of data collection begun.

**Month 4: SPLC amicus brief project**

The SPLC criminal indictment case (litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 4.2) is the most urgent clinic-ready case. Form a three-clinic amicus brief team:
- Lead clinic (Georgetown ICAP) drafts the constitutional argument (selective prosecution as viewpoint discrimination)
- Second clinic (NYU Constitutional Litigation Clinic) drafts the factual section (the retaliatory prosecution dataset coding)
- Third clinic (Michigan Civil Rights Litigation) drafts the remedy section

The amicus brief argues: (a) selective prosecution of a civil rights organization because of its viewpoint violates the First Amendment; (b) the pattern of prosecutorial targeting documented in the 22-case dataset constitutes systematic viewpoint discrimination; (c) the remedy is dismissal of the indictment, not just suppression of evidence, because the constitutional violation is the initiation of the prosecution.

Action: Three-clinic team assembled; draft amicus brief complete; filed within 30 days of clinic-network activation.

**Month 5: CMS/SSA AI FOIA partnership**

Identify clinical programs willing to join the EFF WISeR FOIA litigation and launch parallel SSA AI disability determination FOIA. File FOIA requests at SSA within 30 days of clinic network activation.

Action: Two clinical programs committed; SSA FOIA filed.

**Month 6: Mid-year coordination review**

Review all active projects: shadow docket dataset (on track?), SPLC amicus (filed?), FOIA litigation (progressing?), post-*Slaughter* templates (ready for June?). Adjust resource allocation.

Add 10–15 additional law schools to the active coordination network. Pre-stage the academic record for four plausible constitutional amendments. The four amendments and lead academic institutions:
1. *Proportional representation / ranked-choice voting mandate* — Harvard Election Law Clinic (constitutional authority for federal electoral system mandates)

WISeR and SSA FOIA documents should be received and analyzed by Year 2. Produce a public-facing report (not just a law review article — a policy brief accessible to congressional staff and journalists) documenting the AI denial rate algorithms and their public health implications.

### Year 3+: Constitutional Amendment Academic Infrastructure and Pipeline (Months 24–48+)

**Constitutional amendment campaign support**

When the constitutional amendment campaign enters the state ratification phase (projected Year 3+), law school clinics provide: (a) expert witnesses in state legislative hearings on ratification; (b) model ratification resolution language reviewed for constitutional sufficiency; (c) legal training for state legislators' staff on amendment ratification procedures.

**Student pipeline**

The most durable output of this playbook is not any specific brief or article — it is the 10,000+ law students per year who participate in clinical programs focused on democratic defense. In five to seven years, these students will be practicing attorneys, and many will be practicing in the areas (federal courts, civil rights, labor, immigration) where democratic defense litigation is concentrated.

The pipeline metric: track how many clinic program graduates enter public interest law, government service, or pro bono coordination programs. This is the 10-year return on investment for clinical democratic defense work.

**International coordination**

The AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse should extend coordination to law school clinic networks in allied democratic countries: Canada (Osgoode Hall, University of Toronto), United Kingdom (Oxford Human Rights Hub, UCL Constitution Unit), Germany (Max Planck Institute for Comparative Public Law). Democratic backsliding is a global phenomenon; the academic record produced in one country can inform litigation in another.

## Scenario Decision Trees

### Scenario A: Federal government withdraws funding from a law school because of clinical work

**Trigger**: DOE, NIH, or NSF notifies a law school that federal contracts or grants are suspended because of specific clinical activities or faculty statements.

**Hour 1–24**: The targeted institution's general counsel and clinic director assess the specific funding stream affected, the stated reason, and the applicable First Amendment and academic freedom claims.

**Day 1–7**: AALS coordination: all other Clearinghouse clinics issue statements of solidarity (these statements are not legally binding but create reputational cost for the administration). ABA Task Force for American Democracy issues formal statement of concern. FIRE (Foundation for Individual Rights and Expression) and ACLU academic freedom units are notified.

**Day 7–30**: File APA challenge to the funding withdrawal: (a) the withdrawal is a final agency action subject to APA review; (b) the stated basis (the clinical work) is a constitutionally protected activity; (c) the withdrawal is arbitrary and capricious because there is no rational basis for conditioning research funding on the content of unrelated clinical program activities.

**Day 30+**: AG coalition amicus support. At least five state AGs should file amicus briefs supporting the law school's academic freedom claim; state universities and state-funded clinical programs have direct standing.

**Long term**: Document every funding withdrawal in the AALS Clearinghouse. Publish an annual report tracking federal funding threats to clinical programs. This is the empirical record for a First Amendment challenge to the pattern of suppression.

---

### Scenario B: Major SCOTUS ruling comes down requiring immediate academic response

**Trigger**: SCOTUS issues ruling in *Trump v. Slaughter*, *Watson v. RNC*, or any other major democracy case.

**Hour 1–2**: AALS Clearinghouse coordinator activates the pre-assigned faculty team for the specific ruling scenario.

**Hour 2–48**: Pre-written template filled with ruling-specific details. Reviewed by lead faculty and at least one other clinic director. Submitted to Just Security and Lawfare. Target: published within 72 hours of ruling.

**Day 3–7**: Second wave of analysis: longer law review article format, accounting for implications not visible in the immediate analysis. This goes to the law review that had a pre-commitment for the article.

**Day 7–30**: Amicus brief preparation for any challenge in process. The academic analysis becomes the amicus brief; the law school clinics that produced the analysis have the institutional standing to file the amicus without identifying individual client interests.

---

### Scenario C: A clinic is asked to take a case that creates institutional conflict with the law school's administration

**Trigger**: A clinic director is asked by the AALS Clearinghouse or an outside organization to take on a case that the law school administration believes creates reputational or financial risk (e.g., a case challenging a federal funding restriction that the university is trying to negotiate around).

**Resolution protocol**:
- The clinic director's first obligation is to their client and their professional responsibility rules (Model Rule 5.4 governs institutional pressure on lawyers).
- The AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse provides an institutional home for the case if the clinic is blocked — a case can be transferred to another clinic's supervision if the original clinic faces institutional constraints.
- The AALS and ABA Task Force are the external accountability mechanisms: if a law school administration improperly pressures a clinical director to drop a case for non-legal reasons, that is itself an ABA accreditation issue (clinical programs are required to operate under professional independence standards).
- The clinical community should have a stated solidarity norm: we do not abandon cases because of institutional pressure from administration; we transfer them to willing clinics rather than drop them.

## Conflict Mitigation

### Conflict 1: Clinical Independence vs. Coalition Coordination

**Tension**: Clinics have professional responsibility obligations to their clients; they cannot coordinate with other organizations on case strategy in ways that compromise client confidentiality or client direction of the representation. Coordinating the AALS Clearinghouse to produce a specific academic record for specific litigation may seem to compromise clinical independence.

**Resolution**: The coordination concerns amicus briefs (not client representation), academic publications (not privileged communications), and legislative record production (not case strategy). None of these functions compromise client representation. A clinic can produce a law review article analyzing the constitutional basis of Schedule Policy/Career without disclosing anything about its client's case. The Clearinghouse coordination protocol should explicitly separate: (a) academic record production (fully coordinated); (b) amicus brief production (coordinated on legal argument, not on any client's specific case); (c) direct representation (never coordinated — each clinic represents its own clients independently).

### Conflict 2: T14 vs. Regional School Resource Disparity

**Tension**: The highest-capacity clinics (Harvard, Yale, Georgetown, NYU) have the most resources, the most prestigious faculty, and the most access to publication venues that reach SCOTUS clerks. Regional schools have less of these. A coordination network that only involves T14 schools is both less geographically distributed and less representative of the democratic defense work happening nationwide.

**Resolution**: The AALS Clearinghouse coordination model assigns work by capacity and geography, not prestige. Shadow docket empirical coding can be done by students at any accredited law school with competent supervision. SAVE Act voter registration legal services work is most relevant in Texas, Florida, and Arizona — not in Massachusetts. The coordinating clinic for academic publications may be Harvard or Yale; the coordinating clinic for voter registration legal services in Arizona is University of Arizona James E. Rogers College of Law or Arizona State Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law.

Design the work assignments to match the geographic and doctrinal strengths of each school. Build in explicit commitments to include regional schools in the coordination network.

### Conflict 3: Academic Independence vs. Advocacy Function

**Tension**: Law school clinics produce scholarship. Scholarship is supposed to be objective, not advocacy. If clinic directors are seen as coordinating with advocacy organizations on what academic positions to take, this undermines the credibility of the scholarship — which is precisely what makes it valuable to courts.

**Resolution**: The coordination concerns research design (what questions to study) and publication coordination (ensuring articles reach relevant venues at relevant times), not the conclusions. The conclusions must follow from honest analysis of the evidence. If the empirical shadow docket study reveals that the shadow docket is not being abused, that finding should be published. The coordination function is: (1) ensure the research gets done before it is needed; (2) ensure the research reaches the audiences that need it. These are legitimate scholarly coordination functions. The AALS Clearinghouse model is correct: it is a clearinghouse for resources and coordination, not an advocacy organization that directs scholarly conclusions.

## Resource Requirements

**AALS Rule of Law Clearinghouse staffing**: The current Clearinghouse appears to be operated with existing AALS staff. Full activation as an operational coordination mechanism requires one dedicated coordinator position ($90,000–$120,000 annually) and a technology infrastructure budget ($15,000–$25,000 for secure document sharing, videoconferencing, and case tracking).

**Academic publication pipeline**: Law review articles require faculty time (typically 1–3 months of concentrated research and writing, above regular course load). Honoraria are modest or zero for law review publications. The primary cost is faculty time, which is covered by existing law school budgets. Incremental cost: course release buy-outs for three to four faculty participants ($20,000–$30,000 per buy-out, depending on institution).

**Student clinical projects**: Clinical projects are funded through existing clinical program budgets. Incremental cost for projects that require outside data collection (surveys, archival research, expert consultation): $10,000–$25,000 per project.

**Amicus brief production**: Amicus brief filing costs (printing, filing fees, courier) are modest ($1,000–$3,000 per brief). The primary cost is attorney time, which is covered by clinical program budgets or pro bono contributions.

**Network expansion (regional schools)**: Adding regional schools to the coordination network requires outreach and relationship-building ($5,000–$10,000 in travel for in-person meetings) and coordination support ($15,000–$20,000 in stipends for participating regional school clinic directors who lack existing resources).

**Total incremental annual cost**: $200,000–$350,000. The primary funder should be a democracy-focused foundation (Hewlett, Ford, Open Society) specifically for the AALS Clearinghouse operational budget and the faculty buy-out fund. The law schools themselves cover most of the cost through existing clinical program budgets.
