# Environmental Regulation Rollbacks Tracker
## Trump Administration, 2025–2026

**Purpose**: A rule-by-rule reference document for advocates, attorneys, journalists, and citizens defending environmental law against the current deregulatory push. Organized by agency. Entries appear in reverse chronological order of finalization (or announcement if not yet final). Enforcement and budget degradation is tracked alongside formal regulatory rollbacks because the two are functionally inseparable.

**Confidence note**: Rules marked [FINAL] are published in the Federal Register. Rules marked [PROPOSED] are in the notice-and-comment stage and not yet legally effective. Rules marked [ADMIN ACTION] are agency policy shifts, executive orders, or guidance changes that do not require formal rulemaking. Litigation status is current as of April 26, 2026, and may have changed.

## I. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)

### 1. Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding
**Rule**: Rescission of the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding and Motor Vehicle GHG Emission Standards Under the Clean Air Act
**Code**: 40 CFR Parts 85, 86, 88; the 2009 Endangerment Finding itself carries no standalone CFR citation but underpins dozens of downstream regulations
**Status**: [FINAL]

**Dates**: Proposed August 2025; finalized and published Federal Register February 18, 2026 (FR 2026-03157); effective March 2026

**What changed**: The 2009 Endangerment Finding — the scientific and legal cornerstone established under *Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007) — determined that six greenhouse gases (CO2, methane, nitrous oxide, HFCs, PFCs, SF6) endanger public health and welfare, and required EPA to regulate them under the Clean Air Act. The Trump EPA rescinded the finding entirely, simultaneously repealing emissions standards for light-, medium-, and heavy-duty motor vehicles. This is the single most consequential environmental regulatory action of the second Trump term. Without the Endangerment Finding, EPA loses its principal statutory basis for regulating GHGs from any source category under Clean Air Act Section 202.

**Stated justification**: EPA claimed the agency had failed to conduct a "thorough review" of the underlying air quality criteria before the 2009 finding and that the scientific record has "evolved." EPA further argued the finding improperly relied on international scientific consensus (IPCC) rather than independent agency analysis.

**Real-world impact**: If the rescission survives litigation, it removes the legal framework underpinning power plant emissions rules, vehicle tailpipe standards, and oil and gas methane regulations. The WRI estimated the Endangerment Finding covers roughly 80% of all U.S. greenhouse gas emissions. Health modeling by the Clean Air Task Force projects tens of thousands of additional premature deaths annually from increased GHG-linked pollution over a multi-decade horizon.

**Litigation**: Earthjustice, NRDC, Environmental Defense Fund, and a coalition of 22 state attorneys general filed challenges immediately upon publication. Cases are consolidated in the D.C. Circuit. Harvard Law's Salata Institute projects a D.C. Circuit ruling by late 2026 and potential Supreme Court consideration by mid-2027. No preliminary injunction has been granted as of the compilation date.

**Previous rule finalized**: December 15, 2009 (74 FR 66496); reaffirmed and updated multiple times through 2022.

### 2. Mercury and Air Toxics Standards (MATS)
**Rule**: National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants — Coal- and Oil-Fired Electric Utility Steam Generating Units
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 63, Subpart UUUUU
**Status**: [FINAL — 2024 amendments repealed]

**Dates**: Proposed June 17, 2025 (FR 2025-10992); finalized February 19, 2026

**What changed**: EPA repealed the 2024 Biden-era amendments to the MATS rule. The repealed provisions include: (a) the revised, more stringent filterable particulate matter (fPM) emission standard for existing coal-fired units — serving as a surrogate for non-mercury HAP metals; (b) the tighter mercury standard for lignite-fired units; (c) the mandatory use of Continuous Emissions Monitoring Systems (CEMS) for PM compliance. The underlying 2012 MATS rule (the baseline mercury standard) was not repealed. Approximately 68 coal-fired power plants were given compliance exemptions in April 2025 while this repeal was being finalized.

**Stated justification**: EPA claimed the 2024 amendments were finalized without adequate cost-benefit analysis and imposed "unreasonable" technology requirements on existing plants. The agency argued the 2012 rule already adequately controls HAP metals.

**Real-world impact**: The Clean Air Task Force (February 2026) estimated the 2024 amendments would have prevented approximately 1,500 additional premature deaths annually and 48,000 additional asthma attacks per year. The rollback preserves significantly higher HAP metal emissions — including arsenic, chromium, and nickel — from the affected coal units through at least the 2030s.

**Litigation**: Earthjustice filed suit (February 2026). Challenge is at the D.C. Circuit. No injunction as of compilation date.

**Previous rule finalized**: Biden 2024 MATS amendments, May 7, 2024 (89 FR 38508). Original MATS rule: February 16, 2012 (77 FR 9304).

### 3. National Ambient Air Quality Standards — Particulate Matter (PM2.5)
**Rule**: Reconsideration of the NAAQS for Particulate Matter (PM)
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 50, specifically 50.7 (annual PM2.5 primary standard)
**Status**: [ADMIN ACTION — EPA moved to vacate in court; rulemaking pending]

**Dates**: Biden rule finalized March 6, 2024 (lowering annual PM2.5 primary standard from 12.0 to 9.0 µg/m3). EPA filed motion in D.C. Circuit on November 24, 2025, asking the court to vacate the 2024 rule, which would restore the 2012/2020 standard of 12.0 µg/m3.

**What changed**: EPA is asking a court to undo its own 2024 rulemaking — an unusual procedural posture — rather than conducting a new rulemaking. This would reinstate the 2020 standard of 12.0 µg/m3 as the governing limit. The 2024 rule had tightened the annual limit by 25% based on evidence linking fine particle exposure to cardiovascular disease, lung cancer, and premature death at concentrations below 12 µg/m3.

**Stated justification**: EPA claimed the Biden administration conducted the 2024 rulemaking without conducting the "thorough review of the underlying air quality criteria" required by the Clean Air Act. Fourteen industry groups had previously petitioned for review of the 2024 rule.

**Real-world impact**: EPA's own 2024 analysis estimated the 9.0 µg/m3 standard would prevent up to 4,500 premature deaths annually, 2,000 hospitalizations, and 800,000 asthma cases per year. Reverting to 12.0 µg/m3 would forgo those gains. Communities near industrial facilities — disproportionately low-income and communities of color — would remain exposed to pollution levels the scientific record now associates with measurable mortality risk.

**Litigation**: California and multiple Democratic-led states are opposing EPA's vacatur motion. The D.C. Circuit had originally granted oral argument on the industry challenge; that case is now entangled with EPA's own motion. No final ruling as of compilation date.

**Previous rule finalized**: 2024 Biden rule (89 FR 16202, March 6, 2024). Prior standard: 2012 rule at 12.0 µg/m3 (77 FR 38890), maintained in 2020.

### 4. Power Plant Greenhouse Gas Standards (Clean Power Plan successor)
**Rule**: Greenhouse Gas Standards and Guidelines for Fossil Fuel-Fired Electric Generating Units
**Code**: 40 CFR Parts 60, 63
**Status**: [PROPOSED repeal — not yet final]

**Dates**: Proposed June 11–17, 2025; public comment closed August 2025; final rule expected 2026

**What changed**: EPA proposes to rescind both the 2015 New Source Performance Standards (NSPS) and the 2024 Carbon Pollution Standards (Clean Power Plan 2.0) for new, modified, and existing fossil fuel-fired power plants. The proposal would eliminate: requirements for carbon capture and storage (CCS) on new gas turbines; CO2 limits for coal-fired units; and co-firing or fuel-switching requirements for existing gas plants.

**Stated justification**: EPA argued that CCS technology is not "adequately demonstrated" and that the 2024 rule exceeded the agency's authority. The agency estimates $1.2 billion in annual compliance cost savings.

**Real-world impact**: The Union of Concerned Scientists estimated the 2024 Clean Power Plan 2.0 would reduce CO2 emissions from the power sector by approximately 75% below 2005 levels by 2040 and prevent 1,300 premature deaths annually from associated co-pollutant reductions. The proposed repeal would preserve unrestrained fossil fuel combustion at the nation's largest stationary GHG source category.

**Litigation**: Multiple environmental organizations pre-filed notices of intent to challenge. Pending finalization before litigation can proceed.

**Previous rule finalized**: Biden 2024 rule (89 FR 39798, May 9, 2024). 2015 NSPS (80 FR 64510, October 23, 2015).

### 5. Clean Air Act — California Vehicle Emissions Waiver
**Rule**: California State Motor Vehicle and Engine Pollution Control Standards — Advanced Clean Cars II, Advanced Clean Trucks, and Heavy-Duty Omnibus Low NOX
**Code**: Clean Air Act Section 209(b) waivers; 42 U.S.C. § 7543(b)
**Status**: [FINAL — Congressional Review Act disapproval]

**Dates**: Biden EPA granted California's Advanced Clean Cars II waiver January 6, 2025. Congress passed three joint resolutions under the Congressional Review Act (CRA). President Trump signed all three resolutions on June 12, 2025, nullifying: (1) the ACC II waiver (zero-emission vehicle mandate by 2035); (2) the Advanced Clean Trucks waiver; (3) the Heavy-Duty Omnibus Low NOX waiver.

**What changed**: California had held a special authority under Section 209(b) of the Clean Air Act since 1970 to set its own, more stringent vehicle emissions standards. Seventeen additional states had adopted California's standards, collectively covering approximately 40% of the U.S. vehicle market. The three CRA resolutions stripped California of all three recently granted waivers and — per the CRA's text — may prohibit EPA from ever issuing "substantially similar" rules in the future.

**Stated justification**: Congress and the administration framed the waivers as federal overreach, arguing the California standards amounted to a de facto national EV mandate without statutory authorization.

**Real-world impact**: The Union of Concerned Scientists estimated the Advanced Clean Cars II rule would have prevented 690 million metric tons of CO2 emissions through 2040 and saved consumers an aggregate $37 billion in fuel costs. The rollback effectively freezes California's ability to require zero-emission vehicles through at least 2035.

**Litigation**: Eleven state attorneys general filed suit on June 12, 2025, the same day Trump signed the resolutions. The central legal question is whether the CRA can validly be applied to EPA waiver decisions (which are not "rules" under the standard APA definition). The D.C. Circuit is the likely venue; no injunction granted as of the compilation date.

**Previous waivers granted**: ACC II waiver published January 6, 2025 (90 FR 1326).

### 6. Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) Standards
**Rule**: Corporate Average Fuel Economy Standards — Model Years 2022–2031
**Code**: 49 CFR Part 531, Part 533
**Agency**: National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA/DOT), with EPA GHG standards in parallel
**Status**: [PROPOSED — not yet final]

**Dates**: Trump White House announced December 3, 2025; NHTSA proposed rule published December 2025; comment period closed early 2026.

**What changed**: Biden's CAFE standards required a 2% annual improvement in fleet fuel economy, reaching approximately 50.4 mpg (combined fleet average) by model year 2031. The Trump NHTSA proposal would reset the baseline to model year 2022 levels and increase efficiency by only 0.5% per year — a reduction of roughly 75% in the required rate of improvement. Additionally, Congress eliminated civil penalties for CAFE non-compliance via the "One Big Beautiful Bill" (Pub. L. 119-21, Section 60012), removing the enforcement mechanism for standards already in effect.

**Stated justification**: The administration argued that stringent CAFE standards increase vehicle prices and impair consumer choice, particularly by incentivizing EV adoption.

**Real-world impact**: The Biden administration estimated its standards would save almost 70 billion gallons of gasoline through 2050 and prevent more than 710 million metric tons of CO2 emissions. Earthjustice stated the rollback would lock in higher emissions from the passenger vehicle fleet — the single largest source of U.S. transportation GHG emissions — for the full life of model years 2025–2031.

**Litigation**: Earthjustice (December 2025 statement) and UCS are preparing challenges. Pending finalization.

**Previous rule finalized**: Biden NHTSA final rule (88 FR 22438, April 13, 2023).

### 7. "Good Neighbor Plan" — Cross-State Air Pollution (NOX)
**Rule**: Federal "Good Neighbor Plan" for the 2015 Ozone NAAQS; Revised Cross-State Air Pollution Rule (CSAPR)
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 97
**Status**: [ADMIN ACTION — reconsideration announced; Phase 1 proposed January 27, 2026]

**Dates**: EPA announced rollback March 12, 2025; Phase 1 reconsideration proposed January 27, 2026

**What changed**: The 2023 Biden-era Good Neighbor Plan required 23 upwind states to reduce NOX emissions from power plants and industrial sources to prevent smog from drifting into downwind states that cannot meet the 2015 ozone NAAQS on their own. The Trump EPA's Phase 1 reconsideration proposed approving state implementation plans from eight previously non-compliant states — Alabama, Arizona, Kentucky, Minnesota, Mississippi, Nevada, New Mexico, and Tennessee — without requiring additional NOX reductions. Effectively, upwind pollution from these states contributing to downwind ozone nonattainment would go unaddressed.

**Stated justification**: EPA cited cooperative federalism principles and argued the Biden-era SIP disapprovals for these eight states were too aggressive.

**Litigation**: The 2023 Good Neighbor Plan was paused by the Supreme Court (June 2024) pending D.C. Circuit review. The D.C. Circuit scheduled oral argument for April 25, 2025; litigation was subsequently placed in abeyance at EPA's request pending reconsideration. The abeyance means the rule has not been enforced in any of the 23 covered states since June 2024.

**Previous rule finalized**: Biden Good Neighbor Plan, June 5, 2023 (88 FR 36654).

### 8. PFAS Drinking Water Standards (Partial Rollback)
**Rule**: National Primary Drinking Water Regulation for PFAS
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 141
**Status**: [ADMIN ACTION — rescission proposed; PFOA/PFOS standards retained but delayed]

**Dates**: Biden rule finalized April 10, 2024. EPA announced modifications May 14, 2025. Proposed rescission rule expected fall 2025 (not yet published as of April 2026).

**What changed**: The Biden EPA set enforceable maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) for six per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS): PFOA (4 parts per trillion), PFOS (4 ppt), PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA ("GenX"), and the Hazard Index mixture of three PFAS. The Trump EPA announced it will: (a) retain PFOA and PFOS MCLs but delay compliance deadlines from 2029 to 2031; (b) rescind the regulations for PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA/GenX, and the HI mixture. An estimated 6–45 million Americans receive water from systems that exceed one or more of the four PFAS standards being rescinded.

**Stated justification**: EPA claimed the four lesser-regulated PFAS lacked sufficient scientific basis to support an MCL and that the compliance timeline for PFOA/PFOS was unworkable for smaller water utilities.

**Real-world impact**: PFAS are associated with kidney cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, and developmental harm. The NRDC estimated that the partial rescission would leave millions of Americans with no enforceable federal protection against four documented toxic PFAS in drinking water.

**Litigation**: Expected from environmental groups upon publication of the formal rescission rule. No litigation filed as of compilation date because the formal rule has not yet been published.

**Previous rule finalized**: Biden PFAS drinking water rule, April 10, 2024 (89 FR 32532).

### 9. Waters of the United States (WOTUS) — Clean Water Act
**Rule**: Revised Definition of "Waters of the United States"
**Code**: 40 CFR Parts 110, 112, 116, 117, 120, 122, 123, 230, 231, 232, 300, 302, 401
**Status**: [PROPOSED — not yet final]

**Dates**: EPA and Army Corps published proposed rule November 2025; public comment period closed January 5, 2026.

**What changed**: The proposal would implement the Supreme Court's 2023 *Sackett v. EPA* ruling in the most restrictive way possible, limiting federal jurisdiction over wetlands to only those with a "continuous surface connection" to a "relatively permanent" water body — eliminating federal protection for seasonal streams, ephemeral channels, isolated wetlands, and most non-adjacent wetlands. An estimated 50–60% of the nation's wetlands and a significant percentage of streams that contribute to drinking water would lose Clean Water Act coverage. The proposal follows interim guidance EPA issued in fall 2023 that already narrowed enforcement pending rulemaking.

**Stated justification**: EPA argued the proposal faithfully implements *Sackett* and reduces regulatory uncertainty for landowners and developers.

**Real-world impact**: Wetlands perform flood buffering, water filtration, and habitat functions worth an estimated $23,000/acre/year in ecological services. An analysis by the Environmental Defense Fund projects that the proposed rule, if finalized, would remove federal protection from more than half of U.S. wetland acres, with particular impacts in Western states where water systems are seasonal and non-contiguous.

**Litigation**: Environmental groups will challenge any finalized rule on both statutory and *Sackett* interpretation grounds. Industry groups may challenge from the other direction if any post-*Sackett* jurisdiction is retained. The WOTUS definition has been in continuous litigation since 2015.

**Previous rule**: Biden Revised WOTUS rule (88 FR 3004, January 18, 2023), substantially narrowed by the Supreme Court's *Sackett* decision.

### 10. Methane Waste Emissions Charge — Oil and Gas
**Rule**: Waste Emissions Charge for Petroleum and Natural Gas Systems (40 CFR Part 99)
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 99; IRA Section 60113
**Status**: [FINAL — rule revoked via CRA; statutory obligation under IRA disputed]

**Dates**: Rule proposed 2024; EPA revoked 40 CFR Part 99 via Federal Register notice May 19, 2025. President Trump signed H.J.Res.35 (P.L. 119-2) on March 14, 2025, which voided the implementing rule via CRA.

**What changed**: The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) created a "waste emissions charge" — a fee on excessive methane emissions from oil and gas operations that escalates from $900/ton in 2024 to $1,500/ton by 2026. The Congressional Review Act disapproval voids the implementing regulation (40 CFR Part 99). The "One Big Beautiful Bill" (Pub. L. 119-21, Section 42113) further delayed any fee obligation to 2034 and rescinded unobligated grant funds for methane reduction. The underlying IRA statutory obligation remains in the law but is unenforceable without regulations.

**Stated justification**: Industry-aligned arguments framed the fee as a tax on domestic energy production that increases gasoline prices.

**Real-world impact**: EPA projected the methane fee would incentivize elimination of 140 million metric tons of CO2-equivalent methane emissions over its life. The Harvard EELP noted the statutory fee obligation under IRA §60113(c) technically remains on the books but is practically unenforceable absent implementing regulations.

**Litigation**: Environmental groups have flagged that rescinding the implementing rule while the statutory fee obligation remains may present a legal anomaly that future administrations or courts could address. No direct litigation filed against the CRA disapproval, which is generally insulated from judicial review.

### 11. NEPA Environmental Review Requirements (Cross-Agency)
**Rule**: Removal of National Environmental Policy Act Implementing Regulations
**Code**: 40 CFR Part 1500 et seq. (former CEQ regulations)
**Agency**: Council on Environmental Quality (CEQ) — affects all federal agencies
**Status**: [FINAL]

**Dates**: Interim final rule February 19, 2025; Final rule rescinding all CEQ NEPA regulations published January 8, 2026 (FR 2026-00178); effective immediately.

**What changed**: The CEQ NEPA regulations — in place since 1978 and last comprehensively revised in 2020 and 2022 — established a uniform framework governing when and how all federal agencies must conduct environmental impact statements (EIS) and environmental assessments (EA). CEQ's final rule eliminated this entire framework. Agencies are now instructed to use their own non-binding guidance. The updated CEQ guidance that replaced the regulations: does not require analysis of cumulative environmental effects; does not require consideration of climate change; explicitly states agencies need only analyze "the proposed action at hand" rather than connected actions; and eliminated environmental justice analysis requirements.

**Stated justification**: A federal court had vacated the Biden-era Phase 2 NEPA rule on February 3, 2025, finding CEQ lacked authority to issue binding regulations. CEQ used this as the hook to rescind all of its regulations, including those from 1978 that the court's ruling did not address.

**Real-world impact**: NEPA is often called the "environmental Magna Carta." Eliminating uniform EIS requirements means projects — pipelines, highways, federal leases, dams — could proceed without systematic analysis of their effects on air, water, wildlife, or communities. The Congressional Research Service warned that eliminating CEQ's binding framework creates substantial legal uncertainty and opens federal permitting to procedural challenges on a project-by-project basis.

### 12. EPA Enforcement and Staffing Degradation
**Type**: Administrative/budget action
**Status**: [ONGOING]

**Dates**: Layoffs announced February–March 2025; 23% staffing cut confirmed July 18, 2025; proposed FY2026 budget cuts announced May 2025; FY2027 budget proposal submitted 2026

**What changed**: As of July 18, 2025, EPA had terminated 3,707 of 16,155 employees — a 23% reduction. The administration projects approximately 33% of total EPA staffers eliminated by the end of 2025. Budget proposals include: a 55% overall cut to EPA's operating budget; a $254 million cut to Superfund program funding; a 47% cut to Superfund cleanup accounts; a $61 million cut and 181-position reduction in the civil enforcement division; dismantlement of the EPA's Integrated Risk Information System (IRIS), which generates the agency's toxicological assessments. The Office of Research and Development (ORD) — EPA's scientific research arm — faced proposed restructuring that would severely curtail independent scientific assessment capacity.

**Stated justification**: The administration framed cuts as eliminating "wasteful" and "duplicative" regulatory overhead.

**Real-world impact**: The Environmental Protection Network (a group of former EPA officials) described the proposed cuts as "a wrecking ball." The civil enforcement division cuts reduce EPA's capacity to bring enforcement actions against violators. Superfund funding cuts directly affect cleanup at 1,350+ National Priority List sites. IRIS program cuts degrade the scientific basis for future health-based regulations.

**Litigation**: The EPA's direct funding cuts are subject to ongoing congressional appropriations litigation, but individual budget decisions are largely within executive discretion. No comprehensive injunction against personnel cuts has been issued.

## II. Department of Interior (DOI)

### 13. Arctic National Wildlife Refuge — Oil and Gas Leasing
**Rule**: Coastal Plain Oil and Gas Leasing Program
**Code**: 43 CFR Part 2360
**Status**: [FINAL — leasing reinstated October 2025]

**Dates**: Biden administration paused ANWR leasing January 2021 via EO. Interior reinstated the Coastal Plain leasing program October 2025, opening 1.56 million acres of the Refuge's Coastal Plain to oil and gas leasing.

**What changed**: The Biden administration had cancelled 2021 lease sales and conducted environmental reviews concluding drilling posed unacceptable risks to the calving grounds of the Porcupine caribou herd and to the Gwich'in people's subsistence culture. The Trump Interior reversed these decisions and relaunched the leasing program. The Coastal Plain is the primary calving ground for the Porcupine herd (approximately 218,000 animals) and the only major American habitat for polar bears denning on land.

**Stated justification**: Energy independence and fulfillment of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which opened ANWR to drilling.

**Real-world impact**: USGS estimates the Coastal Plain holds 4.3–11.8 billion barrels of technically recoverable oil. Scientific assessments project significant disruption to caribou calving (calving success rates drop by up to 40% in areas with drilling activity, per peer-reviewed studies). Drilling would release methane from permafrost and potentially damage carbon-rich soils.

**Litigation**: NRDC, Earthjustice, and Gwich'in Nation filed suit. Litigation ongoing in D.C. District Court.

### 14. Endangered Species Act — Regulatory Rollbacks
**Rule**: Four simultaneous proposed rule changes to ESA implementing regulations
**Code**: 50 CFR Parts 17, 402, 424
**Status**: [PROPOSED — four simultaneous rulemakings announced November 19, 2025]

**Dates**: Trump administration announced four proposed ESA regulatory changes November 19, 2025. Additionally, a separate proposed rule issued April 16, 2025, would revise the definition of "harm" to remove "habitat modification" — potentially the most consequential single ESA change.

**What changed — four November 2025 proposals**:
1. **Blanket rule elimination**: Would remove FWS's "blanket 4(d) rule" that automatically extends all ESA Section 9 take prohibitions to "threatened" species. Without the blanket rule, each threatened species would require a separate rulemaking to obtain take protections, creating a massive regulatory gap.
2. **Economic impact analysis in critical habitat**: Would reinstate the 2020 Trump-era rule allowing economic impacts to be weighed when designating critical habitat.
3. **Critical habitat exclusion**: Would reinstate the 2020 rule allowing exclusions from critical habitat based on economic, national security, or "other relevant impacts."
4. **Interagency consultation rollback**: Would restore the more permissive 2019 "joint regulations" governing Section 7 consultation, limiting what agencies must consider regarding indirect and cumulative effects.

**April 2025 "harm" definition proposed rule**: Would eliminate "habitat modification or degradation" from the definition of ESA "harm" under 16 U.S.C. § 1538. Under the current definition (confirmed by *Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter*, 1995, Supreme Court), destroying a species' habitat constitutes "take" even without directly killing animals. The proposed revision would drastically narrow take liability and was described by the Center for Biological Diversity as potentially the "largest rollback of endangered species protections in history."

**Stated justification**: Interior framed the proposals as restoring "regulatory certainty" and reducing "burdens" on landowners and developers. The administration's DOI press release invoked a return to "2019 interpretations."

**Real-world impact**: The U.S. has an estimated 1,300 listed threatened and endangered species. Elimination of the blanket 4(d) rule would immediately strip automatic protections from all threatened (non-endangered) species. Loss of the habitat-modification definition of "harm" would effectively legalize most habitat destruction as not constituting unlawful "take."

**Litigation**: The Center for Biological Diversity, Earthjustice, and multiple conservation organizations have filed or announced intention to challenge. The April 2025 "harm" definition rule would face direct challenge under *Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter* as contrary to settled Supreme Court interpretation. No injunction as of compilation date on the November proposals (which remain in proposed status).

### 15. National Monument Boundaries — Bears Ears, Grand Staircase-Escalante, Northeast Canyons
**Rule**: Presidential Proclamation (Antiquities Act reductions)
**Status**: [UNDER REVIEW / LIKELY IMMINENT as of April 2026]

**Dates**: Trump administration began monument review spring 2025; Interior Secretary Burgum publicly stated intent to reduce Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante in April 2025. Trump issued proclamation reopening Northeast Canyons and Seamounts Marine National Monument to commercial fishing February 6, 2026.

**What changed / what is pending**: Bears Ears National Monument (1.35 million acres, southeastern Utah) — the administration is considering boundary reductions that would expose uranium and potash deposits. Grand Staircase-Escalante (1.87 million acres, southern Utah) — the administration is considering reductions that would expose coal reserves. During Trump's first term, Bears Ears was reduced 85% and Grand Staircase by half; Biden restored both in 2021. The legal question — whether a president may reduce a monument proclaimed by a predecessor — has never been definitively resolved, and a reduction proclamation is expected to trigger immediate Antiquities Act litigation. Northeast Canyons and Seamounts (4,913 square miles off the New England coast): Trump's February 2026 proclamation fully reopened it to commercial fishing; NOAA rescinded the commercial fishing prohibition in April 2026.

**Stated justification**: Energy development, reduced regulatory burden on commercial fishing industry, "American energy dominance."

**Litigation**: NRDC v. Trump (Bears Ears) litigation standing from Trump's first term. Conservation Law Foundation is challenging the Northeast Canyons reopening. The Antiquities Act litigation question — whether Presidents may shrink monuments — remains unresolved; NRDC and allies would file immediate challenges to any reduction proclamation.

### 16. National Petroleum Reserve-Alaska and BLM Onshore Leasing
**Rule**: National Petroleum Reserve-Alaska (NPR-A) — Reversion to 1977 Management Rule; BLM Oil and Gas Leasing Decisions (Seven-State EIS Termination)
**Code**: 43 CFR Part 2360; various BLM planning documents
**Status**: [FINAL — administrative actions]

**Dates**: BLM announced termination of ongoing EIS for oil and gas leasing decisions in Colorado, Montana, New Mexico, North Dakota, South Dakota, Utah, and Wyoming (approximately 3.5 million acres) — spring 2025. NPR-A reverted to 1977 rule (reopening 82% of 23-million-acre reserve to leasing) — March 2026.

**What changed**: Biden's NPR-A rule (2024) had placed 13 million acres of intact tundra and wetland off-limits to leasing, protecting subsistence resources and migratory bird habitat. Trump's reversion reopens that entire area. The BLM's decision to terminate environmental impact statement preparation for 3.5 million onshore acres across seven states removes the legally-required NEPA documentation for leasing decisions in those areas. The Interior's broader directive under Secretary's Order 3418 directs BLM to remove "burdensome regulations" from all extractive leasing.

**Real-world impact**: The NPR-A encompasses globally significant wetlands critical to millions of migratory birds and caribou. Leasing expansion across seven Western states affects critical habitat for sage-grouse, mule deer, pronghorn, and other species.

**Litigation**: Earthjustice and NRDC are challenging the NPR-A rule reversion. The EIS termination decisions may be challenged on NEPA grounds by affected states and conservation groups.

## III. NOAA / Department of Commerce

### 17. Pacific Islands Heritage Marine National Monument — Commercial Fishing
**Rule**: Presidential Proclamation / NOAA Fisheries Guidance
**Status**: [PARTIALLY BLOCKED BY COURT]

**Dates**: Trump proclamation April 17, 2025, titled "Unleashing American Commercial Fishing in the Pacific." NOAA guidance lifting commercial fishing ban issued thereafter. Federal district court in Honolulu issued preliminary injunction August 2025, blocking NOAA guidance and reinstating fishing ban in the monument area. Litigation ongoing.

**What changed**: The Pacific Islands Heritage National Marine Monument (nearly 500,000 square miles of Pacific Ocean including the Marianas Trench, Rose Atoll, Pacific Remote Islands, and Wake Atoll areas) had been closed to commercial fishing since 2014–2016 designations under Presidents Bush and Obama. Trump's proclamation ordered reopening to commercial fishing and directed review of all other marine national monuments. NOAA issued guidance lifting the commercial fishing prohibition. The court injunction halted implementation pending a final ruling.

**Stated justification**: Administration argued monument fishing restrictions unfairly disadvantaged American fishermen and that commercial extraction was compatible with conservation.

**Real-world impact**: Marine protected areas consistently demonstrate 50–300% higher fish biomass compared to adjacent fished areas (per NOAA's own scientific assessments). Reopening would expose some of the deepest and most biodiverse marine environments on Earth — including seamount ecosystems supporting undiscovered species — to commercial trawling.

**Litigation**: Federal district court in Honolulu entered a preliminary injunction in August 2025. The court found plaintiffs demonstrated likely success on the merits. The case continues in the District of Hawaii.

### 18. Northeast Canyons and Seamounts Marine National Monument — Atlantic
**Rule**: Presidential Proclamation; NOAA commercial fishing regulation rescission
**Status**: [FINAL — litigation filed; no injunction]

**Dates**: Trump proclamation February 6, 2026. NOAA rescinded commercial fishing prohibition April 2026 (SBA Office of Advocacy confirmed reopening April 8, 2026).

**What changed**: The Northeast Canyons and Seamounts Marine National Monument (4,913 square miles, approximately 130 miles south of Cape Cod) was designated by President Obama in 2016 and protects deep-sea canyons and seamounts hosting endangered corals, sea turtles, and large whale species. The commercial fishing ban — long-finned squid, Atlantic mackerel, and others — was lifted. Some gear restrictions remain.

**Stated justification**: Relief for New England commercial fishing industry; claimed that the monument designation overstepped presidential Antiquities Act authority.

**Litigation**: Conservation Law Foundation and NRDC have challenged. CLF previously won protections for the monument in litigation during Trump's first term. No injunction as of compilation date.

### 19. NOAA Staffing and Structural Degradation
**Type**: Administrative/budget action
**Status**: [ONGOING]

**Dates**: February 27, 2025: approximately 800 NOAA employees fired. Plans reported to gut NOAA Fisheries and transfer most functions to U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, with NOAA's budget cut by approximately $1.6 billion (from $6.0B to $4.4B).

**What changed**: NOAA's capacity to conduct fishery stock assessments, climate modeling, weather forecasting, and marine conservation enforcement has been directly degraded. NOAA Fisheries — which manages 200+ fish stocks worth over $224 billion in economic activity — faces possible functional elimination as an agency. The structural transfer to USFWS would dismantle decades of specialized oceanographic and fisheries science expertise.

**Real-world impact**: Fishery stock assessments are the legal basis for sustainable catch limits under the Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act. Without adequate stock assessment capacity, overfishing becomes more likely and enforcement of sustainable yield limits becomes impossible. Climate modeling capacity loss affects agricultural, public safety, and disaster preparedness functions far beyond conservation.

## V. Department of Energy (DOE)

### 22. Appliance and Equipment Energy Efficiency Standards — Broad Rollback
**Rule**: Multiple simultaneous proceedings covering 16+ product categories
**Code**: 10 CFR Part 430 (consumer products); 10 CFR Part 431 (commercial equipment); governed by the Energy Policy and Conservation Act (EPCA), 42 U.S.C. §§ 6291–6316
**Status**: [PROPOSED — not yet final; one withdrawal finalized]

**Dates**: February 2025: DOE postponed implementation of seven Biden-era efficiency standards. March 2025: DOE officially withdrew four standards (electric motors, ceiling fans, dehumidifiers, external power supplies). May 9, 2025: Trump signed memorandum directing DOE to review and rescind appliance standards for dishwashers, toilets, showerheads, and washing machines. May 12, 2025: DOE pre-published dozens of proposed rules to withdraw covered product determinations and rescind existing standards for 16+ categories.

**What changed — categories affected**: Air conditioners and heat pumps (compliance delayed); gas instantaneous water heaters (new "natural gas" efficiency category created); walk-in coolers and freezers (effective date postponed); electric motors (standard withdrawn); ceiling fans (standard withdrawn); dehumidifiers (standard withdrawn); external power supplies (standard withdrawn); dishwashers, showerheads, washing machines, toilets (under proposed rescission). In many cases, DOE proposes to eliminate entire product categories from regulation rather than merely weaken individual standards.

**The anti-backsliding legal conflict**: EPCA Section 325(o) explicitly prohibits DOE from setting any efficiency standard that is "less stringent than a previous final standard" after that standard has been published in the Federal Register. At least one federal circuit court has interpreted this provision as a hard prohibition on backsliding. DOE's proposed counter-arguments: (a) the anti-backsliding provision does not apply to regulatory standards, only to statutory floors; (b) withdrawing a "covered product determination" is categorically different from weakening a standard. These arguments are widely viewed as legally weak. A coalition of 12 state AGs filed opposition comments July 15, 2025. Manufacturers — who had invested in compliance — overwhelmingly opposed the rollbacks (30+ comments opposing; zero supporting).

**Real-world impact**: The Appliance Standards Awareness Project estimates that efficient appliance standards have saved American consumers approximately $500 billion in energy costs since 1990 and reduced electricity demand equivalent to 240 large power plants. Rolling back standards would increase utility bills, raise domestic GHG emissions, and strand manufacturing investments already made to comply with the Biden-era rules.

**Litigation**: Multiple state AGs and environmental groups have announced intent to challenge finalized rollbacks under the EPCA anti-backsliding prohibition. No finalized rule challenged as of compilation date.

## VI. Cross-Agency / White House Actions

### 23. Social Cost of Carbon — Eliminated from Regulatory Analysis
**Rule**: Executive Order + OMB directive disbanding Interagency Working Group on Social Cost of Carbon
**Status**: [FINAL — administrative action]

**Dates**: Interagency Working Group disbanded January 30, 2026. Agencies directed to use 2003 estimates for social cost of carbon in regulatory analysis.

**What changed**: The Biden administration's social cost of carbon (SCC) was approximately $190/ton of CO2 (2020 dollars), reflecting peer-reviewed estimates of economic damages from climate change. The Trump administration disbanded the IWG and directed agencies to use the 2003 Bush-era estimate of approximately $7/ton. This 96% reduction in the SCC value makes emissions reductions appear far less cost-effective in regulatory analyses, systematically biasing rulemaking against climate protections. When agencies perform cost-benefit analysis for any new rule, using $7/ton rather than $190/ton makes polluting vastly cheaper in accounting terms.

**Stated justification**: The administration argued the Biden SCC was inflated by including global climate damages rather than limiting analysis to purely domestic costs.

**Real-world impact**: The SCC is used in regulatory impact analyses across dozens of agencies for hundreds of rules annually. Its dramatic reduction will make it mathematically easier for EPA, DOE, and DOT to justify rolling back environmental rules and harder to justify new protections. A federal district court ruled January 30, 2026, that DOE's Climate Working Group violated the Federal Advisory Committee Act; broader litigation on the SCC methodology is being developed.

### 24. Paris Agreement Withdrawal
**Status**: [FINAL]
**Date**: U.S. withdrawal became effective January 27, 2026.

The U.S. formally re-withdrew from the Paris Agreement on Climate Change. The withdrawal eliminates the U.S. Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitment to reduce GHG emissions 50–52% below 2005 levels by 2030, removes U.S. participation in international climate finance mechanisms, and signals to domestic courts that international climate commitments will not inform regulatory interpretation. This is the second U.S. withdrawal from Paris; the first (2017–2021) was reversed by President Biden on his first day in office.
