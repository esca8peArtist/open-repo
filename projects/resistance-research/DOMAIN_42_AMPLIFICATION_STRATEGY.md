## Executive Summary

The Domain 42 research—Drug Policy, Regulatory Capture, and Democratic Legitimacy—identifies three interlocking mechanisms that insulate federal drug policy from democratic accountability:

1. **Regulatory capture**: DEA (law enforcement agency) controls scheduling despite CSA statutory design favoring HHS (health agency)
2. **Felony disenfranchisement feedback loop**: Communities most affected by enforcement are systematically excluded from policy-making due to conviction-based disenfranchisement
3. **Federal-state democratic conflict**: 24 states have enacted legalization via ballot initiative; federal framework prevents implementation

The June 29 DEA hearing (Docket DEA-1362) is a critical inflection point. The deadline to file a participation notice is **May 28, 2026 (16 days from today)**. This amplification strategy provides:

- **Media calendar** (May 12-28) with journalist targeting by sector and week
- **Sector-specific messaging** for drug policy, civil rights, academic, and federalism audiences
- **Organization preparation checklist** (internal briefing, social media, talking points, docket filing instructions)
- **Impact assessment framework** (metrics, decision tree, contingency triggers)

## Part 2: Sector-Specific Messaging Variants

**Core framing**: Regulatory capture as mechanism preventing democratic accountability

Key messages:
- DEA controls scheduling despite CSA statutory design favoring HHS (health)
- Mason Marks (Yale Law Journal) documented how DEA's administrative practice diverged from statutory design
- April 23 DOJ order locked this imbalance in place
- Reform pathway: APA procedural reform for independent ALJ pool + HHS statutory authority restoration

**Materials to provide**: Domain 42 Section 2 (regulatory capture) + Section 6.1 (APA procedural reform) + Mason Marks Yale Law Journal citation

### Civil Rights & Disenfranchisement Organizations (Category B)

**Core framing**: Democratic exclusion feedback loop linking criminal enforcement, felony disenfranchisement, and political representation

Key messages:
- Drug enforcement targets Black communities at 3.64x rate despite equal usage
- Felony disenfranchisement removes voting rights from enforcement targets at disproportionate rates
- Legalization alone doesn't close this gap (Weill Cornell May 1 data shows disparities persist/increase under partial legalization)
- Reform pathway: Voting rights restoration + democratic design of enforcement accountability

**Materials to provide**: Domain 42 Section 3 (disenfranchisement feedback loop) + Weill Cornell May 1 data + Sentencing Project + ACLU data
