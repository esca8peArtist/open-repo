## Section 3: Public Comment Submission Strategy

### 3.1 Organizing 15–30 Organizations for Timely Public Comment

The participation notice (due May 28) is not the same as a written comment to the administrative record. Participation notices establish the right to testify or submit written evidence at the June 29 hearing. Written comments are a separate track — they go directly to the docket as written evidence, do not require a prior participation notice in all circumstances, and typically remain open for 30+ days after the hearing concludes.

For organizations that cannot file a participation notice by May 28 (those with longer internal approval chains, such as NAACP LDF and Weill Cornell), the written comment track is the participation pathway. Written comments to DEA-1362 should reference the same democratic exclusion framing and the same empirical evidence as the participation notices — they have equal standing in the administrative record.

**Coordination target: 15–30 organizations.** Based on the contact matrix in `domain-42-dea-briefing-contacts.md` and `domain-42-outreach-prioritization.md`, the realistic participation universe is:
- 8 Tier 1 organizations (Category A): institutional capacity to file participation notices within 5 days of outreach
- 5 Tier 2 organizations (Category B): participation plausible with 7–14 days of lead time
- 4 state AG offices (Category C): likely to issue public statements rather than direct filings
- 3+ SSDP chapters per SSDP national activation: volume count but each is a separate filing
- Administrative law faculty: 1–2 expert witness submissions (higher value than volume)

### 3.2 What Domain 42 Research Emphasizes — Three Elements for Every Comment

Every public comment that draws on Domain 42 research should include at least one element from each of the three structural mechanisms, regardless of the organization's primary framing:

**Element 1 — Regulatory capture**: The scheduling process has been captured by DEA, a law enforcement agency with enforcement-biased institutional incentives, producing scheduling outcomes systematically more restrictive than HHS public health expertise and the CSA's statutory text require. Citation: Mason Marks, "Separation of Drug Scheduling Powers," Yale Law Journal (2024); Regulatory Review, February 2026. The June 29 hearing is adjudicated by DEA employees — the capture extends to the proceeding structure itself.

**Element 2 — Felony disenfranchisement feedback loop**: Drug enforcement produces felony convictions at racially disparate rates (3.64x arrest rate for Black Americans; 11x felony arrest rate in New York City 2023). Those convictions produce disenfranchisement. The communities most subjected to enforcement — and therefore most affected by scheduling decisions — have reduced political representation in precisely the jurisdictions where enforcement is most aggressive. The 4 million disenfranchised Americans documented by the Sentencing Project are not background data; they are evidence of a structural exclusion mechanism that insulates the scheduling regime from democratic accountability.

**Element 3 — Federal-state democratic conflict**: 24 states enacted recreational cannabis legalization through direct democracy mechanisms (Colorado Amendment 64: 55.32%; California Prop 64: 57.13%; Michigan Prop 18-1: 56%) and legislative action. 68% of Americans support full legalization (Gallup 2024, highest recorded figure). The federal Schedule I framework overrides these democratic outcomes. The SAFER Banking Act has passed the House seven times with bipartisan support and has not received a Senate floor vote in eight introduction cycles. This is not democratic disagreement — it is a structural failure of democratic resolution.
