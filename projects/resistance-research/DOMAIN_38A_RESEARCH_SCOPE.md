## Section 1: Core Research Questions — What the Document Answers

Domain 38-A addresses the following questions, all of which are answered in the existing document. This section documents the answers so the distribution campaign can be tailored to each audience segment's primary question.

**Q1: What does Section 702 actually authorize, and what is the "backdoor search" problem?**

Section 702 authorizes surveillance of foreign persons located outside the U.S. without individual warrants. The FISC approves "certifications" (programmatic frameworks) rather than individual warrants. The backdoor search problem: the government cannot target a U.S. person under Section 702, but it can and does query databases of 702-collected communications using U.S. persons' identifiers (names, emails, phone numbers) without a warrant. This is the structural Fourth Amendment violation documented in United States v. Hasbajrami (EDNY, December 2024). See Domain 38-A Sections 1.1, 1.2, and 2.1.

**Q2: What happened in the April 2026 reauthorization fight and what is the June 12 deadline?**

Four consecutive legislative failures (clean five-year renewal, 18-month Trump extension, 10-day stopgap, Johnson's Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act with CBDC attachment) produced a 45-day extension signed April 30. Section 702 authority runs through June 12, 2026. The Cotton-Warner deal secured declassification of the March 17, 2026 FISC compliance opinion within 15 days (approximately May 15 release). See Domain 38-A Section 1.3 for the full vote sequence.

**Q3: What does the March 2026 FISC opinion contain, and why does it matter?**

The opinion documents that the FBI's claimed remediation of unauthorized querying — which DOJ reported to Congress in early 2025 — did not actually solve the problem, and that the off-books querying extends beyond the FBI to the NSA and CIA. The FISC found that the FBI's "filtering tools" used to conduct queries outside the formal query reporting system were never counted or audited. The true number of U.S. person queries in 2024-2025 is unknown and unknowable under current reporting frameworks. The opinion's full contents remain classified until approximately May 15. See Domain 38-A Sections 1.2 and 6.1.

**Q4: What is the Hasbajrami ruling and why is it the most important pending surveillance case?**

Judge LaShann DeArcy Hall (EDNY) held on December 2, 2024 — publicly released January 21, 2025 — that the FBI's warrantless backdoor searches of Section 702 databases ordinarily violate the Fourth Amendment. It is the first such ruling in the 18-year history of the program. The ACLU is pressing the ruling before the Second Circuit. A favorable Second Circuit ruling before June 12 would materially strengthen the warrant-requirement argument in the Senate. See Domain 38-A Section 2.1 and Section 12.1.

**Q5: What oversight mechanisms have collapsed, and what does functioning oversight look like?**

PCLOB was dismantled by presidential firing January 27, 2025; a federal district court ruled the firings unlawful May 21, 2025; PCLOB remains inoperable. The DOJ OIG has been characterized as having been "cowed into inactivity." The FISC is structurally one-sided (no adversary). The FBI domestic terrorism infrastructure (DTOS) has been gutted. Against this: the UK's double-lock model (IPA 2016), Canada's NSIRA (cross-agency review authority), Australia's IGIS (proactive inspection authority), and New Zealand's bipartisan parliamentary committee provide the reform architecture. See Domain 38-A Sections 3, 5, and 7.

**Q6: What is the Government Surveillance Reform Act and what does it actually require?**

GSRA (S. 4082, introduced March 12, 2026, Wyden-Lee-Lummis-Warren) contains: warrant requirement for U.S. person queries with genuine emergency exceptions; data broker loophole prohibition (cannot purchase what you cannot warrantlessly collect); PCLOB restoration with statutory independence protections; FISC opinion transparency (mandatory publication of redacted opinions, amicus appeal rights); RISAA "electronic communication service provider" definition rollback. Section-by-section summary from Wyden's office is at https://www.wyden.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/section-by-section_for_government_surveillance_reform_act_of_2026pdf.pdf. See Domain 38-A Section 10.1.

**Q7: What are the June 12 outcome scenarios?**

Scenario A (15-25% probability): Three-year reauthorization with GSRA core provisions. Scenario B (50-60%): Three-year reauthorization without warrant requirement, possibly with PCLOB restoration and enhanced attorney-level approval. Scenario C (20-30%): Second short-term extension forcing fall negotiation. See Domain 38-A Section 14.
