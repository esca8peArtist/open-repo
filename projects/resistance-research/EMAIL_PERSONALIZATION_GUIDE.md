# Email Personalization Guide — Phase A Institutional Outreach

*Built April 27, 2026 (Session 522). This guide gives you the structural skeleton for each institutional category. It does not write your emails for you — it tells you what goes where, what the value proposition is, and what tone to use. The existing `distribution-institutional-outreach-templates.md` has fully drafted Category 1-4 templates; this file adds the academic, think tank, labor, civil rights, and foundation categories with new framing frameworks.*

*Time required per email: 20-30 minutes for a truly personalized email. It is better to send 10 well-personalized emails than 50 generic ones. Every bracketed item is a personalization point that separates your email from the automated institutional noise recipients receive daily.*

---

## Before You Write Any Email: The Research Step

Spend 10-15 minutes on each recipient before drafting. Look up:

1. **Their most recent published work** (paper, article, testimony, blog post) — reference it by title or finding in your opening
2. **Their institutional affiliation's current campaigns or research priorities** — align your framing to what they are actively working on
3. **Whether they have commented publicly on any of the issues the proposal addresses** — if yes, cite it; if no, use their general area
4. **Any connection you share** — a co-author, a former professor, a mutual colleague, a conference you both attended — mention it if authentic

This research is not optional for high-priority contacts (Pillars 1-2). It is the difference between an email that gets deleted and one that generates a response.

## Template Category A: Law School Faculty Outreach

### Tone and Framing

Academic framing. Position the research as scholarly work, not advocacy. Use passive constructions where appropriate. Lead with evidence, not conclusions. The proposal is CC 4.0 — emphasize that it is a resource, not a product.

Academics respond to: (1) novel empirical findings, (2) rigorous sourcing, (3) gaps in existing literature they could fill, (4) crossover relevance to their current research.

Do not: pitch the proposal as important or urgent. Let the content make that case. Do not ask for endorsement — ask for feedback. Academics are far more comfortable providing feedback than endorsing.

### Template A-1: Constitutional and Election Law Faculty

**Subject line options (choose one; personalize the bracketed item):**
- Independent Research on [Electoral Reform / Judicial Independence / Prosecutorial Accountability] — Free Resource
- 35-Domain Democratic Renewal Framework — Seeking Scholarly Feedback on [Domain X]
- Research on [specific topic] that may be relevant to your work on [their work area]

**Email structure:**

---

[First name],

I'm reaching out because your recent work on [specific paper, article, or testimony — cite by title or key finding] directly overlaps with a domain I've been researching, and I think the analysis may be useful to your work — and I would value your feedback on where it falls short from a scholarly perspective.

I'm an independent researcher — not affiliated with any institution. Over the past two years I've built a 35-domain framework for democratic structural reform, grounded in comparative constitutional law, longitudinal movement research, and the evidentiary record from functioning democracies. The framework is published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0.

The domain most directly relevant to your work is [Domain X] — [one sentence on what it covers]. The analysis includes:

- [Most relevant specific finding or argument, with one data point]
- [Second relevant finding — connect to their published work or specialty]
- [Third relevant element — this should be the one most likely to be new information to them]

[If their work is cited in the domain: "Your work on [topic] is cited in this section — the analysis builds substantially on [specific contribution]."]

The full domain document is [link]. The full 35-domain proposal is [link]. Both are CC 4.0 — cite, excerpt, and adapt freely.

I have two requests:

First, feedback. Specifically: [identify one or two concrete gaps or questions you genuinely want their input on — e.g., "The prosecutorial accountability section's treatment of Imbler v. Pachtman — does the critique of absolute immunity doctrine hold up under current Second Circuit precedent?" or "The redistricting analysis relies heavily on the efficiency gap metric — given the critiques of that metric in the Rucho decision, does the framing need to be restructured?"]. Practitioner and scholarly feedback is the most valuable input I could receive.

Second, if you find these materials useful for your work, I would welcome citation in your research or introduction to colleagues working on [related topic].

[Your name]
[Optional: any institutional affiliation or relevant credential — only include if it adds credibility]

---

**Personalization checklist for Template A-1:**
- [ ] Specific paper/article cited in opening
- [ ] Domain number and one-line description inserted
- [ ] Three relevant findings identified (not generic — specific to this recipient's specialty)
- [ ] One genuine feedback question (not a placeholder — show you read their work)
- [ ] Links inserted (Gist URLs for proposal and domain document)
- [ ] Subject line personalized

**Subject line options:**

[Name],

**The Litigation Tracker** [link] documents active federal cases in [relevant areas — e.g., immigration enforcement, civil service dismantling, constitutional accountability], with case names, current court, status, next deadlines, and links to case pages. **The [relevant domain document]** [link] covers:

## Template Category B: Think Tank Outreach

### Tone and Framing

Policy/research framing. Think tank audiences are professional policy researchers. They respond to rigor, citation density, and current relevance. The proposal's connection to active legislative vehicles and current litigation is the key value proposition.

Do not position this as a critique of their work. Position it as complementary research that extends their analysis or provides comparative data they may find useful.

Think tanks respond to: (1) novel international benchmarks they haven't used, (2) fiscal quantification that supports their policy case, (3) cross-domain synthesis that bridges their issue-specific work, (4) research tools (trackers, frameworks) that can be cited in their own publications.

---

**Subject line options:**
- 35-Domain Democratic Renewal Research — Complementary Data for [Program Name]

- Independent Research on [Topic] — May Be Useful for [Specific Think Tank Program]

**[Secondary relevant domain]**: [Two sentences — connect to a different program or publication of theirs if possible.]

[If they have cited the proposal's sources or rely on the same data: "The [Domain X] section relies substantially on [organization]'s own [specific publication] — I've tried to synthesize it in ways that may be useful for your team's current work on [specific topic]."]

I've built a 35-domain democratic renewal framework — independently researched, published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0. The sections most relevant to your work focus on [congressional power / civil service integrity / war powers / separation of powers] — areas where I think a genuine cross-partisan case can be made on structural grounds.

Specifically, [Domain 34 (Congressional Power-of-the-Purse Restoration) / Domain 2 (Civil Service Integrity) / Domain 19f (War Powers)] covers:

- [Separation-of-powers argument that crosses ideological lines]
- [Historical or comparative precedent that is not ideologically loaded]

Full proposal: [link]. Domain document: [link]. CC 4.0.

**Personalization checklist for Template B-2:**
- [ ] Their specific publication cited by title
- [ ] The framing is institutional/structural — not progressive policy

## Template Category C: Labor Union Outreach

### Tone and Framing

Worker dignity framing. Lead with the connection to their members' immediate material interests, not the structural framework. Labor union communications staff receive many policy research deliveries — differentiate by being specific about which union members are directly affected and how.

The two most important elements for labor outreach: (1) the fiscal case for each reform (how much money would members gain or retain), and (2) the organizing strategy analysis (what the research says about what builds durable power).

---

### Template C-2: Sector-Specific Unions (Education, Healthcare, Public Employees)

**Framing difference**: These unions have a specific policy environment (state-level bargaining laws, sector-specific regulations, OBBBA Medicaid impacts for healthcare). Lead with the domain most specifically relevant to their sector.

**Key differences from C-1**:
- For education unions (NEA, AFT): Lead with Domain 10 (education) and Domain 27 (academic freedom under siege)
- For healthcare unions (SEIU Healthcare, NNU): Lead with Domain 31 (OBBBA Medicaid crisis) — 11.8M people losing coverage affects healthcare worker employment directly
- For public employee unions (AFSCME, AFGE): Lead with Domain 2 (Schedule F and civil service dismantling — this is an existential threat to their membership's employment status)

## Template Category D: Civil Rights Organization Outreach

### Tone and Framing

Justice framing. Lead with the documented harm, not the structural analysis. Civil rights organizations are practitioner organizations — they need research they can use in litigation, advocacy, and communications, not academic frameworks. The litigation tracker and the domain-specific documented harms are the lead value propositions.

Demonstrate that you understand the racial justice dimensions of each domain — the proposal integrates these throughout, but your email needs to make them explicit.

---

### Template D-2: Immigration and Intersectional Civil Rights

**Framing difference**: Immigration organizations (UnidosUS, Mijente, NILC) work at the intersection of civil rights, labor, and criminal justice. Lead with Domain 16 (immigration), but connect explicitly to the intersectional domains.

**Key points for immigration framing**:
- Domain 16 includes: end mass detention, community-based alternatives ($20/day vs. $140-200/day for detention), independent immigration courts, pathway to legal status
- Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) directly threatens immigrant communities through selective enforcement
- Domain 14 (criminal justice) and Domain 16 are structurally linked — mass criminalization is a prerequisite for mass deportation
- The fiscal case: community-based alternatives to detention save $4.4B+/year compared to current detention costs

## Template Category E: Foundation/Funder Outreach

### Tone and Framing

Movement-building framing. Foundations respond to: (1) demonstrated reach and uptake (cite any responses from institutional outreach), (2) clear theory of change, (3) gap the research fills that other funded work doesn't, (4) potential for follow-on work or sustained impact.

Do not ask for money in your first email. The goal of the first foundation contact is to get the program officer to read the research and schedule a call. The ask for follow-on support comes in the conversation, not the first email.

Critical note on timing: Send foundation outreach only after you have Phase A momentum — at least 4-6 weeks into the calendar, when you can reference initial uptake. A cold foundation email with no distribution record is much weaker than one that says "Harvard Law's election law clinic has integrated this into their research framework" (or equivalent).

This is not an email — it is a direct application to the Open Society Fellowship program. The fellowship is for individuals working on open society issues who are not affiliated with an institution. The 35-domain proposal qualifies on its face.

## Tone Guide: What Works in Each Context

| Audience | Lead With | Do Not Lead With |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Law school faculty | Specific finding relevant to their published work; request for feedback | The proposal's importance; urgency; political stakes |
| Law school clinics | The litigation tracker; specific legal arguments | The structural framework; international comparisons |
| Think tanks | Novel empirical data; connection to their existing publications | Critique of their past work; ideological framing |
| Labor unions | Fiscal estimates; member-specific impact; organizing strategy evidence | Constitutional frameworks; comparative policy |
| Civil rights orgs | Documented harm; litigation hook; trackers | Abstract structural analysis |
| Foundations | Theory of change; gap analysis; demonstrated uptake | Grant request (save for second contact) |

## Follow-Up Protocol (All Categories)

**When to follow up**: 10-14 days after the initial email, if no response.

**How to follow up**: One sentence. "Following up on my email from [date] — happy to send a shorter summary or route to a specific colleague if that would be more useful." Do not re-pitch.

**After one follow-up, stop**. The email was received. If it is useful to them, they will respond. Repeated follow-ups damage credibility in institutional contexts.

**Track responses**: Note who responded, what they said, what they asked for, and whether they offered to make introductions. This data shapes your Week 9+ outreach.
