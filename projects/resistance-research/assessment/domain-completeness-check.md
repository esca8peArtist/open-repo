## Part II: Gap Analysis — Five Candidate Areas

The task specified five candidate gap areas. Below is an assessment of each against the current 25 domains.

---

### Candidate A: Land/Water Rights and Indigenous Sovereignty

**Current coverage**:
Domain 15 (Environment and Climate) contains subsection 15d which codifies tribal co-management of federal lands using the Bears Ears Inter-Tribal Coalition model, extended to all culturally significant federal public lands. It cites New Zealand's Whanganui River legal personhood as a model for centering indigenous ecological relationships. Domain 22 (Reparations and Racial Justice) includes New Zealand's Treaty of Waitangi and Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission on Indian Residential Schools among its international precedents. Domain 11 (Healthcare) mentions tribal lands as a target for National Health Service Corps expansion.

**What is NOT covered**:
- Federal trust responsibility and the history of treaty violations as a current legal and political crisis
- Tribal data sovereignty (who controls census data, health data, enrollment data about Native peoples — documented as an active 2026 issue by the Indigenous Data Sovereignty Summit)
- Indian Child Welfare Act attacks (Haaland v. Brackeen — litigation context remains active)
- Native American birthright citizenship as a distinct legal category (NARF documented Supreme Court arguments in April 2026 where the federal government argued Tribal Nations were always under U.S. authority in ways that contradict centuries of sovereignty doctrine)
- Budget cuts to Indian Country programs (Trump FY 2026 budget contains hundreds of millions in Indian Country cuts without tribal consultation)
- Mining and resource extraction on tribal lands (Boundary Waters/Iron Range — Indigenous lawmakers calling this an "assault on tribal sovereignty," April 2026)
- Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls (MMIWG) crisis — federal response architecture
- Tribal court jurisdiction, particularly for non-member defendants following McGirt v. Oklahoma (2020) and its subsequent political rollbacks

**Assessment**: The current framework treats indigenous issues as supporting evidence within environmental and racial justice domains. It does not address the distinct legal-political architecture of tribal sovereignty — the nation-within-a-nation status, the federal trust responsibility, treaty rights, or the 2026 active attacks on that framework. This is a structural gap. However, see the severity assessment in Part III before concluding.

---

### Candidate B: Disability Justice and Medical Autonomy

**Current coverage**:
Domain 18 (Social Safety Net) subsection 18e is explicitly titled "Disability benefits reform" and covers SSI asset limits, disability determination process reform (reducing 19-month wait to 90 days), the five-month SSDI waiting period, and realistic disability criteria for modern labor markets. Domain 11 (Healthcare) covers mental health parity and rural access including telehealth expansion. Domain 7 (Rights Protection) covers disability-accommodated remote voting as a long-term voting technology application. Domain 17 (Labor) covers workplace safety and accommodation standards.

**What is NOT covered**:
- The $1 trillion Medicaid cut (One Big Beautiful Bill Act) as a direct disability crisis: CBO estimates 11.8 million individuals losing coverage, with home and community-based services (HCBS) — the programs that allow disabled people to live independently rather than in institutions — being the first cut by states. Idaho is already weighing eliminating state disability programs (March 2026).
- Work requirements for Medicaid as a disability justice issue — disabled people who do not meet the narrow SSA definition but cannot meet work requirements are eliminated from coverage
- The ADA enforcement infrastructure under attack via DOGE and DOJ Civil Rights Division gutting
- Institutionalization risk as Medicaid HCBS cuts force re-institutionalization — the Olmstead decision's community integration mandate depends on funding that is now being eliminated
- Medical autonomy for disabled people: forced treatment, guardianship abuse, reproductive coercion of disabled women
- The democracy dimension: 61 million disabled Americans whose civic participation depends on accessible polling places, accessible government services, and accessible public information — all of which are being degraded simultaneously

**Assessment**: Disability justice is substantially covered within Domain 18 and Domain 11, but the 2026 Medicaid crisis has escalated it to a distinct emergency that the existing subsection does not fully capture. The question is whether this warrants a new domain or a subsection expansion. See Part III.

### Candidate C: Immigration and Border Crisis

**Current coverage**:
Domain 16 is fully dedicated to Immigration and Citizenship — five subsections covering end to mass detention, independent immigration courts, comprehensive reform, dismantling the surveillance-enforcement complex, and birthright citizenship protection. Domain 7 subsection 7b provides additional civil liberties standards for immigration enforcement.

**Assessment**: Immigration is comprehensively covered. Not a gap.

## Part III: Three Additional Candidates Not in the Original List

The gap analysis above and current April 2026 research surface three additional candidates that warrant assessment.

---

### Candidate F: Reproductive Rights and Bodily Autonomy (STRONGEST CANDIDATE)

**Current coverage**:
The evidence deepening file `rights-protection-evidence.md` contains a substantial Section 7e ("Reproductive and Bodily Autonomy Rights") covering the post-Dobbs landscape (31 million women of reproductive age in states with bans, 13 states with 2025 bills proposing homicide charges, digital surveillance of reproductive health, the data broker loophole). This section cites France's 2024 constitutional abortion amendment and Germany's Section 218 reform commission as international benchmarks.

**However**: This material is in an evidence-deepening file, NOT in the main `democratic-renewal-proposal.md`. The proposal itself mentions reproductive rights exactly four times — three of those are passing references (the Dobbs decision as an example of SCOTUS overreach, Ireland's abortion referendum as a citizens' assembly case study, and two mentions of abortion in the context of other arguments). There is no Domain 7 subsection, no reform architecture, no fiscal analysis, and no implementation pathway for reproductive rights in the main proposal.

**What is missing from the proposal**:
- The Women's Health Protection Act as the primary federal statutory vehicle — it would establish a federal statutory right to provide and access abortion care, overriding state bans. As of April 2026, it is Senate-blocked but has all Democratic senators as cosponsors.
- The Trump administration's specific April 2026 attacks: rescission of Biden-era emergency abortion guidance (putting lives at risk in medical emergencies), threatening states with federal funding cuts for protecting abortion access, a Medicaid rule targeting abortion providers (set to expire April 2026 per Stateline)
- State-level ballot initiative successes — voters in multiple states have passed constitutional amendments protecting abortion rights, but Republican legislators are now introducing laws to make citizen initiatives harder (documented as a direct democracy attack)
- Contraceptive access and the Dobbs majority's implicit threat to Griswold (contraception), Lawrence (same-sex relationships), and Obergefell (marriage equality)
- Gender-affirming care bans — 26 states with bans on gender-affirming care for minors, several extending to adults; federal attempts to withdraw Medicaid funding for gender-affirming care
- Maternal mortality — the U.S. has the highest maternal mortality rate of any wealthy democracy (32.9 per 100,000 live births vs. 3-8 in peer nations); Black maternal mortality is 69.9 per 100,000 — a racial justice dimension
- EMTALA enforcement: the administration's position that emergency rooms are not required to perform abortions even when necessary to save a patient's life

**Severity assessment (April 2026)**: High and escalating. The Dobbs decision eliminated a right held for 49 years. The current administration is actively using federal funding leverage to attack state-level protections. 31 million women of reproductive age live under bans. The downstream threat to Griswold and Obergefell is not hypothetical — it is the explicit invitation of the Thomas concurrence, and the doctrinal logic of the Dobbs majority applies equally.

**Conclusion**: Reproductive rights and bodily autonomy is the clearest gap in the framework. It is partially researched (in the evidence file) but has no domain-level treatment in the main proposal. The rights protection domain (7) addresses surveillance, protest, immigration, and privacy in detail but does not contain a reform architecture for reproductive rights or gender-affirming care. This warrants Domain 26.

### Candidate G: University Autonomy and Academic Freedom

**Current coverage**:
Domain 10 (Education) covers K-12 equity, civics education, teacher compensation, and debt-free college but does not address university institutional autonomy, faculty governance, tenure protections, or academic freedom as a democratic institution.

**What is missing**:
- The Columbia settlement as a template: $200M+ payment plus governance restructuring and policy changes demanded by the administration in exchange for restoring federal funding — a model of federal coercive leverage over private institutional autonomy
- Federal funding as a coercion mechanism: the administration has used funding freezes as leverage to force universities to change admissions policies, crack down on protests, eliminate DEI programs, and restructure governance
- The V-Dem Academic Freedom Index: U.S. institutional university autonomy has fallen over 50% in a decade, with the sharpest decline in 2025-2026 (April 2026 Chemistry World)
- Accreditation weaponization: the administration is rewriting accreditation rules to change who oversees institutional quality determination, which determines access to federal student aid — a mechanism for defunding resistant institutions
- State-level attacks: one state eliminated tenure for new hires at public universities (February 2026); faculty course content restrictions across 20+ states
- The democracy-specific dimension: universities are the primary institution producing the researchers, journalists, lawyers, and civil servants on whom democratic governance depends; gutting them is an infrastructure attack

**Severity assessment (April 2026)**: Significant and rapidly escalating. The Columbia settlement as a template is specifically alarming because it demonstrates that coercive leverage can produce institutional capitulation. However, this crisis is substantially downstream of the executive power and institutional integrity failures already covered in Domain 2 (DOGE and civil service) and Domain 7 (protest rights, data weaponization). The mechanism — using federal funding as coercion — is the same mechanism addressed in Domain 2, applied to a different sector.

**Conclusion**: Warrants a subsection in Domain 10 (Education) rather than a standalone domain. The university autonomy crisis is real and severe but derives from the same executive overreach mechanisms as Domains 2 and 7. A new Domain 10f or Domain 10g ("University Autonomy and Academic Freedom as Democratic Infrastructure") would integrate it at the right structural level.

### Candidate H: Civil Society and Nonprofit Sector Protection

**Current coverage**:
The proposal addresses organized labor (Domain 17), civic education (Domain 3), and press freedom (Domain 8) but does not specifically address nonprofits and civil society organizations as an infrastructure category requiring protection.

**What is missing**:
- The administration's attempt to strip Harvard's tax-exempt status — legally constrained by 26 U.S.C. § 7217 (presidential prohibition on directing IRS investigations) but representing a direct threat
- DOGE staff embedded at the Vera Institute of Justice and CREW — targeting organizations specifically because of their accountability work
- Public Service Loan Forgiveness exclusions for nonprofits the administration deems to have "substantial illegal purpose"
- The chilling effect: foundation CEOs increasing spending on legal defense rather than programmatic work; $5.2M emergency fund from private foundations for rule-of-law organizations
- Legal framework gaps: the IRS independence protection in § 7217 is not well-known, not well-enforced, and could be circumvented through indirect pressure
- The democratic function of nonprofits: civic organizations, legal defense funds, mutual aid networks, and advocacy groups are the organizational infrastructure of democratic resistance

**Severity assessment (April 2026)**: Significant but partially covered. The civil society protection question is real. However, it maps closely onto the institutional integrity failures in Domain 2 (executive overreach, DOGE embedding), the whistleblower protections in Domain 7d, and the campaign finance reforms in Domain 7g (which address the nonprofit political spending architecture). The IRS independence issue is a tax policy enforcement question that touches Domain 5 (Fiscal Reform).

**Conclusion**: Does not warrant a standalone domain at this time. The civil society threat is a cross-cutting application of mechanisms already addressed in Domains 2, 5, and 7. A monitoring note in CHECKIN.md is appropriate, and a cross-reference in Domain 7 would capture it.

## Part IV: Overall Assessment

**Framework completeness verdict**: One significant gap identified. Domain 26 should be created.

**The gap**: Reproductive rights, bodily autonomy, and gender-affirming care have substantial evidence in the research archive (rights-protection-evidence.md Section 7e) but no reform architecture in the main proposal. This is the only crisis area of comparable scale and severity to the existing 25 domains that lacks a dedicated treatment with the standard format: structural problem, proposed reforms with evidence, international benchmarks, fiscal analysis, and implementation pathway.

**The comparison test**: Every other major US crisis domain at comparable severity level has its own domain. The 31 million women under abortion bans is a larger directly affected population than some domains that have full treatment. The administration's April 2026 active attacks (funding leverage, EMTALA rescission, Medicaid rules) are at least as severe as many domains added since April 2026. The international benchmark landscape is rich (France's 2024 constitutional amendment, Ireland's citizens' assembly model, WHO guidelines on reproductive health as a human right).

**Domains that do not need to be added**: Indigenous sovereignty (covered in Domains 15 and 22, though depth could be increased), disability justice (covered in Domains 11 and 18, though the 2026 Medicaid crisis warrants a deepening note), immigration (Domain 16 is comprehensive), media ownership (Domain 8 is comprehensive), housing (Domain 13 is comprehensive), university autonomy (warrants a subsection in Domain 10, not a new domain), civil society protection (cross-cutting, covered by existing domains).

## Part V: Domain 26 Outline — Reproductive Rights, Bodily Autonomy, and Gender Justice

*Approximate scope: 200-word outline as requested*

**Title**: Domain 26: Reproductive Rights, Bodily Autonomy, and Gender Justice

**The structural problem**: The Dobbs decision (June 2022) eliminated the federal constitutional right to abortion, creating a two-tier country where reproductive rights depend on geography. As of 2026, 31 million women of reproductive age live under bans or severe restrictions. The current administration is using federal funding leverage to attack state-level protections and has rescinded emergency abortion care guidance. The Dobbs doctrinal logic — rights not "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition" lack constitutional protection — is an explicit threat to Griswold (contraception), Lawrence (same-sex relationships), and Obergefell (marriage equality). Separately, 26 states have banned gender-affirming care for minors, several extending to adults. The U.S. maternal mortality rate (32.9 per 100,000) is the highest in the wealthy world; Black maternal mortality (69.9) constitutes a racial justice emergency.

**Proposed reform structure** (five subsections):

- **26a. Federal statutory right to abortion access** — Women's Health Protection Act as the primary vehicle; prohibit states from banning abortion before fetal viability; restore EMTALA emergency abortion mandate; block federal funding leverage against state protections
- **26b. Contraceptive access and Dobbs doctrinal limits** — codify Griswold by statute; federal contraceptive access guarantee; prohibit criminalization of contraception or travel for reproductive healthcare
- **26c. Gender-affirming care protection** — federal standard prohibiting state bans on evidence-based gender-affirming care; Medicare/Medicaid coverage parity; anti-discrimination protection for gender identity in healthcare
- **26d. Maternal health equity** — federal maternal mortality review mandate with racial disaggregation; funding for Black maternal health initiatives modeled on Momnibus Act; Medicaid expansion for postpartum coverage to 12 months (currently 60 days in many states)
- **26e. Reproductive surveillance prohibition** — extend the data broker loophole ban (Domain 7c/21) specifically to reproductive health data; prohibit law enforcement purchase of location data revealing clinic visits; Right to Contraception Act framework

**International benchmarks**: France's April 2024 constitutional amendment embedding abortion as a "guaranteed freedom" (the strongest protection anywhere); Ireland's citizens' assembly model proving constitutional change is achievable even on toxic political terrain; Canada's 1988 Morgentaler decision creating a rights-based framework without gestational limits; WHO Essential Medicines List (mifepristone) as the global healthcare standard.

**Fiscal**: $2-4B/year for comprehensive reproductive health coverage through Medicaid; $500M-1B/year for maternal health equity programs; net positive through reduced maternal mortality costs (each maternal death costs an estimated $1.2M in lost productivity and healthcare costs).

**Cross-domain connections**: Domain 7 (surveillance prohibition, Dobbs data risk), Domain 11 (healthcare parity, maternal mortality), Domain 22 (racial justice, Black maternal mortality), Domain 6 (Supreme Court accountability — Dobbs as the central example), Domain 21 (digital surveillance of reproductive data), Domain 15 (environmental justice — disproportionate pollution burden on communities with abortion bans).

## Part VI: Recommendations

1. **Create Domain 26** using the outline in Part V. It has the research foundation in `domain-deepening/rights-protection-evidence.md` Section 7e and needs: a `domain-26-reproductive-rights.md` deepening file and full integration into `democratic-renewal-proposal.md` in the standard domain format.

2. **Deepen Domain 10** with a new subsection (10f or 10g) on university autonomy and academic freedom, covering the Columbia settlement template, federal funding coercion, accreditation weaponization, and V-Dem's 50% decline in U.S. institutional autonomy. This is a subsection expansion, not a new domain.

3. **Add a deepening note to Domain 18** (Social Safety Net, subsection 18e) referencing the $1 trillion Medicaid cut's specific impact on HCBS and the institutionalization risk for disabled people — the 2026 Medicaid crisis escalates the disability section materially beyond what was written before the One Big Beautiful Bill Act passed.

4. **No further standalone domains** are warranted at this time. The 26-domain framework (25 current + Domain 26 on reproductive rights) covers the major structural crisis areas of US democracy as of April 2026 without redundancy.
