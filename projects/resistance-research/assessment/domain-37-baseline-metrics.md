# Domain 37: Pre-Distribution Baseline Metrics

**Established**: April 30, 2026
**Measurement window**: May 2026 – November 4, 2026 (Election Day)
**Purpose**: Six quantified pre-distribution baselines enabling rigorous pre-post measurement of framework impact on election protection coordination. Feeds `feedback-integration-roadmap.md` for the 6-month post-distribution impact assessment.

---

## Why Baselines Matter

Distribution impact cannot be attributed without a fixed pre-distribution snapshot. Domain 37's interference mechanisms are active and evolving — the legal and political landscape will change materially between now and November 4, 2026. This document locks six quantified baselines as of April 30, 2026, establishes the measurement protocol for each, and provides success criteria and failure modes that distinguish framework influence from ambient change.

The six metrics map directly to the five interference mechanisms documented in Domain 37. Each metric meets four criteria: (a) directly tied to a documented mechanism; (b) measurable via public sources at weekly or monthly frequency; (c) attributable — there is a plausible causal pathway through which framework distribution could influence the outcome; and (d) actionable — it maps to a specific advocacy window in Domain 37's activation timeline.

## Metric 1: DOJ Voter Roll Litigation — Active Case Count and Judicial Posture

**Baseline (April 30, 2026): 23 active cases**

The DOJ launched suits against 30 states and the District of Columbia demanding unredacted voter registration files, asserting authority under the Civil Rights Act of 1960 and the NVRA. As of April 30, the DOJ stands 0-for-6 at the district court level:

- **Dismissed**: California, Oregon, Michigan, Massachusetts, Rhode Island (all in appellate pipeline), plus Arizona (April 28, 2026 — Judge Brnovich ruled voter rolls are "not a document subject to request by the Attorney General" under the CRA of 1960)
- **Active / decision pending**: approximately 9 states, including Washington (allowed to proceed April 7, 2026)
- **Active / briefing ongoing**: approximately 8 additional states
- **Parallel challenge**: Common Cause v. DOJ (D.D.C., filed April 21, 2026) — seeks to block national voter database construction

At least 12 states have voluntarily complied or agreed to comply (Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming). Oklahoma received a settlement in exchange for compliance.

**Target states for state AG counter-suit tracking**: Washington, Connecticut, New York, New Jersey, Colorado, Minnesota, Illinois, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Maryland.

**Measurement metric**: Track new DOJ suits filed, district court dismissals, appellate decisions, and state AG counter-suits or amicus filings per month. Log each ruling against the legal arguments documented in Domain 37 Sections II.A–II.D.

**Success indicator**: Framework accelerates favorable state AG counter-suits by more than 3 months relative to the pre-distribution baseline trajectory. Proximate signal: an AG's motion or brief incorporates the SAVE error-rate argument (81% false positives from Missouri) or the CASA post-Anderson injunction architecture documented in Domain 37.

**Primary sources**: [UW State Democracy Research Initiative tracker](https://statedemocracy.law.wisc.edu/tracker-doj-lawsuits-states-voter-data/) | [Brennan Center DOJ tracker](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/tracker-justice-department-requests-voter-information) | [Democracy Docket active cases](https://www.democracydocket.com/cases/)

## Metric 3: CISA Election Security Budget — FY27 Appropriations Trajectory

**Baseline (April 30, 2026): $707 million proposed cut; election security program zeroed out**

Budget context:
- **FY25 CISA baseline**: approximately $3.0 billion total (Biden-era enacted level)
- **FY26 enacted**: approximately $2.6–2.7 billion (Congress narrowed the Trump proposal from a 16% cut to 4.6% in the FY26 appropriations cycle; $39.6 million was preserved for election security advisors and EI-ISAC)
- **FY27 proposed (April 7, 2026)**: complete elimination of the election security program — information-sharing support, dedicated election security advisors in every CISA region, EI-ISAC, and MS-ISAC funding all zeroed out; 867 positions eliminated; net funding reduction approximately $360 million
- **Congressional precedent**: The FY26 cycle demonstrated that bipartisan appropriators will fight to narrow cuts; Rep. Andrew Garbarino (R-NY) is the key Republican voice on record

**Authorization vs. appropriations gap**: CISA's authorizing legislation (the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency Act of 2018) imposes no funding floor on election security programs, meaning appropriators retain full discretion. This is the legal vulnerability the administration is exploiting.

**Measurement metric**: Track House Appropriations Subcommittee on Homeland Security markup date, proposed CISA allocation, election security line item, amendment activity, and final enacted level. Key markup window: June–September 2026.

**Success indicator**: Framework strengthens the budget defense argument, contributing to reversal of at least 50% of the proposed election security program cuts. Proximate signal: a floor or committee amendment restoring EI-ISAC funding cites the election infrastructure void argument documented in Domain 37.

**Primary sources**: [Nextgov CISA FY27 coverage](https://www.nextgov.com/cybersecurity/2026/04/trump-proposes-cutting-cisa-election-security-program-fy27-budget/412672/) | [CyberScoop appropriations tracking](https://cyberscoop.com/cisa-budget-135m-dhs-2026-house-appropriations/) | [Congress.gov Appropriations Committee](https://www.congress.gov/committees)

## Metric 4: Section 3 (14th Amendment) Litigation Readiness

**Baseline (April 30, 2026): 0 active Section 3 proceedings targeting 2026 election administration officials**

Post-*Trump v. Anderson* (March 4, 2024), the Supreme Court held that only Congress acting through legislation can enforce Section 3 disqualifications for federal officeholders. State-initiated administrative disqualifications of federal officials are foreclosed. What the baseline captures:

- **24-state AG coalition**: Active on the March 31 mail voting EO challenge; not yet specifically organized on Section 3 mechanics for 2026 officials
- **Jena Griswold**: Running for Colorado AG (primary undecided as of April 30); her candidacy creates a pathway to Section 3 enforcement standing in Colorado if she wins the June primary and general election
- **CREW factual record**: The most developed documentary record for Section 3 proceedings exists at CREW, covering January 6 coordination acts by specific current officials
- **Congressional enforcement bill**: No Section 5 enforcement legislation has been introduced as of April 30, 2026

**Measurement metric**: Track new Section 3-related filings (name, court, defendants, procedural status), state AG coalition statements referencing specific election officials, congressional bill introductions for a Section 5 mechanism, and pre-election coordination meetings among state AGs on election-night rapid-response positioning.

**Success indicator**: Framework accelerates favorable Section 3 litigation positioning by at least 30 days — proximate signal being a federal declaratory action or state AG coalition letter naming Olsen, Albus, or Harvilicz on Section 3 grounds that incorporates Domain 37's legal analysis.

**Primary sources**: [Just Security Section 3 clearinghouse](https://www.justsecurity.org/90972/clearinghouse-14th-amendment-section-3-litigation/) | [Democracy Docket election protection tracker](https://www.democracydocket.com/cases/) | [CREW litigation tracker](https://www.citizensforethics.org/legal-action/)

## Attribution Checkpoints

**Week 4** (first checkpoint): M1 new dismissals citing SAVE error-rate standing; M4 new Section 3 filings or coalition letters. If zero signals: confirm organizations received materials; note 4–8 week institutional adoption lag.

**Week 8**: M2 named officials facing formal oversight inquiry; M3 pre-markup advocacy referencing election security void. Decision: if all six metrics show zero movement, increase direct bridge-node outreach (Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, state AG staff).

**Pre-August 7 NVRA quiet period**: M1 critical — is injunctive relief in place in all compliant states? Framework non-adoption by this date is the highest-urgency failure mode.

**Election Night**: M4 critical — were Fulton County-type seizure attempts made? Was rapid-response capacity pre-positioned? These are the primary November 4 attribution data points.
