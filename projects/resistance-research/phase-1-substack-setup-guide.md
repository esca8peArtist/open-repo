# Phase 1 Substack Setup Guide

**Status**: Ready to execute. No path decision required — Substack setup is identical across Path A, Path A+37, and Path B.

**Time required**: 90–120 minutes for full setup. Core account + first post: 45 minutes.

---

## 1. Publication Name Strategy

Your publication name is the first thing institutional contacts see when you share a Substack link. It determines whether the research is read as serious policy scholarship or activist commentary. The framing decision has downstream consequences for every institutional outreach email you send, so make it deliberately.

### Name Options by Audience Positioning

**Option A — Structural / Neutral Register**: "Democratic Governance Review" or "American Governance Research"

Use this if your primary Tier 1 audience is law schools, think tanks, and congressional staff. This register signals: peer-reviewed, evidence-based, analytically detached from partisanship. The word "democratic" functions as an adjective describing a system of governance, not a party affiliation. Institutional gatekeepers at organizations like the Brennan Center, Just Security, or Yale Law are more likely to forward something under a neutral-register name. Downside: lower general audience discoverability; will not trend on social media.

**Option B — Movement / Civic Register**: "Democratic Renewal Project" or "Renewing American Democracy"

Use this if your primary launch channel is Substack itself (organic discovery, referrals, social sharing) and your secondary channel is institutional outreach. "Project" signals ongoing work, not a one-time manifesto. "Renewal" signals recovery rather than revolution, which is the correct framing for centrist institutional partners — it does not alienate the movement audience because the word also resonates with activists. This is the default option for most distribution paths.

**Option C — Analytical / Diagnostic Register**: "American Democracy Index" or "Democracy Status: U.S."

Use this if your primary audience is journalists and academics who follow the V-Dem, Freedom House, and International IDEA measurement frameworks. "Index" signals quantitative rigor. This framing pairs well with the existing research that documents the 28-point drop in U.S. democracy scores. Downside: implies regular quantitative updates, which creates an ongoing obligation.

**Recommended for Path A / Path A+37**: Option B ("Democratic Renewal Project"). It is specific enough to be searchable, broad enough to encompass 35+ domains, and calibrated for the warm-credentialed-but-not-institutional-insider readership that Substack discovery algorithms serve.

**Recommended for Path B**: Option A if you have confirmed law school or think tank partnership. Option B otherwise.

### Publication Tagline

The tagline appears under your publication name in search results, email subject lines, and the browser tab. It should be a single sentence (under 140 characters) that answers: what is this, and why does it matter right now?

Recommended: "Evidence-based structural reform analysis for a democracy in crisis — 35 domains, documented, distributable, CC 4.0."

Alternative (more direct): "A 35-domain blueprint for democratic recovery. Every reform implemented somewhere. Every claim sourced. Free to use."

Alternative (for Option A / Option C framing): "Independent research on institutional design, democratic erosion, and structural reform pathways. April 2026 and ongoing."

## 2. Account Setup: Step-by-Step

### 2.1 Create the Substack Account

1. Go to substack.com. Click "Start writing."
2. Enter your email address. Substack requires an email — use the address you will also use for institutional outreach, so replies go to one inbox.
3. Choose your subdomain: `[publicationname].substack.com`. Keep this short and clean; it is permanent once set. For "Democratic Renewal Project": `democraticrenewal.substack.com`. Check availability before finalizing.
4. Publication name: Fill from Section 1 above.
5. Tagline: Fill from Section 1 above.
6. Description (longer): Substack shows this on the About page and in Google indexing. Use the executive summary opening paragraph — the one beginning "The United States democracy score dropped 28 points in a single year." It is already written, SEO-ready, and accurate. Do not write a new description from scratch.

### 2.2 Profile Settings

- **Logo / icon**: Use a simple, high-contrast graphic. A stylized U.S. Capitol dome outline, a simple text mark, or the CC logo against a solid background. Avoid photos of yourself for the publication icon — it signals personal newsletter rather than institutional research.
- **Your bio (author page)**: "Independent researcher. 35-domain Democratic Renewal proposal published April 2026 under CC 4.0. Contact: [your email]." Do not overstate credentials, do not understate the scope of the work.
- **Social links**: If you have a public GitHub (with the Gist files), link it. LinkedIn if professional network is part of outreach strategy.

### 2.3 Payment Settings

Set to **free** for initial launch. You are distributing research, not operating a subscription media business. Enabling paid subscriptions at launch creates friction for institutional contacts who would forward the work — they do not want to send colleagues to a paywalled site.

If 90 days post-launch you have over 500 subscribers and are receiving institutional engagement, revisit. A "founding supporter" tier at $5/month with explicit framing ("supports ongoing research and monitoring infrastructure") is viable at that point.

## 3. SEO Optimization

Substack has basic but meaningful SEO. The following settings improve discoverability without requiring technical skills.

### 3.1 Post Title Conventions

Each post title is indexed by Google. Format: **[Domain or Topic]: [Specific Claim or Question]**

Examples:
- "Electoral Reform: Why 90% of House Races Are Decided Before Election Day"
- "Prosecutorial Weaponization: The 22-Case Pattern and What It Proves"
- "War Powers and Venezuela: What the OLC Memo Actually Said"
- "Democratic Renewal: A 35-Domain Proposal for Structural Reform" (launch post)

Avoid: vague titles like "What's Happening to Democracy" or "Reform Is Possible." Specific titles rank; vague titles do not.

### 3.2 Tags and Categories

Substack allows post tags. Use 3–5 per post, drawn from the following list:
- Primary tags (use on every post): `democracy`, `constitutional reform`, `2026 elections`
- Domain-specific tags: `electoral reform`, `judicial independence`, `immigration`, `war powers`, `civil rights`, `environmental policy`, `labor rights`, `healthcare`, `prosecutorial accountability`, `academic freedom`, `surveillance`
- Audience-specific tags: `policy research`, `civic action`, `legal analysis`

### 3.3 Post Descriptions (SEO Meta)

Substack allows a custom "preview description" per post — the text that appears in Google search results. Write 1–2 sentences that include the primary keyword phrase. Example for the launch post: "A 35-domain evidence-based proposal for democratic structural reform in the United States, documenting institutional erosion and reform pathways with international precedents. Published April 2026 under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0."

### 3.4 About Page

The About page is often the second page a first-time reader visits. Write it in the third person for institutional credibility: "The Democratic Renewal Project is an independent research initiative publishing evidence-based structural reform analysis for a democracy in crisis. The core work — a 35-domain proposal for democratic recovery — documents 160+ movement case studies, comparative constitutional law, and the evidentiary record on what has worked in functioning democracies. All materials are published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 and are free to use, share, and adapt with attribution."

## 4. Initial Post Framework

The first three posts determine whether first-time readers subscribe. The existing `distribution-substack-drafts.md` contains seven post drafts. The recommended publication sequence for Phase 1 is:

### Post 1 (Day 0 — Launch): Overview / Series Introduction

Use the existing Post 1 draft from `distribution-substack-drafts.md` verbatim, with two modifications:
1. Update domain count to 35 (drafts reference 28; framework now contains 35).
2. Replace all `[link]` placeholders with the canonical Gist URLs from `DISTRIBUTION_GIST_URLS.md`.

This post announces the project, frames the crisis, introduces the blueprint, and drives subscriptions. It should go live on the same day you send Batch 1 institutional emails — the Substack link is the destination those emails reference.

### Post 2 (Day 7): First Deep Dive

Use the existing Post 2 draft (Electoral Reform) or substitute the domain most relevant to your Tier 1 Batch 1 audience. If you are sending Batch 1 primarily to war powers and national security organizations, leading with Domain 28 (War Powers, Venezuela) is more coherent than leading with Domain 1 (Electoral Reform).

The post should embed the domain's central finding — one number, one specific claim — in the first two sentences. Do not build to the conclusion; lead with it.

### Post 3 (Day 14): The Trackers as Documentation

Use Post 4 from `distribution-substack-drafts.md` (Living Documents / Tracker overview). This post serves a different function: it introduces the supplementary trackers as collaborative documentation tools that readers can contribute to. This is the post most likely to generate practitioner engagement from organizations actively monitoring First Amendment suppression, environmental rollbacks, and consent decree defiance.

### Posts 4–7 (Weeks 3–6): Domain-Specific Series

Publish one domain deep-dive per week, drawn from the existing post drafts. Sequence recommendation: prioritize domains where your Batch 1 institutional contacts have active work (so they see Substack content that is directly relevant to their dockets) and domains with near-term advocacy windows (FISA 702, NVRA August 7, HHS Medicaid guidance comment period).

## 5. Embedding Domain Content

The full 35-domain proposal is too long for a single Substack post (~150,000 words). The distribution strategy uses Substack for discovery and relationship-building, with the Gist links (or GitHub) as the canonical document repository. Embed as follows:

### Inline Summary + Full Document Link

Each domain-specific post should:
1. Open with the domain's most striking finding (one sentence, specific claim with source).
2. Provide a 300–500 word analytical summary of the domain's key arguments and reform proposals.
3. Include 2–3 bullet points on "What implementing this looks like" with international precedents.
4. Close with the direct link to the full domain document: "Read the complete Domain [N] analysis: [Gist link]."

### Tracker Embeds

The four trackers (Litigation, First Amendment Suppression, Environmental Rollbacks, Police Consent Decree) are updated documents; embed them as "living reference" links rather than full inline content. Each mention of a tracker should include: what it documents, how often it is updated, and the direct link.

### Executive Summary Placement

Pin the executive summary link to the top of your Substack "About" page and include it in every post's Call to Action section. The two-page executive summary (`democratic-renewal-executive-summary.md`) is the highest-leverage single document for general audiences — it converts first-time readers to subscribers and institutional contacts to engagers faster than the full proposal.

## 6. Subscriber Welcome Sequence

Substack supports automatic welcome emails to new subscribers. This sequence is triggered when someone subscribes. Configure in Settings → Emails → Welcome email.

### Welcome Email Template (Copy Directly)

---

Subject: You're subscribed — here's where to start

Thank you for subscribing to [Publication Name].

The project is a 35-domain evidence-based proposal for democratic structural reform — every reform documented somewhere it has been implemented, every claim sourced, everything published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (which means you can share it, adapt it, and use it freely).

Here is the fastest way into the material:

**Start here**: The two-page executive summary covers the full scope in 10 minutes: [executive summary Gist link]

**Go deeper**: The full 35-domain proposal — electoral reform, judicial independence, immigration, war powers, prosecutorial accountability, healthcare access, academic freedom, and more: [full proposal Gist link]

**For attorneys, advocates, and organizers**: The Litigation Tracker documents active federal cases with current status and next deadlines: [litigation tracker Gist link]

**For environmental advocates**: The Environmental Rollbacks Tracker documents every significant deregulatory action since January 2025: [environmental tracker Gist link]

**For First Amendment work**: The First Amendment Suppression Tracker documents press crackdowns, anti-protest laws, and academic freedom violations case by case: [first amendment tracker Gist link]

Over the next several weeks, I will publish one domain analysis per week as part of the series. Each post goes deep on a specific structural problem and the reform evidence.

If any of this is immediately useful to your work — or if you have feedback on what the proposal gets wrong or what is missing — reply directly to this email. I read every response.

[Your name]

---

### Welcome Sequence Timing

Substack's free tier supports a single automated welcome email. The message above covers the primary function. Beyond that, the "sequence" is the regular post cadence (weekly) — subscribers who stay through three posts are likely engaged readers.

If you upgrade to Substack's paid tier (not necessary for launch), you can configure a 3-email onboarding drip:
- Day 0: Welcome email (above)
- Day 3: Domain deep-dive excerpt with link to full post
- Day 7: Tracker introduction with offer to contribute
