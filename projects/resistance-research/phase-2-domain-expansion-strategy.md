# Phase 2 Domain Expansion Strategy

*Produced May 7, 2026. Based on gap analysis of the 35-domain framework (complete as of May 1, 2026) and research into underrepresented movement sectors.*

---

## Executive Summary

Phase 1 produced 35 domains covering institutional democracy crises: electoral reform, judicial independence, war powers, civil service resilience, healthcare implementation, surveillance, AI governance, trade policy, higher education, prosecutorial weaponization, civil society suppression, tribal sovereignty, and election security, among others. The coverage reflects the crisis-in-progress framing appropriate for a document aimed at researchers, legal organizations, and state-level reform coalitions.

Phase 2 must expand the frame in two directions simultaneously:

1. **Deeper into justice movement sectors** that are underrepresented in Phase 1 despite having robust organizing infrastructure and high leverage for democratic restoration
2. **Further into cross-cutting issues** where Phase 1 domains touch the edge of a problem that deserves its own analytical frame

The 15 candidate domains ranked below are the highest-priority expansion targets. They are ordered by a composite score on five dimensions: adoption likelihood, movement infrastructure depth, leverage multiplier, content availability, and urgency. The top five are ready to research immediately post-Phase 1 feedback. The full 15 can be completed in 8–10 research sessions.

The most important strategic finding: **the abolitionist, reproductive justice, and environmental justice sectors are the three highest-leverage broker domains in American progressive organizing**. They each contain federated networks touching every other sector on this list. Gaining traction in any one of these three opens distribution pathways into the remaining twelve. The Phase 2 outreach strategy should therefore begin with these three, not end with them.

## Part II: Prioritization Framework

### Scoring Rubric (Five Dimensions, 1–5 Scale Each)

**1. Adoption Likelihood** — How receptive is this domain's core organizer community to a corpus-based research/distribution approach? High score = established research culture, documented relationship with policy advocacy, experience consuming analytical documents. Low score = primarily experiential or communal organizing culture where externally produced research has limited uptake.

**2. Movement Infrastructure Depth** — Does this sector have existing federation structures, national networks, or coordinated advocacy organizations capable of distributing and adopting a domain document? High score = multiple national orgs with staff capacity, regional networks, listservs, convenings. Low score = dispersed local groups without national coordination.

**3. Leverage Multiplier** — Does adopting this domain unlock access to other sectors or coalitions? High score = the domain's organizations serve as "bridges" to multiple other sectors. Low score = isolated sector with minimal cross-movement connection.

**4. Content Availability** — Is there sufficient existing research, organizing work, and documented evidence to produce a strong domain document? High score = robust academic and advocacy literature, multiple primary sources, documented legislative/litigation landscape. Low score = gaps in public documentation, proprietary or oral-history knowledge.

**5. Urgency** — Is there a time-critical advocacy window, pending litigation, or legislative deadline that makes this domain more valuable now than six months from now? High score = hard external deadline within 12 months. Low score = ongoing but no specific urgency.

## Part III: The 15 Candidate Domains — Ranked and Scored

### Tier 1 — Research Immediately (Months 1–2)

---

**Domain 44: Reproductive Rights, Abortion Access, and Reproductive Justice**
*Score: 5+4+5+5+5 = 24/25*

The most undeniable gap in Phase 1. Three years post-Dobbs, 22 states have near-total abortion bans or severe gestational restrictions. More than 31 million reproductive-age women live under bans. The criminalization apparatus (prosecution of patients, providers, helpers) is accelerating. Medication abortion supply chains are under threat. The ballot initiative route — 7 states have voted to enshrine abortion rights since Dobbs — is the primary democratic mechanism for restoration and is explicitly tied to Domain 33 (ballot initiative suppression). The Trump administration has signaled pursuit of federal enforcement mechanisms (Comstock Act reactivation) that would override state protections. A dedicated reproductive rights domain is not redundant with the healthcare domain; it covers criminalization, constitutional rights, ballot campaigns, and the reproductive justice framework connecting this issue to racial justice, economic justice, and disability rights.

Movement infrastructure depth: exceptional. Center for Reproductive Rights (litigation), Planned Parenthood Action Fund (electoral/advocacy), National Abortion Federation (clinical network), Reproaction (direct action), National Network of Abortion Funds (material support network), SisterSong (reproductive justice intersectional), In Our Own Voice: National Black Women's Reproductive Justice Agenda.

**Primary contacts for Phase 2**: Center for Reproductive Rights (New York), SisterSong (Atlanta), National Network of Abortion Funds.

Urgency: Hard. Comstock Act litigation timeline, 2026 state ballot initiative campaigns (signatures due spring 2026 in multiple states), congressional federal abortion ban proposals.

**Domain 45: Criminal Justice Abolition — Carceral System and Reform-Abolition Spectrum**
*Score: 4+5+5+5+4 = 23/25*

Phase 1's Domain 14 in the proposal covers policing and criminal justice structurally. What it lacks is the abolitionist analytical frame and the specific policy mechanisms: cash bail, mandatory minimums, plea bargaining coercion, prosecutorial discretion (distinct from Domain 29's weaponization focus), prison conditions, solitary confinement, re-entry barriers, and the democratic exclusion of the incarcerated and formerly incarcerated. The 2026 context makes this urgent: felon disenfranchisement affects an estimated 4.4 million people; the OBBBA includes measures expanding mandatory minimums; the DOJ civil rights division has been gutted; consent decrees are under attack (already tracked in consent-decree-defiance-tracker.md but without a standalone domain). The abolitionist movement has arguably the deepest movement infrastructure of any sector in this list — Critical Resistance, the Movement for Black Lives, the Ella Baker Center, Incarcerated Workers Organizing Committee, the National Lawyers Guild. Its cross-movement reach is exceptional: nearly every other sector on this list has an explicitly abolitionist wing.

**Primary contacts**: Critical Resistance (Oakland), M4BL Policy Table, Brennan Center Justice Program, Worth Rises.

Leverage: Maximum. M4BL's ecosystem of 50+ organizations touches every racial justice, economic justice, and community organizing sector. This is the highest leverage multiplier domain on the list.

**Domain 46: Environmental Justice — Frontline Communities, Sacrifice Zones, and the Racism of Pollution**
*Score: 4+5+5+5+4 = 23/25*

Domain 15 in the proposal covers climate and environment from a democratic policy reform perspective: EPA authority, climate targets, Paris Agreement, clean energy transition. Environmental justice is not the same thing. EJ addresses the documented reality that pollution, toxic sites, and environmental degradation are systematically concentrated in communities of color and low-income communities — a direct product of racially discriminatory land use, zoning, and regulatory decisions. The Trump administration's first 100 days eliminated the EPA's Office of Environmental Justice, revoked the Biden EJ executive orders (EO 12898 update), gutted EJSCREEN (the EPA's mapping tool for cumulative environmental burden), and paused enforcement of the Clean Air Act in dozens of communities, most of them majority-minority. The Climate Justice Alliance (100+ frontline organizations) and the National Black Environmental Justice Network constitute one of the most federated movement structures in the progressive ecosystem. The EJ frame connects directly to Indigenous sovereignty (Domain G), reproductive rights (air and water pollution as reproductive harm), housing justice, and anti-abolitionism (proximity to industrial pollution correlates with higher rates of asthma, cancer, and other conditions that drive poverty and incarceration).

**Primary contacts**: Climate Justice Alliance (Washington DC), WE ACT for Environmental Justice (New York), Deep South Center for Environmental Justice (New Orleans), National Black Environmental Justice Network.

**Domain 47: LGBTQ+ Rights and Trans Liberation Under Systematic Legal Attack**
*Score: 4+4+4+5+5 = 22/25*

2025 was the sixth consecutive record-breaking year for anti-trans legislation: 111 bills enacted, highest on record. The specific legal attacks include: healthcare bans for trans youth (32 states as of 2026), military exclusion (reinstated January 2025), name/gender marker access restrictions (federal ID policy changes), sports bans (25 states), bathroom bills (14 states), and "don't say gay" educational restrictions (18 states). This is a democracy domain for three reasons: (1) systematic exclusion of a minority from public life is a civil rights crisis; (2) the anti-trans legal architecture is being built by the same networks (ADF, Liberty Counsel) attacking other rights domains; (3) trans and queer organizations are major nodes in the broader progressive organizing ecosystem with deep roots in labor, healthcare, immigration, and abolitionist movements. The merger of NCTE and TLDEF into Advocates for Trans Equality (2024) is a signal of movement consolidation under pressure. HRC's 74 million "Equality Voter" mobilization is potentially one of the largest electoral organizing infrastructures in the country.

**Primary contacts**: Advocates for Trans Equality, Lambda Legal, GLSEN (education), National LGBTQ Task Force (Creating Change conference is a distribution venue).

**Domain 48: Tenant Rights, Housing Justice, and the Financialization of Housing**
*Score: 4+4+4+5+4 = 21/25*

Domain 13 in the proposal covers housing policy in a structural reform frame: zoning reform, affordable housing construction, Section 8 funding. What it doesn't cover is tenant organizing — the movement infrastructure of tenant unions, rent stabilization campaigns, anti-displacement work, and the specific democratic threat of housing financialization. The 2026 context: the OBBBA cut HUD funding by $26.7 billion (43% reduction), eliminating the HOME Investment Partnerships Program and slashing Community Development Block Grants. Corporate landlords (Invitation Homes, Progress Residential, Blackstone) now own more than 600,000 single-family homes. Tenant organizing has experienced a documented renaissance since 2020: tenant unions in 15 major cities, statewide tenant union federations in California, New York, and Minnesota, and the formation of the National Tenant Union Federation in 2023. The housing justice frame connects directly to disability justice (accessible housing), reproductive rights (housing stability as a health determinant), immigration (deportation and eviction as correlated enforcement), and racial justice (racial wealth gap is primarily a housing wealth gap).

**Primary contacts**: National Tenant Union Federation, National Housing Law Project, Right to the City Alliance, National Low Income Housing Coalition.

**Domain 49: Food Systems, Agricultural Justice, and Rural Organizing**
*Score: 3+4+4+4+4 = 19/25*

Zero Phase 1 coverage. The food system domain sits at the intersection of multiple crises: USDA program cuts (farm safety net programs cut by $14B in the OBBBA); corporate consolidation of agricultural markets (four firms control 80%+ of beef processing, 66% of pork); the destruction of the National Institute of Food and Agriculture (NIFA) research apparatus; and the specific democratic threat of rural community collapse as consolidation eliminates family farms. The Farm Bill — a $900+ billion, five-year omnibus covering nutrition programs (SNAP, WIC) and farm programs — was overdue by two years as of 2026 and subject to the most severe proposed cuts in its history. The food sovereignty frame (pioneered by La Via Campesina) connects agricultural policy to Indigenous land rights, immigrant farmworker rights, and environmental justice. The National Family Farm Coalition, with its La Via Campesina affiliation, is one of the few organizations with organic ties to the international peasant movement that can lend global legitimacy to US organizing.

**Primary contacts**: National Family Farm Coalition, National Black Food and Justice Alliance, Northeast Organic Farming Association, Rural Coalition.

**Domain 50: Digital Justice — Algorithmic Discrimination, Broadband Equity, and Platform Accountability**
*Score: 4+4+4+5+3 = 20/25*

This is distinct from Domain 36 (AI in government decision-making) and Domain 21 (government surveillance). Digital justice addresses: algorithmic discrimination in private-sector hiring, credit, housing, and insurance; the broadband equity gap (21 million Americans lack broadband access, disproportionately rural and minority); platform content moderation as a civil rights problem (Black users disproportionately deplatformed); and the democratic accountability gap in private algorithmic systems. The OBBBA canceled the $42.5 billion BEAD broadband buildout program in 2025 (later partially restored at $14B), creating a documented access equity reversal. The Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law's Digital Justice Initiative is a natural Phase 2 contact with existing capacity. The NAACP Legal Defense Fund and EFF are active in algorithmic discrimination litigation.

**Primary contacts**: Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law (Digital Justice), Color of Change (tech accountability), EFF, National Digital Inclusion Alliance.

**Domain 51: Sex Work Rights — Decriminalization, FOSTA-SESTA, and Harm Reduction**
*Score: 3+3+3+4+3 = 16/25*

Zero Phase 1 coverage. Sex workers' rights organizations are a distinct movement with specific policy demands (decriminalization, repeal of FOSTA-SESTA, healthcare access without surveillance) and a documented organizational infrastructure: SWOP-USA's 27 chapters, Decriminalize Sex Work (the primary legislative advocacy org), Woodhull Freedom Foundation, and the Red Umbrella Fund internationally. FOSTA-SESTA (2018) criminalizes online platforms that facilitate sex work advertising, which has concentrated violence, eliminated harm reduction tools, and pushed the industry underground. Fair and Just Prosecution's 2025 issue brief on decriminalizing sex work represents a new convergence between prosecutorial reform and sex worker rights. The cross-movement connections are strong: sex worker organizations are deeply integrated with trans liberation networks (trans women are disproportionately represented in the sex worker population), abolitionist networks (SWOP Behind Bars explicitly calls for prison abolition), and harm reduction networks. Its adoption likelihood score is lower than other domains because sex worker organizations have historically been wary of external analysis that misrepresents lived experience.

**Primary contacts**: Decriminalize Sex Work, SWOP-USA, Woodhull Freedom Foundation, Red Umbrella Fund.

**Domain 52: Youth Civic Power — Organizing Infrastructure, Voter Access, and Civic Education**
*Score: 4+4+4+4+4 = 20/25*

Young voters (18–29) had their highest turnout in a generation in 2020 (53%) but faced specific structural barriers in 2024: campus polling place closures, ID requirements targeting student IDs, and the documented administrative barriers to student voter registration at colleges in Republican-controlled states. The 2025 OBBBA included riders restricting Department of Education engagement with voter registration programs. The 2026 context is acute: student visa revocations (6,000+) have had a documented chilling effect on campus political organizing; federal DEI program elimination has dismantled campus civic engagement infrastructure at many institutions. Youth organizing networks (NextGen America, Campus Vote Project, Students Learn Students Vote) constitute a distinct organizing sector with different communication channels (TikTok, Discord, campus organizing) and different political dynamics than any other domain.

**Primary contacts**: Campus Vote Project, Students Learn Students Vote, NextGen America, Young Invincibles.

**Domain 53: Religious Freedom and Church-State Separation Under Christian Nationalism**
*Score: 3+3+4+5+4 = 19/25*

Zero Phase 1 coverage as a standalone domain. The Christian nationalist project is among the most systematically documented political movements in contemporary American politics: Project Blitz/Freedom for All, the Alliance Defending Freedom's state legislative infrastructure, the Federalist Society pipeline producing federal judiciary appointments, and the Supreme Court's string of cases expanding religious exemptions (303 Creative, Kennedy v. Bremerton) while restricting contraceptive access. The democratic stakes are direct: the Establishment Clause is the constitutional barrier to theocratic governance; when it erodes through court doctrine and administrative accommodation, it affects every other rights domain. The analytical angle for this domain is not theological — it is institutional: the Christian nationalist movement as a political organizing infrastructure with specific legislative, litigation, and electoral components. Americans United for Separation of Church and State and the Freedom From Religion Foundation have the strongest litigation and research capacity in this sector.

**Primary contacts**: Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Freedom From Religion Foundation, Baptist Joint Committee for Religious Liberty.

**Domain 54: Mental Health, Psychiatric Survivor Rights, and Community Crisis Response**
*Score: 3+3+3+4+4 = 17/25*

The psychiatric survivor and mad liberation movements are underrepresented in nearly all progressive organizing frameworks, including this one. The specific 2026 issues: the administration's push to expand forced institutionalization and revive long-term psychiatric hospitalization (reversing 50 years of deinstitutionalization progress); the documented failure of psychiatric emergency rooms as mass incarceration feeders; the gutting of the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline funding in the OBBBA; and the specific vulnerability of people with psychiatric diagnoses under benefit termination proceedings. The cross-movement connections are strong with disability justice, abolitionism (jails as de facto psychiatric institutions), and housing justice (homelessness and psychiatric crisis are correlated). The National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI), MindFreedom International, and the Icarus Project are distinct organizational nodes.

**Primary contacts**: MindFreedom International, National Coalition for Mental Health Recovery, Disability Rights Advocates.

---

**Domain 55: Economic Democracy — Worker Ownership, Community Wealth Building, and Participatory Budgeting**
*Score: 3+3+3+4+3 = 16/25*

Beyond labor rights (Domain 17 in the proposal) and antitrust (Domain 20), there is a distinct "economic democracy" movement focused on alternative ownership and governance structures: worker cooperatives (3,500+ in the US, 300,000 worker-owners), community land trusts (300+ in the US), community development financial institutions (CDFIs), and participatory budgeting (used in 60+ US jurisdictions). The US Federation of Worker Cooperatives, the Democracy Collaborative, and PolicyLink are the anchor organizations. This domain connects directly to the democratic theory section of research-notes.md (participatory budgeting evidence base is documented there) and adds a constructive, affirmative dimension to the framework's predominantly defensive posture.

**Primary contacts**: Democracy Collaborative, US Federation of Worker Cooperatives, New Economy Coalition.

---

**Domain 56: Public Lands and Extractive Industry Governance**
*Score: 3+4+3+5+4 = 19/25*

The Trump administration's first 100 days included the most aggressive public lands opening to extractive industry since the Reagan administration: Bears Ears and Grand Staircase monuments reduced, ANWR drilling permits advanced, National Forest management plans overridden, offshore drilling expanded. This is not adequately covered by Domain 15 (climate) or Domain G (tribal sovereignty), though it intersects both. The specific democratic angle: public lands are democratically owned assets — 640 million acres of federal public land managed "for the benefit of all Americans." Their extraction without democratic deliberation is a democratic accountability problem as well as an environmental one. The Center for Biological Diversity, Western Environmental Law Center, and Earthjustice have the primary litigation dockets.

**Primary contacts**: Center for Biological Diversity, Western Environmental Law Center, Earthjustice.

---

**Domain 57: Migrant and Refugee Rights Beyond Detention**
*Score: 4+4+3+4+5 = 20/25*

Domain 16 in the proposal covers immigration policy comprehensively. What it doesn't address as a distinct frame is migrant rights as a labor and human rights issue beyond detention: the specific vulnerability of TPS holders (hundreds of thousands of whom face termination without status as of 2026), DACA recipients (636,000 as of 2025), H-2A agricultural worker exploitation, asylum case backlog (3.7 million cases), and the democratic exclusion of approximately 11 million undocumented residents from civic life. The labor angle is distinct: undocumented workers pay an estimated $96 billion in taxes annually and are denied the civic rights their labor supports. This domain would complement Domain 16 by focusing on affirmative rights and dignity rather than primarily the enforcement and detention apparatus.

**Primary contacts**: National Immigration Law Center, United We Dream, Centro de los Derechos del Migrante, RAICES.

---

**Domain 58: Pension Security, Social Security, and Retirement Justice**
*Score: 3+3+3+5+4 = 18/25*

Zero Phase 1 coverage as a standalone domain. Social Security is the largest US social insurance program (67 million beneficiaries, $1.4 trillion in annual benefits) and the administration's most politically explosive potential cut. The OBBBA did not cut Social Security directly (it remains politically protected), but the OBBBA does accelerate the Social Security Trust Fund insolvency timeline by approximately three years through revenue and GDP impacts. The SSA's staffing crisis (7,000 jobs cut) creates processing delays for claims independent of benefit changes. State and local public pension systems are under attack in 14 states, with Republican legislatures attempting to shift from defined benefit to defined contribution plans — a wealth transfer from workers to financial managers. AARP (38 million members) and the Economic Policy Institute are the primary research and advocacy nodes.

**Primary contacts**: AARP, Economic Policy Institute, National Public Pension Coalition, Social Security Works.

### What Would Trigger Acceleration

4. **Creating Change 2027 program submission** — The National LGBTQ Task Force typically opens program submissions in summer 2026; submitting Domain 47 as a session resource is a high-leverage distribution mechanism

### What Would Trigger Deferral

Phase 2 research should be deprioritized if:
- Phase 1 distribution feedback indicates the target audience is not yet ready for additional domain content (volume fatigue)
- A specific domain sector is actively in litigation at a stage where an external document could harm ongoing advocacy (check with Legal Director contacts before releasing)
- Organizational contacts indicate their capacity is occupied by a crisis that makes 6-month planning impossible (common in advocacy orgs)
