## I. State Legislative Dynamics Post-Callais (Louisiana v. Callais, April 29, 2026)

**Summary of Findings**

Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026, 6-3) fundamentally altered the evidentiary standard for Voting Rights Act Section 2 claims. The decision raises the threshold from "discriminatory effect" to "intentional discrimination," which has immediate implications for majority-minority districts across the country.

The practical consequence: existing majority-minority districts created to comply with Section 2 are now vulnerable to racial gerrymander challenges. Multiple states have begun mid-decade redistricting in response.

**Immediate Cascade States** (as of mid-May 2026)

- **Louisiana**: Governor suspended U.S. House primaries (originally May 16) the day after ruling. SB 121 passed Senate 27-10 (May 14), collapsing prior two-district Black majority configuration into single Baton Rouge-to-New Orleans corridor. Shift to jungle primary to accommodate compressed timeline.
- **Tennessee**: Governor called extraordinary legislative session (effective May 5). New maps passed both chambers (May 7), signed same day. Memphis 9th District — sole Democratic seat, majority-Black, held 19 years by Rep. Steve Cohen — split into three parts. Cohen announced (May 15) he would not seek re-election in any gerrymandered district. First state to pass Callais-motivated mid-decade redistricting.
- **Florida**: Pre-Callais special session April 2026; new maps signed, facing legal challenge; primary August 18.
- **South Carolina**: House passed redistricting authorization resolution; bills introduced targeting 6th District (only Democratic-held, majority-Black seat). Senate status: stalled.
- **Georgia**: Gov. Kemp announced no pre-2026 election redistricting; possible action for 2028 cycle.
- **States signaling interest**: Arizona, New Jersey, New York, Washington discussing new maps per NCSL.

**Scale of Impact**: Voting Rights Lab (May 11, 2026) identified 8 states redrawing or on verge of redrawing maps for 2026 — a modern record. These 8 states account for approximately 40% of all U.S. House districts.

**Congressional Response Options** (per CRS)

- Race-neutral redistricting standards (independent commissions)
- VRA amendments codifying modified Gingles framework
- Prohibitions on mid-decade redistricting

None have advanced through Republican-controlled Congress.

**Democratic Governance Implications**

- States with Republican legislative majorities can redraw maps mid-cycle using partisan justifications now insulated from VRA challenge
- Same maps can be constructed to eliminate majority-minority districts
- Federal check on racially motivated redistricting substantially removed
- Campaign Legal Center described VRA's enforcement mechanism as "eviscerated"
- Courts retain jurisdiction over explicit racial intent claims, but evidentiary burden substantially raised

## II. SCOTUS 2025-2026 Term: Status Update

**Major Completed Decisions**

- **Louisiana v. Callais** (April 29, 2026): Voting Rights Act Section 2 decision (see Section I above)

**Major Pending Decisions** (expected by late June/early July 2026)

**Trump v. Barbara (No. 25-365) — Birthright Citizenship**
- Oral arguments: April 1, 2026 (sitting president attended)
- Question: Whether EO 14160 restricts birthright citizenship under Fourteenth Amendment for children of parents present temporarily or without lawful status
- Case origin: ACLU class action, District of New Hampshire
- Expected decision: Late June or early July 2026
- Note: This is NOT a tribal sovereignty case; tribal Indian exception was discussed as historical narrow exception only

**Watson v. Republican National Committee (No. 24-1260) — Mail Ballot Grace Periods**
- Oral arguments: March 2026
- Question: Whether Mississippi's statute permitting mail-in ballots postmarked by Election Day and received within 5 business days to be counted conflicts with federal law establishing uniform national Election Day
- Justices' signals: Alito and Thomas signaled skepticism toward state law during argument
- Expected decision: Late June 2026
- Impact scope: If SCOTUS invalidates Mississippi provision, affects 13+ states with analogous grace-period statutes; direct implications for November 2026 midterm elections
- States affected: Washington, and at least 12 others

**Other Remaining Cases**
- At least 11 significant cases remain
- Immigration enforcement, transgender athlete cases (decision pending after January 2026 arguments), additional executive power questions (Iran conflict litigation)

**Forward-Looking Implications**

- End-of-June decision window falls during active 2026 midterm election cycle
- *Watson v. RNC* decision restricting mail ballot grace periods would require immediate state legislative/administrative responses in 13+ states before November — potential election administration chaos
- *Trump v. Barbara* upholding EO 14160 would constitute first judicial modification of Citizenship Clause since Fourteenth Amendment adoption, with cascading consequences for immigration enforcement and civil status law
- Overall pattern: Roberts Court has moved consistently toward contracting federal enforcement of voting rights
- Term as a whole represents significant rebalancing of federal-state election law toward contraction of both federal voting-rights enforcement and state discretion to expand ballot access

## III. FISA Section 702 Reauthorization: June 12 Deadline

**Status as of Mid-May 2026**

- April timeline: Repeated short-term extensions replaced long-term deal that collapsed in mid-April
- April 29-30: Congress passed 45-day extension (House: 261-111; Senate: unanimous consent)
- **Current expiration**: June 12, 2026
- The previous long-term House proposal (3-year extension, 235-191) died in Senate over unrelated "digital dollar" ban rider

**Reform Proposals Still Unresolved**

**Bipartisan Government Surveillance Reform Act**
- Sponsors: Rep. Davidson (R-OH), Rep. Lofgren (D-CA), Sen. Wyden (D-OR), Sen. Lee (R-UT)
- Provisions: Warrant requirement for U.S.-person query results, stop government purchase of data from commercial brokers, criminal penalties for improper queries
- Status: Not cleared both chambers

**House Proposal** (passed 235-191, died in Senate)
- Provisions: Attorney approval before targeted U.S.-person reviews, written justifications to ODNI, criminal penalties up to 5 years
- Central gap: No full warrant requirement

**The Warrant Requirement Fault Line**

- Intelligence community position: Warrant requirement would "inhibit efficacy of FISA tool and endanger national security"
- Civil liberties coalition (ACLU, EFF, Brennan Center): Warrantless backdoor searches unconstitutional
- Definitional dispute: What constitutes a "query"? Automated algorithmic search? (American Prospect, May 11)

**Democratic Governance Implications**

- FISA reauthorization encapsulates structural tension: Executive Branch consistently resists judicial check on U.S.-person queries
- Repeated short-term extensions lock in status quo while reform coalitions fragment
- Digital dollar rider episode illustrates how unrelated partisan priorities weaponize reauthorization negotiations
- Institutional alignment between intelligence agencies and senior congressional leadership overrides bipartisan civil liberties coalition
- FISA 702 represents clearest area where Executive surveillance power operates with minimal judicial oversight

**June 12 Deadline**

Republicans have 3-4 weeks to reach consensus before expiration. Wiley LLP noted repeated extensions have created "ongoing uncertainty for communications providers" regarding compliance obligations. EFF (April 30) called 45-day extension a temporary reprieve requiring continued advocacy.

## IV. War Powers Legislative Status: U.S.-Iran Conflict

**Timeline and Context**

- Military operations began: ~February 28, 2026 (Operation Epic Fury)
- Congressional notification: March 2, 2026 (triggered 60-day War Powers Resolution clock)
- Ceasefire: April 7, 2026
- **60-day deadline expired**: ~May 1, 2026

**The Schiff Amendment and Senate Votes**

S.J.Res. 184 / H.Con.Res. 38 directed President to remove U.S. armed forces from hostilities with Iran absent congressional authorization.

Senate votes (multiple attempts Feb-May 2026):
- Multiple early votes failed along party lines
- April 30, 2026 (hours before 60-day deadline): Senate rejected 47-50 (Rand Paul + Susan Collins crossed party lines)
- May 13, 2026 (seventh vote): Senate rejected 49-50 (Lisa Murkowski joined Paul and Collins — closest margin yet)
- House: Tied 212-212 on first attempt (all vacancies/absences prevented passage); third attempt also failed

**The Schiff Israel Amendment**: Clarified that War Powers language would not affect U.S. support for Israel's self-defense following Iran's attacks on Israeli cities — attempt to build political cover. Did not move the needle as of May 13.

**Administration's Legal Position**

- May 1: Trump sent letters to Speaker Johnson and Senate President Grassley asserting hostilities "have terminated"
- Trump cited April 7 ceasefire: "There has been no exchange of fire between United States Forces and Iran since that date"
- Defense Secretary Hegseth: "We are in a ceasefire right now, which our understanding means, the 60-day clock pauses or stops"
- Administration position: Ceasefire pauses or stops the 60-day clock

**Constitutional Law Assessment** (Lawfare, May 1)

- No precedent in War Powers Resolution history for ceasefire pausing 60-day clock
- Legal scholar Curtis Bradley: Unaware of "any precedent in support" of administration interpretation
- Resolution's text does not include pause mechanism
- Every administration since 1973 has questioned War Powers Resolution constitutionality; Rubio restated this position

**Congressional Response**

Congress took no enforcement action after May 1. Members left Washington for recess after May 13 vote without triggering automatic consequences.

**Durable Implications for Democratic Governance**

1. **Vote trajectory**: Pattern of failing 47-50, then 49-50 — margin narrowing. Sustained conflict or re-escalation could flip vote. Threshold remains 60-vote cloture for most procedures.

2. **"Terminated hostilities" precedent**: Administration's argument sets precedent (even without judicial validation) for future administrations to invoke ceasefire declarations to restart 60-day clock — significant expansion of executive war-initiation authority.

3. **AUMF absent**: Congress has not passed Authorization for Use of Military Force for Iran operations. Executive relying on Article II authority + "terminated hostilities" claim.

4. **CRS outlines legislative options**: In12678 ("U.S.-Iran Ceasefire and Negotiations: Assessment and Issues for Congress") but Congress has not acted.

5. **Allied-support carve-outs pattern**: Schiff Israel amendment illustrates recurring dynamic — adding allied-support carve-outs to limit bipartisan opposition; structural feature of all post-Cold War war powers debates.

**Institutional Finding**: Congressional unwillingness to enforce War Powers Resolution even when statutory deadline expires; executive successfully reframed "ceasefire" as clock-stopping mechanism without legal or judicial accountability.
