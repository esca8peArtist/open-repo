## PRIORITY ALERT: FISA Section 702 Expires April 30

This is the most time-sensitive item in this document. FISA Section 702 surveillance authorities expire in four days. What happens before and after April 30 has direct implications for civil liberties, surveillance of political dissidents, and the legal authority of the Kash Patel-led FBI to conduct warrantless searches of American communications.

## Section 1: FISA Section 702 — Surveillance at Inflection Point

### What It Is

Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act authorizes the NSA to collect communications of foreign targets outside the United States — including when those communications involve Americans at one end. The FBI currently conducts roughly **200,000 warrantless searches per year** of Section 702-acquired data for information about U.S. persons. No warrant, no probable cause finding, no individual judicial approval.

The program has a documented abuse record: the FBI used it to query the communications of Black Lives Matter protesters, members of Congress, journalists, and donors to political campaigns. Under Kash Patel at the FBI, these authorities would be wielded by an apparatus openly hostile to democratic accountability.

### Critical Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| April 20, 2026 | Original expiration date |
| April 17, 2026 | Speaker Johnson's 5-year "clean" reauthorization failed — 20 Republicans joined most Democrats to block it |
| April 17, 2026 | Congress passed a 10-day stopgap extension |
| **April 30, 2026** | **Current hard expiration — no extension is in place beyond this** |
| Post-April 30 | If no deal: Section 702 collection ceases on new targets; existing data remains accessible |

### Current Legislative Proposals

**Johnson's Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act (3-year reauthorization)**
- Monthly reviews by Civil Liberties Protection Officer
- Attorney-level (not field supervisor) approval for U.S. person queries
- Expanded congressional access to FISC proceedings
- GAO audits of targeting mechanisms
- Enhanced criminal liability for knowing violations
- Does NOT include a warrant requirement

**Wyden-Lee Government Surveillance Reform Act**
- Would require FBI to obtain a court-issued warrant based on probable cause before querying Section 702 data for U.S. person information
- Bipartisan: Sen. Ron Wyden (D-OR) + Sen. Mike Lee (R-UT)
- Backed by EFF, ACLU, Brennan Center, CDT, Asian Americans Advancing Justice, Demand Progress, and 15+ other organizations

**Sen. Tom Cotton's position**: Pushing for an 18-month "clean" extension with no warrant requirement and no meaningful oversight.

### Strategic Implications

The stakes here are not abstract. A clean extension hands an FBI led by Kash Patel — who has explicitly targeted political opponents — the legal authority to run 200,000 warrantless searches annually on American communications. The surveillance power aligns directly with the administration's stated interest in investigating journalists, former officials, and political opponents.

If Section 702 expires without reauthorization: collection on new foreign targets stops; existing collected data remains available. This would be a meaningful constraint but not a complete cessation of surveillance capacity.

**Monitoring priority (starting April 28)**: Watch for any Senate floor action before April 30. The warrant amendment vote that Sen. Paul is forcing will be the key procedural moment. A warrant requirement that passes both chambers would be a landmark civil liberties victory; a clean extension or the Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act (without warrant requirement) would represent a significant defeat.

## Section 2: Voting Rights Act — The Supreme Court's Most Consequential Cases

The Voting Rights Act is facing a two-front attack at the Supreme Court this term. Both cases are pending and could be decided any day before the end of June. Together they could leave Section 2 of the VRA unenforceable in practice.

### 2.1 Louisiana v. Callais — The Constitutional Question

**Docket**: SCOTUS (reargued October 2025; decision pending)
**Question presented** (as narrowed by the Court's August 2025 order): Whether the State's intentional creation of a second majority-minority congressional district violates the Fourteenth or Fifteenth Amendments to the U.S. Constitution.

**Background**: After years of Section 2 litigation in *Robinson v. Landry*, Louisiana enacted a second majority-Black congressional district to comply with VRA requirements. White voters then sued, arguing the race-conscious map itself violated the Equal Protection Clause. The case was argued in March 2025, then reargued in October 2025 with a narrowed question that goes directly to the constitutionality of VRA compliance districts.

**What's at stake**: If the Court holds that intentionally drawing a majority-minority district to comply with Section 2 violates the 14th or 15th Amendments, it creates a constitutional paradox: the VRA requires race-conscious remedies, but the Constitution prohibits them. This would effectively nullify Section 2 as a tool against racial vote dilution — finishing the demolition that *Shelby County* (2013) began by gutting Section 5.

NPR analysis: "A Supreme Court ruling could bring historic drop in Black representation in Congress." CNN: "The Supreme Court's conservatives could significantly alter the 2026 election."

**Key actors**: NAACP Legal Defense Fund (argued for Louisiana/plaintiffs defending the district); ACLU filed briefs; Brennan Center; Constitutional Accountability Center.

**Decision expected**: End of June / early July 2026.

### 2.2 Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe — Private Right of Action

**Docket**: SCOTUS cert petition (filed September 2025; conference held November 2025, then rescheduled)
**Court below**: Eighth Circuit (May 14, 2025 — private plaintiffs cannot enforce Section 2 of the VRA)
**Stay**: Supreme Court granted a stay on July 24, 2025, temporarily restoring private enforcement rights pending cert decision

**Background**: The Eighth Circuit held that private plaintiffs cannot sue to enforce Section 2 of the VRA — neither directly nor through 42 U.S.C. § 1983. This ruling affects every voter in the seven Eighth Circuit states: Arkansas, Iowa, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, and South Dakota. Under this ruling, only the federal government (the DOJ) can enforce Section 2. The DOJ under Trump has shown zero interest in doing so.

**What's at stake**: The combination of *Callais* and *Turtle Mountain* could leave Section 2 completely inert. Even if *Callais* preserves the substantive right, *Turtle Mountain* could eliminate every private plaintiff's ability to vindicate it. The only enforcement mechanism would be a Trump DOJ that has actively refused to bring VRA cases.

**Cert status**: Petition filed September 2025; briefing concluded October 2025; scheduled for November 7 conference, then removed and rescheduled. As of April 26, 2026, certiorari has not been granted or denied — the Court appears to be holding the petition pending *Callais*.

**Key actors**: Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians; Spirit Lake Tribe; Native American Rights Fund; ACLU (amicus); Brennan Center; Campaign Legal Center.

### 2.3 Watson v. Republican National Committee — Mail Ballot Grace Periods

**Docket**: SCOTUS (argued March 23, 2026; decision expected this term)
**Question presented**: Whether federal Election Day statutes preempt a state law allowing absentee ballots postmarked by Election Day but received up to 5 business days later.
**Lower court**: Fifth Circuit reversed district court, held Mississippi grace period preempted.

**Background**: Mississippi allows absentee ballots postmarked by Election Day and received within 5 business days to count. The RNC sued, arguing this violates federal statutes requiring elections to be held on a single Tuesday. The Fifth Circuit agreed. If the Supreme Court affirms, grace periods in **approximately 30 states** would be eliminated — directly affecting military and overseas voters as well as ordinary mail voters.

**What's at stake**: Balls and Strikes analysis of oral argument: "The Supreme Court's conspiracy-brained justices are ready to limit mail-in voting." The case was argued in March and a decision is expected before the term ends in late June/early July. An adverse ruling could affect the fall 2026 midterm elections by eliminating mail ballot grace periods before November.

**Key actors**: Elias Law Group (defending Mississippi law); ACLU of Mississippi; Constitutional Accountability Center; Public Rights Project.

## Section 3: Trump Executive Action on Elections — Active Federal Litigation

### 3.1 Trump Election Executive Orders (Comprehensive Challenge)

**Courts**: Multiple federal district courts; permanent injunction issued by D.C. district court
**Filed by**: Coalition of 23 states (led by Pennsylvania Gov. Josh Shapiro; AGs of California, Massachusetts, Nevada, Washington); LULAC; ACLU; Brennan Center.

**Background**: Trump issued a first executive order in March 2025 attempting to assert presidential control over federal elections. In March 2026, he issued a second order attempting to require USPS to police mail ballot eligibility. As of April 2026:

- Multiple provisions of the first order are subject to a **permanent injunction** in *LULAC v. Executive Office of the President* (D.C. district court)
- The second order is being challenged in a fresh multi-state lawsuit
- Federal judges have "uniformly ruled against" DOJ efforts to force states to hand over voter data

**Voter data litigation**: DOJ has sued 29 states and D.C. to force turnover of unredacted voter files with sensitive personal information. Federal judges in five states — California, Massachusetts, Michigan, Oregon, Rhode Island — have dismissed those lawsuits. New litigation filed April 21, 2026 by a coalition of voting rights groups argues the DOJ is illegally assembling a **national voter surveillance database**.

**Strategic implications**: The voter data litigation is a direct attempt to build federal infrastructure for voter purges and targeted disenfranchisement before the 2026 midterms. The multi-district dismissal record is the most encouraging legal trend in this category — courts are not buying DOJ's theory that it is entitled to states' complete unredacted voter rolls.

## Section 4: Gerrymandering and Mid-Decade Redistricting

### 4.1 Missouri — Mid-Decade Redistricting (Wise v. State of Missouri)

**Docket**: State courts (Missouri Supreme Court ruled 4-3, March 24, 2026); federal challenge ongoing
**Filed by**: Campaign Legal Center (CLC); ACLU.

**Background**: In June 2025, the Trump administration pressured Republican-controlled states to redraw congressional maps mid-decade to benefit Republicans in 2026. Missouri's Republican-dominated legislature complied, drawing a new map that splits Kansas City's metro area into three parts to dilute urban (Democratic) voters.

**March 24 ruling**: The Missouri Supreme Court upheld the map 4-3, ruling that nothing in the Missouri Constitution explicitly prohibits mid-decade redistricting.

**Ballot initiative**: People Not Politicians verified enough signatures as of March 23 to qualify a citizen initiative for the November 2026 ballot that would repeal the new map. This is the most viable remaining avenue to block the gerrymander.

**Why it matters broadly**: Missouri is the test case for a Trump-coordinated, multi-state mid-decade redistricting strategy. If it stands — and courts will not block it — the model will spread. The states affected include Florida, Virginia, Wisconsin, and New York. This is arguably the most consequential structural threat to the 2026 elections.

**Key actors**: Campaign Legal Center; ACLU; Democracy Docket; People Not Politicians (ballot initiative effort).

## Section 5: First Amendment, Section 230, and Platform Censorship Litigation

### 5.1 Moody v. NetChoice / NetChoice v. Paxton — Remand Status

**Dockets**: On remand to Fifth Circuit (Texas law) and Eleventh Circuit (Florida law) following Supreme Court vacatur, July 1, 2024.
**Original parties**: NetChoice (industry group) v. Texas AG; NetChoice v. Florida AG.

**Background**: In 2021, Florida (HB 20) and Texas (HB 1) enacted laws restricting large social media platforms' ability to engage in content moderation. The Supreme Court ruled in July 2024 that platforms' content moderation is expressive activity protected by the First Amendment — but vacated and remanded because the lower courts had not conducted a proper facial challenge analysis.

**Current status**: The Fifth and Eleventh Circuits are conducting the remanded analysis. No final decisions have been issued as of April 2026. The outcome will determine whether states can effectively compel platforms to carry speech they would otherwise moderate — with implications for conservative "anti-censorship" legislation nationwide.

**Strategic implications**: This line of cases directly affects how effectively organized networks can spread disinformation on social platforms, as well as whether platforms can enforce their own community standards. If the Texas/Florida laws are upheld on remand, platforms operating in those states would be legally prohibited from moderating constitutionally-protected speech, including from white nationalist and authoritarian-adjacent accounts. This is a direct democracy-integrity concern.

### 5.2 Anderson v. TikTok — Section 230 Algorithm Liability

**Court**: Third Circuit (decided August 2025)
**Decision**: Section 230 does NOT shield platforms from harm caused by their own algorithmic recommendation of third-party content. This creates a major circuit split with other circuits.

**Background**: Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act provides broad immunity to online platforms for third-party content. The Third Circuit's August 2025 ruling narrowed this immunity when the platform's own algorithms amplify harmful content — treating the recommendation itself as platform-created content not covered by the immunity shield.

**Current posture**: Sen. Josh Hawley filed an amicus brief in March 2026 urging SCOTUS to hear *Doe v. X Corp.* (a separate Section 230 case involving CSAM on Twitter) to clarify the scope. The Supreme Court has so far declined to address Section 230 directly in recent terms. The Third Circuit's split may eventually force the Court's hand.

**Strategic implications**: A narrowed Section 230 that encompasses algorithmic amplification would expose platforms to liability for how they surface and promote content — creating pressure toward more conservative recommendation practices. This has complex implications: it could reduce algorithmic amplification of extremist content, but it could also create chilling effects on political speech and further incentivize large platforms to self-censor.

## Section 6: Structural Legal Actions — Additional Monitoring Targets

### 6.1 Mail Ballot Grace Periods — State-Level (Parallel to Watson v. RNC)

Beyond Watson v. RNC, parallel state-level attacks on mail ballot access are active in multiple circuits. The legal landscape for mail voting in fall 2026 will be substantially shaped by the Watson decision. **Monitoring priority**: Watch for the Watson decision in late June; if adverse, immediate tracking of which states will eliminate grace periods before November 2026 filing deadlines.

### 6.2 Noncitizen Local Voting Rights (Burlington, VT)

**Court**: Vermont Supreme Court (argued October 2025; decision pending)
**Issue**: Whether municipalities can extend local election voting rights to noncitizens.
**Stakes**: Sets precedent for whether localities in pro-democracy jurisdictions can expand the franchise at all, or whether state constitutional citizenship requirements foreclose any extension. Lower stakes than national VRA cases but important for federalism and local democracy frameworks.

### 6.3 MALDEF: Texas and Arkansas Redistricting (Cert Petition)

MALDEF filed in February 2026 seeking Supreme Court review of redistricting decisions in Texas and Arkansas. Combined with the active VRA docket, this represents a coordinated legal effort to establish circuit-level and Supreme Court precedent across the entire redistricting landscape before the 2026 election.
