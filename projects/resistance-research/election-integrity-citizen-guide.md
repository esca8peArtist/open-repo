# Election Integrity as a Citizen Observer — Practical Guide

**Bottom line**: Citizens have meaningful formal roles in election integrity beyond voting. Poll watchers are appointed by parties or candidates; election observers operate under different (often broader) frameworks. The most accessible entry point for most citizens is the 866-OUR-VOTE volunteer program, which requires no party affiliation and no prior experience. The Carter Center is actively recruiting nonpartisan observers in five states for 2026. For lawyers, the NAACP and Lawyers' Committee have specific legal volunteer programs. This guide covers the formal landscape, how to get involved, and what to document.

## Poll Watchers vs. Election Observers: What's the Difference

These terms are often confused. The legal distinction matters.

### Poll Watchers

Poll watchers are formally appointed by a political party, candidate, or (in some states) a qualified organization, and credentialed by the relevant election authority. They are present at polling locations to observe the process on behalf of their appointing authority.

**Key features:**
- Require formal appointment — you cannot self-appoint
- Credentials issued by state or county election authority
- Rights and access defined by state statute — what they can observe, where they can stand, whether they can challenge voters (few states permit this)
- Generally one or two per party per polling location
- Can observe throughout Election Day including ballot closing procedures

**Who appoints poll watchers**: Political parties, candidates for office, or sometimes registered nonpartisan organizations (varies by state). Individual citizens cannot simply show up as poll watchers — you must be appointed through one of these channels.

**Limitations**: Poll watchers observe but generally cannot challenge or interfere. Most states prohibit poll watchers from communicating with voters, election workers, or anyone except the precinct captain. Violations can result in removal from the polling place.

### Election Observers

Election observers operate under a different framework that varies significantly by jurisdiction. Some states use the term interchangeably with poll watcher; others have a distinct observer category for accredited nonpartisan organizations. International and Carter Center-style observers fall in this category.

**Key distinction from poll watchers**: Nonpartisan observers are not agents of a party or candidate. They document and report on the process as a whole, without advocacy interest in any outcome.

## How to Become a Poll Watcher

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Check your state's laws**: The National Conference of State Legislatures (ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/policies-for-election-observers) maintains a state-by-state comparison of observer policies. Requirements vary dramatically — some states allow broad access, others are quite restrictive.

2. **Contact a political party or candidate**: If you want to serve as a partisan poll watcher (the most common route), contact the Democratic or Republican Party in your county, or a candidate's campaign directly. They recruit and formally appoint poll watchers.

3. **Contact a nonpartisan organization**: Organizations like Common Cause, the League of Women Voters, and NAACP often have poll observer programs that provide formal appointments in states where nonpartisan organizations qualify.

4. **Get your credentials**: Your appointing authority will provide official credentials or appoint you through the state/county election authority's system. You must carry these credentials on Election Day.

5. **Complete required training**: Many states and all serious appointing organizations require training before Election Day. Do not show up untrained — you can be removed for violating protocols.

### State-Specific Examples

- **Georgia**: The Georgia Secretary of State has a formal poll watcher training program (sos.ga.gov/page/poll-watcher-training). Contact your party or county election office to be formally appointed.
- **New Mexico**: New Mexico requires formal appointment by an authorized authority (party, candidate, or county). Self-appointment is not permitted. Contact sos.nm.gov for current procedures.
- **Nevada**: Contact the Nevada Secretary of State (nvsos.gov) and your party for appointment procedures.

## Election Protection Volunteer Programs (The Accessible Route)

If you are not connected to a political party or candidate, the most accessible route to meaningful election integrity work is the **866-OUR-VOTE Election Protection program** operated by the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law.

### 866-OUR-VOTE Basics

- **Hotline**: 866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683) or text 866-687-8683
- **Website**: 866ourvote.org
- **Spanish-language hotline**: 888-VE-Y-VOTA
- **Coalition**: 300+ national, state, and local partners
- **Non-partisan**: Assists all voters regardless of party affiliation

The hotline operates on Election Day and during early voting periods, connecting voters experiencing problems with trained Election Protection volunteers who can answer questions, document problems, and escalate to attorneys when needed.

### How to Volunteer with 866-OUR-VOTE

Email volunteer@866ourvote.org to inquire about volunteering opportunities. The program needs:

- **Hotline volunteers**: Staff phone banks on Election Day and during early voting, answering voter questions and documenting problems
- **Field volunteers**: Stationed at polling locations to assist voters and report problems (requires appropriate state-level credentials in some jurisdictions)
- **Legal volunteers**: Attorneys who can provide rapid legal advice when voter rights issues arise

The NAACP runs parallel voter protection programs: blackvoiceschangelives.org/voter-protection-legal-volunteers for attorneys and trained advocates.

## Carter Center 2026 Nonpartisan Observer Program

The Carter Center — which has observed more than 100 elections internationally — is conducting nonpartisan election observation in the US for 2026 in five states: **Georgia, New Mexico, Nevada, Michigan, and Montana**.

### How to Sign Up

- **Website**: cartercenter.org/country/united-states-of-america/
- **Eligibility**: Must be a US citizen and legal resident of the state in which you want to volunteer
- **Mailing list**: Register at electionstandards.cartercenter.org/about/opportunities/ to receive recruitment notices as they are posted
- **Michigan partnership**: Ford Presidential Foundation and the Carter Center partnered to launch the Michigan Election Observation Network specifically for 2026

### What Carter Center Observers Do

The Carter Center trains observers in nonpartisan methodology. Observers:
- Attend pre-election training sessions on observation methodology and legal frameworks
- Are deployed to polling locations, early vote centers, and ballot counting facilities
- Document what they observe using standardized protocols
- Do not advocate for any party or candidate
- Report findings to the Carter Center for post-election reports and public disclosure

The Carter Center's US observation focuses particularly on close adherence to established procedures, treatment of provisional ballots, accessibility for voters with disabilities, and chain of custody for ballots.

## Chain of Custody Monitoring

Chain of custody is one of the most important — and underappreciated — aspects of election integrity. It refers to the documented sequence of custody and control for ballots and voting equipment from the moment they are printed through tabulation and certification.

### What Chain of Custody Documentation Covers

At each stage of the election process, election officials document:
- Who is handling ballots or equipment
- Where items are being transported
- What time transfers occur
- Who witnesses and signs off on each handoff

When there are anomalies — equipment changes, boxes that show signs of having been opened, broken seals — they are documented, and additional witness attestation is added to the record.

### How Observers Monitor Chain of Custody

Observers with access to ballot counting facilities watch for and document:
- Whether all procedures are being followed per the published procedures
- Whether all required witnesses are present during ballot handling
- Whether anomalies (seal breaks, equipment substitutions) are being properly documented
- Whether observers are being allowed access consistent with state law

### Documenting What You Observe

If you observe something that appears irregular:
1. **Do not confront or challenge directly** — this can get you removed and eliminates your ability to continue documenting
2. **Write down everything immediately** — time, location, what you saw, who was involved
3. **Take photos or video if permitted** — observer access to cameras varies by jurisdiction
4. **Report to your organization's legal contact immediately** — if with 866-OUR-VOTE, call the support line; if with the Carter Center, contact your coordinator
5. **File with the appropriate authority** — depending on the irregularity, this may be the county election board, your state Secretary of State, or (for federal races) the Election Assistance Commission

## What to Document and How

### Before Election Day

- Know the specific procedures for your state and county — download and print the election administration plan from your county election board
- Know what observer access you are legally entitled to — this varies by state
- Know the chain of custody procedures for your jurisdiction

### On Election Day: What to Record

For any incident or irregularity, document:
- **Time and location** (polling place name, address, specific location within the facility)
- **What happened** (describe in neutral, factual terms — what you saw and heard, not your interpretation)
- **Who was involved** (descriptions if you do not know names, badge numbers for election workers, party affiliation if relevant)
- **Who witnessed** (other observers, voters, election workers who saw the same thing)
- **What action was taken** (was the problem reported? To whom? What was the response?)

### Specific Things to Watch For

**Voter access issues:**
- Voters being turned away without proper notice of provisional ballot rights
- Polling place access barriers for voters with disabilities
- Long lines or equipment failures that may suppress turnout
- Harassment or intimidation of voters outside the legally required distance

**Ballot handling:**
- Ballots being handled outside established chain-of-custody procedures
- Observers being denied legally required access to counting procedures
- Provisional ballots not being offered to voters who are eligible

**Voting machine irregularities:**
- Machines being taken offline and not replaced within required timeframes
- Inconsistencies in machine seals or tamper-evidence procedures
- Tabulation results that deviate from expected paper ballot totals

### Reporting Irregularities

**In the moment:**
- Call 866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683) for voter assistance and documentation
- Contact your organization's legal coordinator
- Report to the precinct captain or election judge if you have standing to do so

**Post-Election:**
- File complaints with your state Secretary of State or Board of Elections for procedural violations
- Report to the US Election Assistance Commission (eac.gov) for issues affecting federal races
- Contact the Lawyers' Committee or NAACP LDF for potential litigation on systemic issues
- Report to Votebeat (votebeat.org) — election-specific nonprofit journalism that documents chain of custody and procedural issues
