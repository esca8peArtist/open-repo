## Executive Summary

This document fixes seven quantified baselines as of May 5, 2026 — before Domain 37 enters distribution. Each metric includes a current count or status, sub-metric breakdown, source citations with confidence ratings, and a measurement protocol for tracking post-distribution change. The metrics span DOJ voter roll litigation, CISA election security funding, election-denier federal appointments, Section 3 litigation readiness, state AG coalition coordination, election protection NGO readiness, and academic/media attention. Section 8 provides an attribution framework. Section 9 provides a tracking dashboard template in CSV form.

Together these baselines define the pre-distribution state against which any framework-attributable impact can be measured. The framework's marginal contribution is coordination and acceleration, not origination. Attribution is assessed through vocabulary markers, structural convergence, and timing against the counterfactual baseline trend.

## Metric 2: CISA Election Security Budget and Staffing

**Baseline (May 5, 2026): FY2026 enacted election security earmark $39.6M (program functionally defunded by EI-ISAC termination); FY2027 proposed cut of $707M gross / $360M net from FY2026 CISA total; election security program proposed at zero**

### Sub-Metric 2.1 — FY2026 Enacted Budget (Current Allocation)

CISA FY2026 total appropriation: approximately $2.7 billion (down ~$135M / 4.6% from FY2025).

Election security specific earmark: $39.6M nominally preserved by Congress, but the underlying EI-ISAC cooperative agreement with the Center for Internet Security was terminated in early 2025, defunding the operational program while the line item technically remained. The earmark without an operating program is a shell allocation.

### Sub-Metric 2.2 — FY2027 Proposed Cut

FY2027 proposed CISA total budget: approximately $2.34B.
Gross proposed reduction: $707M from prior year baseline.
Net proposed reduction from FY2026 enacted: approximately $360M.
Election security line: proposed at zero — the FY2027 budget eliminates the election security program as a named line item.
Positions eliminated under FY2027 proposal: 860–867 positions, per administration budget documents.

### Sub-Metric 2.3 — EI-ISAC Termination Status

EI-ISAC cooperative agreement with the Center for Internet Security: terminated in February 2025. The EI-ISAC provided real-time threat intelligence sharing across 3,500+ election offices. Termination was announced as saving "approximately $10 million a year." As of May 2026, the EI-ISAC operates in a paid-subscription capacity only — state and local election offices that can afford to pay privately may continue receiving services; those that cannot do not.

Current EI-ISAC operational status: severely degraded. Estimated participation loss: the majority of the 3,500+ participating jurisdictions are small county offices without budget for paid subscription services.

### Sub-Metric 2.4 — MS-ISAC Cuts

MS-ISAC (Multi-State Information Sharing and Analysis Center): CISA cut $10M in funding announced in early 2025. The MS-ISAC provides cybersecurity services to state and local governments, including election offices. Prior to the cut, it served thousands of state and local governments. After the cut, projected to lose approximately two-thirds of state and local election members who cannot afford paid services.

### Sub-Metric 2.5 — Regional Election Security Advisors

Pre-2025 status: CISA deployed regional election security advisors in field offices across the country, providing direct technical assistance to state and local election officials.

Current status (May 2026): Program halted. Advisors reassigned or departed. NASS (National Association of Secretaries of State) sent a formal letter to DHS Secretary Kristi Noem in February 2026 requesting preservation of these positions — no public response as of May 5, 2026.

Count of positions at risk under FY2027 proposal: all remaining CISA election-adjacent positions, estimated at 50–80 field advisory roles.

### Sub-Metric 2.6 — CISA Staff Reassignments

CISA total staffing: approximately 3,300 employees when Trump took office in January 2025. By December 2025, approximately 2,400 — a loss of approximately 900 people. Election security staff is a disproportionate share of that reduction; the CISA election security review conducted in early 2025 was not made public despite the agency's historical transparency practice.

Staff status indicator from officials: Minnesota Secretary of State Steve Simon stated election officials "do not have a sense of whether we can rely on CISA for these services as we approach a big election year in 2026." Maine Secretary of State Shenna Bellows: trust with the Trump administration is "absolutely demolished."

### Measurement Protocol

Track appropriations subcommittee markups at June 30, 2026; full House markup; Senate markup; conference report.

Primary metric: final FY2027 CISA election security line item amount vs. zero proposed baseline.

Attribution indicator: Congressional floor or committee statements citing election interference analysis; any restoration of EI-ISAC, MS-ISAC, or regional advisor funding above zero; NASS letter campaign gaining additional co-signers after distribution.

**Sources**: [Nextgov/FCW, April 2026 — FY27 elimination proposal](https://www.nextgov.com/cybersecurity/2026/04/trump-proposes-cutting-cisa-election-security-program-fy27-budget/412672/), HIGH confidence; [The Next Web — $700M cut detail](https://thenextweb.com/news/cisa-budget-cut-700-million-trump-fy27), HIGH confidence; [Votebeat, Feb. 24, 2026 — CIS memo, EI-ISAC defunding](https://www.votebeat.org/2026/02/24/center-internet-security-memo-election-funding-cut/), HIGH confidence; [The Record (Recorded Future News) — MS-ISAC $10M cut](https://therecord.media/cisa-cuts-10-million-isac-funding), HIGH confidence; [StateScoop — secretaries of state concern](https://statescoop.com/federal-cuts-election-security-secretaries-state-2025/), HIGH confidence.

## Metric 4: Section 3 (14th Amendment) Litigation Readiness

**Baseline (May 5, 2026): No active Section 3 disqualification proceedings targeting 2026 election-administration officials; enforcement confined to Congressional action or federal proceedings under Anderson; 0 active private enforcement suits against current officeholders**

### Sub-Metric 4.1 — State AGs With Section 3 Enforcement Authority

Post-Anderson (Trump v. Anderson, March 4, 2024): states retain concurrent authority to enforce Section 3 for state offices. For federal offices, only Congress can provide enforcement mechanism.

States whose AGs have explicitly asserted Section 3 enforcement authority for state offices: no state AG has publicly filed a Section 3 proceeding as of May 5, 2026. At least 23 state AGs are in active election protection litigation (the mail ballot EO coalition), building the factual record that is the predicate for future Section 3 proceedings.

Colorado context: Secretary of State Jena Griswold, who pursued Section 3 ballot challenges in the 2024 cycle, is running for Colorado AG and qualified for the 2026 primary ballot at the March 28 state assembly (41.78% of assembly vote). As AG, she would have standing and stated inclination to pursue Section 3 enforcement proceedings against officials who attempted to reverse the 2020 results.

### Sub-Metric 4.2 — Private Enforcement Suits Filed to Date

Private enforcement suits filed under Section 3 targeting current 2026-election-administration officials: 0 as of May 5, 2026.

CREW (Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington) maintains the most developed factual record documenting January 6 participation by individuals in current federal positions — the predicate for any private enforcement action. CREW's record has not yet been the basis for a filed Section 3 proceeding targeting active election-administration officials.

### Sub-Metric 4.3 — SCOTUS Section 3 Interpretation Trends

Trump v. Anderson (2024): All nine justices held states cannot enforce Section 3 for federal offices; Section 5 grants Congress exclusive enforcement authority. The unanimous ruling eliminates state-administrative disqualification but preserves: (a) federal court enforcement once a proper federal proceeding is established; (b) Congressional enforcement legislation under Section 5; (c) state enforcement for state offices.

Post-Anderson trend: No Supreme Court development post-Anderson further narrowing or expanding Section 3 scope as of May 2026. The Anderson framework is settled; the question is the enforcement mechanism gap.

Current federal enforcement statute: None. No Section 5 enforcement statute enacted in the 119th Congress as of May 2026.

### Sub-Metric 4.4 — Timeline Readiness

Pre-election Section 3 window: Declaratory actions establishing a factual record of specific officials' engagement in 2020 reversal could be filed at any time. A pre-election declaratory judgment (establishing that a person is disqualified but not yet removing them from office) requires several months to litigate. For a November 2026 election, filings targeting election-night officials would need to be initiated by July 2026 at the latest to obtain pre-election relief.

Post-election Section 3 window: Officials who act to interfere with 2026 results face a cleaner factual record for post-election accountability proceedings. This window is the more practically realistic near-term target under current legal doctrine.

### Measurement Protocol

Track CREW filings, state AG public statements on Section 3, and Congressional Section 5 bill introductions.

Day 60 post-distribution: whether any declaratory action is filed establishing factual record.
Day 120 (pre-election): whether any preliminary injunction sought in a Section 3 proceeding.
Day 180 (post-election): number of Section 3 disqualification proceedings filed against officials who interfered with 2026 results.

Attribution indicator: Section 3 briefs or Congressional testimony citing the "post-Anderson enforcement architecture" framing developed in Domain 37; use of "federal executive interference" as a term of art in Section 3 filings.

## Metric 5: State AG Coalition Coordination

Case filed in U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts. Court ordered the Trump Administration to file its response by May 7, 2026; hearing on motions scheduled June 2, 2026.

- NAAG (National Association of Attorneys General): bipartisan membership body; issued a 2024 bipartisan statement condemning election-related violence; does not operate a dedicated election protection working group.

- Republican AG defections: Georgia AG Chris Carr joined a 25-state amicus brief in a noncitizen voting case that cuts against the administration's position on strict proof-of-citizenship requirements — demonstrating that the election-integrity issue is not monolithically partisan among Republican AGs.

### Sub-Metric 5.2 — Joint Amicus Filings on Election Security/Voting Rights Since 2024

3. Multiple individual-state filings opposing DOJ voter roll suits (some states filed as intervenors in other states' cases)

Total estimated coordinated filings since 2024: approximately 5–8 documented multi-state actions on election administration issues. Comprehensive PACER-based count has not been completed; this is a MEDIUM confidence estimate based on tracking from Democracy Docket, Votebeat, and state AG press releases.

### Sub-Metric 5.3 — Republican AG Defections on Voting Rights

Republican AGs whose stated positions cut against administration election-interference strategy:
