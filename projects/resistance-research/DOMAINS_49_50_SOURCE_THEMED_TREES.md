### Theme A: Statutory Amendment Pathways
*Research Zone 1. Core question: Why is statutory NEPA amendment the only adequate remedy after Marin Audubon + CEQ rescission + Seven County?*

**Cluster anchor**: Marin Audubon v. FAA (November 2024, D.C. Circuit) + Federal Register 2026-00178 (CEQ NEPA regulations rescinded January 8, 2026).

**Key sources with annotations** (8 primary; see index for full 28-source Zone 1 inventory):

**[A1] Federal Register 2026-03157 — GHG Endangerment Finding Rescission**
Annotation: Finalized February 12, 2026; effective April 20, 2026. The terminus of the 15-month demolition sequence. All references in the document must use "finalized/effective" not "proposed." The effective date is the urgency anchor for pre-midterm distribution.
Research question it answers: What was the final action that completed the removal of all regulatory hooks through which EJ communities generated procedural standing?

**[A2] Federal Register 2026-00178 — NEPA Regulations Removal**
Annotation: Effective January 8, 2026. Removed all CEQ NEPA regulations simultaneously — eliminated requirements for climate change/EJ consideration, Indigenous knowledge, and early community consultation. The enabling action for the Endangered Finding to remove the last layer.
Research question: What specific participation rights did the NEPA rescission eliminate, and what was the effective date?

**[A6] Marin Audubon Society v. FAA — D.C. Circuit (November 12, 2024)**
Annotation: 2-1 panel decision holding CEQ lacks authority to issue binding NEPA regulations. This is the critical prior legal development that the January 8, 2026 CEQ rescission followed. The decision makes executive restoration of CEQ regulations legally vulnerable even under a favorable future administration — statutory amendment is the only reliable restoration mechanism.
Research question: Why can't a future favorable administration restore participation rights through executive action? Answer: Marin Audubon makes CEQ regulatory authority legally uncertain; statutory amendment is more durable.
Framing guardrail: Do not overstate. This is a 2-1 panel, not en banc or SCOTUS. Say "current legal landscape makes statutory reform the most reliable path" — not "CEQ authority is permanently gone."

**[D8] National Law Review — Split D.C. Circuit Discards CEQ NEPA Regulations**
Annotation: Covers Marin Audubon; documents that the 2-1 decision discarded 40+ years of NEPA regulation through a panel decision. The "40+ years" framing is accessible and impactful for non-legal audiences. Companion to the Sidley Austin analysis.

**[N4] SCOTUS Seven County Infrastructure Coalition v. Eagle County (May 29, 2025)**
Annotation: 8-0 Kavanaugh majority; "deference squared" — agencies now get deference both on what effects to analyze and on the sufficiency of the analysis. Excluded specific Gulf Coast EJ communities from NEPA review. This is the SCOTUS adverse precedent that D.C. Circuit cases must work around.
Research question: What did SCOTUS do to judicial review of NEPA compliance, and how does it compound the CEQ regulatory rescission?

**[N5] CRS — Seven County "Deference Squared" Analysis (LSB11333)**
Annotation: The best single source for congressional audiences on Seven County. Explains that CEQ regulatory rescission removed procedural requirements while Seven County removed judicial check on compliance — together eliminating both layers of community protection. Use for congressional staff contacts.

**[J1] Earthjustice — SPEED Act Statement**
Annotation: The SPEED Act would permanently codify the NEPA rollback as statutory law — permanently foreclosing the statutory amendment pathway that Domain 49's Tier 2 reform architecture relies on. This is the legislative urgency element: statutory reform is possible only if the SPEED Act fails.
Research question: What is the legislative threat to the reform architecture itself?

**[D2] Georgetown Environmental Law Review — Massachusetts to Major Questions**
Annotation: Most rigorous legal analysis of whether the Endangerment Finding rescission survives Massachusetts v. EPA (2007). Documents the major questions doctrine threat to the rescission rationale. Use for law school clinic and litigation contacts.

Integration callout: Theme A sources (A1-A6, D1-D13, J1-J3, N4-N5) together establish the legal architecture for the reform argument. Do not treat them as background facts — they are the argument. Every Zone 1 research session should trace from the specific regulatory text to the democratic participation consequence.

### Theme B: Electoral Cascade Analysis
*Research Zone 2. Core question: How does VRA redistricting (post-Callais) restore EJ community electoral power after administrative channels are eliminated?*

**Cluster anchor**: 2026 Civic Information Index (Listening Post Collective) + Louisiana v. Callais (May 2026 VRA redistricting) + UCS vicious cycle documentation.

**Key sources with annotations**:

**[C1] UCS — Voting Rights and Environmental Justice (2018)**
Annotation: The single most important source for establishing the democratic design argument: states with restrictive voting policies have worse health outcomes; legislatures in extreme gerrymander states are less willing to expand Medicaid; environmental burden → civic suppression → further environmental harm. This is the core mechanism that connects Domain 49's EJ analysis to the VRA redistricting cascade argument.
Research question: What is the causal mechanism through which environmental burden produces democratic disengagement? This source answers it empirically.

**[C2] UCS — Building a Healthier Democracy**
Annotation: "Polluters prefer politicians who are not accountable to frontline communities." The most quotable single line in the Domain 49 source library. Documents that the vicious cycle operates through racial geography — the geographic co-location of gerrymander and pollution burden is deliberate.

**[C3] PMC 12779674 — Environmental Civic Engagement, Diverse Communities (2026)**
Annotation: 2026 peer-reviewed study — the strongest academic anchor available. Finds "resistant capital" (understanding systems of oppression) is the strongest predictor of environmental civic participation for Black, Asian, and Latine/Hispanic individuals. This is the mechanism explaining why removing EPA EJ infrastructure specifically damages communities with the highest civic engagement motivation.

**[C4] 2026 Civic Information Index — Listening Post Collective**
Annotation: First Civic Information Index to integrate environmental resilience data alongside voter registration metrics. Documents Appalachian and Black South gaps: 40%+ of registered voters in these regions didn't vote in the last presidential election. Lower civic health scores consistently associated with communities with higher percentages of Black, Hispanic/Latino, and Native American residents. This is the geographic anchor for both the redistricting cascade and the Appalachian intersection with Domain 50.
Research question: Where specifically does the vicious cycle produce measurable civic exclusion? This source answers with geographic precision.

**[H7] Earthjustice — NJ Appellate Court Affirms NJ Environmental Justice Law (January 2026)**
Annotation: January 5, 2026 — the NJ Appellate Division upheld NJ's Environmental Justice Rules against industry challenge on all grounds. Operative holding: "economic benefits cannot be used to allow new sources of pollution in overburdened communities." This is the strongest state-level judicial endorsement of cumulative impact analysis yet decided, and it is the model for VRA redistricting case arguments: political benefits to incumbents cannot be used to allow more than de minimis dilution of EJ community electoral power.

**[H3] EJStatebyState.org — State EJ Law Database**
Annotation: Eight states with cumulative impact bills: California, Connecticut, Georgia, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina. The 42-state gap is the redistricting relevance map: most EJ communities are in unprotected states (Gulf Coast, Appalachia, industrial Midwest). The gap defines which states need VRA redistricting protection most urgently and have no state EJ law backstop.

**[H9] Center for Progressive Reform — Federal-State Conflicts Over Environmental Justice**
Annotation: Documents Trump EO directing AG to challenge state EJ laws as unconstitutional. This is the escalating threat to the state-tier reform argument: even the eight-state partial protection is under active federal attack. The redistricting cascade becomes more urgent if the state EJ floor is also challenged.

**[C12] Nonprofit Quarterly — Queer Ecological Framework for EJ in Appalachia**
Annotation: The specific source for the 49-50 geographic intersection. Documents organizations working at the LGBTQ+/EJ intersection in Appalachian communities. Cross-domain bridge source — cite in Zone 2 redistricting context and in Zone 4 coalition context.
Integration callout: This source simultaneously feeds Theme B (Zone 2, redistricting geography) and Theme D (Zone 4, coalition activation). Do not use in only one zone.

**Key sources with annotations**:

### Theme C: Legal Strategy
*Research Zone 3. Core question: What are the state constitutional alternatives after Skrmetti, and what does the Little v. Hecox decision (pending July 1) require us to add?*

**Cluster anchor**: US v. Skrmetti (A4) + Kalarchik v. State (I6) + Little v. Hecox / West Virginia v. BPJ (I7) + Lambda Legal state court strategy (A7, H1).

**Key sources with annotations**:

**[A4] US v. Skrmetti — SCOTUS Opinion (June 18, 2025)**
Annotation: 6-3; Roberts majority; rational basis review applied to TN SB1. The ADF strategic vehicle was designed to avoid heightened scrutiny: the law classifies based on age and medical use, not sex or transgender status, and the majority accepted that framing. Do not overstate what Skrmetti held — it did not say trans people lack Equal Protection rights, Bostock is overruled, or adult care bans are automatically constitutional. Over-reading Skrmetti undermines credibility with Lambda Legal.

**[A5] CRS — Skrmetti Legal Summary (LSB11329)**
Annotation: Congressional Research Service nonpartisan analysis. Most credible source for characterizing the holding precisely for congressional and judicial audiences. CRS sources carry automatic credibility on the Hill and in court filings. Use when precision about the holding matters most.

**[A7] State Court Report — Skrmetti Rippling Through State Courts**
Annotation: Post-Skrmetti state court landscape; documents which states have broader state constitutional protections. Lambda Legal's post-Skrmetti strategy focuses on state constitutional claims. This source established the theoretical framework; Kalarchik (I6) is now the operational proof of concept.

**[I6] Kalarchik v. State — Montana Supreme Court (April 14, 2026)**
Annotation: Held: "Transgender discrimination is, by its very nature, sex discrimination." Montana state constitution's equal protection clause applies heightened scrutiny — not Skrmetti's rational basis floor. This is not a liberal-state development: Montana is an active anti-trans legislative state, and its Supreme Court still applied heightened scrutiny. This is Lambda Legal's state court strategy succeeding in practice.
Research question: Is the state court alternative actually viable after Skrmetti? Kalarchik answers: yes, already working.

**[I7] Little v. Hecox / West Virginia v. BPJ — SCOTUS Trans Athletes (Pending)**
Annotation: Oral arguments January 13, 2026; decision expected by end of June 2026. Do not proceed with Zone 3 research without first checking whether this decision has issued. When issued: update Pillar 5 (Constitutional Standards) immediately. If the court upholds bans under rational basis, Skrmetti's rational basis floor now covers an additional sphere of trans life (athletics in addition to healthcare). State court alternative analysis (Kalarchik) becomes even more important as the post-decision response frame.

**[H4] ACLU — SCOTUS Trans Healthcare Ruling Explanation**
Annotation: ACLU accessible explanation of Skrmetti for general distribution audiences. Use for contacts without constitutional law backgrounds. Lambda Legal's FAQ (A8) is for LGBTQ+ law audiences; ACLU's explanation is for general policy audiences.

**[H7] 4th Circuit — States Can Compel Trans Adults Coverage Rules**
Annotation: "If a State can reasonably ban it, of course a State can reasonably refuse to pay for it." This is the roadmap sentence for extending Skrmetti to adult care bans and Medicaid coverage restrictions. This 4th Circuit extension is the most dangerous post-Skrmetti development for the healthcare access analysis.

**[M1] Lambda Legal / ACLU / NYCLU — Federal Class Action Against DOJ/NYU Langone**
Annotation: Filed June 1, 2026. DOJ grand jury subpoena compelling NYU Langone to hand over trans youth health records 2020-2026. Fourth and Fifth Amendment claims. The subpoena was empaneled in the Northern District of Texas — ADF's home forum-shopping jurisdiction. The chilling effect on healthcare access (deterring trans youth from seeking care) is a harm not captured in the Skrmetti constitutional analysis. This case extends the domain's constitutional framework into a new dimension.

The EO 14173 constitutional theory that protects LGBTQ+ nonprofits from "DEI" defunding may also apply to OEJECR's closure under the same EO authority. Flag for paired legal advocacy outreach.
