## TIER 1: Primary Causal Evidence (Read in Full Before Writing Begins)

These seven sources constitute the domain's evidential backbone. Each establishes a causal relationship, not merely a correlation. All must be engaged at the level of methodology and effect size in full production.

**[C1] Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, Zhang — Dallas Federal Reserve Working Paper 2517, revised March 2026**
"Household Finance Shapes Political Participation: Evidence from Mortgage Refinancing" (revised title from original "Relieving Financial Distress Increases Voter Turnout")
Full paper: https://www.dallasfed.org/~/media/documents/research/papers/2025/wp2517r1.pdf
Abstract page: https://www.dallasfed.org/research/papers/2025/wp2517
SSRN: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5253585
**Causal role**: Domain's primary causal anchor. HARP eligibility quasi-experiment shows mortgage payment relief causally increases 2012 voter turnout. Effect concentrated among households with larger payment reductions and politically independent voters. This is the only large-scale quasi-experimental study directly demonstrating that financial relief causes increased electoral participation. Required reading.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in executive summary and Section 1.1.
**Access**: Open access PDF, confirmed.

**[C2] Slee, Gillian and Desmond, Matthew — Politics and Society, Vol. 51, No. 1, 2023**
"Eviction and Voter Turnout: The Political Consequences of Housing Instability"
https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/00323292211050716
Princeton repository: https://collaborate.princeton.edu/en/publications/eviction-and-voter-turnout-the-political-consequences-of-housing-/
NLIHC summary: https://nlihc.org/resource/evictions-associated-lower-voter-turnout
**Causal role**: Instrumental variable study using commercial evictions as instrument for residential evictions. Effect sizes: a 1 pp increase in eviction rate produces a 0.46 pp decline in voter turnout overall; 5.54 pp decline in low-eviction neighborhoods where eviction is most disruptive. IV estimate: a 1 pp reduction in evictions would have increased 2016 voter turnout by 2.73 pp. Required reading.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in Section 1.3 and Section 2 (Pathway 2).
**Access**: Paywalled at Sage; Princeton repository may have working paper version. Confirm before production.

**[C3] Kuk, John; Hajnal, Zoltan; Lajevardi, Nazita — PNAS, 2024**
"Residential Mobility and Persistently Depressed Voting Among Disadvantaged Adults in a Large Housing Experiment"
https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306287121
PMC open access: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11098094/
**Causal role**: Moving to Opportunity housing voucher lottery as natural experiment. Induced residential moves depressed voter participation among low-income Black and Hispanic adults for nearly 20 years. Mechanism is social capital disruption and registration lapse, not housing quality. Counterintuitive finding: housing assistance does not automatically enable civic participation. Required reading.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in Section 1.3 and Section 2 (Pathway 2).
**Access**: Open access via PMC, confirmed.

**[C4] Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, Zhao — Science, Vol. 341, 2013**
"Poverty Impedes Cognitive Function"
https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1238041
Harvard IQSS summary: https://www.iq.harvard.edu/news/mullainathan-and-shafir-explore-cognitive-effects-scarcity
APA summary: https://www.apa.org/monitor/2014/02/scarcity
**Causal role**: Foundational experimental study. Indian farmers in pre-harvest (financial stress) period demonstrate measurably reduced cognitive performance — equivalent to a 13–14 IQ point reduction — compared to the same individuals in post-harvest (financial relief) period. Cognitive load from financial scarcity impairs reasoning on tasks unrelated to the financial problem. Establishes "bandwidth tax" concept empirically.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in Sections 1.4 and 2 (Pathway 3).
**Access**: Open access via Science.org; IQSS and APA summaries free.

**[C5] Journal of Economic Psychology meta-analysis, 2024**
"Financial Scarcity and Cognitive Performance: A Meta-Analysis"
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167487024000102
**Causal role**: Updates and confirms Mani et al. (2013) across multiple study designs. The "bandwidth tax" from financial scarcity reducing cognitive performance is replicated across experimental and observational designs. Critical for establishing the scarcity framework is not a single-study finding.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — pair with C4 in citation to establish replication standard.
**Access**: ScienceDirect — confirm access before production.

**[C6] Bae, Jaehee and Haselswerdt, Jake — Perspectives on Politics, 2023**
"Gig Work, Economic Precarity, and Political Behavior"
LSE USAPP Blog summary: https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/usappblog/2026/04/02/the-gig-economy-is-reshaping-how-people-work-and-what-they-think-about-social-protections-and-politics/
**Causal role**: Survey of 1,500+ service-sector and gig workers. Main-job gig workers vote at lower rates than traditional workers despite higher expressive participation (protests, official contacts). Identifies schedule predictability — not political disengagement — as the mechanism: gig workers shift to flexible-schedule participation and away from schedule-dependent participation (voting). Confirms gig economy structural unavailability pathway.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in Section 1.5 (gig economy) and Section 2 (Pathway 6).
**Access**: Perspectives on Politics (Cambridge) — confirm access; LSE blog summary open.

**[C7] Rastogi, Ankit and Laurison, Daniel — Sociological Perspectives, 2025**
"Higher Turnout, Greater Inequality? A Precinct-Level Analysis of Income Inequality in U.S. Presidential Voting, 2016 to 2020"
https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23780231251338441
**Causal role**: Documents widening income-participation gap: top-decile vs. bottom-decile precinct gap grew from 17 pp (2016) to 27 pp (2020). Key finding: higher overall turnout does not close the gap — high-income voters mobilize more effectively, widening the gap under turnout-increasing conditions. Establishes the inequality-amplification dynamic.
**Relevance tier**: ESSENTIAL — cite in Section 1.1 on the widening income-participation gap.
**Access**: Sage — confirm access; may be paywalled.

## TIER 3: Peer-Reviewed Social Science Studies

**[P1] Uslaner, Eric and Brown, Mitchell — Russell Sage Foundation**
"Inequality, Trust, and Civic Engagement"
https://www.russellsage.org/sites/default/files/u4/Uslaner%20and%20Brown.pdf
**Summary**: Income inequality reduces interpersonal and institutional trust, which mediates civic engagement. The participatory gap between rich and poor increases with higher economic inequality through the trust mechanism — not merely through resource constraints. Establishes the stigma/outsider-status pathway's social trust foundation.
**Relevance**: Section 2 (Pathway 7 — social stigma). Open access PDF confirmed.

**[P2] ScienceDirect, 2025 — Childhood Poverty and Political Participation**
"Childhood poverty and political participation: The role of family, gender and economic mobility"
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379425001350
**Summary**: Childhood poverty reduces adult political participation, with the effect partially mediated by economic mobility. Women and girls in poverty face the largest participation suppression. Establishes intergenerational pathway: economic precarity is not merely a current-generation problem.
**Relevance**: Section 2 cross-support. Confirm access before production.

**[P3] Rock the Registration — Journal of Politics, 2022**
"Rock the Registration: Same Day Registration Increases Turnout of Young Voters"
https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/714776
**Summary**: Same-day registration increases youth turnout by 3.1–7.3 percentage points. Since young people are disproportionately geographically mobile — and the housing instability pathway particularly affects them — SDR is the direct reform response to the eviction-registration-lapse mechanism.
**Relevance**: Section 5 (Reform Architecture — Tier 1). Confirm open access version.

**[P4] Scholars Strategy Network — Working-Class Political Engagement**
"Strengthening Political Engagement Among Working-Class and Low-Income Communities"
https://scholars.org/contribution/strengthening-political-engagement-among
**Summary**: Synthesizes research on barriers to political engagement for low-income workers. Identifies multi-barrier environment: no campaign contact, no monetary contribution capacity, logistical barriers (job security fears, transportation, phone/computer access). Confirms no single intervention addresses the full barrier set.
**Relevance**: Section 2 context and Section 6 (movement leverage). Open access confirmed.

**[P5] Cambridge Core — "Economic distress and voting: evidence from the subprime mortgage crisis"**
Political Science Research and Methods, Cambridge University Press
https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/political-science-research-and-methods/article/abs/economic-distress-and-voting-evidence-from-the-subprime-mortgage-crisis/D958A49361C3F0B59A9D6E667BB49E31
**Summary**: Additional causal evidence on economic distress and voting using subprime mortgage crisis variation. Confirms the general finding of C1 (Dallas Fed WP2517) using a different identification strategy.
**Relevance**: Section 1.1 and Pathway 1 — use as supporting citation alongside C1. Confirm access.

**[P6] BFI Working Paper — "Scarcity and Inattention" (Bhanot, Linos et al., 2022)**
https://bfi.uchicago.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/BFI_WP_2022-76.pdf
**Summary**: Updated behavioral research on cognitive load from financial scarcity reducing attention to civic tasks. Updates Mullainathan/Shafir framework with experimental evidence on attention to government communications and public programs.
