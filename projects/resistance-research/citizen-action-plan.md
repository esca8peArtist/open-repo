# Citizen Action Plan: A Practical Guide to Upholding Democracy

**Evidence-based, specific, and actionable guidance for ordinary citizens**

---

## Introduction

### Why Individual Action Matters

The research on civil resistance is clear: mass participation is the decisive variable in movement success. Erica Chenoweth's landmark study found that campaigns achieving active participation from 3.5% of the population have historically never failed. In the United States, that means approximately 11.5 million people — a large number, but one achieved by organizing across millions of individual decisions to show up.

No single person changes politics alone. What changes politics is the aggregation of individual actions into organizational structures capable of sustained pressure. The Civil Rights Movement was not a spontaneous eruption — it was built over decades by individuals who joined the NAACP, showed up to precinct meetings, registered voters, trained as legal observers, and did unglamorous organizational work between the headline-making moments.

You are not a bystander to history. You are a potential participant in it.

### How to Think About Your Contribution

Before choosing where to invest your time and energy, consider four variables:

**1. Your skills**: Are you a lawyer? A teacher? A writer? An organizer? Someone with community relationships in a specific neighborhood? A data analyst? A graphic designer? Your professional skills are likely most valuable when applied to civic purposes.

**2. Your time**: This guide organizes actions by time commitment. Be realistic. Consistent action at a lower level beats sporadic action at a higher level. A person who makes five constituent calls per month for two years accomplishes more than someone who shows up to one march.

**3. Your risk tolerance**: Not everyone can engage in all forms of action. People with certain immigration statuses, employment situations, health conditions, or family obligations have real constraints. The guide includes options across the full risk spectrum.

**4. Your community**: The most effective organizing happens where you already have relationships — your workplace, neighborhood, religious community, professional association, or social network. Don't go looking for activism; bring activism to where you already are.

### The Single Most Important Principle

**Consistent local action beats occasional national action.**

Calling your specific member of Congress on a specific bill beats attending a national march. Showing up to a school board meeting beats signing an online petition. Volunteering on a city council campaign beats watching national election returns on television. The local is where you have leverage, relationships, and the possibility of changing outcomes.

## Tier 1: Actions Anyone Can Do (1-2 hours/week)

These are the baseline civic actions that every person capable of participating should be doing. They require minimal time, no special skills, and no significant financial resources.

---

### Action 1: Register to Vote and Vote in EVERY Election

**Why it matters**: Voter turnout in local elections is typically 5-15%. In primary elections — where candidates are selected before the general election — turnout can be as low as 5-8%. This means that small, organized groups of consistent voters have disproportionate power to determine who appears on ballots and who wins close races.

**Step-by-step**:

**1. Check your registration status** (do this today, even if you think you're registered):
- Visit **vote.gov** — official U.S. government voter registration resource
- Select your state; most states have online registration lookup
- Confirm your name, address, and party registration are correct
- If you've moved recently, you likely need to re-register

**2. Register or update your registration**:
- Online: **vote.gov** links to every state's registration system
- In person: Your county election office, many DMV locations, public libraries
- By mail: Download form from your state's election website
- Registration deadlines vary by state; check your state's deadline (some states allow same-day registration)

**3. Build your election calendar**:
- **vote.gov** and **ballotpedia.org** list upcoming elections by state
- Sign up for your county election office's email list for election notifications
- The elections most people miss: primaries (often May or June); school board elections (often November of non-presidential years); special elections (any time)
- Set a recurring calendar reminder to check your ballot three weeks before every election

**4. Research candidates and ballot measures before voting**:
- **ballotpedia.org**: Nonpartisan information on candidates and ballot measures at all levels
- **votesmart.org**: Candidate positions, voting records, financial disclosures
- **opensecrets.org**: Campaign finance information for federal and many state races
- **FollowTheMoney.org**: State-level campaign finance data
- Your state and local newspaper's endorsements (even if you disagree, reading the reasoning is useful)
- Candidate forums: Search your city/county name + "candidate forum" on YouTube and local news sites
- For judges: State and local bar associations publish ratings of judicial candidates; the nonpartisan approach to judicial selection matters

**5. Vote**:
- Know your polling place: **vote.gov** or your county election website
- Know your state's ID requirements: **ncsl.org/elections-and-campaigns/voter-id-laws** lists every state's rules
- If voting by mail: Request your ballot at least three weeks in advance; mail it back at least one week before the election or drop it at an official drop box
- If voting in person: bring a friend or family member who might not otherwise vote

**Practical note on primaries**: Primary elections choose party nominees. In many states, primaries are more decisive than general elections in one-party-dominant areas. Primary electorates are dramatically smaller and more engaged than general election electorates — your vote has more power.

### Action 2: Contact Your Representatives Systematically

**Why it matters**: The Indivisible Guide (written by former congressional staffers) documents a central insight from the inside: **constituent calls move votes**. Members of Congress track contact volume on specific issues by their own constituents. A spike in constituent calls on a pending vote reaches members' attention in a way that national petitions do not.

The key word is "constituent." Calls from people outside a member's district are logged but carry far less weight. Your calls to your own representatives matter.

**Finding your representatives**:
- U.S. House members (by zip code): **house.gov** (enter zip code in top right corner)
- U.S. Senators (by state): **senate.gov** → "Find Your Senators"
- State legislators: **openstates.org** (enter your address)
- All levels including local officials: **commoncause.org/find-your-representative/**
- For local officials (mayor, city council, school board): Your city/county government website

**The hierarchy of contact effectiveness** (from most to least impactful):
1. In-person meetings with the member or senior staff in district offices
2. Phone calls to district offices (not Washington) during business hours
3. Phone calls to Washington offices
4. Handwritten personal letters mailed to district offices
5. Emails to official websites (use sparingly; most are processed by low-level staff)
6. Social media tags (useful for public pressure but don't substitute for direct contact)
7. Online petitions (minimal impact on their own)

**How to call** (step-by-step):
1. Find your representative's district office phone number (listed on their official website)
2. Call during business hours (9am-5pm local time)
3. A staff person will answer, not the representative
4. Say: "My name is [Name] and I'm a constituent from [City, Zip Code]. I'm calling to [support/oppose] [specific bill or action]. I would like Representative/Senator [Name] to [specific ask]. Thank you."
5. You don't need to be an expert; you don't need to argue. Staff take the message and log it.
6. Keep a log: date, issue, what you said, response (if any)

**Sample script for a specific call** (adapt as needed):
> "Hello, my name is [Name]. I live in [City] and I'm a constituent in [District/State]. I'm calling to oppose the [specific bill/executive action]. I believe [one-sentence reason]. I'm asking [Representative/Senator] to [specific ask — vote no, issue a statement, co-sponsor an alternative]. Could someone confirm you've received my message? Thank you very much."

**Make it systematic**:
- Choose one issue area you care most about
- Call on that issue every time there is a specific upcoming vote or decision
- Track your calls in a simple spreadsheet or notebook
- When you hear your representative has been helpful on an issue you care about, call to thank them — positive reinforcement works too

**When calls matter most**:
- When a vote is imminent (within 2 weeks)
- When the representative's position is unclear or persuadable
- When there is a spike in constituent contact organized by national groups — add your voice
- During committee markups (when bills are being written/amended) — often more impactful than the final vote stage

### Action 3: Subscribe to and Support Local Journalism

**Why it matters**: Local newspapers and news outlets have lost over 2,500 since 2005. When they disappear, local government operates with dramatically less accountability. Studies show that communities without local newspapers have higher levels of municipal corruption, less voter participation in local elections, and higher borrowing costs for local governments (because no one is scrutinizing the budget).

Supporting local journalism is infrastructure investment.

**Step-by-step**:
1. Identify your local journalism ecosystem:
   - Your local daily or weekly newspaper (even if it is struggling)
   - Local investigative outlets (often nonprofit): search "[your city] + nonprofit news" or check **inn.org** (Institute for Nonprofit News) for members
   - Local public radio station
   - Neighborhood or community newsletters (Substack, independent blogs)
2. Subscribe and pay for the content you read
3. Share important local investigative stories on social media and with friends
4. Submit tips about local government misconduct: most newsrooms have a tips page; investigative outlets often have SecureDrop addresses for sensitive tips
5. Write letters to the editor — they are read and do influence local opinion

**Minimum action**: Subscribe to your local newspaper, even if it is imperfect. The accountability it provides is worth the cost.

### Action 4: Join One Local Organization and Show Up Regularly

**Why it matters**: Organizations are the vehicle through which individual action becomes collective power. Joining and showing up consistently — not spectacularly — is the foundational act of civic life.

**How to find the right organization**:

**Step 1**: Identify your priority issues (pick one or two maximum to start)
- Voting rights and elections → League of Women Voters, Indivisible, local Democratic party
- Civil liberties → ACLU affiliate (every state has one; aclu.org/affiliates)
- Racial justice → NAACP chapter (naacp.org), local civil rights organizations
- Environment → Sierra Club chapter (sierraclub.org/chapters), local environmental groups
- Labor rights → Your workplace union (if one exists), Jobs with Justice local chapter
- Immigrant rights → Local immigrant rights organizations (search your city + "immigrant rights organization")
- General civic engagement → League of Women Voters, Common Cause affiliate

**Step 2**: Find the local chapter
- Most national organizations have chapter finders on their websites
- **Mobilize.us**: Lists upcoming volunteer events for progressive organizations
- Indivisible chapter finder: **indivisible.org/local**
- Meetup.com (search your issue area and location)

**Step 3**: Show up — and keep showing up
- Attend the first meeting or event with no commitment beyond showing up
- Introduce yourself; tell people what you care about and what you can contribute
- After two or three meetings, ask: "What's the most useful thing I could help with?"
- Consistent presence over months builds trust and influence within the organization more than sporadic heroic contributions

**What makes an effective local organization**:
- Has specific goals and tracks progress toward them
- Gives members meaningful things to do (not just attending meetings)
- Has a theory of change (who needs to do what for the goal to be achieved)
- Maintains consistent membership and leadership rather than cycling entirely with elections

### Action 5: Attend One Public Meeting Per Month

**Why it matters**: Elected officials at the local level — school boards, city councils, county commissions — make decisions affecting millions of people in meetings where two or three people from the public sometimes show up. Showing up is itself a form of accountability.

**What meetings to attend**:
- City/town council meetings
- School board meetings
- County commission or board of supervisors meetings
- Planning commission (makes land use decisions)
- Police oversight board
- Budget hearings (when officials decide how to spend public money)
- Town halls and constituent meetings called by your elected officials

**How to find meetings**:
- Your city or county government website (usually under "Government" → "Meetings" or "Agendas")
- Your school district website → "Board" → "Meetings"
- **municode.com**: Many localities post codes and meeting information

**How to participate effectively**:
- Check the agenda before going — know what is being decided
- Most meetings have public comment periods; you typically sign up in advance or at the beginning of the meeting
- Time limits are typically 2-3 minutes per speaker; prepare and practice
- State your name and address at the beginning (this is often required and establishes you as a constituent)
- Focus on one specific point; connect it to the specific item being decided
- Ask a clear question or make a specific request rather than just venting
- Bring others — a showing of ten people has more impact than one person

**What to do after**:
- Follow up with the relevant official or staff member
- Track whether commitments made in the meeting are kept
- Report to your organization or network about what happened

### Action 6: Secure Your Digital Life

**Why it matters**: Digital security protects your personal information, prevents surveillance and targeting, and protects the organizations you work with. It is not paranoia — it is basic civic hygiene for the current environment.

**Step 1: Download and use Signal for sensitive communications**

Signal is the gold standard encrypted messaging application, used by journalists, lawyers, and security professionals worldwide.

1. Go to **signal.org** (not the app store; ensure you get the authentic app)
2. Download for iPhone or Android
3. Register with your phone number
4. After setup, go to Settings → Privacy → Default Timer for New Chats → Set to 1 week (enables disappearing messages by default)
5. Ask the people you communicate with about sensitive topics to also use Signal
6. Use Signal calls instead of regular phone calls for sensitive conversations

What Signal protects: The content of your messages and calls; who you are communicating with (to Signal); your message history

What Signal does NOT protect: The fact that you use Signal; metadata that can be accessed from your phone (who you called, when) through other means; physical surveillance

**Step 2: Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on important accounts**

For email, social media, and any organizing platforms:
1. Go to account security settings
2. Look for "Two-factor authentication" or "Two-step verification"
3. Enable it using an authenticator app (Google Authenticator or Authy) rather than SMS when possible — SMS-based 2FA can be intercepted
4. Priority accounts: Email (most important — all your other accounts can be recovered through email), social media, cloud storage, financial accounts

**Step 3: Use a password manager**

1. Download **Bitwarden** (free, open-source) at bitwarden.com
2. Create a strong master password (a four-word passphrase: "horse-correct-battery-staple" style)
3. Let Bitwarden generate and store unique passwords for every website
4. Never reuse passwords — one compromised password should not compromise all your accounts

**Step 4: Lock down your voter registration**

Your voter registration information may be public in your state. In many states, you can:
- Request a confidential voter address (California, Florida, and other states have Address Confidentiality Programs for people facing safety concerns)
- Remove your information from commercial data aggregators (use OptOutPrescreen.com, DataBrokerWatch.com)

**Step 5: Know your rights in encounters with law enforcement**

- You have the right to remain silent (5th Amendment) — say: "I am invoking my right to remain silent."
- You have the right to an attorney (6th Amendment) — say: "I want to speak to a lawyer before answering any questions."
- You do not have to consent to a search, but you should not physically resist if an officer proceeds anyway — say: "I do not consent to this search."
- At protests: Write your attorney's contact number on your arm in permanent marker before going; do not rely on your phone being accessible
- National Lawyers Guild legal support hotlines: In many cities, the NLG posts a legal observer hotline number before major demonstrations

**ACLU "Know Your Rights" resources**: **aclu.org/know-your-rights** — specific guides for encounters with police, at protests, at airports, and more.

### Action 7: Canvassing and Phone Banking

**Why it matters**: Door-to-door canvassing has been studied more extensively than almost any other campaign tactic. Meta-analysis of hundreds of experiments finds that high-quality canvassing conversations increase voter turnout by approximately 6-8 percentage points per conversation — the highest-documented effect of any single campaign contact method.

This is not a marginal effect. In races decided by hundreds or thousands of votes, canvassing is often decisive.

**How to get started**:
1. **Find a campaign or organization to volunteer with**: Mobilize.us lists canvassing opportunities in your area; your local Indivisible chapter; state Democratic Party; issue-based organizations doing voter contact
2. **Attend training**: Every serious campaign trains canvassers. The training covers how to use the canvassing app, the specific script, and how to handle common situations.
3. **Canvassing apps**: Most campaigns use MiniVAN (Democratic campaigns) or similar apps that map houses, track contacts, and integrate with voter files. You install the app, and it shows you which doors to knock and records your results.

**Best practices for doorstep conversations**:
- The goal is not to debate or persuade — it is to identify supporters and make sure they vote
- Listen more than you talk
- Don't read a script mechanically; use the script as a guide for the points to hit
- If someone is hostile, thank them for their time and leave — that's fine
- Keep your body language open and non-threatening; stand to the side of the door, not directly in front of it
- Always be respectful; you are an ambassador for your organization

**Phone and text banking**: Lower effect than door-knocking but can be done from home. Organizations provide scripts and systems. Effective for volunteer recruitment and event reminders in addition to voter contact.

### Action 8: Voter Registration Drives

**Why it matters**: An estimated 21+ million eligible Americans are not registered to vote. Many of these are young people, people who have recently moved, and people from lower-income and minority communities. Targeted voter registration in these communities increases the electorate in ways that, over time, shift political outcomes.

**How to become a voter registration volunteer**:

**Option 1: Through an organization**
- League of Women Voters (lwv.org): Has trained voter registration volunteers for over a century; find your state league at lwv.org/local-leagues
- Rock the Vote (rockthevote.org): Focused on young voters; has tools for organizing registration drives
- Fair Elections Center (fairelectionscenter.org): Legal resources and training for voter registration work
- NAACP local chapters: Voter registration is a core function

**Option 2: Through your employer, school, or institution**
- Many states allow organizations to conduct voter registration drives on their premises
- Universities and colleges often have official voter registration infrastructure
- Some states allow automatic voter registration at government agencies (ask your HR department or union)

**Key operational considerations**:
- Know your state's specific rules: Some states have certification requirements for voter registration volunteers; check with your state election office or the League of Women Voters
- The best locations: DMV offices, public libraries, community events, college campuses, public housing developments, unemployment offices, social service agencies
- Bring clipboards, pens, and printed registration forms from your state's election office
- Also bring a tablet with the online registration link for your state if allowed
- Collect completed forms promptly and submit them before the registration deadline

**Common mistakes**:
- Failing to check your state's specific submission rules (completed forms often must be submitted within a specific number of days)
- Not following up to confirm that registrations were processed (check with newly registered voters a few weeks after submitting)

### Action 9: Attend and Organize Protests

**Know your legal rights at protests before you go**:

- You have First Amendment rights to protest in public spaces (sidewalks, parks, plazas) without a permit in most cases for small gatherings
- Larger demonstrations may require permits in some jurisdictions — check with local authorities or the organizing group
- You can film police officers and public officials in public spaces in most states
- Police can lawfully order a crowd to disperse if they declare an unlawful assembly; you must be given a clear order and opportunity to leave before being arrested for failure to disperse
- Counter-protesters have rights too; avoid physical confrontation
- You can be arrested for blocking traffic or entering private property without permission

**What to bring**:
- ID (or nothing at all — you are not legally required to carry ID in most states, though police may ask for it)
- A written list of contact numbers (attorney, trusted friend) in permanent marker on your arm
- Water
- No alcohol or drugs
- Minimal personal information — lock your phone before leaving home (use a passcode, not fingerprint/face ID)

**What makes a protest strategically effective** (beyond just showing up):
- Clear, specific demand connected to a decision-maker who can actually act on it
- Sufficient numbers to demonstrate a meaningful constituency
- Media coverage — have a prepared statement, a spokesperson, and a visual hook
- Connection to ongoing organizing (a march that ends with nothing happens once; a march that ends with voter registration tables and sign-ups for the next meeting builds capacity)

**Legal observer roles**: National Lawyers Guild trains legal observers who attend demonstrations to document police conduct and provide information to people arrested. This is high-value volunteer work that doesn't require you to be a lawyer. Training information: **nlg.org/observer-materials**

### Action 10: Participate in Your Local Democratic Party (or Build Third-Party Alternatives)

**Why this matters**: Primary elections determine which candidates appear on general election ballots. Primary electorates are dramatically smaller and more ideologically engaged than general electorates. The people who control party infrastructure — precinct committee people, county central committee members, party officers — have outsized influence on candidate selection, platform, and resource allocation.

The Democratic Party in particular has enormous infrastructure (voter file, campaign finance mechanisms, professional staff) that is available to candidates who win the primary. Influencing who controls that infrastructure at the local level is high-leverage work.

**How to participate**:

**Step 1: Find your local party structure**
- Democrats: Your county Democratic Party or state Democratic Party website has information on local meetings and how to get involved
- Republicans: Same structure; county Republican Party websites
- Third parties: Green Party, Libertarian Party, Working Families Party, and others have varying local chapter structures

**Step 2: Attend a meeting**
- County party meetings are typically open to registered members of that party
- Bring specific ideas and questions; generic enthusiasm is less useful than specific expertise or willingness to do specific tasks

**Step 3: Become a precinct committee person or captain**
- Most parties have precinct committee positions that go unfilled
- This is an elected or appointed position within the party structure
- It gives you standing in endorsement votes, platform debates, and officer elections
- Ask how to run at your first party meeting, or contact your county party directly

**Step 4: Work toward party positions if desired**
- County chair, state committee member, and other positions give increasing influence over party resources and direction
- These positions require sustained relationship-building within the party structure

### Action 11: Support and Monitor Local Government

**Public Records Requests (FOIA at the local level)**:

Federal agencies are subject to the Freedom of Information Act. State and local agencies are subject to state open records laws (often called "Sunshine Laws" or "Public Records Acts"). These laws give citizens and journalists the right to request government documents.

**How to file a public records request**:

1. Identify the government agency holding the records you want
2. Find their public records contact: agency website → "FOIA" or "Public Records" or "Transparency"
3. Write a request letter specifying: what documents you want (be specific — "all emails" is less useful than "emails from City Manager regarding the new police contract, January through June 2025"), the date range, and the format you want
4. File by email or mail (some agencies have online portals)
5. Follow up if you receive no response within the agency's specified timeframe (usually 5-10 business days for an acknowledgment)
6. If the agency denies your request, you can appeal or, in some cases, file suit — state attorney general offices often handle complaints about open records law violations

**Resources**: MuckRock.com provides free public records request templates, tracks requests, and helps users navigate the process.

**Attending budget hearings**:
- Your city and county hold public budget hearings, usually in the spring for the following fiscal year
- Budget hearings are often the most important (and least attended) public meetings
- Speakers who connect budget choices to specific community impacts are the most effective

**Advisory boards and commissions**:
- Most cities and counties have planning commissions, budget advisory committees, parks boards, police oversight boards, environmental commissions, and others
- These positions are typically appointed by local elected officials
- Application process: find the list of boards on your city/county website, note vacancies, write a letter of interest to the relevant official's office
- Service is usually modest time commitment (one meeting per month); expertise in the board's subject area is valued

### Action 12: Economic Actions

**Researching where you spend money**:
- **Open Secrets (opensecrets.org)**: Campaign contributions by company and industry
- **2ndVote (2ndvote.com)**: Rates corporations on conservative values (useful for understanding corporate political activity regardless of your politics)
- **Goods Unite Us (goodsuniteus.com)**: Political giving by major brands; subscription app shows which companies contribute to which political causes
- **Buycott (buycott.com)**: App for scanning product barcodes to see corporate political activity

**Active boycotts**: Major organized boycotts are typically organized by national groups; check:
- NAACP Economic Department: **naacp.org/campaigns/economic-program**
- Sleeping Giants (advertiser pressure on extremist media): **slpng.gs**

**Credit unions vs. big banks**:
- Credit unions are member-owned cooperatives rather than shareholder-owned corporations; they typically have no corporate political giving
- To find a credit union: **mycreditunion.gov** or **creditunionlocator.org**
- Transferring your primary banking relationship is a meaningful but manageable project

**ESG and values-aligned investing**:
- ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) investing screens out corporations on specific criteria
- As You Sow (**asyousow.org**): Tools for evaluating investment portfolios' alignment with social values; they file shareholder resolutions
- If you have a 401(k) or IRA, you may be able to choose ESG options; check with your plan administrator

### Action 13: Run for Local Office

**What offices are available**:
- **School board**: Perhaps the highest-impact low-visibility elected office; sets curriculum, hires superintendents, controls billion-dollar budgets in large districts. Low turnout (5-10%) means small organized groups of voters can determine outcomes.
- **City or town council**: Zoning, policing policy, local budget, land use
- **County commission/board of supervisors**: Election administration in many states; property tax; county-level law enforcement and judicial appointments
- **Water district, soil conservation district, community college board**: Low-visibility elections with real authority; often run with minimal campaigns
- **State representative**: Many state legislative races are effectively determined in primaries; competitive races have small electorates
- **Judiciary**: Many states elect trial court judges and some appellate judges

**Is running for office right for you?**
Ask:
- Do I have roots and relationships in this community?
- Am I willing to ask people for money?
- Can I manage the public scrutiny (including of my social media history)?
- Do I have the time for the campaign and, if elected, the office?
- Am I prepared to follow through for the full term if elected?

**How to run**:

**Step 1: Research the office**
- Contact your county election office to get filing requirements, filing deadlines, and any qualifying procedures
- Find out whether the race is partisan or nonpartisan
- Research who currently holds the seat and when it is up

**Step 2: Build your base before filing**
- Have conversations with at least 50 people who you think would support and possibly volunteer for your campaign before filing
- Attend the relevant public bodies' meetings to understand the issues and make yourself known

**Step 3: File**
- Get the filing paperwork from your county election office
- Pay the filing fee (often small; some can be waived with qualifying signatures)
- Comply with campaign finance registration requirements

**Step 4: Build your campaign team**
- You need at minimum: a campaign manager (can be a trusted volunteer); a treasurer (legal requirement for reporting); volunteers to help with voter contact

**Resources for candidates**:
- **Run for Something (runforsomething.net)**: Support for progressive candidates under 40 for down-ballot races; candidate support system
- **Emerge America (emergeamerica.org)**: Training program for Democratic women; application-based; multi-month program
- **She Should Run (sheshouldrun.org)**: Encouragement and resources for women considering running for office
- **New American Leaders (newamericanleaders.org)**: Training for immigrant community members
- **Higher Heights (higherheights.org)**: Supporting Black women in electoral politics

### Action 14: Become a Poll Worker or Election Official

**Why this is critical**: America's elections are administered by a patchwork of approximately 10,000 local election jurisdictions, staffed largely by temporary workers and volunteers. Poll worker shortages are acute — 54% of jurisdictions reported difficulty recruiting poll workers in 2022. Without trained, competent poll workers committed to democratic administration, elections cannot function.

Becoming a poll worker or election official is one of the most direct forms of democratic participation available.

**How to apply**:
1. Find your local election authority: **eac.gov/help-america-vote** has a state-by-state finder
2. Complete the application (usually online through the county election office)
3. Attend required training (typically one day to a half-day before each election)
4. Receive compensation (most jurisdictions pay poll workers; rates vary from $100-$400 per election day)

**What poll workers do**: Set up polling places before voting begins; verify voter eligibility and assist voters in signing in; operate voting equipment; assist voters who need help; close polls and process results; maintain neutrality and professionalism throughout

**More advanced roles**:
- **Election judge or chief judge**: Supervisory role in a polling place; more responsibility, more pay, more training
- **Absentee ballot processing**: Many counties need workers to process mail-in ballots; less visible than polling place work but equally important
- **Election office staff**: Many county election offices hire seasonal staff for major elections; check your county election office's employment listings

### Action 15: Organize Your Workplace

**Know your rights first**:

Under the **National Labor Relations Act (NLRA)**:
- Most private sector employees (with exceptions: agricultural workers, domestic workers, independent contractors, supervisors) have the right to organize and join a union
- You have the right to discuss wages, hours, and working conditions with coworkers
- Retaliation for union organizing activity is illegal (though enforcement has been uneven)
- You can engage in "protected concerted activity" — taking collective action with coworkers on workplace issues — without being in a formal union

**How to start a union organizing campaign**:

1. **Talk to coworkers informally**: Find out who else has concerns; identify trusted people in different departments
2. **Connect with a union**: The Emergency Workplace Organizing Committee (EWOC, ewoc.org) provides free organizing training and support; other national unions (SEIU, UAW, CWA, UFCW) have organizing departments
3. **Build an organizing committee**: Identify leaders across different departments, shifts, and demographics
4. **Majority sign-up**: Once you have majority support, you can either petition the NLRB for an election or (in some cases) request voluntary recognition from your employer
5. **NLRB election**: The National Labor Relations Board administers union elections; you file a petition with signed authorization cards from 30% of the bargaining unit (aim for 65%+ before filing)

**Resources**:
- Emergency Workplace Organizing Committee: **ewoc.org** — Free organizing training and support
- NLRB: **nlrb.gov** — Official information on your rights and the election process
- AFL-CIO: **aflcio.org** — National union federation with organizing resources

**For employees at institutions**: Academics, graduate students, journalists, nurses, and other professional workers have organized successfully with CWA, UAW, AFT, and others.

### Action 16: Start or Lead a Local Organization

**Mutual aid networks**:

Starting a mutual aid network requires minimal bureaucratic structure:
1. Identify a specific need in your neighborhood or community
2. Find three to five committed people to help
3. Create a simple coordination mechanism (a Signal group, a shared spreadsheet, a weekly meeting)
4. Publicize what you offer (flyers, Nextdoor, neighborhood Facebook groups)
5. Deliver on what you promise and build relationships

Resources: **mutualaidhub.org** (directory and resources); Dean Spade, *Mutual Aid: Building Solidarity During This Crisis*

**Formal organization structure options**:
- **Informal (no filing required)**: Works for mutual aid and small local groups; no tax benefits for donors but minimal administrative burden
- **501(c)(3) nonprofit**: Tax-exempt charitable organization; donations are tax-deductible; prohibits partisan electoral activity; requires IRS filing and ongoing compliance
- **501(c)(4) social welfare organization**: Tax-exempt but donations not deductible; can engage in some electoral advocacy (not its primary purpose); allows more political activity than (c)(3)
- **PAC (Political Action Committee)**: Can make candidate contributions and independent expenditures; regulated by FEC (federal) or state agencies
- **State party committee**: If you want to build party infrastructure in your area

For most local organizing, starting informally and incorporating as a 501(c)(4) when you have sufficient activity and funding is the practical path. **Consult a nonprofit attorney** before incorporating; many state bar associations have lawyer referral services.

### Action 19: Financial Support

**How to give strategically**:

The evidence on where money has most impact in politics:
- **Local races**: A $500 contribution to a school board or state legislative candidate is orders of magnitude more valuable than the same amount to a presidential campaign awash in money
- **Infrastructure organizations**: Organizations doing training, voter registration, legal work, and capacity building often have less glamorous fundraising environments than high-profile campaigns but produce more durable change
- **Between cycles**: Organizations need operating funds between election years; that's when donations are less common and more valuable

**Giving vehicles**:
- **Recurring monthly giving**: Predictable income allows organizations to plan; even $20/month is more valuable than one $240/year donation
- **Donor circles and giving collectives**: Groups of donors coordinating their giving to increase collective impact; **Solidaire Network** (solidairenetwork.org) and **Resource Generation** (resourcegeneration.org) do this work
- **Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)**: Allow you to take the tax deduction when you fund the account, then distribute to organizations over time
- **Estate planning**: Including civic organizations in your estate is the largest gift most people can make; consult a planned giving professional

**Finding organizations worth supporting**:
- **Charity Navigator (charitynavigator.org)**: Evaluates nonprofit financial health and accountability
- **GiveWell (givewell.org)**: Rigorous effectiveness research (primarily international development; less comprehensive for domestic political organizations)
- **Movement Voter Project (mvp.vote)**: Identifies high-impact local progressive organizations for donor support

### Action 20: Documenting and Archiving

**Safely documenting government abuse**:

If you witness potential civil rights violations or government misconduct:
1. Know your state's recording law — most states allow recording in public spaces; some "two-party consent" states require consent for audio recording (though courts have generally held that police in public spaces have no expectation of privacy)
2. Start recording as soon as possible; note the time, date, location
3. Don't narrate over the video while recording — let events speak for themselves; you can narrate your observations separately
4. Back up the recording immediately to cloud storage
5. Don't edit or enhance the original footage
6. Report to: ACLU (aclu.org); NAACP; relevant journalists; law enforcement oversight bodies if available

**Legal observer training**:
- National Lawyers Guild trains legal observers: **nlg.org/observer-materials**
- Legal observers document arrests, police conduct, and other activity at demonstrations
- They wear distinctive green hats and are trained to observe without participating

**Archiving public records**:
- The Internet Archive's Wayback Machine: **web.archive.org** → "Save a page" — archives any web page you submit
- Government Data Preservation: The Internet Archive's **archive-it.org** is used by institutions for systematic archiving
- If you identify a government website containing important data that may be at risk of removal, submit it to Wayback Machine and notify digital preservation organizations

**Chain of custody for evidence**:
- If documenting something that may be used in legal proceedings, maintain the original unedited file
- Create a contemporaneous written log of what you observed, when, and under what circumstances
- Don't distribute widely until you have consulted with a lawyer about how to preserve the evidentiary value

## Emergency Protocols

### What to Do When Democratic Norms Are Violated Suddenly

Democratic crises — sudden attacks on courts, press freedom, elections, or civil liberties — require rapid coordinated response.

**Step 1: Get the facts first**
- Verify information through multiple reliable sources before acting or sharing
- Trusted sources for constitutional/legal emergencies: Lawfare Blog (lawfaremedia.org); Just Security (justsecurity.org); Brennan Center (brennancenter.org); Protect Democracy (protectdemocracy.org)
- The first accounts of fast-moving events are often incomplete or inaccurate; wait for confirmation

**Step 2: Contact your representatives immediately**
- Call your U.S. Senators' and Representative's district offices on the same day
- Short, direct: "I'm a constituent calling about [the specific action]. I urge [the senator/representative] to [specific action: issue a statement, introduce legislation, vote against, demand a hearing, etc.]."

**Step 3: Support relevant organizations that are responding**
- ACLU, NAACP, and similar organizations are often the fastest to respond legally; a donation during a crisis is immediately useful
- Indivisible and similar networks coordinate constituent response rapidly

**Step 4: Mobilize your network**
- Contact people in your existing organization or network about the situation
- Coordinate response rather than duplicate effort — find out what is already being organized

**Step 5: Document and preserve**
- Screenshot and archive web pages, executive orders, official statements before they can be changed or deleted

### Rapid Response Resources

- **ACLU emergency response**: aclu.org/legal-support (for individuals needing legal help)
- **National Lawyers Guild legal support**: 212-679-6018
- **National Protest Hotline**: Varies by region; NLG chapters list local hotlines
- **If you are arrested**:
  - Remain silent except to state: "I am invoking my right to remain silent and right to an attorney."
  - Provide your name and ID if required in your state (varies)
  - Your attorney's phone number or the NLG hotline number should be written on your arm
  - You have the right to make at least one phone call after arrest

### Staying Safe While Resisting

**Physical safety at demonstrations**:
- Know the planned exit routes for any demonstration you attend
- Avoid isolated situations; stay with a group
- Wear sturdy, comfortable shoes; dress in neutral colors for lower visibility
- Bring water, medications you might need, a snack
- Have a designated meeting point if separated from your group

**Legal safety for organizers**:
- Consult an attorney before engaging in civil disobedience — understand the specific charges and likely consequences in your jurisdiction
- Organizations doing civil disobedience work typically provide legal support; confirm this before you rely on it
- Know the difference between arrest for civil disobedience (accepted as a political act, which creates a different dynamic with prosecutors) and arrest you want to contest

**Emotional and psychological safety**:
- Activism is emotionally demanding; burnout is real and common
- Build in time for rest, non-political social connection, and self-care — not as a luxury but as a sustainability strategy
- Identify a community of people doing this work who can provide mutual support
- Recognize signs of burnout in yourself and others: loss of motivation, cynicism about whether anything matters, emotional exhaustion
