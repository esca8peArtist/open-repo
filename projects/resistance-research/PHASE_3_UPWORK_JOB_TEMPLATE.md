**Project Overview**

We are seeking a researcher with PhD-level training in constitutional law, political science, or public policy for a substantive research consulting engagement. The project is Phase 3 of a multi-year democratic governance research initiative that has produced research used by policy advocates, academic scholars, and legislative staff working on US democratic reform.

Phase 3 research covers two companion domains:

**Domain K — Federal Judiciary Restructuring and Supreme Court Reform**
A comprehensive analysis of legislative proposals, constitutional feasibility arguments, and international precedent for reforming the US federal judiciary. The deliverable is a 15,000–20,000-word synthesis document plus three standalone distribution materials (500-word executive summary, 1,200-word policy brief, 2,000-word legislative guide). Substantial preliminary research is already complete — you are building on a structured source base and research outline, not starting from zero.

**Domain H — Constitutional Resilience Architecture**
An extension and update of an existing 6,800-word production document analyzing how the US Constitution's structural gaps enable democratic backsliding, and what reform architecture can close those gaps. The extension work covers international case studies (Germany, Ireland, New Zealand), legislative updates, and post-June 2026 litigation and ballot measure developments. Target final document: 10,000–12,000 words integrated.

The two documents are companion pieces designed to be distributed together to the same audience: Senate Judiciary Committee staff, constitutional law scholars, and democracy reform advocacy organizations (Brennan Center for Justice, Fix the Court, American Constitution Society).

**Research Scope by Domain**

**Domain K research zones** (Weeks 1–7):
- Zone K-1: Verify and update legislative inventory (H.R. 1074, SCERT Act, Raskin shadow docket package, Manchin-Welch amendment, cameras bill, Goldman-Booker ethics act) through October 2026 using Congress.gov primary sources
- Zone K-2: Analyze constitutional feasibility of statutory Supreme Court term limits (Brennan Center active/senior justice approach; Article III "good Behaviour" opposing argument; 2021 Presidential Commission minority views)
- Zone K-3: Survey international precedent (Germany Bundesverfassungsgericht 12-year term; Canada mandatory retirement at 75; France, Australia, UK comparative models; 47-state domestic comparison)
- Zone K-4: Produce three standalone distribution materials: executive summary (500 words, for congressional staff), policy brief (1,200 words, for constitutional law scholars), section-by-section legislative guide (2,000 words, for advocacy organizations)

**Domain H extension work** (Weeks 8–13):
- Extension A: Deepen international case studies — Germany's constructive vote of no confidence and Article 21 eternity clause as US reform models; Ireland's Citizens' Assembly as a constitutional deliberation bypass mechanism; New Zealand's Royal Commission electoral reform pathway

- Extension C: Integrate November 2026 ballot measure results (Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, Utah anti-ballot-initiative measures; Kansas judicial selection referendum) into existing constitutional resilience analysis

**Writing Standard**

Phase 3 research documents are hybrid academic-advocacy writing — not law review articles, not op-eds. The target reader is a Senate Judiciary Committee staffer or a constitutional law scholar at ACS who has 30 minutes to read and needs to understand both the evidence base and the actionable conclusion.

Standards: lead with the most important finding (not background context); every section ends with a clear implication; citations are inline hyperlinks where possible; confidence levels stated explicitly where evidence is genuinely uncertain; active voice throughout; technical legal terms defined briefly on first use in each section; no Wikipedia as a primary source.

**Before applying**: Review the writing standard and verify your published work meets it. We will request a writing sample of at least 4,000 words as part of the application review. We check 3–5 citations against primary sources before advancing candidates to interview.
