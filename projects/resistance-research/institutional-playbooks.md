# Sector-Specific Institutional Playbooks
## Democratic Renewal Framework — Eight Constituency Implementation Guides

**May 2026 — Production Ready**

These eight playbooks translate the 35-domain democratic renewal framework into concrete, constituency-specific action plans. Each playbook is self-contained. Each assumes the reader is a real person in the named institutional role who has received or reviewed the proposal and is asking a practical question: what do I do next, what are my constraints, and who do I work with?

The playbooks do not recapitulate framework theory. They start with leverage — what power this constituency actually has — and move through actionable domains, Year 1 to Year 3 sequencing, case studies, constraints, and alliance structures. Read your own playbook first. Use the cross-constituency alliance matrix at the end to identify your primary coordination partners.

**Framework reference**: All domain numbers (e.g., "Domain 6") reference sections of `democratic-renewal-proposal.md`. Wave assignments follow the implementation roadmap in Part IV of that document.

## 1. State Attorneys General Coalitions

**Who this is for**: State attorneys general, chief deputies for civil rights, solicitors general, multistate litigation coordinators, and civil rights deputy AGs in Democratic-coalition states. Also applicable for legal coalition staff at Democracy Docket, ACLU Voting Rights Project, Brennan Center, and Constitutional Accountability Center who work directly with AG offices.

---

### TL;DR (100 words)

You are the single most consequential institutional actor in the first 18 months. The 22-state Democratic AG coalition has filed over 70 lawsuits since January 2025, winning approximately 79% of resolved cases. Your leverage is parens patriae standing, multistate coordination, and federalism theory — none of which require congressional action. Year 1 priorities: complete litigation triage mapping all active cases to the 35-domain framework, build the post-Slaughter rapid-response architecture before the ruling lands, and lock in your 2026 midterm pre-litigation infrastructure. Your critical constraint is the 6-3 Supreme Court shadow docket, which can reverse any favorable injunction within days.

---

### Leverage Analysis

The AG coalition's power rests on four structural advantages that no other constituency can fully replicate.

**Parens patriae standing** allows state AGs to sue on behalf of their state's citizens without identifying individual plaintiffs. This resolves the standing problem that defeats many civil rights challenges before they reach the merits. The Maryland v. Noem model — where AG Brown challenged DOGE data access to Social Security Administration databases on the theory that SSA capacity harm injured Maryland's Medicaid administration — shows how broadly this theory can be applied. Every domain in the framework that involves a cooperative federal-state program creates a potential parens patriae standing theory.

**Litigation infrastructure** is the second advantage. AG offices have staff, expert relationships, filing capability, and established federal court relationships that can be activated within hours of a crisis. Private plaintiffs who are equally harmed take weeks or months to assemble counsel, standing declarations, and a litigation team. AG offices can file TRO motions on the same day an executive action is signed. The AFGE model — filing suit against Schedule F within hours of Trump's inauguration on January 20, 2025 — is the template, but AG offices can move even faster because they do not need to recruit clients.

**Multistate coordination** is the third advantage. When 15 or more state AGs file simultaneously or in close succession, the political cost of a shadow docket stay rises. The Supreme Court is less willing to use an emergency, unreasoned order to reverse an injunction that is simultaneously in effect in a dozen states — the visibility and political cost of that intervention is higher. The 24-state IEEPA tariff coalition, which filed simultaneously in the Court of International Trade and won, demonstrates the power of coordinated multistate pressure.

**Federalism standing theory** is the fourth advantage. State AGs can raise constitutional arguments — anti-commandeering doctrine, cooperative federalism violations, Article I election administration authority — that individual plaintiffs cannot. These structural constitutional claims are harder for the Supreme Court to dismiss on standing grounds, even when they are difficult on the merits.

The structural constraint is real and must be planned around: the Supreme Court's 6-3 conservative majority has demonstrated willingness to reverse favorable lower court injunctions on the shadow docket, often within days and without full briefing. Every litigation strategy must account for the possibility that a favorable TRO or preliminary injunction will be stayed before it can have operational effect.

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: Parens patriae standing for amicus briefs in major judicial independence cases. State AGs in Maryland demonstrated this model when challenging DOGE data access to SSA. When impeachment proceedings are threatened against district judges for issuing adverse rulings, AG offices in states where those judges sit have standing to intervene. Domain 6 also produces the post-Slaughter response architecture discussed in Year 1 sequencing below.

**Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: Clearest and most established standing theory for election interference challenges under Article I, Section 4. States have sovereign authority over their own election administration. Federal interference with state election administration — ICE deployment near polling sites, voter roll purge directives, mail ballot rejection mandates — creates direct state injury. The pre-litigation infrastructure for 2026 midterm interference scenarios should be built in 2025-2026 before any crisis event occurs.

**Domain 23 (Trade and Tariffs)**: The 24-state AG coalition's IEEPA challenge produced the SCOTUS Learning Resources v. Trump ruling (February 20, 2026, 6-3) that struck down IEEPA tariff authority. This is the framework's highest-profile AG victory to date and establishes the model: simultaneous multistate filing plus private plaintiff parallel tracks plus pre-staged academic record equals maximum pressure.

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization)**: AG intervention in federal prosecution matters is limited, but state-court civil rights actions under state statutes against the same underlying conduct are not. When DOJ targets civil society organizations for protected activities, state AGs in states where those organizations operate can bring state civil rights enforcement actions on state law theories. The SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026) is the triggering case; state AGs in Alabama, Georgia, Florida, and coalition states have independent authorities that did not require federal permission to deploy.

**Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse)**: States have standing to challenge federal fund impoundment when it harms state-administered cooperative programs. Medicaid funding, education block grants, environmental cooperative agreements, and housing programs all involve explicit federal-state funding obligations. When the executive branch impounds or redirects congressionally appropriated funds that states depended on for program operation, the state's fiscal injury gives it direct standing.

### Year 1: Constraint-Building (Months 1–12)

The Year 1 objective is completing the litigation infrastructure that allows rapid deployment against the full range of democratic emergencies. This is not reactive — it is pre-positioning.

**Months 1–3: Litigation triage and domain mapping.** The AG coalition's 70+ active cases should be formally mapped to the 35-domain framework. Every case should be assigned a primary domain and, where applicable, secondary cross-domain connections. Each domain should have a designated "lead AG office" responsible for coordinating amicus recruitment, expert declaration networks, and parallel litigation timing for that domain. Massachusetts leads election interference; New York leads financial regulation; Washington leads environmental; California leads immigration; Maryland leads civil service and cooperative federalism. This assignment matrix should be documented in writing and shared with the Democracy Docket and ACLU Voting Rights Project so that civil society coordination can align with AG coalition lead assignments.

**Months 3–6: Post-Slaughter response architecture.** Trump v. Slaughter (expected June 2026 ruling) will overrule or significantly narrow Humphrey's Executor, eliminating or weakening the constitutional protection for independent agency officers. When this ruling lands, the AG coalition needs a response architecture already built. The architecture has three components: (1) state analog legislation — at least 15 states should have pre-drafted bills creating state-law equivalents of NLRB, FTC, and CFPB protections that are not dependent on federal agency independence; (2) state enforcement activation — within 30 days of the ruling, coalition state AGs should activate state antitrust, consumer protection, and labor enforcement authorities to substitute for compromised federal enforcement; (3) constitutional amendment support — within 30 days, a bipartisan amicus letter to Senate Judiciary should be filed supporting the statutory or constitutional response. The 30-day response target means all of this work must be substantially drafted before the ruling is issued.

**Months 6–9: Civil rights litigation infrastructure integration.** The AG coalition should be formally integrated into the standing coordination infrastructure that links Democracy Docket, the ACLU Voting Rights Project, and the Brennan Center. Specifically: for every major civil rights case in these organizations' dockets, an AG office should be identified as potential amicus filer and notified of upcoming briefing deadlines 60 days in advance. This allows AG offices to prepare amicus briefs rather than scrambling to respond after a crisis.

**Months 9–12: 2026 midterm pre-litigation.** The 2026 midterm elections create the highest-urgency election interference scenarios. Venue analysis should be completed for every realistic interference scenario — ICE at polls, voter roll purges, mail ballot rejections, certification disputes — identifying the best circuit for a TRO, the standing theory, and the expert declaration network. TRO templates should be pre-written for each scenario, requiring only factual inserts after a specific crisis event occurs. The Democracy Docket has done significant work on this architecture; the AG coalition's value-add is having the state parens patriae standing component pre-documented.

---

### Year 2: Legislative Advocacy and State-Floor Strategy (Months 12–24)

Post-2026 midterm, the AG coalition's role expands from litigation-only to legislative advocacy. The 2026 midterm results will recalibrate the coalition's composition: if Democrats gain state legislative chambers, new AGs in newly competitive states may join; if the Senate composition shifts, the AG coalition's legislative advocacy voice becomes more influential.

The primary Year 2 legislative objective is building a state-law floor strategy — a network of state constitutional and statutory protections that survive federal rollback. This means: state civil service protections that are not dependent on federal Schedule F/Career reversal (Domain 2); state voting access protections that exceed federal minimums (Domain 1); state consumer protection and antitrust enforcement that substitutes for compromised federal enforcement (Domain 23). Each coalition AG should assess what their state legislature can enact without federal prerequisite and prioritize those state-floor provisions.

The AG coalition also has a legislative advocacy role in supporting federal statutory fixes for the domains where post-Slaughter damage is most severe. The Saving the Civil Service Act, the NLRB Independence Act, and the PRESS Act all benefit from formal AG coalition endorsement and targeted advocacy with Senate Judiciary Committee staff. AG offices with alumni in Senate committee positions should be the coordination points.

---

### Year 3+: Proactive Constitutional Architecture

By Year 3, the objective shifts from defensive constraint (stopping the damage) to proactive structural reform. State AGs are essential to two Year 3 tasks.

**Interstate compact architecture.** The AG coalition's legal drafting capacity should be deployed to develop interstate compacts that institutionalize the cooperative enforcement mechanisms built in Years 1 and 2. A multistate election security compact, a multistate consumer protection enforcement compact, and a multistate environmental enforcement compact would each create durable, federally independent enforcement capacity that persists regardless of which party holds federal executive power.

**Constitutional amendment support.** The four most likely constitutional amendments in a reform scenario — proportional representation implementation, Senate structural reform, Supreme Court term limits, and civil service constitutional protection — each require 34 states to ratify. State AGs are the legal drafting resource for the state ratification legislation. Pre-drafting the ratification legislation for each amendment, with state-specific constitutional analysis, is work AG offices can begin in Year 3.

### Case Study: The IEEPA Tariff Coalition (2025–2026)

The 24-state AG challenge to IEEPA tariffs is the definitive model for Phase 3 AG deployment. The case demonstrates every element of a well-executed AG coalition action.

The architecture had three simultaneous tracks. First, the multistate AG coalition filed simultaneously in the Court of International Trade, creating immediate multi-circuit pressure. Second, private plaintiffs (importers with concrete economic injury) filed parallel tracks in the same courts, providing additional standing and factual record. Third, academic record was pre-staged before argument: law school clinics and trade law scholars had published analyses of IEEPA's scope limitations before the cases were filed, giving the courts an established intellectual framework for the constitutional holding.

The result — SCOTUS Learning Resources v. Trump, 6-3, striking down IEEPA tariff authority on February 20, 2026 — validated the theory and produced a durable precedent. The CIT subsequently ordered tariff refunds. This is the model: simultaneous multistate filing, private plaintiff parallel tracks, academic record pre-staged. Every major AG coalition action in the 35-domain framework should be built to this architecture.

The lesson for future actions: the academic pre-staging is the component most frequently omitted and most valuable. Courts cite law review articles in their opinions. When the academic record does not exist, courts must develop the doctrine from first principles, which takes longer and produces less reliable outcomes. Law school clinic partnerships (see Playbook 6) exist precisely to produce this pre-staged record.

### Constraints

**Political pressure in competitive states.** Democratic AGs in states where they face competitive reelection in 2026 or 2028 must balance litigation aggression with electoral vulnerability. Not every AG can join every action. The coalition's coordination function should identify which AGs are electorally constrained and which can carry the load for a given action — electorally safe AGs should take the lead roles; competitive AGs should contribute amicus briefs rather than lead filings. Do not let electoral constraint prevent the coalition from acting; let it inform who leads.

**Shadow docket vulnerability.** Every favorable injunction is vulnerable to an immediate stay by the 6-3 Court. This constraint does not mean litigation is futile — a TRO that buys 14 days of protection during an election can determine the outcome even if the Supreme Court eventually stays it. The constraint means AGs should never rely on a single injunction as a durable solution; every litigation strategy should have a next step planned for the shadow docket reversal scenario.

**Jurisdictional limits on federal prosecution intervention.** Federal prosecution matters have limited AG intervention authority. State AGs cannot block federal grand jury subpoenas, cannot file motions in federal criminal cases as of right, and cannot intervene in ongoing federal investigations. The available tools — state civil rights actions on parallel state law theories, amicus briefs on constitutional questions in the criminal case, state bar referrals for prosecutorial misconduct — are real but indirect. The AG coalition should not overstate its capacity to directly defend against prosecutorial weaponization; its role is amplification and parallel pressure, not primary defense.

**Coordination costs.** Managing 22 AG offices with distinct staff, political constraints, and institutional cultures requires sustained investment in coordination infrastructure. Without a formal coordination structure — identified point-of-contact at each office, secure communication channel, lead-state assignment matrix — the coalition will fragment under crisis pressure. This infrastructure must be built before the next crisis, not after.

### Natural Allies

State legislators (provide state-law floor for AG enforcement), civil service unions (provide factual record of institutional harm that supports AG standing), law school clinics (provide academic pre-staging for every major AG filing), civil rights coalitions (provide parallel advocacy pressure that raises political cost of shadow docket reversal), religious coalitions (provide constituent mobilization that makes electoral cost of AG coalition victories visible).

---

### Conflicts and How to Navigate Them

**With federal courts on universal injunctions.** Post-Trump v. CASA, universal injunctions that protect non-parties may be limited. AGs who prefer state-court jurisdiction for their parens patriae claims may resist framework proposals that rely on broad federal court injunctions. Navigation: frame the complementary tools — state parens patriae standing in federal court, APA vacatur as an alternative to injunctive relief, class action fortification — as non-competing with state-court jurisdiction expansion. AGs can use all of these tools simultaneously.

**With trade unions on tariff policy.** Building trades unions historically supported protective tariffs; civil service unions administer tariff enforcement programs. When AGs challenge IEEPA tariff authority, they may be in nominal conflict with union positions. Navigation: the IEEPA challenge is an authority question (does the president have statutory power to impose these tariffs?), not a trade policy question (should tariffs exist?). AGs and unions can maintain distinct positions on trade policy while agreeing that tariff imposition without clear statutory authority violates separation of powers.

**Measurement milestones (Year 1)**:
- 70+ active cases mapped to domain framework: Months 1–3
- Post-Slaughter response architecture pre-drafted: Months 3–6
- Lead-state assignment matrix documented and shared with Democracy Docket: Months 1–3
- 2026 midterm TRO templates pre-written for five interference scenarios: Months 9–12
- At least one bipartisan AG amicus letter filed in response to post-Slaughter damage: Month 6+

## 2. Civil Service Unions — AFGE, NTEU, AFSCME

**Who this is for**: AFGE and NTEU political directors, legislative affairs staff, litigation coordinators, and local chapter leadership. Also applicable for AFSCME (state and local government workers) legislative affairs staff and AFL-CIO federal employee policy staff.

---

### TL;DR (100 words)

You are the institutional backbone of Wave 1 resistance. AFGE's inauguration-night litigation against Schedule F established the template: pre-draft the legal theory, insert facts when the executive action drops, file within hours. Your leverage is collective action capacity, congressional lobbying infrastructure, and the 2026 electoral mobilization program. Your primary domains are Domain 2 (civil service protection) and Domain 17 (labor rights). The biggest constraint is that you are fighting on two fronts simultaneously: defending your members' employment and defending the institutional structures that protect collective bargaining itself. Resource allocation between these two requires explicit prioritization, not improvisation.

---

### Leverage Analysis

Civil service unions occupy a distinctive position among the eight constituencies. Unlike career civil servants themselves, who have leverage through institutional knowledge and statutory protections but cannot engage in collective political action, unions combine collective action capacity, electoral mobilization infrastructure, congressional lobbying relationships, and litigation capability into a single institutional actor.

AFGE represents 1.3 million federal workers. NTEU represents 150,000+ federal employees across 31 agencies. AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local government workers provide a state-level analog to the federal organizing structure. The AFL-CIO readmission of SEIU in 2025 consolidated labor movement coordination, meaning civil service union priorities can be elevated to the full federation's political infrastructure in a way that was not possible before.

The unions' primary institutional leverage in the current crisis is standing: AFGE filed the first lawsuit against Schedule F on inauguration night 2025. This litigation posture — pre-drafting legal theories based on anticipated executive actions — is a model the framework adopts for all constituencies. The Second Amended Complaint in the AFGE-AFSCME-AFL-CIO consolidated case (March 4, 2026) demonstrates sustained litigation capacity over more than a year of escalating executive pressure.

The unions also have the political infrastructure that other legal-focused constituencies lack. AFL-CIO's 2022 voter mobilization reached 8 million households in swing states. The 2026 program targets an unprecedented ground game with civil service protection as a central message — not just labor rights, but the institutional stability that democratic governance depends on.

---

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 2 (Civil Service and Institutional Integrity)**: Principal plaintiffs in Schedule Policy/Career litigation. The Saving the Civil Service Act (H.R. 492/S. 134) is the primary legislative vehicle, with union sponsorship and documented bipartisan congressional support. The MSPB Reconstruction Act — converting the Merit Systems Protection Board to an independent Article I court with supermajority confirmation — is the structural reform that makes Domain 2 durable rather than reversible.

**Domain 17 (Labor Rights)**: PRO Act restoration, sectoral bargaining pilots, anti-union-busting statutory reform. Domain 17 is the unions' self-interest domain; it also connects to the broader democratic argument because union density is one of the strongest documented predictors of democratic resilience in comparative political science research.

**Domain 26 (Government Accountability)**: The Inspector General system is a direct union interest. IG offices investigate civil service abuses, provide independent reporting on agency dysfunction, and create the evidentiary record that supports congressional oversight. The IG firing pattern since 2025 directly harms union members' ability to report misconduct and seek protection.

**Domain 6 (Judicial Independence)**: The NLRB quorum loss — a direct consequence of post-Slaughter judicial capture — stopped union certification elections and fell 30% in 2025. Restoring NLRB independence is simultaneously a union organizing capacity issue and a Domain 6 judicial independence issue.

**Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse)**: DOGE fund impoundment directly affected union-member jobs. AFGE members in agencies that lost funding through unauthorized impoundment have standing theory for Domain 34 litigation. The union can be a plaintiff or amicus in ICA enforcement actions.

### Year 1: Litigation Consolidation and Electoral Integration (Months 1–12)

**Months 1–3: Litigation consolidation.** AFGE, NTEU, and AFSCME should ensure their active Schedule Policy/Career cases are coordinated with consistent legal arguments — conflicting theories across cases create precedential problems. Assign a coordination attorney from AFL-CIO's legal staff to maintain a joint legal brief archive, ensure all co-plaintiffs are working from the same doctrinal framework, and flag any case where a procedural development requires joint strategy consultation. The goal is a coordinated legal narrative across all civil service cases, not independent litigation siloed by union.

**Months 3–6: Congressional testimony mobilization.** AFGE's Annual Legislative Conference typically draws 600–800 members for congressional visits. Reframe the 2026 conference around the 35-domain framework with member case studies matching specific domain violations: Schedule Policy/Career reclassification (Domain 2), DOGE fund impoundment affecting agency operations (Domain 34), NLRB quorum loss affecting collective bargaining rights (Domain 17). Ten to fifteen individual member case studies — not policy arguments, but specific people affected by specific actions — are more effective in congressional testimony than aggregate statistics.

**Months 6–9: State affiliate mobilization.** AFSCME's 1.6 million state and local government workers are in every congressional district. When federal agency capacity is degraded, state agencies absorb the consequences. State AFSCME leadership should communicate the Domain 9 (federalism) analysis to their members: when federal environmental enforcement capacity drops, state environmental agencies bear the litigation load; when federal Medicaid administration capacity drops, state Medicaid offices absorb the administrative burden. This frames AFSCME members' self-interest as connected to federal institutional capacity, motivating state legislative advocacy on Domain 2 (state civil service protections) and Domain 9 (cooperative federalism defense).

**Months 9–12: 2026 electoral integration.** AFL-CIO's 8-million-household mobilization program should explicitly frame civil service protection as the 2026 midterm message. The electoral argument: every domain in the framework — healthcare, environmental protection, consumer protection, immigration due process — depends on federal agencies staffed by merit-based career civil servants rather than political loyalty hires. The civil service is not a bureaucratic abstraction; it is the implementation capacity for every program voters depend on. This message has cross-partisan appeal that union GOTV programs should test in swing-district households.

---

### Year 2: Legislative Priority and MSPB Reconstruction (Months 12–24)

Post-midterm, the unions' legislative agenda requires explicit prioritization among competing priorities. The proposed priority order: (1) Saving the Civil Service Act — reversal of Schedule Policy/Career, the most direct damage to union members; (2) NLRB Independence Act — NLRB quorum restoration and structural protection; (3) PRO Act — broader labor rights restoration.

The prioritization rationale: Saving the Civil Service Act has the broadest bipartisan appeal because it addresses institutional stability rather than purely union organizing rights; it should lead. NLRB independence is the structural foundation for labor rights enforcement; without it, PRO Act gains are unenforceable. PRO Act is the unions' strongest self-interest priority but faces the most partisan resistance; it is third in line, not abandoned.

The MSPB Reconstruction Act — converting MSPB to an independent Article I court — is a Year 2 legislative objective that should be introduced alongside the Saving the Civil Service Act. MSPB has been without quorum since May 2025, creating a 2,100% case backlog. Conversion to an Article I court with supermajority confirmation and guaranteed quorum provisions makes MSPB structurally resistant to the removal-and-vacancy strategy used to neutralize it.

---

### Year 3+: Constitutional Amendment Campaign

The constitutional protection of civil service merit principles — prohibiting the president from creating political-loyalty hiring systems through executive order alone — requires a constitutional amendment in the long term. Statutory protection can be reversed with the next administration; constitutional protection cannot.

Civil service unions provide the organizational infrastructure for the 34-state ratification campaign. AFGE's 1,200+ local chapters, AFSCME's state federations, and AFL-CIO's state bodies collectively have presence in every state that would be needed for ratification. The Year 3 task is converting that presence into an amendment ratification infrastructure: identifying state legislative champions in each state, pre-drafting ratification legislation, and building the public education campaign that moves civil service protection from a policy abstraction to a constitutional value with popular support.

### Case Study: AFGE Inauguration-Night Litigation (January 20, 2025)

AFGE filed suit against Schedule F's reinstatement within hours of Trump's inauguration. This was not improvised. Legal theory had been pre-drafted based on the anticipated executive order text; the critical factual details were inserted after the EO was signed; the filing was complete within the inaugural day.

The operational lesson: pre-staged litigation means having a shelf of pre-written briefs for anticipated executive actions, each requiring only factual insertions to file. This applies to every anticipated domain escalation. If the administration is expected to reclassify additional civil service positions, the legal theory should already be written. If the MSPB is expected to lose additional quorum members, the emergency injunction motion should be pre-drafted.

The second lesson is less obvious: filing fast matters even when you expect to lose at the Supreme Court. An early filing creates a litigation record, signals that institutional actors are monitoring and responding, raises the political cost of each escalation, and sometimes produces favorable district court rulings that impose real operational constraints on the administration even after a Supreme Court stay. AFGE's litigation did not stop Schedule Policy/Career; but it imposed documentary, procedural, and political costs that mattered.

---

### Constraints

**Post-Slaughter legal environment.** MSPB without quorum means civil service appeals have nowhere to go; fired employees have no administrative remedy while litigation proceeds. The union's litigation is defensive — its primary function is creating a record and imposing costs, not providing immediate relief to individual members. Managing member expectations about what litigation can deliver in this environment is essential.

**Resource depletion.** Federal workforce union membership has declined significantly since 2024 as members leave or are forced out. Dues revenue is declining in proportion. The unions are fighting on two fronts — defending existing members and defending the institutional structures that allow organizing — with diminishing resources. Cross-union cost-sharing arrangements for the civil-service-specific litigation should be formalized so no single union bears the full litigation cost.

**Cross-union coordination on trade policy.** Building trades unions historically supported protective tariffs; civil service trade enforcement staff (Customs, USTR, Commerce) now administer those same tariffs while the AG coalition is challenging their legal authority. Tactical separation is the solution: AGs challenge tariff authority, not the workers who administer them; unions maintain a no-targeting agreement ensuring that litigation challenging executive authority does not characterize federal workers as adversaries.

**Measurement milestones (Year 1)**:
- Joint civil service litigation coordination attorney designated: Month 1
- Congressional testimony with 10+ individual member case studies delivered: Months 3–6
- AFL-CIO 2026 GOTV program includes civil service protection as top-three message: Months 6–9
- Saving the Civil Service Act co-sponsor count reaches 40+ House members: Months 9–12

## 3. Labor Unions — AFL-CIO, SEIU, Building Trades

**Who this is for**: AFL-CIO political staff and state federation leadership, SEIU legislative and political directors, building trades government affairs staff, and state labor council directors in competitive states.

---

### TL;DR (100 words)

You are the largest organized constituency in the coalition. AFL-CIO's reunification with SEIU in 2025 means 12.5 million federation members plus 3.8 million SEIU members can be mobilized under a common political framework for the first time since 2005. Your electoral leverage is structural: union households represent 20% of the electorate in swing states. The constraint is that your own organizing rights are under attack — NLRB quorum loss stopped certification elections 30% in 2025. You are simultaneously a constituency and a target. Your Year 1 objective is integrating the 35-domain framework into the 2026 GOTV infrastructure and identifying one bipartisan legislative win to demonstrate cross-aisle capacity.

---

### Leverage Analysis

Organized labor's structural leverage in the American political system rests on three foundations that have been weakened but not eliminated: electoral mobilization capacity, legislative lobbying relationships, and direct member economic power.

Electoral mobilization is the strongest current lever. AFL-CIO's 2022 voter mobilization reached 8 million voters in swing states; the 2026 program targets a larger ground game in more targeted geographies. In the seven states with the most competitive Senate races (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Nevada, Arizona, Georgia, Montana), union household voting preference shifts the outcome when mobilization is effective. This is structural: union households are not uniformly Democratic, but when union households receive sustained information about labor rights, civil service protection, and economic policy, their Democratic preference increases by 8–12 percentage points compared to non-contacted union households (AFL-CIO internal mobilization research, cited in framework Section 4.2).

Legislative lobbying relationships remain significant despite the hostile congressional environment. AFL-CIO has relationships with Republican members — primarily from manufacturing and building trades districts — that create occasional bipartisan openings. The Protect America's Workforce Act (bipartisan House majority, first legislation directly overturning a Trump executive order) demonstrated this capacity. Identifying the next bipartisan legislative vehicle and mobilizing the full federation behind it is the Year 1 strategic task.

AFL-CIO's readmission of SEIU in 2025 consolidated labor for the first time since the 2005 split. This matters for the 35-domain framework specifically because it allows domain lead responsibility to be assigned across the full federation without inter-federation coordination problems. AFL-CIO can assign Domain 17 (labor rights) to AFL-CIO's legislative staff, Domain 11 (healthcare) to SEIU (which represents healthcare workers), and Domain 3 (economic justice) to a joint task force — without the coordination overhead that would have been required when SEIU was outside the federation.

---

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 17 (Labor Rights)**: PRO Act restoration, minimum wage legislation, anti-union-busting statutory reform. This is labor's core self-interest domain and the natural lead. But Domain 17 should not be the only domain in labor's portfolio, because treating union interests as the sole driver reduces the coalition's cross-constituency appeal.

**Domain 3 (Economic Justice and Worker Power)**: Sectoral bargaining pilots, worker cooperative expansion, CDFI sector development. Domain 3 is the affirmative economic vision that goes beyond defending existing rights. Sectoral bargaining — negotiating wage and working conditions at the industry level rather than firm by firm — is the international model that explains why German manufacturing unions are stronger than American ones despite similar union density histories. The 2026 legislative program should include sectoral bargaining pilots in willing states.

**Domain 11 (Healthcare Access)**: OBBBA Medicaid cuts fall disproportionately on low-income workers who are union members. SEIU represents 1.1 million healthcare workers who simultaneously administer and depend on Medicaid. This creates a distinctive constituency argument: the people who staff the healthcare system are also the people losing coverage. SEIU's healthcare policy expertise should anchor the federation's Domain 11 advocacy.

**Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: AFL-CIO Resolution 14 commits the federation to voting rights as a core labor issue. SAVE Act documentary requirements would disenfranchise union households with non-standard documentation of citizenship. The federation's GOTV program and voting rights advocacy are naturally linked; Domain 1 should be part of the integrated message.

**Domain 15 (Environmental Justice)**: This is the domain where labor's internal coalition is most fragile. Building trades unions historically supported fossil fuel employment and opposed climate regulation that threatened construction jobs in extraction industries. Just transition provisions — explicit guarantee of green construction employment for displaced fossil fuel workers, apprenticeship pipeline from extraction to renewable construction, wage parity requirements in Domain 12 infrastructure programs — are the bridging mechanism. Without explicit just transition provisions, Domain 15 advocacy fractures the building trades coalition. With them, it creates the broadest possible labor support for climate infrastructure investment.

### Year 1: Framework Integration and Electoral Infrastructure (Months 1–12)

### Leverage Analysis

Religious institutions provide what no other constituency can replicate: trusted, non-partisan messenger capacity at the community level, combined with physical infrastructure for civic organizing that does not depend on any digital platform.

The 380,000 congregations with 70 million weekly attendees represent the largest voluntary meeting infrastructure in the United States. The administration's Religious Liberty Commission (challenged by Interfaith Alliance in April 2026) represents the primary strategic threat to religious coalition organizing: an attempt to monopolize "religious liberty" as a framework that protects only conservative evangelical and traditional Catholic positions while excluding the majority of faith traditions. Any playbook must explicitly contest this frame. Religious liberty has historically protected the sanctuary movement, the Catholic Worker movement, Jewish immigration advocacy, and Muslim civil rights organizing — all of which are directly implicated in the current democratic crisis.

### Case Study: Civil Rights Movement — Black Church Infrastructure (1955–1965)

The Civil Rights Movement's organizational success rested on five things the Black church network provided: physical infrastructure (meeting spaces for mass meetings, strategy sessions, and training), trusted messenger capacity (pastors as credible voices who could mobilize their congregations), financial sustainability (collections, denominational support, foundation grants), legal coordination (connecting the movement to lawyers like Thurgood Marshall and Constance Baker Motley), and moral legitimacy (framing the movement's demands in terms that claimed the moral high ground from segregation defenders).

The current resistance coalition needs the same five elements. Faith communities can provide the first two (physical infrastructure and trusted messenger capacity) without additional capacity-building. Financial sustainability requires denominations to make explicit institutional commitments. Legal coordination requires faith coalition organizations to maintain relationships with the AG coalition, law school clinics, and civil rights legal organizations (see Alliance Clusters in Section 9). Moral legitimacy requires faith leaders to name what is happening in explicit moral terms — not "policy concern" but "injustice" — which is a cultural and theological choice that each denomination must make for itself.

### Constraints

**Internal political diversity.** The religious coalition spans political traditions that share some values and diverge on others. Evangelical and conservative Catholic constituencies may share commitments to immigrant dignity and community care while diverging from mainline Protestant and Jewish positions on reproductive rights, LGBTQ protections, and church-state separation. Managing this diversity requires explicit domain-specific composition: the coalition can be broader on immigration (Domain 16), healthcare (Domain 11), and racial justice (Domain 22) than it can be on reproductive rights (Domain 7g). Allow the coalition composition to vary by domain rather than requiring every organization to endorse every position.

**Administration frame capture.** The Religious Liberty Commission is attempting to narrow religious liberty to a framework that protects only institutions refusing services to LGBTQ individuals. This is a deliberate frame-capture strategy that must be contested directly: religious liberty in American history has been invoked by sanctuary churches, civil rights marches, Quaker conscientious objectors, Jewish immigration advocates, Muslim civil rights organizations, and Native American religious freedom claimants. The frame belongs to all of these traditions, not to the subset the commission is attempting to represent.

**Capacity concentration.** The 380,000 congregations are powerful in aggregate but unevenly distributed in organizing capacity. Urban Black churches and mainline Protestant congregations in large cities have professional staff, established civic engagement programs, and leadership with political experience. Rural evangelical churches and smaller immigrant congregation communities have deep community trust but less formal organizing capacity. Playbook strategies should distinguish between constituencies that can mobilize immediately and those that require capacity-building investment before they can deploy.

### Year 3+: Structural Information Environment Reform

### Case Study: CPB Defunding and Rural Public Media Coalition (2025–2026)

CPB defunding eliminated federal support for 1,500+ stations. The aftermath revealed an unexpected political dynamic: the stations most dependent on CPB funding were not NPR flagships in urban progressive markets. They were rural public radio stations in Wyoming, Montana, South Dakota, and other Republican-represented states where public radio is often the only local broadcast news source. Republican members of Congress from those districts began receiving constituent complaints about the loss of local weather reporting, agricultural market data, and emergency broadcast capacity that their communities had relied on.

The template for advocacy: identify the specific CPB-dependent services that rural Republican constituents rely on; connect the incumbent Republican member to the specific stations in their district; frame the ask as restoring a community service, not defending public media as an institution. This is not cynical — it is constituency responsiveness. The stations that lost CPB funding serve real communities with real needs, and those communities have congressional representation that can be made responsive to them.

The lesson for Domain 8 advocacy: media reform legislation that is framed as protecting progressive journalism outlets will fail in divided government. Media reform legislation framed as restoring community information infrastructure — weather, emergencies, agricultural markets, local government coverage — can achieve bipartisan support.

## 6. Law School Clinics

**Who this is for**: Clinical program directors, supervising attorneys, and administrative deans at the Harvard Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, NYU Constitutional Litigation Clinic, Yale Rule of Law Clinic, Georgetown Institute for Constitutional Advocacy and Protection, and equivalent programs at regional law schools. ### TL;DR (100 words)

You are connective tissue: linking the AG coalition's litigation needs to pre-staged academic records; connecting empirical research to brief-quality analysis; producing the next generation of democratic defense attorneys while defending democracy now. Your unique institutional advantage is that accepting clients who cannot afford representation is your purpose, not a cost. Federal agency and civil rights organization conflicts that prevent commercial firms from taking cases are not your conflicts. Year 1 priority: the post-Slaughter rapid-response architecture. ### Leverage Analysis

Law school clinics' leverage rests on a structural advantage that no other litigation institution has: accepting cases for public interest reasons is their institutional purpose, not a deviation from it. Commercial law firms can do pro bono work, but accepting a case that conflicts with a paying client, creates reputational risk with government clients, or produces no revenue is a sacrifice. For clinics, accepting precisely these cases is the mission.

This means clinics can take on SPLC defense amicus work (Domain 29), civil service union support litigation (Domain 2), and environmental consent decree enforcement (Domain 15) without the institutional friction that affects commercial firms. More importantly, clinics can accept the cases that create important precedents but are too resource-intensive for individual advocacy organizations to litigate alone.

The second source of leverage is academic record production. Clinics can write law review articles, working papers, and amicus briefs simultaneously with litigation — the same faculty member supervising litigation can produce scholarship on the same legal questions. This dual function is unavailable to practitioners who must maintain client confidentiality and cannot publish their litigation theories. Pre-staged academic records — published analyses that establish the doctrinal framework courts will use before the case is argued — are the most underappreciated element of strategic litigation success.

The third source of leverage is attorney pipeline. Every clinic student who litigates a democratic defense case under faculty supervision becomes a democratic defense attorney. The Yale, Harvard, and NYU clinics have alumni throughout the federal judiciary's law clerk pipeline, at major advocacy organizations, in state AG offices, and in public interest law firms. Building the next generation of democratic defense attorneys is itself a contribution to the framework's long-term objectives.

The legal theory — beneficiaries of government programs are entitled to know the basis for automated denial decisions — is a tractable APA due process argument.

**Domain 2 (Civil Service)**: The AFGE Second Amended Complaint (March 4, 2026) needs ongoing academic record support. Law review analysis of Schedule Policy/Career under post-Loper Bright administrative law doctrine — specifically how courts should analyze presidential reclassification authority without Chevron deference — fills a genuine evidentiary gap that district courts in civil service cases are currently resolving without academic guidance.

---

### Year 1: Post-Slaughter Rapid-Response Architecture

The most urgent Year 1 task for law school clinics is pre-staging the post-Slaughter response before the ruling lands.

Trump v. Slaughter will produce a ruling — either full overruling of Humphrey's Executor, functional narrowing that preserves the doctrine in form while gutting it in practice, or prospective-only application — in June 2026. Each outcome requires different doctrinal analysis on different timelines. The clinic rapid-response model, demonstrated after Loper Bright (June 28, 2024), is instructive: Harvard produced four analyses within 72 hours of the ruling. The model requires pre-assigned faculty teams per expected outcome; pre-written doctrinal framework with factual gaps to fill after the ruling; identified publication venue (Just Security, Lawfare, Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale Law Journal Online); and 72-hour publication commitment.

This same architecture should be applied to every major expected SCOTUS ruling: Watson v. RNC (mail ballot grace periods), any Domain 35 cert grants from the October 2026 term, and any shadow docket orders with major doctrinal implications.

The SPLC amicus coalition is the parallel Year 1 task. Clinics at 10–15 major law schools should produce coordinated amicus filings in the SPLC criminal case — not identical briefs, but a coordinated academic record that addresses the case's distinct legal questions. Harvard's amicus should focus on the bank fraud theory's contradiction of Thompson v. United States. Yale's amicus should address the selective prosecution doctrine. NYU's amicus should analyze the First Amendment association rights implications. Georgetown's amicus should address the prosecutorial ethics rules violations. This coordination produces a comprehensive academic record without any one clinic bearing the entire burden.

---

### Year 2: Legislative Record-Building

Clinic teams should produce legislative findings drafts for the three priority statutory bills: Saving the Civil Service Act (Domain 2), NLRB Independence Act (Domain 6/17), and PRESS Act (Domain 7/8). Legislative findings — the formal statement of congressional purpose and factual basis embedded in legislation — determine whether courts give the statute's objectives deference when it is challenged. Clinic-quality legislative findings, with peer-reviewed citations and empirical analysis, are significantly stronger than findings drafted by congressional staff under time pressure.

The model: identify the lead Senate sponsor's staff; offer to draft legislative findings for the domain-relevant provisions; provide citations that will be useful in the committee hearing record and the ultimate court challenge. This is not partisan advocacy — it is providing expert legal drafting assistance to a legislative process, which is an established law school function.

---

### Year 3+: Constitutional Amendment Academic Architecture

The four most plausible constitutional amendments — proportional representation implementation enabling legislation, Senate structural reform, Supreme Court term limits, and civil service constitutional protection — each require academic pre-staging before the amendment campaign can build political momentum. The pre-staging work: historical precedent analysis (how have similar reforms been implemented in comparative constitutional systems?), constitutional text analysis (what language achieves the reform's objectives without creating new problems?), and ratification strategy analysis (what is the political pathway through 34 states for each amendment?).

Assigning each amendment to a lead law school center — with faculty lead responsible for producing the academic record and coordinating the network of constitutional law scholars who will engage with the question — is Year 3 planning work that should begin in Year 2. The goal is that when the political conditions for amendment ratification exist, the academic record is already established and the legal community has already worked through the constitutional design questions.

---

### Case Study: Harvard Law School Post-Loper Bright Rapid-Response (June 2024)

After Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (June 28, 2024), eliminating Chevron deference, Harvard Law produced four analyses within 72 hours. The rapid-response was possible because the school had pre-assigned faculty teams to the expected ruling categories, pre-written the doctrinal framework, and identified publication venues in advance. The analyses were cited in briefs within weeks of publication.

The model applies to every major expected ruling. The key operational element is pre-assignment: knowing in advance which faculty member is responsible for which ruling scenario, so that after the ruling drops, the analysis flows from a pre-established process rather than starting from scratch. For June 2026, every major law school should have pre-assigned teams for Slaughter, Watson, and any shadow docket order on election administration.

The efficiency gain from pre-assignment is not just speed — it is quality. Pre-written frameworks allow the post-ruling analysis to focus on the actual holding's specific language rather than rebuilding the doctrinal background from scratch. The best rapid-response analyses are the ones where 70% of the work was completed before the ruling landed.

---

### Constraints

**Pressure on clinics from federal funding leverage.** Harvard's clinic is under direct pressure through the $2.2B funding freeze. NYU, Yale, and Georgetown clinics are at risk of similar pressure if they accept clients adverse to federal interests. The institutional response — documented in the Domain 27 playbook — is to apply the four-track threat audit (funding leverage, DEI prohibition self-censorship, visa revocations, accreditation weaponization) to the clinic program specifically and maintain explicit faculty commitment to client representation independence.

**Supervision ratio limits output.** Clinical programs are constrained by the number of cases faculty can supervise while maintaining educational quality. The clinics' amicus brief function — producing brief-quality academic analysis that other litigators incorporate — is a force multiplier that does not require case supervision and should be expanded as a complement to direct litigation.

**Publication timeline mismatch.** Standard law review publication takes 12–24 months; crisis timelines operate in days and weeks. Just Security, Lawfare, and law review online companions all publish on crisis timelines. Clinic faculty should prioritize these venues for framework-relevant research and reserve traditional law review publication for the comprehensive scholarship that develops after the crisis response.

**Measurement milestones (Year 1)**:
- Post-Slaughter three-scenario analysis templates drafted (pre-ruling): Month 5 (before June 2026)
- SPLC amicus coalition assembled with 8+ law school clinics: Month 3
- Domain 6 shadow docket empirical project assigned and underway: Month 3
- Legislative findings draft submitted for Saving the Civil Service Act: Months 6–9

## 7. State Legislators

**Who this is for**: Democratic caucus policy directors, committee counsel, legislative directors, and bill-drafting staff in states with Democratic chambers, competitive chambers, or meaningful bipartisan blocs. Also applicable for state caucus communications directors and legislative liaisons at advocacy organizations working to move bills in state legislatures.

---

### TL;DR (100 words)

You are both Wave 1 actors (passing state civil service protections and voting access legislation that does not depend on federal action) and Wave 3 actors (ratifying constitutional amendments through 34 states). The 2026 midterms determine your Wave 3 capacity: 26 Secretary of State races and three state supreme court seats in 2026 will determine whether 2030 redistricting is controlled by reform coalitions or autocratization mechanisms. Year 1 priority: use the Minnesota omnibus model to pre-draft Wave 1 legislation for every state where a 2026 chamber gain or trifecta is achievable. Do not wait for the election to start drafting.

---

### Leverage Analysis

State legislators have leverage at two distinct time horizons that require completely different strategies.

In the immediate term — Waves 1 and 2 — state legislators can enact state-law floors for domains where federal rollback is underway. State civil service protections (Domain 2) that are not dependent on federal Schedule Policy/Career reversal; state voting access protections (Domain 1) that exceed federal minimums; state consumer protection and antitrust enforcement (Domain 20) that operates independently of federal agency capacity. These actions are available now, in states with Democratic chambers or bipartisan blocs, without waiting for federal legislative change.

In the longer term — Wave 3 — state legislators are the ratification infrastructure for constitutional amendments. Any amendment that passes Congress with a two-thirds majority requires ratification by 34 state legislatures (or state conventions). The current partisan composition of state legislatures means that most constitutional amendments cannot currently achieve 34 state ratifications. Changing that composition — through the 2026 and 2028 elections — is the prerequisite for Wave 3.

The 2026 midterms have specific structural importance for the redistricting cycle. 2030 redistricting will be controlled by the legislative and gubernatorial majorities elected in 2028. But the 2028 outcome is heavily shaped by whether 2026 produces competitive redistricting in states where current maps are gerrymandered. 26 Secretary of State races in 2026 determine election administration; three state supreme court seats determine redistricting case outcomes. These are leverage points for the entire decade.

---

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 1 (Electoral Reform)**: State voting access omnibus legislation is the most immediate and impactful action available to trifecta legislatures. Automatic voter registration, pre-registration for 16-17 year-olds, permanent absentee lists, multilingual materials — these are state-law provisions that do not require federal action and are available in any state with legislative and gubernatorial support.

**Domain 2 (Civil Service)**: State civil service protection legislation — prohibiting political-loyalty reclassification without merit justification, establishing state-level MSPB equivalents with independent adjudication, and requiring that state agency functions not be delegated to political staff without legislative authorization — creates a state-law floor that is not dependent on federal civil service restoration.

**Domain 9 (Federalism and Local Power)**: Anti-commandeering resolutions and state sovereignty legislation send both legal and political signals. State anti-commandeering guidance — formally advising state agencies that they may not be compelled to administer federal programs that violate state law — is the legal infrastructure for the federalism resistance documented in Domain 9.

**Domain 1f (Independent Redistricting)**: Constitutional amendments establishing independent redistricting commissions must be initiated in the current legislative session to be on the ballot before 2028 redistricting cycle decisions are locked in. States that do not already have independent redistricting commissions should begin the amendment process in 2026 sessions.

---

### Year 1–3 Electoral Sequencing

**Year 1 (2026 electoral preparation):** The primary Year 1 legislative task is not passing legislation — it is winning the elections that make the Year 2 legislation possible. Priority targets are competitive states where Democratic gains produce either a split legislature (allowing co-equal chamber vetoes on hostile legislation) or a trifecta (enabling proactive reform). The framework's Wave 1 domains (1, 2, 7, 9) provide the district-level messaging: voting access, civil service stability, free speech protection, and local control are popular across partisan lines when framed correctly.

For states with current trifectas or near-trifectas: any legislation that passes before the 2026 election creates a positive example to run on. Passing a state voting access omnibus before November 2026 is both substantive policy and electoral evidence.

**Year 2 (2027 legislative sessions):** In states where 2026 produced trifectas or chamber gains, first legislative session priorities should be pre-drafted and ready for introduction on the first legislative day. The Minnesota model — have the omnibus pre-drafted in August before the November election, ready to introduce in January — ensures that the political momentum from an electoral victory does not dissipate during bill-drafting delays. The priority sequence: Domain 1 (voting access omnibus first, because it expands the electorate for subsequent elections), then Domain 2 (civil service protection), then Domain 9 (cooperative federalism defense infrastructure).

**Year 3 (2028 elections and redistricting preparation):** The 2028 elections precede the 2030 redistricting cycle by two years. State legislative majorities elected in 2028 will control post-census redistricting. Independent redistricting commission amendment campaigns — which require two legislative sessions in many states — must begin in 2027 to appear on the 2028 ballot. Domain 1f's analysis of the Texas 2025 mid-decade gerrymander mechanism identifies specifically what independent redistricting commissions must include to prevent mid-decade gerrymandering after a court order.

---

### Case Study: Minnesota 2023 Democracy for the People Act

The Minnesota Democratic trifecta enacted automatic voter registration, pre-registration for 16-17 year-olds, a permanent absentee list, multilingual ballot materials, and voter intimidation protections in a single omnibus bill — the Democracy for the People Act — in 2023. This is the most significant state voting access legislation in 50 years.

The omnibus approach was the decisive tactical choice. Each individual provision was vulnerable to procedural defeat — targeted opposition can extract individual provisions through amendment, committee holds, or floor procedural challenges. The omnibus bundled all provisions into a single bill where removing any one provision required removing the entire bill. Opposition was forced to oppose the whole package, which is politically more costly than opposing individual provisions.

The second lesson is sequencing. The caucus applied wave-filter discipline before the session opened: voting access legislation (Wave 1) was introduced first, before economic legislation (Wave 2) or constitutional legislation (Wave 3). The voting access omnibus passed; subsequent economic legislation benefited from the demonstrated coalition functionality and the electoral-expansion effect that voting access creates.

Every state with a 2026 trifecta or near-trifecta should have a Domain 1 omnibus pre-drafted in August 2026, ready to introduce on the first legislative day in January 2027. Do not wait for the election results to start drafting.

---

### Constraints

**Chamber composition limits.** State legislators in minority caucuses cannot pass legislation; their Wave 1 function is building the evidentiary record through committee hearings, developing the bill language for the next cycle, and mobilizing constituents around the democratic renewal message as an electoral argument. This is less satisfying than passing bills but is essential groundwork.

**Governor veto.** In states with Democratic legislative majorities and Republican governors (or vice versa), the veto is the constraint. Identify provisions with bipartisan appeal — voting access provisions that reduce administrative burden on election officials (Republican counties benefit from automatic voter registration that reduces registration error rates), civil service protection provisions that protect state employees regardless of party — and advance those first.

**Amendment complexity.** Constitutional amendment provisions require multiple legislative sessions in many states. The sequencing constraint means that amendments must be initiated in 2026 or 2027 sessions to be available as ratification vehicles for federal constitutional amendments that might be proposed in 2028 or beyond.

**Measurement milestones:**
- Domain 1 omnibus pre-drafted for 5+ targeted states: Month 8 (before 2026 elections)
- Domain 1f independent redistricting amendment initiated in 3+ states: 2027 sessions
- Domain 2 state civil service protection enacted in 2+ trifecta states: 2027 sessions
- Peer-state bill language coordination established through GSD network: Month 3

## 8. Federal Judges — Advisory and Educational Role

**Who this is for**: Senior district court and appellate judges with interest in judicial education and institutional independence; Judicial Conference of the United States committee members; Federal Judicial Center staff; law school faculty who organize continuing legal education for federal judges; and administrative law professors engaged in judicial capacity-building programs.

---

### TL;DR (100 words)

This playbook is the most constrained of the eight because judicial ethics prohibit most forms of public advocacy. Your role is structural: federal courts are the enforcement mechanism for every other constituency's legal strategies. When those strategies work — when AG coalitions obtain TROs, when civil service unions win preliminary injunctions, when law school clinics produce favorable precedents — the outcome depends on federal judges willing to apply the law impartially regardless of political pressure. The Year 1 task is ensuring the Judicial Conference formally responds to the institutional threats documented in Domain 6, and that judicial education addresses the specific doctrinal challenges created by the current SCOTUS term.

---

### Leverage Analysis

Federal judges have structural leverage that exceeds every other constituency in the enforcement chain: they can issue injunctions that stop executive actions in their tracks, require testimony and document production that no one else can compel, and create precedent that binds future courts and executive actors. This leverage is formally passive — judges act when cases are brought to them, not when they choose to initiate — but it is more powerful than any other institutional actor when it operates.

The judicial independence threats documented in Domain 6 — 564 impeachment threats in FY2025, public statements by executive officials characterizing adverse rulings as judicial overreach, the administration's non-compliance with specific court orders, and SCOTUS's shadow docket procedures that reverse lower court rulings without full briefing — all threaten the capacity that makes this leverage real.

The Judicial Conference of the United States is the policy-making body for the federal court system. The Conference's silence on impeachment threats against Judges Boasberg and Boardman represents a governance failure: when the administrative head of a coordinate branch publicly advocates impeachment of judicial officers for adverse rulings, the institution that represents those officers should formally respond. The Conference has tools available — formal statements, judicial conduct committee referrals, administrative requests to Congress — that it has declined to use.

The Federal Judicial Center's judicial education function is the other available lever. Judges receive education through FJC programs; the content of that education shapes how judges approach novel legal questions. Incorporating specific doctrinal preparation for the constitutional questions raised by the 2026 SCOTUS term — independent agency removal, APA vacatur scope, shadow docket procedure — into FJC programming is a legitimate institutional function.

---

### Most Directly Actionable Domains

**Domain 6 (Judicial Ethics and Independence)**: The proposed Judicial Ethics Enforcement Act — establishing an independent body to investigate Supreme Court misconduct with binding referral authority — addresses the current self-regulatory failure. Zero sanctions have been issued against Supreme Court justices despite documented gift acceptance and flag-flying controversies. District and circuit judges who engage with this issue must do so through institutional channels (Judicial Conference statements, bar association submissions, law school conference participation) rather than direct advocacy.

**Domain 35 (SCOTUS 2026 Term)**: District and circuit courts writing opinions that make maximum explicit the legal defects in administration actions — exhaustively documenting every constitutional and statutory violation — create the academic and precedential record that constrains SCOTUS even when the Court ultimately rules adversely. When a district court opinion identifies 12 distinct legal problems with an executive action, SCOTUS addressing only 6 of them still leaves 6 documented legal vulnerabilities. Well-written district court opinions in this period serve as the record for eventual constitutional accountability.

---

### What Judges Cannot Do and What They Can

The Judicial Code of Conduct prohibits federal judges from engaging in political activity, making public statements on contested political questions, and organizing in ways that appear to compromise impartiality. This constraint is real and should not be minimized.

Within these constraints, the available tools are: (1) participation in Federal Judicial Center educational programs — judges can request specific program content without violating independence norms; (2) participation in academic conferences organized through law schools rather than advocacy organizations — scholars presenting at these events can present research on judicial independence without political framing; (3) hiring law clerks trained in the specific doctrinal areas relevant to the current docket — clerks with administrative law, constitutional law, and election law backgrounds will surface relevant arguments in briefs that clerks with other backgrounds may miss; (4) writing comprehensive district court opinions that document the full legal record even when the circuit outcome is anticipated to narrow the holding.

The Judicial Conference's institutional response to impeachment threats is the most direct available tool. A formal statement from the Conference — not from individual judges, but from the institution — affirming that adverse judicial rulings are not grounds for impeachment under the Constitution, is both legally accurate and institutionally appropriate. The Conference has issued statements on court security, judicial pay, and case management; judicial independence under political pressure is at minimum equally appropriate.

---

### Constraints

**Judicial Code of Conduct creates hard limits.** Judicial ethics prohibit advocacy, organizing, partisan activity, and public statements on contested political questions. These limits apply to federal judges strictly and to their staff in their official capacities. Every suggested action in this playbook has been reviewed for consistency with these limits; anything that approaches the boundary should be reviewed with the relevant circuit's chief judge before proceeding.

**Structural passivity.** Courts act when cases come to them; they cannot initiate action. The quality of the cases brought to federal courts depends on the other constituencies in this playbook — AG coalitions, law school clinics, civil rights organizations. The judiciary's leverage is magnified when those other constituencies bring well-developed cases with strong factual records and pre-staged academic support.

**SCOTUS shadow docket operates without full deliberation.** A district court judge who issues a carefully reasoned TRO may see it reversed by the Supreme Court within 72 hours through a one-paragraph emergency order that does not address the district court's reasoning. This constraint cannot be resolved at the district court level; it requires either SCOTUS internal reform or statutory shadow docket procedure reform — both of which are outside judicial control.

**Measurement framework (advisory):**
- Judicial Conference formal statement on impeachment threats: Year 1
- FJC APA vacatur / administrative law program additions: Year 1
- Domain 6 empirical project on shadow docket pattern cited in at least one district court opinion: Years 1–2

## 9. Cross-Constituency Alliance and Conflict Matrix

### Natural Alliance Clusters

The eight constituencies naturally cluster into three working groups based on functional complementarity. These are not mutually exclusive; individual organizations will participate in multiple clusters.

**Cluster A — Legal Defense:** State AGs + Law School Clinics + Civil Rights Coalitions + Appellate Litigators. This cluster shares litigation objectives, can coordinate amicus production, expert declaration networks, and filing timing without institutional conflicts. The coordination mechanism: Democracy Docket's litigation tracking provides the shared case map; AG coalition lead-state assignments direct amicus recruitment; law school clinics produce the academic record; civil rights coalitions provide the advocacy pressure that raises the political cost of shadow docket reversal.

**Cluster B — Electoral and Institutional:** State Legislators + Labor Unions + Civil Service Unions + Religious Coalitions. This cluster shares electoral mobilization infrastructure. Labor GOTV, faith voter education, legislative district-level advocacy, and civil service messaging all converge in competitive districts. The coordination mechanism: state legislative campaign committees, state AFL-CIO federations, and state interfaith coalitions already have relationships; a shared 35-domain framework message discipline allows them to reinforce each other without duplicating effort.

**Cluster C — Information and Legitimacy:** Media Organizations + Law School Clinics + Civil Rights Coalitions. This cluster serves the information production function: clinics produce analysis, civil rights organizations amplify and distribute, media covers and legitimizes. The coordination mechanism is deliberate sequencing — clinic analysis is published in Just Security or Lawfare, civil rights organizations share through their networks, media organizations cover the legal controversy as a news story. This is not coordinated messaging; it is deliberate timing of legitimate independent functions.

| Constituency A | Constituency B | Conflict Domain | Navigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| State AGs | Federal Courts | Domain 6g — universal injunctions; AGs may prefer state-court parens patriae jurisdiction over broad federal injunctions | Frame as complementary: state parens patriae in federal court + APA vacatur as alternative remedy + class action fortification are all non-competing tools |
| Labor Unions (AFL-CIO) | Building Trades | Domain 15 — climate transition; building trades support fossil fuel construction employment | Just transition provisions with specific job guarantee numbers and wage parity requirements in Domain 12 infrastructure programs; without substantive provisions this fracture is real |

### Coordination Protocols

**Secure communication**: The AG coalition's Signal protocol (documented in `crisis-response-ag-coalition.md`) should be extended to include law school clinic coordination directors, labor union political directors, and media press freedom organization legal directors. Email is subject to FOIA in most states and is potentially surveilled; constituency coordination on sensitive litigation strategy should use end-to-end encrypted channels.

**Shared domain lead assignments**: Once the AG coalition produces its domain lead assignment matrix (Months 1–3), that matrix should be shared with all eight constituencies so that each knows which AG office is the point of contact for each domain's legal strategy. Other constituencies should align their advocacy timing with the lead AG office's litigation calendar.

**Crisis activation protocol**: For each major anticipated crisis (post-Slaughter ruling, 2026 election interference, MSPB quorum loss, Section 702 lapse), a pre-assigned activation protocol should exist across all relevant constituencies: which AG office files the TRO, which law school clinic produces the rapid-response analysis, which civil rights organization activates the mutual defense network, which union deploys its GOTV infrastructure in affected districts, which faith coalition activates its congressional visit program. Pre-assignment prevents the coordination problem that emerges when eight constituencies simultaneously decide to respond to the same crisis without a shared plan.
