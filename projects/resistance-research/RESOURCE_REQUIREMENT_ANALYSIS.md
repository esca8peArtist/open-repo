## Opening Context: The Efficiency Argument

Before presenting budget detail, the most important finding of this analysis must be stated clearly: the total resource envelope for Phase 1–3 democratic renewal implementation ($7M–$16M over four years) is dramatically below comparable campaign spending in the same political arena. This is not a limitation — it is the efficiency argument for why infrastructure investment is the highest-leverage entry point for democratic renewal work.

For scale comparison:
- Obama 2008 campaign: $750M raised and spent on a single presidential campaign [1]
- Total 2022 ballot measure spending: $945M across all state measures [2]
- Total 2022 federal + state election spending: $16.7B [3]
- MacArthur Foundation democracy protection commitment (2025-2026): $100M [4]
- Ford Foundation democracy protection commitment (2026): $60M [5]
- Democracy Fund annual grant spending (2023): $77M [6]

The $7M–$16M Phase 1–3 implementation envelope represents:
- 1.0–2.1% of the MacArthur democracy protection commitment
- 0.04–0.1% of 2022 election cycle spending
- Roughly the cost of a single U.S. Senate campaign in a competitive state

The efficiency argument: infrastructure investment that makes the $16.7B in electoral spending more coordinated, better aimed at structural reform, and connected to durable post-electoral policy capacity is worth far more than its dollar cost relative to adding more dollars to an existing campaign spending ecosystem that already has abundant resources but poor coordination.

## Phase 1: Awareness Infrastructure (3–6 Months) — $50K–$145K

### What Phase 1 Accomplishes

Phase 1 establishes the 40-domain diagnostic framework as a shared reference point for the 150+ target institutions identified in the coalition management playbook. The goal is not advocacy (that is Phase 2); the goal is recognition — establishing that the framework exists, that it is credible, that it represents serious research, and that institutions should engage with it. Phase 1 success is measured by adoption rate across target institutions, which then creates the coalition foundation for Phase 2 organizational work.

Phase 1 is primarily a communications and distribution challenge, not an organizational or legal challenge. The resource requirements are correspondingly smaller than Phase 2 or Phase 3.

### Budget Line 1: Communications Infrastructure

**Substack newsletter platform**: Free tier for up to 1,000 subscribers with basic features; paid plans at $85-$520/month for custom domains and analytics. For Phase 1 distribution to 150+ institutions plus a wider public audience, the paid plan at $85/month is appropriate. Annual cost: **$1,020**. This is negligible.

**Reddit strategy**: Zero direct cost. Organic engagement with relevant subreddits (r/PoliticalScience, r/democracy, r/law, r/Progressive, etc.) requires time investment but no budget. Boosted posts on Reddit cost $5-$40/day depending on targeting; a 30-day Phase 1 Reddit campaign budget: **$3K–$12K**.

**Social media presence**: Zero platform cost for organic. For paid amplification, Twitter/X (for academic and policy audiences), LinkedIn (for institutional decision-makers), and Instagram/TikTok (for broader public awareness): $5K–$15K for a targeted 3-month paid amplification campaign.

**Video and design production**: Explainer videos and professional design for the framework materials improve adoption rates but are not required for Phase 1 awareness. Contractor rates for video production: $3K–$15K for a 3-5 minute explainer video; design for one-page summary materials: $2K–$8K. Total optional production: **$5K–$23K**.

**Partnerships with academic journals and policy newsletters**: Zero direct cost, but requires time investment. Law reviews (Harvard Law Review, Yale Law Journal, NYU Law Review) publish invited commentaries; policy newsletters (Politico, The Hill, Democracy Journal, The American Prospect) publish op-eds; academic blogs (Lawfare, Just Security, Balkinization) publish analysis pieces. Each publication requires one to two weeks of writing time and editorial engagement. No budget needed; staff time is the resource.

**Phase 1 Communications Subtotal: $9K–$50K**

### Budget Line 2: Institutional Outreach

**Target**: 50 priority universities + 30 priority think tanks + 30 priority advocacy organizations = 110 priority institutions, with travel to the 20 highest-priority for in-person briefings.

**Travel budget for in-person briefings**: 20 institutions, averaging one-day trips with economy airfare ($300–$600) + hotel ($150–$250/night) + ground transportation and meals ($50–$100). Total: $500–$950 per trip × 20 trips = **$10K–$19K**.

**Staff time for outreach email campaigns**: Outreach to 110 institutions requiring personalized emails (PHASE_1_EMAIL_TEMPLATES.md provides templates; each requires 15-30 minutes of personalization). 110 institutions × 30 minutes = 55 hours. At $75/hour for a policy communications professional: **$4,125**. Round to **$5K** including follow-up management.

**Workshop hosting**: Two regional convenings (East Coast and West Coast) for 20-30 institutions each, to present the framework and facilitate discussion. Room rental: $1K–$3K/venue; catering: $50–$100/person × 50 people = $2.5K–$5K; materials: $500–$1K. Total for two workshops: **$8K–$18K**.

**Virtual briefings**: Five to eight virtual briefings using existing Zoom infrastructure, for institutions that cannot attend in-person. No direct cost; staff time for preparation and facilitation: 20 hours = **$1.5K**.

**Phase 1 Institutional Outreach Subtotal: $24K–$44K**

### Budget Line 3: Distribution and SEO Infrastructure

**GitHub Gist hosting**: Zero cost. The existing DISTRIBUTION_GIST_URLS.md infrastructure documents that this is already deployed.

**Custom domain and basic web infrastructure**: A simple website (not a full organizational presence — a document distribution portal) costs $15–$30/month for hosting + $15/year for domain. Annual cost: **$200–$375**. Negligible.

**SEO and discoverability**: Academic SEO is primarily about citation and linking, not paid search. For democratic renewal research to appear in Google Scholar and academic search results, the most effective approach is PDF distribution with proper metadata, DOI registration through a participating institution, and SSRN posting. SSRN posting is free; DOI registration through SSRN's affiliated institutional network costs $0 with an institutional account. **No direct budget required.**

**Paid search advertising**: Optional, for driving institutional decision-makers to the distribution hub. Google Search ads for policy-relevant keywords ($5–$15/click in policy spaces): $5K–$15K for a 90-day Phase 1 campaign. Likely lower priority than organic distribution; include as optional. **$0–$15K**.

**Phase 1 Distribution and SEO Subtotal: $0–$15K**

### Phase 1 Funding Sources

**Ford Foundation** committed $60M in April 2026 specifically "to defend the rule of law, fortify voting rights, and boost civic participation," with grants to organizations including Campaign Legal Center, Veterans for All Voters, and All Voting is Local. [5] A Phase 1 awareness grant of $50K-$100K is at the low end of Ford's individual grant scale. Ford's Civic Engagement and Government program has provided $1B+ in grants over ten years. [5]

**Democracy Fund** made $77M in grants in 2023 ($48M paid out directly). Democracy Fund explicitly funded SiX (State Innovation Exchange) and has democracy education as a core program area. Phase 1 awareness is consistent with Democracy Fund's investment thesis. [6]

**MacArthur Foundation** committed $100M to democracy protection, with initial grantees including Protect Democracy Project ($10M) and smaller grants to State Infrastructure Fund ($1M). Phase 1 fits MacArthur's "essential elements of democracy" framing and would be competitive for a sub-$150K grant. [4]

**Open Society Foundations** U.S. Programs fund comparative democracy research, civic participation, and political education — all directly applicable to Phase 1 distribution. OSF has historically been among the largest funders of research distribution and advocacy infrastructure.

**Self-funding model**: Given the minimal Phase 1 cost ($50K-$145K), partial or complete self-funding through existing budgets of partner organizations is feasible. If 10 Category A coalition partners each contribute $5K-$15K to a shared Phase 1 communications fund, the entire Phase 1 budget is covered without foundation dependency.

## Phase 2: Organization Infrastructure (6–12 Months) — $600K–$1M

### What Phase 2 Accomplishes

Phase 2 converts Phase 1 awareness into operational coalition infrastructure: coalition coordinators hired, state-by-state coordination active, litigation infrastructure pre-positioned, and the three-tier model operational. Phase 2 success is measured by the number of partner institutions with active coordination relationships, the number of state-level campaigns active, and the number of pre-positioned legal filings ready.

Phase 2 is the operationally intensive period — it requires full-time professional staff and sustained organizational effort. The resource requirements reflect this.

### Budget Line 1: Coalition Coordination Staff

**Coalition coordinator roles (2–3 FTE)**: Based on 2025 nonprofit advocacy compensation data, a senior coalition coordinator in Washington DC earns $75K-$95K annually; a mid-level coordinator $55K-$75K; a junior coordinator $45K-$65K. [7] With benefits at 30% of salary:

- Senior coalition director (1 FTE): $95K + $28.5K benefits = **$123.5K**
- State coordination manager (1 FTE): $70K + $21K benefits = **$91K**
- Research and communications coordinator (1 FTE): $58K + $17.4K benefits = **$75.4K**

Total for 3 FTE: **$290K annually**.

For 12 months of Phase 2 staffing, plus hiring costs (job posting, recruitment, onboarding): **$310K–$340K**.

**Coordination operations** (meeting facilitation, shared technology platform, travel for quarterly all-coalition convenings): $30K-$50K for the year.

**Coalition Coordination Staff Subtotal: $340K–$390K (12 months)**

### Budget Line 2: State-by-State Infrastructure Support

**State coordination grants to 8–10 priority states**: Based on the Freedom to Marry model of $100K per state (2014 dollars), adjusted for 2026 costs and the greater complexity of democratic renewal work (broader issue scope than single-issue marriage equality): $150K–$200K per state for coalition coordination support. For 8 priority states: **$1.2M–$1.6M total**, which exceeds the Phase 2 budget ceiling. Therefore:

Phased state investment: 3 anchor states in Phase 2 at $150K each (states with the most favorable conditions for democratic renewal ballot initiatives in 2026: Michigan, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin) = $450K; remaining 5–7 states in Phase 3 as resources allow.

**Phase 2 state investment: $450K**

**Legal research and model legislation**: Development of model statutory language for the top 10 domains with state-level implementation pathways (voting rights restoration, redistricting reform, civil service protections, election administration standards, healthcare access, labor rights, education funding, criminal justice reform, environmental protection, housing production). Law school clinic partnerships can absorb much of this at reduced cost:
- Law school clinic partnerships (3–5 clinics, each developing 2–3 domain models): $5K–$20K per clinic = $25K–$100K total
- Legislative drafting review by outside counsel: $25K–$50K
- **Legal research subtotal: $50K–$150K**

**Ballot initiative infrastructure for 2026 target states**: Signature gathering in 3 anchor states for highest-priority ballot measures. Per Ballotpedia data, the average cost of a successful ballot initiative signature drive in 2024 was $3.25M, with the highest-cost states (California, Florida) reaching $23M. [8] For lower-cost states (Michigan, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin), expect $1M–$3M per initiative.

**This exceeds Phase 2 budget capacity.** The resolution: Phase 2 ballot initiative work is limited to pre-production (drafting, polling, coalition alignment, legal review) without launching full signature campaigns. The signature campaigns themselves are Phase 3 investments (or are funded by domain-specific partner organizations: healthcare foundations for Medicaid expansion, labor unions for labor rights measures, etc.). Phase 2 ballot initiative pre-production: **$50K–$100K**.

**Lobbying materials and state legislative advocacy packages**: For each of the 8–10 priority states, a complete legislative advocacy package (one-pagers, testimony templates, model bill text, coalition sign-on letters) for top 5 priority domains. Design and production: $5K–$10K per state = $40K–$80K.

**Phase 2 State Infrastructure Subtotal: $590K–$780K**

*Note: This exceeds the Phase 2 ceiling when combined with coordination staff costs. The resolution is phasing: full state infrastructure in 3 anchor states in Phase 2; 5–7 additional states in Phase 3.*

### Budget Line 3: Litigation Infrastructure

**Pre-positioned legal briefs**: Developing template amicus briefs and complaint frameworks for the highest-probability litigation scenarios (executive election interference, voter purge challenges, redistricting challenges, civil service restoration). The Democratic Attorneys General Association already coordinates multi-state filings; the Phase 2 investment is in supporting the pre-positioning infrastructure that allows rapid response. Partnership with law school clinics (Georgetown Law, Yale Law, Harvard Law) reduces cost.
- Brief development for 5 priority litigation scenarios: $10K–$25K per scenario in outside counsel review = $50K–$125K
- Law school clinic partnership grants: $5K–$15K per clinic × 3 clinics = $15K–$45K
- **Litigation pre-positioning subtotal: $65K–$170K**

**Amicus network coordination**: The 2025 funding freeze case generated 13 amicus briefs from democratic organizations; coordinating this network requires identifying potential amicus filers for each major pending case, maintaining contact lists, and facilitating rapid-response coordination. This is primarily staff time (covered in coalition coordination staff budget) plus legal review of draft briefs.
- Amicus coordination (legal review, coordination): $30K–$60K annually

**Emergency filing capacity**: Retaining outside counsel for emergency TRO and injunction work requires a standing retainer. Retainer with a high-quality administrative law firm: $25K–$75K/year for emergency response capacity.

**Phase 2 Litigation Infrastructure Subtotal: $120K–$305K**

### Phase 2 Funding Sources

**Foundation mix**: No single foundation should fund more than 30% of Phase 2 to prevent dependency. At $800K total (midpoint):
- MacArthur: $240K (30%) — consistent with MacArthur's existing democracy protection grants at this scale
- Ford Foundation: $160K (20%) — consistent with Ford's expanded democracy commitment
- Democracy Fund: $160K (20%) — directly aligned with SiX investment and state infrastructure focus
- Open Society Foundations: $160K (20%) — consistent with OSF's civic participation and advocacy infrastructure program areas
- Small-donor online fundraising and individual donors: $80K (10%) — provides independence from foundation dependency

**Labor union in-kind contribution**: The AFL-CIO, SEIU, and AFSCME political departments have research, communications, and organizing capacity that, valued at market rates, represents $500K–$2M annually in potential Phase 2 equivalent support for democracy-aligned campaigns. The coordination investment required to channel this toward Phase 2 objectives is primarily relational (building trust and alignment) rather than financial.

## Phase 3: Political Action (12+ Months, Multi-Year) — $5M–$15M

### What Phase 3 Accomplishes

Phase 3 is the long-horizon investment that converts the coalition and infrastructure built in Phases 1–2 into durable policy change. It spans 2026–2030, encompassing the 2026 midterms, post-2026 legislative windows, 2028 elections, and the 2030 redistricting cycle. Phase 3 is the most expensive and the most uncertain — it depends on Phase 1–2 outcomes, electoral results, and the political environment in ways that cannot be fully predicted.

### Budget Line 1: 2026 Midterm-Specific Spending ($2M–$5M)

**Federal campaign infrastructure**: A Super PAC or 527 organization can accept unlimited contributions for independent expenditures. A 501(c)(4) can engage in electoral activity as long as it is not the primary purpose. The Phase 1 coalition's approach to electoral participation depends on organizational structure decisions made in Phase 2.

Conservative estimate for electoral infrastructure (candidate vetting, endorsement coordination, small-dollar bundling infrastructure, targeted digital advertising in 20 competitive House districts):
- Candidate vetting and endorsement research: $100K–$200K
- Digital advertising in 20 competitive districts ($50K–$250K per district, depending on market): $1M–$5M
- Small-dollar fundraising infrastructure (ActBlue integration, donor management): $50K–$150K
- Ground game coordination (working through labor unions and existing partner organizations' voter contact programs): $500K–$1M (in cash; labor union in-kind equivalent adds $5M–$20M in value)

**Ballot initiative funding in anchor states**: The Phase 2 pre-production (drafting, polling, coalition alignment) transitions to full campaigns. Signature gathering for 3–5 democracy-related ballot initiatives (ranked-choice voting, redistricting reform, voting rights restoration, Medicaid expansion):
- Signature gathering at $3.25M average per initiative (2024 baseline): $10M–$16M for 3–5 initiatives — exceeds Phase 3 budget and should be funded through domain-specific coalition partners (labor unions fund labor-related measures; healthcare foundations fund Medicaid expansion; etc.)
- Phase 3 ballot initiative coalition coordination and shared infrastructure: $250K–$500K

**2026 midterm-specific total (directly funded): $2M–$5M**

### Budget Line 2: Post-2026 Statutory Reform Campaigns (2027–2030)

**Multi-state legislative campaigns**: Phase 1 framework domains with state-level implementation pathways (from Document 1, Tier 2 analysis). For each priority domain, a two-year state-level legislative campaign (model legislation + advocacy coalition + lobbying) costs $50K–$300K per state, depending on scope.

At 10 priority states × 5 priority domains × $75K average (midpoint): **$3.75M over three years**, or **$1.25M/year**.

**Federal legislative advocacy**: For domains with federal statutory pathways (voting rights restoration, IEEPA reform, civil service protection codification), lobbying and advocacy campaigns require:
- Lobbying disclosure registered lobbyists: $200K–$400K/year
- Grassroots mobilization for federal calls/visits: $300K–$500K/year
- Coalition sign-on coordination: $100K–$200K/year
- **Federal statutory reform annual subtotal: $600K–$1.1M/year**

### Budget Line 3: Research and Iteration

**Quarterly strategic review and rapid learning**: What is working, what is not, and how to adjust. This requires:
- Research staff (1 FTE dedicated to tracking and evaluation): $80K–$120K/year including benefits
- Annual coalition convening (all partners, three-day retreat): $75K–$150K
- Periodic external evaluation (every 18 months): $50K–$100K
- Data infrastructure for tracking adoption, policy wins, coalition health: $25K–$75K/year

**Research and iteration annual subtotal: $200K–$400K/year**

### Phase 3 Funding Model

**Domain-specific foundations** are the primary Phase 3 funding source. The key insight is that different domains have different natural funders — and those funders have existing multi-year commitment cycles:

- Climate and environmental domains (Domains 15, 38): Bloomberg Philanthropies, Bezos Earth Fund, Environmental Defense Fund's investment arm, plus state-level climate foundations. Bloomberg alone has committed $750M+ to climate advocacy.
- Healthcare domains (Domains 11, 12, 39): Robert Wood Johnson Foundation ($14B endowment, healthcare-democracy nexus is a core program area), Commonwealth Fund, California Health Care Foundation. RWJF has explicitly funded healthcare access-democracy connection research.
- Labor and economic domains (Domains 3, 4, 17, 20): Labor unions (AFL-CIO, SEIU, AFSCME, UAW) plus economic justice foundations (Marguerite Casey Foundation, Economic Justice Program at Ford).
- Racial justice domains (Domain 22): NAACP LDF's Capital Partners program, W.K. Kellogg Foundation, JPMorgan Chase racial equity commitments (though corporate commitments have proven volatile post-2022).
- Voting rights and electoral domains (Domains 1, 7, 37): MacArthur, Ford, Democracy Fund, Hewlett Foundation.

**Online grassroots fundraising**: ActBlue processed $2.8B in 2022 midterm contributions. A democratic renewal initiative with credible Phase 1 distribution, visible institutional endorsements, and clear 2026 electoral relevance could generate $500K–$5M in small-dollar fundraising over Phase 3 — providing operational flexibility that foundation-only funding cannot.

**Fiscal sponsorship through existing 501(c)(3) networks**: New Venture Fund (fiscal sponsor for 100+ progressive projects), Tides Foundation, or NEO Philanthropy (which sponsored the State Infrastructure Fund MacArthur grantee) can provide fiscal sponsorship infrastructure without the overhead of forming a new organization. Fiscal sponsor fees: 8–12% of revenue managed. This is the fastest path to Phase 2 launch.

## Comparative Context: The Efficiency Argument in Full

### What $7M–$16M Buys

- **0.04–0.1%** of 2022 election cycle spending ($16.7B) [3]
- **7–16%** of MacArthur's democracy commitment ($100M) [4]
- **12–27%** of Ford's democracy commitment ($60M) [5]
- **9–21%** of Democracy Fund's annual grant spending ($77M) [6]
- **Equivalent to**: 1–2 competitive U.S. Senate campaigns; 3–5 statewide ballot initiative signature campaigns; 30 days of a major presidential primary campaign

### What $7M–$16M Accomplishes That Comparable Electoral Spending Cannot

Electoral spending is consumed in a single campaign cycle and produces no durable infrastructure. Infrastructure investment produces:
- A coalition coordination mechanism that reduces the cost of future coordination
- Model legislation ready for introduction in any state legislature
- Pre-positioned legal briefs that compress litigation response time from weeks to days
- A research and measurement system that makes future investments more effective
- A trained cohort of coalition coordinators who carry institutional knowledge across cycles

This is the strategic logic of infrastructure investment vs. campaign spending: the marginal dollar of additional campaign advertising in a saturated media environment has declining returns; the marginal dollar of coordination infrastructure has increasing returns as the coalition grows and compounds.

### The Philanthropy Landscape Assessment

The foundation landscape for Phase 1–3 funding is unusually favorable in 2026:
- MacArthur committed $100M, with open calls for applications still pending
- Ford committed $60M with explicit focus on all three tiers (rule of law, voting rights, civic participation)
- Democracy Fund committed $77M annually with explicit state-level focus
- 74% of institutional funders expect to maintain or increase democracy funding in 2026 vs. 2025 [9]
- An "All by April" campaign was explicitly pushing foundations to accelerate funding timelines before the 2026 midterms [9]

This window — foundations actively seeking credible, scalable democratic renewal projects to fund — is the optimal Phase 1 launch environment. The Phase 1 awareness investment is small enough that foundation grant timelines do not constrain launch. The Phase 2 organizational investment aligns with the 6-12 month grant cycles of major democracy funders. The Phase 3 multi-year investment aligns with multi-year program grants from Ford, MacArthur, and OSF.

### Risk Assessment for the Resource Model

**Funding concentration risk**: If more than 30% of Phase 2–3 funding comes from any single foundation, a priority shift at that foundation threatens program continuity. Mitigation: diversified funding from the outset; target no more than 25% from any single source.

**Foundation volatility risk**: Corporate and some foundation commitments to social causes have proven volatile (post-2022 ESG retreat). Democracy-focused foundations (MacArthur, Ford, Democracy Fund) have demonstrated multi-decade commitment to the field, reducing this risk. OSF has faced political pressure in multiple countries but maintained U.S. program commitments. Risk: medium-low for core funders.

**Capacity mismatch risk**: The Phase 2 organizational build requires hiring qualified staff within 90 days of funding receipt. The talent pool for experienced coalition coordinators with democratic renewal expertise is limited. Mitigation: establish relationships with candidate staff before funding is confirmed; use fiscal sponsor model to allow immediate engagement pending formal grant.

**Electoral outcome dependency risk**: Phase 3 electoral infrastructure is contingent on electoral outcomes (2026 House composition, state legislative races). The multi-state legislative campaign component is significantly more effective if Democrats gain House seats in 2026. Mitigation: Phase 3 legislative campaigns should be designed to be effective in multiple electoral scenarios, not dependent on a single optimal outcome.

## Minimum Viable Resource Envelope

The critical threshold question is: what is the minimum resource level at which Phase 1–3 can execute credibly?

**Phase 1 minimum viable budget**: $20K. This covers professional design of core materials, targeted outreach to 30 highest-priority institutions, and Substack/Reddit distribution infrastructure. Below this, the framework is distributable but lacks the institutional validation (in-person briefings, workshop hosting) that differententiates it from self-published research.

**Phase 2 minimum viable budget**: $250K. This funds one senior coalition director (at market rates for 12 months) plus operational costs. With one experienced coordinator, the coalition can manage 30–50 partner institutions (rather than 150+) and support 1–2 anchor states. Below this, the coalition lacks professional staffing and cannot sustain the coordination infrastructure.

**Phase 3 minimum viable budget**: $2M over four years. This funds a sustained research and legislative advocacy operation in 3–5 priority states, without major electoral independent expenditure spending. Below this, Phase 3 is essentially Phase 2 extended — without the electoral and legislative capacity that justifies the "political action" framing.

**Total minimum viable**: $2.3M over four years, compared to the target range of $7M–$16M. The minimum viable program is real but limited in scale. The Phase 1–3 framework at minimum viable scale can establish the research base, maintain a core coalition, and influence 3–5 state legislative campaigns — a credible but not transformative democratic renewal contribution. Scaling toward the $7M–$16M range enables the multi-state ballot initiative infrastructure, full coalition coordination, and sustained electoral engagement that produces system-level impact.
