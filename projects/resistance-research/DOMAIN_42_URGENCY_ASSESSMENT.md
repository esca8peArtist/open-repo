## (a) Why the May 28 Deadline Matters for Phase 1 Distribution

The May 28 deadline is the only hard external deadline in the 36-domain framework. Every other advocacy window is a policy window — an optimal period for outreach, not a cutoff after which the advocacy is mechanically unavailable. May 28 is different: it is a formal administrative law deadline established by Federal Register notice. Organizations that do not file written participation notices by May 28 cannot participate in the June 29–July 15 DEA hearing. That is not a probability statement. It is a statutory bar.

For Phase 1 distribution strategy, this creates a constraint that does not exist anywhere else in the framework. Domain 42 cannot be distributed on the standard Tier 1 institutional timeline (6–8 week adoption cycle) and still reach drug policy organizations in time for them to act on the DEA hearing. The 6–8 week cycle is appropriate for research-to-advocacy integration; it is not appropriate when the specific ask is "file a participation notice before May 28." The organizations in the Domain 42 contact list need the research now, not as part of a rolling 30-day distribution sequence.

The strategic consequence is that Domain 42 distribution must be decoupled from the broader Phase 1 distribution decision. The user has not yet made a Path A / A+37 / B decision for the main framework. That decision is not required before Domain 42 Wave 1 launches. The contact list (`execution/domain-42-contact-list.md`) explicitly states that these organizations are not in Batches 1–3 and should not be merged into the standard tier sequence. Domain 42 is a standalone sub-batch with its own urgency logic.

Failing to distribute Domain 42 before May 21 (the hard stop for new outreach) does not simply mean organizations receive the research later. It means they cannot act on the specific ask — hearing participation — for 12 months. The next DEA ALJ hearing on this docket will not occur until at least 2027. The organizations the research is designed to reach (Drug Policy Alliance, NORML, ACLU Criminal Law Reform, NAACP Legal Defense Fund, The Sentencing Project) have the institutional infrastructure to file participation notices and shape what testimony the hearing record contains. The hearing record, once closed, is the basis for any administrative law challenge to the DEA rescheduling outcome. Getting the democratic exclusion architecture argument and the Weill Cornell racial disparity data into that record requires acting before May 28.

## (b) Which Organizations in the Contact List Can Submit DEA Comments

All ten organizations in the Domain 42 contact list have standing to file written participation notices under the Federal Register procedure (91 Fed. Reg. 22777). The participation notice is a written statement — not a formal legal filing — indicating the organization's desire to present evidence or testimony at the June 29 hearing. The three required elements are: name and address of the organization, a brief statement of the subject matter and purpose of the proposed testimony, and an estimate of time required. Any organization can file.

**Category A (Wave 1 — drug policy organizations)**: Drug Policy Alliance, Marijuana Policy Project, NORML, Law Enforcement Action Partnership, Students for Sensible Drug Policy. These five are the most likely filers and should receive Wave 1 today or May 10. They have existing DEA practice, understand administrative hearing procedure, and have institutional infrastructure to file immediately. DPA and NORML in particular have participated in prior DEA proceedings. LEAP's law enforcement voice is unusually credible in DEA administrative proceedings — an endorsement from LEAP of the democratic exclusion framing would shift the hearing's analytical frame.

**Category B (Wave 2 — civil rights organizations)**: ACLU Criminal Law Reform Project, NAACP Legal Defense Fund, The Sentencing Project, Prison Policy Initiative. These four have standing based on the voting rights and disenfranchisement dimensions of the hearing. The ACLU and NAACP LDF have demonstrated DEA participation capacity. The Sentencing Project and Prison Policy Initiative are primarily research organizations — their participation notice ask is narrower (cover or reference the hearing in publications, file if capacity exists). The ACLU Criminal Law Reform Project is the highest-priority Category B contact: it has the institutional infrastructure, the relevant subject matter expertise, and the most direct standing on the democratic feedback loop argument (Section 3 of Domain 42).

**Note on NAACP LDF routing time**: NAACP LDF policy staff have a 10–14 day internal routing cycle for external research. If Wave 2 is sent May 10–12 as planned, LDF receives the research with 16–18 days before May 28 — sufficient for a fast routing decision, but only if the email reaches the right contact (the LDF Policy and Advocacy team, not the general webform) on the first send. Contact verification for LDF before sending is essential.

**Category C (Wave 2 — academic/administrative law)**: Mason Marks (Yale Law), Ohio State Moritz College Drug Enforcement and Policy Center. These contacts are not being asked to file participation notices; they are being asked for methodological feedback and potentially to testify. Mason Marks specifically may be planning to testify independently — Domain 42's regulatory capture analysis extends his Yale Law Journal work and he may want to know about it.

**Category D (Wave 3 — state AGs)**: Colorado, California, Michigan, Washington. These four AG offices have standing based on federalism grounds (32-AG SAFER Banking letter) and active cannabis policy portfolios. The specific ask — does the AG office plan to file comments or a participation notice focused on the federalism dimensions — is lower-conversion than Category A/B asks, but a single state AG filing the federalism argument would be high-leverage for the hearing record.
