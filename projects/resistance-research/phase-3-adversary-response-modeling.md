# Phase 3 Candidate 3A: Adversary Response Modeling

*Democratic Renewal Proposal — Implementation Intelligence Supplement*
*May 2026 — Systematic opposition playbook analysis across all 35 domains*

---

## Framing: Why Opposition Modeling Is a Design Requirement

The democratic renewal proposal's 35-domain framework is a diagnostic and prescriptive document. It identifies what is broken and what should replace it. What it does not do — and what this document supplies — is model how a determined opposition will respond to each reform, and what design features make implementation resilient to those responses.

This is not a theoretical exercise. Every major democratic reform effort in the historical record encountered organized, sustained, and strategically adaptive opposition. The opposition almost always had structural advantages: institutional incumbency, access to legal delay mechanisms, media ecosystems, and — critically — time. Reform coalitions work against a political window. Opposition coalitions work with institutional inertia.

The core finding across five comparative cases and three US historical precedents: **the form of obstruction that defeats reform is almost never the most visible or dramatic form. It is procedural, institutional, and designed to outlast political windows rather than to directly confront democratic mandates.** Spain's democratic transition survived an armed military coup attempt in 1981. It nearly failed because regional administrative gridlock threatened to delegitimize the democratic government before it could consolidate. Reconstruction survived the initial Southern resistance to Black suffrage. It was undone by the withdrawal of federal enforcement in 1877 and a generation of terrorism that the constitutional framework had no mechanism to stop.

The same pattern repeats across domains. Understanding it in advance is the difference between implementation architectures that survive and those that do not.

## Part I: Historical Opposition Patterns in Comparable Cases

### Case 1: Poland 2015–2025 — Judicial Capture, Recovery Attempt, and the Autocratic Enclave Problem

**The capture phase (2015–2023)**

Law and Justice (PiS) entered government in October 2015 with a single-chamber majority and executed a systematic institutional capture in a sequenced order that the V-Dem Institute's "Institutional Order in Episodes of Autocratization" identifies as characteristic of third-wave backsliding: horizontal accountability mechanisms first (Constitutional Tribunal, National Council of the Judiciary, prosecution service), then media, then civil society regulation. PiS stacked the Constitutional Tribunal by refusing to seat opposition-appointed judges and installing party loyalists, transferred judicial nomination authority from judges to parliament (where PiS held a majority), and placed the prosecution service under direct political supervision.

The key technical feature of this capture: all of it used formally legal procedures. PiS passed laws. Courts ruled on those laws using a captured tribunal. The appearance of legality was preserved even as the substance of independent review was destroyed. The Centre for European Reform identifies this as the core difficulty: "illegitimate appointments cannot be reversed while staying within formal legal norms — because those appointments used formally legal procedures."

**The recovery phase (2023–2025) and its obstruction architecture**

The Tusk coalition's October 2023 electoral victory — achieved at 74.4% turnout, the highest in Polish democratic history — produced a working parliamentary majority but not the supermajority needed to overcome the obstruction architecture PiS had built. Five specific mechanisms created persistent obstructions:

*Mechanism 1: Constitutional Tribunal as veto machine.* PiS-appointed Tribunal president Bogdan Święczkowski (a close associate of justice minister Ziobro) retains his position until 2030. Reform legislation referred to this Tribunal faces an indefinite suspension rather than an adverse ruling — suspension is the weapon, not the verdict. President Duda referred both the KRS reform law and the Constitutional Tribunal reform law to this body in October 2024.

*Mechanism 2: Presidential veto with a five-year term.* PiS-aligned President Duda vetoed judicial reform legislation systematically through 2025. The 2025 presidential election result — a PiS-backed candidate defeating the pro-coalition candidate — extended this veto mechanism by five years.

*Mechanism 3: Judicial network resistance.* PiS-appointed judges in regular courts challenged the legitimacy of Tusk-era appointments, creating parallel judicial compositions and refusing to recognize each other's rulings. Courts of appeal declined to recognize proceedings from tribunals whose composition they regarded as illegitimate.

*Mechanism 4: Parallel constitutional legitimacy claim.* PiS's legal allies argued that the Tusk government's refusal to publish Constitutional Tribunal rulings in the Official Journal was itself an unconstitutional act — a "both sides are acting illegally" framing designed to delegitimize democratic restoration and reduce international pressure on Poland's rule-of-law deficit.

*Mechanism 5: Media ecosystem maintenance.* PiS-aligned media (TV Republika, Gazeta Polska) maintained a counter-narrative framing democratic restoration as "revolutionary" anti-constitutional action, sustaining the PiS political base through the transition and keeping the party competitive in polls despite its governance record.

**What succeeded in blocking reform; what did not**

The veto and Tribunal suspension mechanisms succeeded in preventing formal judicial reform legislation from taking effect through 2025. The "both sides" framing achieved limited but real international traction, complicating EU pressure on Poland's rule-of-law record. The media ecosystem prevented the kind of coalition collapse that often follows difficult governing periods.

What failed: PiS could not prevent EU reinstatement of cohesion fund disbursements once the Tusk government demonstrated credible rule-of-law improvements. EU leverage — €17 billion in frozen funds, credibly conditioned on judicial independence metrics — was more effective than any domestic pressure mechanism. International accountability anchors that PiS could not unilaterally exit proved to be the reform coalition's most durable asset.

**US lessons**

The Poland case identifies the single most important structural vulnerability in the US reform context: time-insulated appointments. Federal judges with life tenure, regulatory commissioners with staggered fixed terms, inspectors general with statutory removal protections — each one represents an opposition beachhead that survives an electoral defeat. The reform coalition must anticipate this at the design stage, not discover it during implementation.

The EU leverage mechanism maps onto US treaty obligations, international agreements, and alliance relationships as potential external accountability anchors. Reforms that create institutional obligations with international partners are more durable than those entirely dependent on domestic political conditions.

### Case 2: Spain 1975–1981 — Reforming Through Law Against Structural Hardliner Opposition

**The structural problem: opposition with institutional veto power**

Francisco Franco died on November 20, 1975. The democratic transition was designed not to outmaneuver the *Búnker* hardliner faction that controlled the Francoist Cortes and National Council of the Movement, but to route reform *through* those institutions so that hardliners would formally assent to their own supersession. Prime Minister Suárez's Political Reform Act — which established universal suffrage and a new bicameral parliament — passed the Francoist Cortes in November 1976 and was approved by referendum in December. The hardliners' veto was neutralized because the reform used their own procedural rules.

**Five obstruction mechanisms and their outcomes**

*Búnker institutional blocking.* The concentrated Francoist faction in the Cortes and National Council held genuine blocking power on any measure requiring approval through normal legislative channels. Suárez's solution was to reframe the question: he did not ask for approval of democratization, he asked for approval of a procedure (the Political Reform Act) that would then produce democratization. The hardliners found themselves unable to vote against procedural rules they claimed to be defending.

*Military coup threat as background constraint.* Several officers actively conspired against the transition. This threat was not imaginary — it materialized as the 23-F coup attempt on February 23, 1981, when Colonel Tejero led armed Civil Guards into the Cortes. The coup failed because King Juan Carlos I intervened explicitly: he telephoned military commanders, appeared on national television, and invoked his constitutional authority over the armed forces. The institution of the monarchy — which the reform coalition could not itself command — was the decisive variable.

*Regional separatist violence as delegitimizing pressure.* ETA's campaign killed 67 people in 1978, 76 in 1979, and 95 in 1980, peaks that coincided with the most sensitive constitutional negotiations. This was not coordinated with regime hardliners but had the structural effect of enabling military hardliners to argue that the democratic government could not maintain public order — an indirect delegitimization mechanism.

*Economic crisis as constituency attrition.* Inflation above 16% and unemployment approaching 20% created democratic fatigue. The Moncloa Pacts of October 1977 — a social contract between parties, unions, and business — were specifically designed to contain this mechanism by stabilizing economic conditions before they could delegitimize the transition government.

*Procedural delay through bureaucratic holdovers.* Even after the 1977 elections, Francoist holdovers in the security services and civil bureaucracy could slow implementation, selectively leak information, and signal to military officers that the new government was precarious. This required the reform coalition to demonstrate constant competence and stability.

**What made reform resilient**

Three structural factors: First, the reform used existing legal authority throughout, denying the hardliner claim that democratization was extra-legal and revolutionary. Second, European Community accession served as an external anchor — EC membership required democratic governance, creating a structural incentive for reform that outlasted any individual government and could not be undermined by domestic political opposition. Third, the broad cross-party legitimacy of the 1978 Constitution — which included the Communist Party in negotiations — created stakeholder buy-in that made constitutional revision politically costly even for opponents.

**US lessons**

Route reforms through existing legal authorities wherever possible. Identify external anchors — treaty obligations, alliance requirements, international commitments — that the opposition cannot exit without visible political cost. The Moncloa Pacts lesson is directly applicable: economic stabilization must be sequenced *before* reform advocacy pushes the political window, or economic crisis becomes an obstruction mechanism.

### Case 3: South Korea 2024–2025 — Constitutional Self-Enforcement and Rapid Institutional Reform

**The crisis and its constitutional resolution**

President Yoon Suk-yeol's December 3, 2024 martial law declaration was terminated six hours later by a National Assembly vote of 190–0. This outcome was not the result of mass mobilization alone — it was the result of a constitutional provision that was self-executing: once the National Assembly voted to reject martial law by a majority, the Constitution required its immediate termination regardless of executive intent. The provision did not require anyone's cooperation. It created an automatic legal fact.

This is the primary analytical contribution of the South Korea case: **constitutional design can create automatic termination mechanisms that do not depend on voluntary compliance.** The reform coalition did not need to persuade or pressure the executive to stand down — the constitutional tripwire activated and the martial law declaration became legally void.

**Five opposition responses and their outcomes**

*Constitutional defiance reframing.* Yoon's December 12, 2024 statement attempted to characterize the martial law declaration as a "legitimate act of governance," invoking a "legislative dictatorship" framing analogous to the US "official acts" immunity argument. This reframing succeeded as a base-rallying tool but failed as a legal defense — the Constitutional Court unanimously rejected it in its April 4, 2025 ruling.

*Impeachment boycott as procedural delay.* The PPP-aligned bloc boycotted the first impeachment vote on December 7, 2024, forcing a second vote on December 14 when twelve PPP members defected under public pressure. The delay succeeded in adding two weeks of political uncertainty but not in preventing Yoon's removal.

*CIO jurisdictional challenge.* Yoon's legal team raised constitutional challenges to the Corruption Investigation Office for High-ranking Officials, arguing it lacked jurisdiction over the insurrection case. This delayed arrest proceedings by approximately three weeks, with the first arrest attempt on January 3 physically blocked by the Presidential Security Service before a successful arrest on January 15, 2025.

*PPP partisan consolidation.* The People Power Party chose not to publicly apologize for Yoon's actions (fewer than one quarter of PPP lawmakers did so as of early 2026), consolidating the conservative base at the cost of centrist voters. This illustrates how opposition parties can become institutional obstacles even without operational power — by maintaining electoral incentive structures that reward resistance to reform.

*Constitutional amendment delay.* The Lee administration's September 2025 proposed constitutional amendment (changing presidential terms from a single five-year to two four-year terms) requires PPP cooperation for ratification. The PPP's incentive to delay is structural: any amendment reducing executive power is electorally costly to a party that expects to win the presidency.

**What made reform resilient**

The National Assembly vote was self-executing. The Constitutional Court provided constitutional cover for removal. The Lee administration's July 2025 martial law reforms — specifically banning military and police from entering the Assembly even during declared martial law, and adding physical barrier requirements — were statutory (requiring only a simple majority) rather than constitutional (requiring a supermajority). The reform coalition matched its ambition to the available political tools.

**US lessons**

Self-executing reforms — those that create immediate legal tripwires rather than norm-dependent compliance requirements — are dramatically more resilient. Automatic voter registration, once built, requires active visible disenfranchisement to remove. Independent redistricting commissions, once enacted through ballot initiative, require a separate initiative to repeal. These are not the same as voluntary norms that can erode without a specific legal act.

Criminal prosecution of insurrectionists, if completed before political conditions shift, creates a factual record that forecloses retrospective recharacterization as legitimate governance. The timing imperative is absolute: the South Korea case shows that the institutional reform window is widest while the crisis memory is fresh.

### US Historical Precedent 1: Reconstruction (1865–1877) — The Coalition Withdrawal Problem

Reconstruction is the canonical US case of successful democratic reform followed by catastrophic obstruction. The 14th and 15th Amendments represented genuine constitutional achievements — equal citizenship and Black male suffrage were structurally embedded in the constitutional text. The Reconstruction Act of 1867 established military districts to enforce them. Black men won election to Congress, state legislatures, and local offices across the South.

The obstruction architecture that defeated Reconstruction operated on four levels simultaneously. First, organized paramilitary violence: the Ku Klux Klan, White League, and Knights of the White Camelia used systematic terror to suppress Black political participation and assassinate Black officeholders and Republican officials. Second, state institutional recapture: as Reconstruction governments were overthrown, new state governments enacted vagrancy laws, Black Codes, and eventually Jim Crow legislation that reconstructed racial hierarchy through formally race-neutral legal mechanisms. Third, federal coalition withdrawal: Northern political support for Reconstruction eroded under economic pressure (the Panic of 1873), corruption scandals, and Northern white exhaustion with Southern affairs. Fourth, Supreme Court narrowing: the *Slaughterhouse Cases* (1873), *United States v. Cruikshank* (1876), and subsequent decisions stripped the 14th and 15th Amendments of meaningful enforcement, ruling that the federal government could not protect citizens against private violence — only against state action.

The Compromise of 1877 — Hayes's withdrawal of federal troops from Louisiana and South Carolina in exchange for Democratic acceptance of the disputed 1876 election — completed the coalition withdrawal. Federal enforcement ended. State-sponsored disenfranchisement proceeded for 90 years.

**The specific lessons for resilience design**

The Reconstruction failure identifies three critical vulnerabilities:

*No self-executing enforcement mechanism.* The 14th and 15th Amendments required ongoing federal enforcement. When that enforcement withdrew, the constitutional rights became text without implementation. Any reform that depends on continuing political will to enforce is only as durable as the political coalition that sustains that will.

*No constituency-based enforcement independent of federal action.* The Civil Rights Movement succeeded in part because it created constituency-based enforcement: the NAACP, SCLC, and voting rights organizations became permanent monitors who would litigate regardless of federal enforcement posture. Reconstruction had no equivalent. Freed people's organizations had insufficient resources and faced physical destruction.

*The 20-year rule.* The Compromise of 1877 came 12 years after Appomattox. Organized resistance sustained over a decade eventually outlasted the reform coalition's political will. Any US democratic reform effort must plan for opposition that will persist for 10–20 years after the initial victory.

### US Historical Precedent 2: Wagner Act (1935–1947) — Administrative Circumvention and Statutory Rollback

The National Labor Relations Act established the right to organize and bargain collectively. Employers responded immediately with both defiance and circumvention. Before the Supreme Court upheld the Act in *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel* (1937), employers were actively encouraged by business associations to refuse to comply with the NLRB and to support nationwide injunction filings against its enforcement. Company unions — prohibited by the Act — continued to operate under various renamed forms. Blacklisting, strike-breaking, and discriminatory firings continued despite the statutory prohibition.

The more durable obstruction came after constitutional validity was confirmed: the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947 amended the NLRA to impose a series of restrictions on unions — banning secondary boycotts, requiring anti-communist affidavits from union officials, and granting states the power to pass right-to-work laws. Taft-Hartley passed over Truman's veto with bipartisan support from members who framed it as "balancing" an unbalanced law. The reform's opponents had successfully converted a labor protection statute into a labor restriction statute within twelve years of its passage.

**Lessons for resilience design**

The Wagner Act case demonstrates the legislative reversal vector at its most effective. The mechanism: reframe the reform as "extreme" or "unbalanced," wait for the reform coalition to lose a legislative majority, pass amendments that hollow out the statute while preserving its formal structure. The NLRA still exists; it simply no longer provides the protection it was designed to provide.

Resilience design for this vector requires: (1) constituency-based enforcement that continues even without agency backing; (2) private rights of action that allow beneficiaries to enforce their own rights without relying on the enforcement agency; (3) amendment procedures that require supermajority for hollowing amendments, not just for the original statute; and (4) constitutional entrenchment where feasible.

## Part II: Obstruction Mechanisms by Domain — Eight High-Impact Domains

For each domain, the primary and secondary obstruction mechanisms are identified, with the specific US institutional actors most likely to deploy them and the evidence basis for that prediction.

### Domain 1: Electoral Reform (Redistricting + RCV + Multi-Member Districts)

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Legislative reversal.* The single-member district mandate (Uniform Congressional District Act of 1967) can be reimposed by any Congress with a majority willing to do so. States that adopt RCV by ballot initiative face state legislative "bans" (Indiana, Ohio examples already documented in the framework). Federal proportional representation legislation, if enacted, faces reversal in any Congress where the party benefiting from single-member districts regains control.

*Mid-cycle redistricting.* Texas (2003, 2023), North Carolina (2023), and Missouri (2025) demonstrate the mid-decade redistricting mechanism: even if independent commission maps are in place, a state legislature with the will to litigate can execute a mid-decade gerrymander, implement it for one or two election cycles before courts rule, and accept the constitutional loss as a cost of the partisan gain.

*Administrative non-compliance at state level.* State election officials can delay, defund, or redesign implementation procedures for federal electoral mandates in ways that preserve formal compliance while undermining functional implementation. Florida's 2023 voter roll purge — removing 77,000 registrations without required NVRA notice periods — is the operational template.

**Resilience requirements:** Federal statute prohibiting mid-decade redistricting with automatic stay provisions triggering within 48 hours of implementation; constitutional backup through state ballot initiatives that do not require legislative approval; private right of action allowing any registered voter to challenge redistricting non-compliance without waiting for DOJ.

### Domain 6: Judicial Independence and Accountability

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Self-referential capture.* The existing Supreme Court majority is the primary obstruction actor. Any statutory term limits legislation, court ethics code, or court-packing statute would be reviewed by the court whose composition it affects. The court's majority has demonstrated willingness to invoke major questions doctrine, independent state legislature theory, and similar tools to strike down statutory reforms that threaten its institutional prerogatives.

*Confirmation blockade.* The Senate Republican caucus has demonstrated willingness to hold Supreme Court vacancies open for 295 days (Garland, 2016) and to invoke procedural rules strategically. Any court reform dependent on confirmation of additional justices is vulnerable to this mechanism in any Senate where Republicans hold 41 seats or more.

*Jurisdiction-stripping counteroffensive.* The most aggressive opposition scenario is Congressional Republicans attempting to strip federal court jurisdiction over specific subject areas — immigration, elections, gun rights — as a counter-move against a reform that expands court capacity or independence. Article III grants Congress this authority except over the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction, and the constitutional limits on jurisdiction-stripping are disputed.

**Resilience requirements:** Statutory reforms using existing constitutional authority (term limits as prospective rule for new appointments, not removal of existing justices); state AG enforcement networks to maintain parallel enforcement even when federal judicial resistance is highest; international precedent compilation to reduce the "revolutionary" characterization.

### Domain 13: Housing and Fair Housing Enforcement

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Administrative non-enforcement.* HUD programmatic requirements can be circumvented through regulatory reinterpretation, enforcement staff reductions, or blanket waivers without formally repealing statutory authority. The Trump administration's 2020 rescission of the Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH) rule — replacing a robust implementation requirement with a minimal certification — accomplished through rulemaking what would have required legislation to do directly.

*Local zoning obstruction.* Federal fair housing mandates interact with local zoning authority. States that preempt local zoning reform — as Texas has done through SB 4 and related legislation — can use state law to limit the scope of federal incentive programs without directly violating federal law.

*Sovereign immunity and damage limitations.* The Fair Housing Act's private right of action is subject to sovereign immunity constraints when the defendant is a governmental actor, and damages awards are capped in ways that reduce the deterrent effect of litigation for high-volume violators.

**Resilience requirements:** Private right of action with uncapped compensatory and punitive damages; state concurrent enforcement authority codified to survive federal non-enforcement periods; automatic referral requirements that trigger state AG enforcement when federal HUD enforcement falls below measurable thresholds for 180 consecutive days.

---

### Domain 14: Criminal Justice and Policing

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Consent decree withdrawal.* The Trump DOJ issued a May 2025 executive order directing withdrawal from all existing consent decrees, explicitly characterizing them as interference with local law enforcement authority. This is the most aggressive version of the administrative circumvention mechanism: using executive discretion to undo court-supervised enforcement structures.

*Prosecutorial self-protection.* Reforms targeting prosecutorial misconduct (Brady violations, Batson violations, post-conviction DNA access) face organized opposition from state prosecutors' associations and from the National District Attorneys Association, which has successfully blocked similar reforms in dozens of state legislatures. The mechanism is legislative capture: prosecutors are one of the best-organized electoral constituencies in state politics.

*Frame replacement.* "Soft on crime" framing has proven consistently effective at delegitimizing criminal justice reform, particularly when crime rates increase for any reason during a reform period. Opponents do not need to make accurate causal claims — the associative frame is sufficient to generate media coverage and constituent pressure that stalls implementation.

**Resilience requirements:** Private right of action in consent decree enforcement so that communities can enforce their own settlements without DOJ; statutory sunset prevention in consent decree terms requiring affirmative DOJ action to terminate; federal grant conditionality requiring state compliance with minimum prosecutorial ethics standards.

---

### Domain 21: Data Privacy and Surveillance

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*National security exception broadening.* Every surveillance limitation statute faces the argument that it would impair national security. The historical record shows this argument is consistently used to expand surveillance authority beyond the claimed necessity: Section 702 was originally authorized for foreign intelligence targeting of non-US persons and has been systematically expanded to domestic queries. The mechanism is legislative: attach a national security carve-out to any privacy protection statute, then gradually expand the carve-out through administrative interpretation.

*Commercial data broker parallel infrastructure.* Federal surveillance limitations apply to government actors. Commercial data brokers that purchase and sell location data, communications metadata, and financial records from private sources provide a parallel surveillance infrastructure that government agencies can access without Fourth Amendment warrant requirements. Limiting government surveillance without simultaneously limiting commercial surveillance creates a legal arbitrage that the executive branch will exploit.

*Intelligence community autonomous interpretation.* The NSA, FBI, and CIA have demonstrated willingness to develop novel legal theories for surveillance authority without public disclosure until exposed by whistleblowers or FOIA litigation. The PRISM program, the bulk telephony metadata collection, and the FBI's use of Section 702 for domestic queries all operated for years before disclosure. Any surveillance limitation that does not include independent audit authority with binding referral power will be circumvented through classified interpretation.

**Resilience requirements:** Mandatory sunset provisions with affirmative reauthorization (not passive renewal); independent audit authority in FISC or a new privacy court with binding referral to criminal prosecution; commercial data broker regulation as a companion to government surveillance limitation to close the arbitrage gap.

### Domain 29: Prosecutorial Accountability and DOJ Capture

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Process weaponization as the obstruction.* In this domain, the obstruction mechanism and the problem are the same: a politicized DOJ uses the indictment process to target political opponents, with the formal legal process providing cover. There is no administrative procedural check on a politically motivated prosecution until conviction is appealed — by which point careers, reputations, and organizations have been destroyed regardless of outcome.

*Classification and national security invocation.* The executive branch can classify evidence in politically motivated cases, convert them into national security prosecutions subject to CIPA procedures, and litigate them in ways that deny defendants the ability to challenge the government's factual assertions. The Seth Rich investigation and the SPLC prosecution both show elements of this mechanism.

*Bar association capture.* The March 2026 proposed DOJ rule that would allow prosecution of defense attorneys for "frivolous" motions — paired with state bar rule proposals that would discipline attorneys for politically disfavored arguments — creates an environment where the defense bar self-censors to preserve licensing. This chilling effect on legal representation is the precursor to eliminating effective counsel for politically targeted defendants.

**Resilience requirements:** Independent special counsel trigger mechanisms with automatic activation at three or more measurable indicators of political motivation (contemporaneous statements, prosecutorial selectivity, charging timing relative to political events); mandatory OPR review of all cases meeting political motivation indicators; state AG intervention authority codified to allow state civil rights prosecution when federal prosecution appears vindictive.

### Domain 34: Congressional Power and Redistricting

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Constitutional amendment threat.* Any statutory reform to redistricting, House size, or electoral system faces the argument that it is unconstitutional — and may actually be challenged at the Supreme Court. The Court's current majority has shown willingness to impose constitutional limits on congressional procedural authority. The Purcell principle (courts must not change election rules close to election day) is already being used to reduce the practical effect of court orders striking down gerrymandered maps.

*State legislative resistance.* States that lose redistricting power to independent commissions have litigated aggressively to narrow commission authority. Pennsylvania's Republican-controlled legislature attempted in 2022 to impeach Supreme Court justices who struck down a gerrymandered map. This is the most aggressive form of resistance: if the opposition cannot win in court, it threatens to impeach the judges who rule against it.

*Enforcement gap at the county level.* Federal redistricting requirements are enforced against state-level actors. Substate manipulation — county-level voter registration purges, polling place reductions, precinct boundary manipulations — occurs at a level where federal oversight is thinner and where individual violations are harder to aggregate into actionable patterns.

**Resilience requirements:** Constitutional amendment track proceeding in parallel with statutory track; ballot initiative campaigns in all states with initiative processes, bypassing state legislatures entirely; county-level monitoring infrastructure — civil society organizations with standing to challenge substate violations — built before the next redistricting cycle.

---

### Domain 37: Election Administration and Interference

**Most likely obstruction mechanisms:**

*Administrative defiance of certification obligations.* Rogue county canvassers and state election officials who refuse to certify results — tested in Wayne County, Michigan in 2020 and in several 2022 Arizona counties — represent a soft certification coup mechanism. The mechanism does not require winning in court: it requires creating enough delay and uncertainty that certification is appealed repeatedly until the statutory deadline passes.

*Federal interference with state administration.* CISA's dismantling removed the primary federal technical assistance and threat-sharing infrastructure for state election security. States are now individually responsible for cyber defense against adversaries (Russia, China, Iran) that operate at nation-state scale. The asymmetry is structural and exploitable.

*Voter roll manipulation.* Interstate crosscheck programs, systematic purge campaigns using voter roll data shared without NVRA-compliant notice procedures, and aggressive matching algorithms that remove valid registrations at scale have been documented in multiple states. Systematic voter roll manipulation can be executed over a period of years, before any election, making it difficult to challenge on a timely basis.

**Resilience requirements:** Federal statute creating automatic court authority (not prosecutorial discretion) to compel certification upon failure; independent federal election security agency with statutory independence from DHS; automatic voter registration nationally so that voter roll manipulation requires disenfranchising registered voters — generating immediate political cost — rather than quietly preventing registration.

## Part III: Resilience Design Patterns

### 3.1 Which Obstruction Mechanisms Can Be Designed Around

**Administrative circumvention can be designed around** with three tools: private rights of action (beneficiaries enforce their own rights without relying on the executive agency), state concurrent enforcement (blue-state AGs maintain enforcement during federal non-enforcement periods), and automatic consequence triggers (statutes that impose automatic penalties — funding cutoffs, reporting requirements, public disclosure — when enforcement metrics fall below defined thresholds).

**Process delay can be designed around** with statutory time limits and automatic operation provisions. The constitutional tripwire model from South Korea — where a vote produces automatic legal consequences rather than requiring subsequent compliance by the loser — is the gold standard. Automatic voter registration creates an automatic record; removing it requires a visible legal act. Automatic spending cuts for redistricting non-compliance create consequences that do not require executive branch activation.

**Coalition attrition from economic crisis can be designed around** with sequencing: economic stabilization before aggressive reform advocacy, and constituency-based enforcement that continues when mobilization energy declines. The Moncloa Pacts model — economic stabilization as a precondition for political reform — should be built into the transition planning.

**International precedent removes the "untested radicalism" frame** for specific domains where comparable reforms have documented outcomes in comparable countries. The reform coalition should maintain a standing document of international precedents for every major proposed reform, categorized by country type and implementation outcome.

### 3.2 Which Obstruction Mechanisms Require External Enforcement

**Time-insulated appointments cannot be designed around without external force.** Federal judges cannot be removed by the reform coalition without appearing to violate judicial independence norms. Regulatory commissioners cannot be removed before term expiration without statutory authority the reform coalition may not have. The Poland/Hungary lesson is clear: the only effective responses are (a) waiting out the terms, (b) creating structural reforms that reduce the captured institution's authority without removing its members, or (c) building parallel institutional capacity that achieves the policy goal through a different institutional channel.

**Formal legal non-compliance (court order defiance) requires external enforcement pressure.** When the executive defies court orders, the courts' only institutional tool is contempt — which requires executive branch enforcement to actually impose. In a system where the executive controls the relevant enforcement mechanisms, court order compliance ultimately depends on political cost calculations, not legal compulsion. The reform coalition must build the political cost infrastructure: systematic documentation of defiance, civil society mobilization around defiance events, and constituency-based enforcement through private contempt applications.

**Constitutional amendment obstacles require either supermajority coalition-building or bypass through state action.** Some reforms (Supreme Court term limits, presidential immunity removal, filibuster elimination) require either constitutional amendment — which requires two-thirds of both chambers and three-quarters of states — or creative institutional workarounds. The NPVIC model demonstrates that interstate compacts can achieve constitutionally comparable effects without federal constitutional amendment for appropriate domains.

## Part IV: Coalition-Size Analysis

### 4.1 The Minimum Viable Coalition for Implementation Robustness

Coalition analysis in the democratic reform context must distinguish between three distinct functions: the *mobilization coalition* (the people who turn out to demonstrations, vote, and contact elected officials), the *institutional coalition* (the organizations, legal entities, and officeholders who provide ongoing enforcement capacity), and the *veto-blocking coalition* (the minimum number of legislators, state AGs, and judicial actors needed to prevent reversal of enacted reforms).

These three coalitions have different size requirements and different durability profiles.

**The mobilization coalition** requires the Chenoweth threshold for transformation: active participation from approximately 3.5% of the population (11.6 million people in the US) to cross the no-failure threshold. The October 2025 No Kings protests drew an estimated 7 million (2.1%). This is a substantial base but not yet at the threshold. The critical vulnerability is the Tufekci degradation pattern: digital organizing creates rapid scale but shallow organizational depth, meaning mobilization peaks cannot be sustained.

**The institutional coalition** requires a specific set of actors regardless of total numbers: 21 Democratic attorneys general (currently 29) to maintain enforcement during federal non-enforcement periods; approximately 100 civil society organizations with actual litigation capacity (ACLU, Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, Protect Democracy, and equivalent organizations); and federal judicial majorities in at least three circuits to maintain injunctive authority against the most aggressive executive overreach.

**The veto-blocking coalition** is the most precise calculation. The reform coalition must maintain 41 Senate seats to block statutory reversal of any enacted reform requiring 60 votes to pass. It must maintain 290 House seats to override a presidential veto (two-thirds of 435). It must maintain 34 state legislative chambers to block constitutional amendment. None of these thresholds is unachievable; all of them require specific electoral strategies rather than general mobilization.

### 4.2 Coalition Size and the Defection Risk

Selectorate theory (Bueno de Mesquita et al.) distinguishes between the "winning coalition" — the minimum number of actors whose support is necessary to maintain power — and the broader population. The key insight: reforms that require large winning coalitions to enact but can be reversed by small ones are systematically vulnerable to defection.

The reform coalition's structural vulnerability is that passing major legislation requires 60 Senate votes (cloture) but reversing it requires only 51. This asymmetry means that any reform that could not achieve 60 Senate votes — and virtually no structural democratic reform can achieve 60 Senate votes in the current Senate — must be designed as either (a) self-executing once a 51-vote window exists, or (b) entrenched through constitutional or interstate compact mechanisms that require more than 51 votes to reverse.

### 4.3 The Cross-Party Coalition Problem

The historical record from Spain, South Africa, Poland, and the US Civil Rights Movement is consistent: structural democratic reform that permanently survives requires at least some support from actors within the opposing coalition — not because opposing coalition support is needed to pass reform, but because reforms perceived as purely partisan are systematically targeted for reversal when the political winds shift.

The minimum required cross-partisan elements differ by domain:
- Electoral reform (RCV, redistricting): requires Republican state legislators or governors in at least 3-5 states to demonstrate bipartisan viability
- Judicial independence: requires Republican former officials (judges, AGs, DOJ alumni) willing to publicly support ethics standards and term limits
- Civil service protection: requires business community support for regulatory stability and competent administration
- Campaign finance disclosure: requires polling evidence above 70% support in battleground states to provide political cost for opposition

The 2023 Polish coalition — which included parties from center-left to center-right, unified around democratic restoration but disagreeing on policy — is the operational model. That coalition's internal policy tensions are real and ongoing; they are also manageable because the shared commitment to democratic process provides a stable floor.
