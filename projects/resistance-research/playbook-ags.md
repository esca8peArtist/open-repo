## Executive Summary for Coalition Leads

State Attorneys General are the single most consequential institutional actor available for Wave 1 democratic defense. The 22-state Democratic AG coalition has filed 71 lawsuits against the Trump administration since January 2025 and has won approximately 79% of resolved cases, including the landmark *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (February 20, 2026), which invalidated IEEPA tariff authority in a 6-3 decision with Chief Justice Roberts writing for a cross-ideological majority.

This playbook moves past crisis response — the `crisis-response-ag-coalition.md` covers tactical 72-hour protocols — and into strategic 36-month planning: which domains to lead, how to sequence coalition expansion, how to build durable infrastructure beyond individual cases, and how to resolve the three major conflicts that can fracture the coalition.

The highest-priority task for any coalition lead reading this in mid-2026 is pre-election litigation preparation. The 2026 midterms represent the single highest-consequence window in the 36-month horizon. Pre-written TROs, venue analysis, and circuit assignment strategies should be complete by August 2026.

4. Maryland (AG Anthony Brown) — DOGE / federal data access track; *Maryland v. Noem* standing theory
5. Illinois, Colorado, Washington, Connecticut, Michigan — second wave; file within 48-72 hours of lead

**Litigation record (as of May 2026)**:
- 71 cases filed since January 2025

- Capacity: offices cannot litigate every crisis at full intensity simultaneously

## Domain Targeting: Five Highest-Leverage Areas

### Domain 1: Electoral Reform — Primary Lead

**Standing theory**: States have sovereign authority over election administration under Article I, Section 4 (Times, Places and Manner Clause). A federal executive action that overrides state election administration without congressional authorization violates structural federalism. Parens patriae standing for citizen voting rights under the 15th, 19th, and 24th Amendments.

**2026 priority actions**:

*Pre-election preparation (June–September 2026)*: Complete venue analysis for six election interference scenarios: (a) ICE deployment near polling sites; (b) federal voter roll purge orders; (c) mail ballot rejection directives; (d) refusal to certify state election results; (e) SAVE Act enforcement; (f) federal obstruction of local election administrators. For each scenario, pre-write TRO motion templates with blank fields for factual details. Venue assignment should be confirmed: D.D.C. and 2nd Circuit for northeastern states; 9th and 10th Circuit for western states.

*Shadow docket counter-strategy*: Simultaneous filing across four or five circuits, coordinated with Democracy Docket and ACLU Voting Rights Project. The Supreme Court faces higher political cost for a shadow docket stay that visibly halts TROs in five circuits than in one. Pre-assign "wave 1" and "wave 2" states so that filing sequence is automatic when a trigger event occurs.

*SAVE Act litigation*: AG James's coalition challenge to the SAVE Act's documentary voter registration requirements is the current lead action. Legal theory: the SAVE Act conflicts with the National Voter Registration Act (NVRA) and requires documentary proof of citizenship that the NVRA does not. The standing theory — states must redesign their registration systems, incurring administrative costs — is well established. Priority is getting to the merits before the 2026 registration deadlines.

**Measurement milestone (Month 9)**: At least four circuits have jurisdiction in place for emergency election interference TROs; SAVE Act challenge is at the merits stage.

### Domain 6: Judicial Independence — Coalition Support

**Standing theory**: State AGs can file amicus briefs in major independence cases and can challenge federal actions that damage state-agency cooperative relationships dependent on independent federal regulators. The *AFSCME v. SSA* model (Maryland AG: DOGE data access harmed Maryland's Medicaid administration) is the key standing template.

**Post-*Slaughter* architecture**: If the Supreme Court overrules *Humphrey's Executor* in the *Trump v. Slaughter* decision (expected June 2026), state AGs face immediate exposure. The independent federal agencies that state cooperative programs depend on — the EPA (cooperative enforcement with state EPAs), NLRB (state labor boards), FCC (state PUCs), CFPB (state consumer protection) — lose their independence protections. Pre-position for this ruling now:

- Week 1 post-ruling: File in D.D.C. and at least three circuits challenging the President's removal of specific commissioners who head agencies with direct state cooperative relationships
- Standing argument: Your state's cooperative program is immediately harmed by the removal of the commissioner who authorized your state's grant agreement
- Coordinate with ACLU, AFL-CIO, and state labor boards for parallel filings

**Measurement milestone (Month 6)**: Three pre-staged standing memos covering full overruling, functional narrowing, and prospective-only application of *Slaughter* — drafted, reviewed, ready to file within 72 hours of ruling.

### Domain 23: Trade / IEEPA — Completed Model, Now Template for Expansion

**Status**: *Learning Resources v. Trump* (February 20, 2026) — won. The 24-state AG coalition filed simultaneously with private plaintiffs in the Court of International Trade. SCOTUS held 6-3 that IEEPA does not authorize tariff imposition. Approximately $166 billion in tariff refunds ordered.

**Lessons for future coalition actions**:
1. **Simultaneous filing with private plaintiffs creates redundant standing theories** — the Court can reach the merits even if one track fails on procedural grounds
2. **Court of International Trade is the correct venue** for tariff challenges — AG coalition should have standing memos pre-drafted for CIT filing in any trade authority dispute
3. **6-3 cross-ideological majority demonstrates**: constitutional text arguments win even in this Court when the statutory overreach is clear
4. **Refund mechanism**: Having a clear refund remedy available in the CIT strengthened the case for standing and irreparable harm

**Template application**: Use the *Learning Resources* architecture (multistate simultaneous + private parallel + pre-staged academic record) for the next executive trade authority overreach. The next application may be IEEPA Section 232 national security tariffs, which have a weaker constitutional record than IEEPA and may be vulnerable to the same statutory authority argument.

**Measurement milestone**: Standing memos for Section 232 challenges drafted by Month 3.

### Domain 29: Prosecutorial Weaponization — State Civil Rights Parallel Track

**Standing constraint**: State AGs have limited standing in federal prosecution matters. The federal-state prosecutorial separation limits direct intervention. However, three available tracks exist:

*Track 1 — Amicus briefs*: AGs can file amicus briefs in federal prosecutorial misconduct cases. The SPLC criminal indictment (litigation-tracker-2026.md, Section 4.2) is the highest-priority current case for amicus coordination. The legal theory: selective prosecution on the basis of political viewpoint or the target organization's civil rights work violates the First Amendment. State AGs who have their own civil rights enforcement relationships with SPLC have concrete standing to argue the prosecution disrupts those relationships.

*Track 2 — State court parallel actions*: State AGs can bring state-court civil rights actions under state civil rights statutes against the same conduct targeted by weaponized federal prosecutions. If a federal prosecution targets an organization for immigration legal services, state AGs can bring state civil rights actions against the federal agents conducting the prosecution for violating state civil rights law. This creates a parallel state-court track that the federal court cannot enjoin.

*Track 3 — Joint investigation protection*: State AGs with ongoing joint DOJ investigations can challenge DOJ actions that compromise those investigations. If the administration tries to shut down a joint AG-DOJ human trafficking task force because the AG is politically disfavored, the AG has standing to challenge the disruption of the joint program.

**Measurement milestone (Month 6)**: At least five AGs have signed amicus brief in SPLC case; state-court parallel track theory is documented in a model brief template.

### Domain 34: Congressional Power of the Purse — Federal Fund Impoundment

**Standing theory**: States have standing to challenge federal fund impoundment when those funds are part of state-administered cooperative programs (Medicaid, Title I, SNAP, federal-state transportation programs). The *Pennhurst* line of cases establishes that the federal government cannot unilaterally alter the terms of cooperative grants after states have relied on them.

**DOGE fund impoundment specifics**: DOGE's mass impoundment of appropriated funds (identified in the litigation tracker) is directly actionable by states whose cooperative programs have been defunded. The APA provides the challenge vehicle: DOGE's impoundment decisions are final agency actions subject to arbitrary and capricious review. The standing theory: Massachusetts's Title IV-E foster care program receives federal matching funds under a cooperative agreement; DOGE's impoundment of those funds without statutory authority is an APA violation.

**Priority targets**: HHS cooperative programs (Medicaid, TANF, foster care); Department of Education (Title I, IDEA); EPA cooperative enforcement; DOL unemployment insurance matching. Map which states have active cooperative agreements in which programs and pre-assign lead AG for each program type.

**Measurement milestone (Month 9)**: All 22 coalition states have mapped their three highest-value federal cooperative agreements; TRO templates complete for top 10 cooperative program impoundment scenarios.

## 36-Month Action Plan

### Year 1, Phase 1: Foundation and Pre-Election Preparation (Months 1–6)

**Month 1: Litigation triage and domain assignment**

Convene a one-day coalition coordination session (secure video call; in-person at NAAG Public Interest Section meeting) with civil rights deputy AGs from all 22 states. Agenda:
- Map all 71 active cases to the domain framework
- Assign each domain a "lead AG office" with backup
- Identify the top 10 cases most likely to reach SCOTUS by June 2026 term end
- Review the *Learning Resources* architecture for template application

Action: Produce a one-page litigation triage matrix. Circulate to all 22 offices. This is the coalition's operational document; update it monthly.

**Month 2: Post-*Slaughter* pre-staging**

Pre-assign faculty and outside counsel to post-*Slaughter* analysis teams by ruling scenario. Coordinate with Yale Rule of Law Clinic, Harvard Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, and Just Security editorial team. Produce three pre-written challenge templates: (1) full overruling of *Humphrey's Executor*; (2) functional narrowing (for-cause removal still protected in specific contexts); (3) prospective-only application (prior removals upheld, future protection eliminated).

Action: Three standing memo drafts complete and ready to file within 72 hours of *Slaughter* ruling.

**Month 3: SAVE Act / voting rights pre-election architecture**

Complete pre-election litigation preparation for six election interference scenarios. Venue analysis complete. Circuit assignment protocols documented. TRO motion templates with blank factual fields ready. Democracy Docket parallel filing protocols agreed. ACLU Voting Rights Project parallel protocols agreed.

Action: One-page "Election Emergency Protocol" document distributed to all 22 AG offices and Democracy Docket. Each office knows its role: who files lead, who files wave 2, who coordinates media.

**Month 4: Coalition coordination with civil service unions**

AFGE and NTEU's federal worker litigation (Schedule Policy/Career) creates joint standing theory opportunities: states whose federal-state cooperative programs depend on specific career positions (EPA enforcement officers, Medicaid auditors, federal workforce development liaisons) have parens patriae standing in AFGE's Schedule Policy/Career challenge. Coordinate standing brief with AFGE/NTEU counsel.

Action: Two AG offices join AFGE's Second Amended Complaint as amici or co-plaintiffs on the cooperative program standing theory.

**Month 5: Domain 23 template expansion**

Using *Learning Resources* as the template, draft standing memos for Section 232 national security tariff challenges and for any IEEPA-adjacent executive trade authority claims. Coordinate with Constitutional Accountability Center and WilmerHale trade group.

Action: Section 232 challenge template complete; filed within 30 days of any new IEEPA-style executive trade action.

**Month 6: Mid-year litigation review**

Full coalition review of all 71 cases. Which are on track for SCOTUS cert? Which have been dismissed? Which need additional states to join for political weight? Which have shadow docket vulnerabilities?

Action: Updated litigation triage matrix; shadow docket vulnerability assessment completed for top 15 active cases.

### Year 1, Phase 2: Pre-Election Execution and Post-*Slaughter* Response (Months 7–12)

**Months 7–8: Election interference pre-positioning**

Assess 2026 midterm results:

- Which cases need to be transferred to backup states if a key state's AG changes?

**Month 10: Legislative advocacy activation**

Post-midterm, pivot from pure litigation to legislative advocacy in states with new or expanded Democratic legislative chambers. Priority legislation: state civil service protections mirroring federal Schedule Policy/Career protections; state voting access omnibus (Domain 1 provisions at state level); state Medicaid floor legislation protecting against federal OBBBA rollbacks.

**Month 11: Interstate compact architecture initiation**

Begin drafting model language for two interstate compact proposals: (1) a voting rights compact under which signatory states commit to specific voting access standards regardless of federal minimum; (2) a cooperative enforcement compact for environmental enforcement in states with EPA cooperative agreements being defunded.

**Month 12: Year 1 review and Year 2 planning**

Document lessons learned. Produce a public-facing one-year litigation summary (briefed to Democracy Docket, Just Security, SCOTUSblog). Internal metrics review: win rate, shadow docket losses, circuit court composition relative to expected SCOTUS cert grants.

### Year 2: Legislative Record and State-Law Floor Strategy (Months 12–24)

**Priority 1: State-law floor legislation**

The most durable product of Year 2 is state legislation that creates independent state-law floors for democratic protections that survive federal rollback. Work with state legislative allies to enact in each trifecta state:
- **Voting access omnibus**: Automatic voter registration, pre-registration at 16, permanent absentee lists, multilingual materials — the Minnesota 2023 model. Pre-draft omnibus for any state that achieves trifecta after 2026 midterms; have it ready for Day 1 of the 2027 session.
- **State civil service protection**: State statutory protection for state civil servants that mirrors Saving the Civil Service Act language; prevents state-level Schedule Policy/Career analogues.
- **State Medicaid floor**: State legislation establishing a minimum Medicaid eligibility floor independent of federal OBBBA changes.

**Priority 2: Interstate compact development**

Interstate compacts on voting rights, environmental enforcement, and data privacy are the Wave 3 alternative infrastructure mechanism. AG offices serve as the legal drafting resource for compact language. Target: at least three states sign the voting rights compact before the 2028 election cycle.

**Priority 3: Congressional record building**

AG offices should produce testimony for Saving the Civil Service Act, NLRB Independence Act, and PRESS Act hearings. The testimony frame: as the chief law enforcement officers of 22 states, coalition AGs have direct experience with the consequences of federal civil service degradation, NLRB capture, and press freedom erosion on state-administered programs. This is evidence-building for the post-2028 legislative wave.

**Measurement milestones (Year 2)**:
- At least four states enact voting access omnibus in 2027 sessions
- At least two states sign voting rights interstate compact
- AG coalition testimony submitted in three congressional hearings

### Year 3+: Constitutional Architecture (Months 24–48+)

**Constitutional amendment campaign support**

Four plausible constitutional amendments require state-level ratification campaigns organized through AG networks: (1) proportional representation / ranked-choice voting mandate; (2) Senate reform / equal population principle; (3) SCOTUS term limits and nonpartisan appointment commission; (4) civil service constitutional protection.

AG offices provide two functions in the ratification campaign: (1) legal drafting support for amendment language that survives SCOTUS challenge; (2) state legislative allies who can move ratification resolutions through chambers where Democrats have won majorities.

**Interstate compact final architecture**

The long-term goal is a multistate compact infrastructure that functions as a parallel governance layer: states with cooperative agreements in environmental enforcement, voting rights protection, consumer protection, and labor standards that operate independently of federal rollback. AG offices serve as the permanent legal infrastructure for this compact system.

**Target (Year 3)**: At least 15 states in a voting rights compact; at least 12 states in an environmental enforcement compact; compact infrastructure used as the model for international coordination with democratic allies (Canada, Mexico, EU member states on border and trade issues).

## Scenario Decision Trees

### Scenario A: SCOTUS issues *Trump v. Slaughter* — full overruling of *Humphrey's Executor*

**Trigger**: SCOTUS opinion released (expected June 2026).

**Hour 1–4**: Lead AGs (Massachusetts, New York, Maryland) convene on emergency call. Confirm which specific commissioners in which agencies serve state cooperative programs. Identify the weakest removal — the one with the clearest state harm.

**Hour 4–24**: File TRO in D.D.C. challenging removal of EPA Administrator if the removal is announced; alternatively, file pre-emptive challenge in the circuit where cooperative program harm is most direct. Standing argument: the removal of the EPA Administrator disrupts the cooperative enforcement agreement between the federal EPA and your state EPA, which is funded through a state appropriation that is now stranded.

**Day 2–7**: Wave 2 states file in their circuits. ACLU and Earthjustice file parallel private-plaintiff TROs. Law school clinics produce academic record within 72 hours.

**Day 8–30**: Press SCOTUS for administrative stay pending cert. If cert granted, prepare merits brief. Coordinate with labor unions (AFL-CIO NLRB standing), media organizations (FCC independence standing), and civil rights coalitions (CFPB standing).

---

### Scenario B: Federal election interference in 2026 midterms

**Trigger**: ICE near polling locations, voter roll purge order, or federal directive to reject mail ballots.

**Hour 1–2**: Identify lead state (state where interference is occurring + highest capacity office in affected circuit). Confirm circuit assignment.

**Hour 2–4**: Insert factual details into pre-written TRO template. File with the district court in the affected circuit. Simultaneously contact Democracy Docket and ACLU for parallel filing confirmation.

**Day 1–3**: Wave 2 states (four additional circuits) file. Multi-circuit simultaneous TROs are in place.

**Day 4–7**: If SCOTUS shadow docket stay is threatened, prepare emergency brief citing multi-circuit scope and imminent Election Day irreparable harm. Coordinate with legal academic community for rapid amicus brief (Harvard Election Law Clinic, Brennan Center) to file within 24 hours of any stay application.

---

### Scenario C: DOGE impounds funds for state cooperative program

**Trigger**: Federal agency notifies state that appropriated cooperative program funds have been impounded without statutory authority.

**Hour 1–24**: Confirm the impoundment decision is a "final agency action" under the APA (consult Administrative Conference of the United States model). Identify the specific statutory appropriation and the specific agency decision memo. File APA challenge in D.D.C. (venue: where the federal agency action occurred).

**Day 1–7**: Other states with the same cooperative program file in their home circuits. Coordinate to create multi-circuit simultaneous challenge.

**Day 8–30**: Move for preliminary injunction restoring funding pending merits decision. Coordinate with AFL-CIO (workers in cooperative programs affected), faith community hospitals (if Medicaid cooperative funding), and civil service unions (federal employees who administer the programs).

## Conflict Mitigation

### Conflict 1: AGs vs. Federal Courts on Universal Injunctions

**Tension**: State AGs often seek nationwide injunctions that protect their residents regardless of state. Federal courts, post-*Trump v. CASA*, have limited universal injunction authority. Some AG coalition members may prefer state-court actions that create local protection; others prefer federal courts with broader reach.

**Resolution**: The three-track strategy eliminates the either-or choice:
- Track 1: Federal APA vacatur (nationwide effect by statute — agency action is void, not just enjoined)
- Track 2: State-court actions under state law (available in individual states without universal injunction concerns)
- Track 3: Class action certification (class extending to all affected individuals in multiple states)

The coalition should pre-assign tracks by case type: immigration enforcement cases favor state-court track; statutory authority cases (IEEPA, SAVE Act) favor federal APA vacatur; civil service cases favor class action through AFGE's existing litigation.

### Conflict 2: Trade Policy Cross-Cutting Conflict

**Tension**: Federal civil servants who administer tariff and trade programs may be adversely affected by AG victories that eliminate tariff revenue. AFGE members include customs enforcement officers who depend on tariff administration for their jobs.

**Resolution**: No-targeting agreement between AG coalition and AFGE: AG coalition challenges the legal authority for tariffs, not the federal employees who administer them. AFGE publicly states that it supports the legal challenge as a rule-of-law matter (executive authority must be based on statute) while advocating for worker transition protections in any refund/elimination scenario.

### Conflict 3: AG Electoral Pressure in Competitive States

**Tension**: Democratic AGs in competitive states (Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Nevada) face electoral pressure from joining high-visibility coalition actions that can be characterized as partisan overreach.

**Resolution**: Frame-specific coalition composition. Not every state need join every action. Design coalition actions so that:
- Bipartisan standing is possible: Consumer protection and antitrust actions have Republican AG precedent (Ken Paxton joined antitrust cases; Josh Shapiro built antitrust reputation); recruit Republican AGs for these
- Competitive-state AGs lead on local-harm-specific cases (DOGE impoundment harming their state's cooperative programs) rather than national symbolic actions
- Provide political cover language: "As chief law enforcement officer of [State], I have a constitutional obligation to defend state appropriations and state cooperative programs from unlawful executive action."

## Resource Requirements

**Coalition coordination infrastructure**: DAGA and NAAG Public Interest Section already provide this. Incremental cost: one additional coalition coordinator (shared across offices); annual in-person strategy session ($25,000–$40,000 in travel and logistics).

**Outside counsel**: The coalition has established relationships with Protect Democracy, Democracy Docket, ACLU, WilmerHale, Munger Tolles, Covington & Burling. Pro bono capacity from BigLaw is substantial; the coalition should formalize pro bono agreements to ensure consistent availability.

**Law school clinic partnerships**: Harvard Election Law Clinic, Yale Rule of Law Clinic, NYU Constitutional Litigation Clinic should be on retainer (clinical participation agreements) for rapid amicus brief production. Cost: clinic participation agreements ($0–$5,000 per clinic; clinics benefit from the cases as student training).
