# Tier 1 Email Templates: Final Production Versions

**Built April 29, 2026. Copy-paste ready with bracket substitution.**

Five template variants, each optimized for a different type of relationship and ask. The correct template depends on what the contact can provide and what relationship is most appropriate given their institutional position. The guidance at the top of each template specifies which Tier 1 contacts it best fits.

All templates assume you have read the Email Personalization Guide (`EMAIL_PERSONALIZATION_GUIDE.md`) and spent 10-15 minutes on pre-research per recipient.

---

## Template 1: Meeting Request

**Best for**: Senate staff directors (Whitehouse, Klobuchar, Merkley offices), think tank directors (Weiser/Waldman at Brennan Center, Tanden at CAP, Reynolds at Brookings), civil society executive directors (Bassin at Protect Democracy, Lydgate at States United, Romero at ACLU)

**When to use this template**: When you want a specific call or meeting — not just a document review. Use this template when the contact's institutional role means their engagement is worth more as a conversation than as a written response.

**Time to personalize**: 20-30 minutes per email. The [RECENT WORK] and [SPECIFIC CONNECTION] fields are non-negotiable — a generic opening paragraph gets this template deleted.

---

**Subject line variants (choose one; the [BRACKETS] show where to personalize):**
1. "Meeting request: [Domain X] research — [specific relevance to their current work] — [15/30] minutes"
2. "[Organization name] work on [issue] — new research that may be useful — brief call?"
3. "Independent research on [domain] — [one striking finding] — request 20 minutes"

**Template body:**

---

[First name],

I'm reaching out because [RECENT WORK — cite a specific recent publication, hearing, bill, or public statement by this contact, by title or finding, within the last 6 months]. That work connects directly to a research framework I've completed, and I think 20 minutes would be enough for me to share what may be immediately useful to your [work/team/program].

I'm an independent researcher — not affiliated with any organization or institution. Over the past 18 months I've built a [28/35]-domain proposal for democratic structural reform, grounded in comparative constitutional law, 160+ movement case studies, and the evidentiary record from functioning democracies. The framework is published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 — free for any use.

The domain most directly relevant to your current work is [DOMAIN NAME AND NUMBER]. [Two sentences: what specific finding or analysis in that domain addresses something this contact is currently working on. Be specific. "Domain 28 covers the four statutory fixes the Venezuela operation exposed that Iran-focused AUMF reform does not address" is better than "Domain 28 covers war powers."]

[OPTIONAL — if their organization is cited in the framework: "Your [organization's/your] work on [topic] is cited throughout this section — the analysis builds directly on [specific contribution]."]

I have a specific question I'd value your judgment on: [ASK A GENUINE QUESTION — this signals intellectual honesty and gives the recipient a reason to engage. Examples: "Does the Domain 6 term limits analysis hold up under your reading of the 'inferior courts' clause cases?" or "Is the Brennan Center's analysis of the CASA ruling incorporated accurately in the Domain 6 universal injunction section?"]

Would you be willing to take a [15/30]-minute call at your convenience? I'm available [AVAILABILITY — give 2-3 specific windows, not "anytime"]. Alternatively, I'm happy to send the relevant domain brief by email if a call isn't feasible.

Full framework: [LINK]
Executive summary (2 pages): [LINK]

Thank you for your time.

[YOUR NAME]
[CONTACT INFORMATION — email and phone; include time zone]

---

**Personalization checklist before sending:**
- [ ] [FIRST NAME] filled in correctly
- [ ] [RECENT WORK] is a specific, accurate citation — title, date, finding
- [ ] [DOMAIN NAME AND NUMBER] is the correct domain for this contact's work (cross-check with TIER1_OUTREACH_STRATEGY.md)
- [ ] The two-sentence domain relevance paragraph is specific, not generic
- [ ] [GENUINE QUESTION] is a real question, not a rhetorical one
- [ ] [AVAILABILITY] gives 2-3 specific windows in the next 2 weeks
- [ ] [LINK] is tested and functional before sending
- [ ] BCC to tracking address

## Template 2: Feedback Request

**Best for**: Academic researchers (Chenoweth, Levitsky, Balkin, Stephanopoulos, Allen, Fung), law school clinic directors (Schwartztol, Greenwood, Johnson/CDI), law review editors

**When to use this template**: When the most valuable thing the contact can provide is scholarly correction or validation. Academics are far more comfortable providing feedback than providing endorsement. This template positions you as a researcher seeking peer review, not a researcher seeking a platform.

**Time to personalize**: 20-30 minutes per email. The [THEIR WORK CITED] field is critical — if you cite their work incorrectly, they will know immediately and the request for feedback will be read as careless.

---

**Subject line variants:**
1. "Independent research cites your work on [specific topic] — seeking scholarly feedback"
2. "35-domain democratic reform framework — [domain X] analysis — academic review requested"
3. "Research on [their specific topic] — builds on your [paper/book/testimony] — feedback valued"

**Template body:**

---

[First name],

Your work on [THEIR SPECIFIC WORK — cite by title and year, with one-sentence description of the relevant finding] is cited in Domain [X] of a democratic reform framework I've built — and I'm reaching out because I want to make sure the citation is accurate and that the analysis builds correctly on your research.

I'm an independent researcher — not affiliated with any institution. The framework is a [28/35]-domain proposal for democratic structural reform, grounded in comparative constitutional law, movement case studies, and the evidentiary record. It's published under CC 4.0 — free to cite, adapt, and use.

The analysis that relies most directly on your work: [SPECIFIC ANALYSIS — 2-3 sentences describing how their work is incorporated, including what claim it is used to support. Example: "The resistance meta-analysis section uses your 3.5% threshold as the central organizing metric for the current-moment analysis, and specifically cites your finding that no nonviolent movement with sustained participation above that threshold has ever failed. The analysis also incorporates the evolving 'End of People Power' work's complicating argument about whether the historical threshold holds in high-information, high-repression environments."]

I have two specific questions where your judgment would be most valuable:

1. [SPECIFIC SCHOLARLY QUESTION — this must be a genuine question about whether your analysis is correct, not a rhetorical validation-seeking question. Example: "Is the efficiency gap framing still the most defensible measure of partisan gerrymander severity after Rucho, or has the scholarly consensus shifted toward a different standard?"]

2. [SECOND QUESTION — optional; only include if genuinely useful. Do not pad.]

I am not asking for endorsement — only for correction if something is wrong, and for any observations about gaps in the analysis from your perspective.

Full domain document: [LINK]
Full [28/35]-domain proposal: [LINK]

Thank you for any time you can give this. The framework is at a distribution stage where scholarly feedback is most valuable — before widespread circulation, while there is still time to correct errors.

[YOUR NAME]

---

**Personalization checklist before sending:**
- [ ] [THEIR SPECIFIC WORK] is accurate — author(s), title, year, and the specific finding being cited
- [ ] [SPECIFIC ANALYSIS] correctly describes how their work is incorporated
- [ ] [SPECIFIC SCHOLARLY QUESTION] is a genuine, answerable question that only they could answer well
- [ ] You have actually verified that the citation in the domain document matches what they published
- [ ] No flattery or promotional language in the subject line or body

## Template 3: Speaking Opportunity

**Best for**: Civil society executive directors with active event calendars (Romero/ACLU, Nelson/NAACP LDF, Greenberg/Indivisible, Brest/ACS), policy school directors (Fung/Ash Center, Allen/HKS), conference organizers (APSA, ACS National Convention)

**When to use this template**: When the contact has a platform that could host a presentation or panel. This template is the lightest ask of the five — it does not require the contact to read the full framework, only to consider whether a presentation would be useful to their members/audience. It is the best template for civil society leaders who have large organizational memberships.

---

**Subject line variants:**
1. "Speaking opportunity inquiry: democratic structural reform research — [organization name] audience"
2. "Presentation offer: [28/35]-domain democratic renewal research — [conference/event]"
3. "Research presentation for [organization]: comparative constitutional reform evidence"

**Template body:**

---

[First name],

I'm writing to offer a presentation of a research framework I've completed on democratic structural reform — and to ask whether [ORGANIZATION NAME]'s [SPECIFIC CONTEXT — conference, chapter meeting, policy event, speaker series] would be an appropriate venue.

The framework is a [28/35]-domain proposal grounded in 160+ movement case studies and comparative evidence from 40+ functioning democracies. The most relevant domains for your organization's current work are [DOMAIN 1 NAME] and [DOMAIN 2 NAME] — [one sentence each on why these domains are specifically relevant to this organization's mission and current campaigns].

A 30-45 minute presentation would cover:
- The crisis diagnostic (U.S. democracy score at 57/100; why design flaws, not just bad actors, are the root cause)
- The evidence base (what proportional representation produced in New Zealand; what sectoral bargaining produced in Germany; what media literacy investment produced in Finland; what constitutional civil service protections produced in Poland — the documented outcomes from each major reform category)
- The 2026 midterm window and what it controls for structural reform
- Q&A

All materials are CC 4.0 — free to distribute, share, and use for any organizational purpose.

I can present in person or remotely, at whatever date and format works best for your organization's schedule. I don't require speaking fees or travel support (though if your organization typically provides these I'm happy to discuss).

Would a presentation be useful for your [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE — chapter membership, policy staff, conference attendees]? If so, I'd love to discuss format and timing.

Full proposal and executive summary: [LINK]

[YOUR NAME]
[CONTACT INFORMATION]

---

**Personalization checklist before sending:**
- [ ] [ORGANIZATION NAME] and [SPECIFIC CONTEXT] are accurate — research the organization's event calendar before sending
- [ ] The two domain names are the correct ones for this organization (cross-check with TIER1_OUTREACH_STRATEGY.md)
- [ ] The one-sentence domain relevance notes are specific, not generic
- [ ] The presentation format (30-45 min) is appropriate for this organization's typical events
- [ ] You are clear about the "no fee required" offer if that is accurate

## Template 4: Co-Publication

**Best for**: Think tank fellows and researchers (Reynolds/Brookings, Tucker/Roosevelt, McNicholas/EPI), law review editors, journalists at policy outlets (Goodman/Just Security, Glastris/Washington Monthly, Beauchamp/Vox), Substack newsletter authors with aligned audiences

**When to use this template**: When you are proposing joint work — either an issue brief that the institution publishes under its brand, a co-authored analysis, a submitted piece adapted for their publication format, or a symposium contribution. This template makes the clearest offer of the five and requires the clearest understanding of what the contact's publication standards require.

**Note**: Do not use this template for Tier 1 academic contacts unless they have an active publication pipeline that would benefit from collaboration. Use Template 2 (Feedback Request) for academics; co-publication conversations with academics flow naturally from successful feedback exchanges.

---

**Subject line variants:**
1. "Co-publication proposal: [domain X] analysis adapted for [outlet/journal/brief format]"
2. "[Organization] issue brief opportunity: [domain topic] — independent research base available"
3. "Submission inquiry: [topic] analysis for [Just Security / Washington Monthly / law review]"

**Template body:**

---

[First name],

I'm reaching out with a proposal for a co-publication that I think would be a strong fit for [OUTLET/INSTITUTION NAME]'s [SPECIFIC FORMAT — issue brief / policy brief / online essay / symposium / law review note].

I've completed Domain [X] of a [28/35]-domain democratic reform framework — a [2-3 sentence summary of the specific domain content and its core analytical contribution]. The domain is grounded in [key sources — 3-4 cited specifically] and reaches [1-2 sentence summary of the most significant analytical conclusions].

I'm proposing this as [SPECIFIC FORMAT — an issue brief adapted for [institution]'s policy brief format / a short essay for Just Security / an op-ed for Washington Monthly / a symposium piece for [law review]]. I believe the content fits [OUTLET NAME]'s audience because [SPECIFIC REASON — "Just Security's readership is exactly the national security law community this domain's reform proposals need to reach" or "EPI issue briefs on labor reform reach the AFL-CIO legislative program directly"].

What I am offering:
- The domain analysis as source material, including all citations and source links
- A draft adapted to [OUTLET NAME]'s format and word count
- The underlying research files for fact-checking and editorial review
- CC 4.0 license — [OUTLET/INSTITUTION] would have full rights to publish, adapt, and distribute under its own brand

What I would request:
- [OUTLET/INSTITUTION]'s standard editorial review
- Publication under [OUTLET/INSTITUTION]'s brand (which would extend the research's credibility and reach to your existing readership)
- Attribution to the original framework in the piece or in a footnote

[OPTIONAL: "If this format doesn't fit your current publication program, I'm happy to discuss alternatives — a guest post, a citation in a forthcoming piece your team is working on, or simply using the domain analysis as a research resource."]

Full domain document: [LINK]
Full [28/35]-domain proposal: [LINK]
Executive summary: [LINK]

Would this be a useful collaboration? I'm happy to send a draft adapted to your format if that would be more useful than the source document.

[YOUR NAME]

---

**Personalization checklist before sending:**
- [ ] [OUTLET/INSTITUTION NAME] and [SPECIFIC FORMAT] are accurate — verify the outlet actually publishes this format
- [ ] The domain summary is accurate and specific
- [ ] The reason for fit is genuine, not generic
- [ ] You understand the outlet's typical word count and have either drafted to that length or noted you will
- [ ] CC 4.0 offer is clearly stated

## Template 5: Academic Review

**Best for**: Law review editors (Harvard Law Review, Yale Law Journal, Columbia Law Review, Georgetown Law Journal, NYU Law Review); policy school research directors; APSA abstract submission process; academic conference organizers

**When to use this template**: When the contact can provide the most valuable form of engagement — formal peer review, law review publication, or conference presentation — that establishes the framework's scholarly credentials. This template makes the lightest pitch and the clearest scholarly offer: here is research that has not been formally reviewed; here is what a peer review contribution from you would add to it.

---

**Subject line variants:**
1. "Law review symposium/online forum inquiry: comparative constitutional analysis of democratic reform"
2. "Peer review request: [28/35]-domain democratic renewal framework — [specific domain] analysis"
3. "APSA/conference abstract: resistance meta-analysis + comparative constitutional reform framework"

**Template body:**

---

[First name / Editor-in-Chief / Submissions Editor],

I'm writing to inquire whether [LAW REVIEW / JOURNAL / CONFERENCE NAME] would be interested in [a symposium piece / an online forum contribution / a note submission / a conference paper] on [TOPIC — e.g., "comparative constitutional analysis of judicial independence reform" or "the structural accountability gap in presidential removal mechanisms"].

I've completed a [28/35]-domain framework for democratic structural reform, and Domain [X] addresses [TOPIC] with [2-3 sentence description of the analytical contribution and what makes it distinct from existing scholarship — what question does it answer that existing work hasn't addressed?].

[FOR LAW REVIEW: "The analysis addresses a gap in the existing literature: [SPECIFIC GAP]. The Rucho majority's rejection of partisan gerrymandering claims as nonjusticiable political questions, for example, has produced extensive scholarship on what courts can't do but little comparative analysis of what Congress can do — specifically, what the Article I § 4 'time, place and manner' authority permits and whether a federal proportional representation mandate is constitutional. Domain 1 fills that gap with comparative analysis of what 40+ countries have done and a specific constitutional argument for the U.S. mandate."]

[FOR CONFERENCE: "I'm proposing a paper titled '[TITLE]' for the [conference name] [session topic] panel. The paper draws on a dataset of 160 resistance movements (1917-2024) and comparative constitutional analysis of 40+ functioning democracies to address [RESEARCH QUESTION]."]

The framework is published under CC 4.0 — it is available for citation and is not submitted for exclusive review elsewhere. I would not be asking [law review / conference] to consider exclusive publication rights; I am proposing a contribution that would be open-access on publication.

Full domain document: [LINK]
Full [28/35]-domain proposal: [LINK]

I'd welcome guidance on whether this fits your current submission interests, and I'm happy to prepare a submission in your standard format if it does.

[YOUR NAME]

---

**Personalization checklist before sending:**
- [ ] [LAW REVIEW / JOURNAL / CONFERENCE NAME] is accurate
- [ ] The specific gap in existing literature is real and not already filled by a recent publication (do a quick search before sending)
- [ ] The proposed contribution type (symposium, forum, note, paper) matches what this outlet actually publishes
- [ ] CC 4.0 offer and open access note is included — this makes the proposal more attractive to law reviews that want widely available scholarship

## Subject Line Psychology Guide

Three psychological levers that increase open rates for professional outreach emails:

**Specificity**: "Domain 28 war powers analysis" outperforms "democratic reform research." "Operation Absolute Resolve OLC memo" outperforms "Venezuela."  "Your work on the efficiency gap" outperforms "your redistricting scholarship." The subject line should contain something that only applies to this email, not any email.

**Urgency without alarm**: "Accreditation rulemaking window closes May 2026" is an urgency frame that is accurate and specific — not manufactured urgency. "The 2026 midterm window" is a legitimate time constraint. Avoid artificial urgency ("Don't miss this opportunity!") — it reads as spam.

**Curiosity**: "How the OLC memo delegated its own constitutional determination to the president" creates a knowledge gap. "What four statutory fixes the Venezuela case exposed" implies specific information the reader doesn't have. Subject lines that describe a finding rather than a category perform better with academic and policy audiences.

**What to avoid**: Subject lines that could apply to any email ("Sharing my research"; "Important research opportunity"); subject lines longer than 55 characters (gets cut off in email previews); all-caps or punctuation emphasis ("URGENT: Democratic reform crisis"); the word "partnership" in the subject line (reads as sales email in professional contexts).

## Response Handling Templates

**Auto-reply (if you set one during high-volume outreach periods):**
"Thank you for your message. I'm responding to all inquiries in the order received — I'll reply within 3-5 business days. If you are writing about the Democratic Renewal Project framework, the full proposal is at [link] and the executive summary is at [link]. Both are CC 4.0 — free to cite and use."

**Calendar holding template (when a meeting is requested but no date is set):**
"Thank you for your interest — I'd be glad to schedule a call. My availability in the next two weeks: [3 specific time slots]. A 20-minute call would be enough to cover the most relevant domain analysis for your work; I can extend if useful. [Calendly link or scheduling preference.] Please confirm a time and I'll send a calendar invitation."

**Non-response follow-up sequence:**

*Day 14 follow-up (only if no response):*

[First name],

I wanted to follow up briefly on the email I sent [DATE]. I shared Domain [X] of a democratic reform framework I've built — [one-sentence description]. I realize your schedule is demanding, and I don't want to be a persistent inbox presence.

If the research isn't useful to your current work, no problem at all. If it is, I'm happy to send a shorter domain brief rather than the full document — sometimes a 1-page summary is more useful than a longer analysis.

The full framework is at [link]. Executive summary at [link].

[YOUR NAME]

*No further follow-up after Day 14.* If there is no response to the follow-up, the contact received the email and chose not to respond. Further emails will not change that outcome and will damage the relationship if any future contact happens.

**Handling substantive objections:**

If a contact responds with a specific factual objection ("The Thompson v. United States analysis in Domain 29 is incorrect — the case only applies to § 1014, not § 1343 wire fraud"), do two things:

1. Thank them immediately and specifically: "Thank you — this is exactly the kind of practitioner feedback that improves the analysis."
2. Verify the objection independently before updating the document. If they are correct, update the relevant domain document, note the correction with attribution, and send a corrected version to anyone who has already received that domain. If they are incorrect (or partially correct), respond with the specific counter-analysis and ask for their further input.

Never defend an error. Never ignore a substantive objection. Both responses damage the research's credibility more than the error itself would.
