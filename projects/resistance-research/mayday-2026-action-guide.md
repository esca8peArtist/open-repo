## 1. What Is May Day 2026

### The Historical Root

May Day is not a European import that Americans observe at a distance. It originated here.

On May 1, 1886, between 300,000 and 500,000 American workers walked off the job in a coordinated national strike demanding an eight-hour workday. Chicago was the center of gravity, with more than 40,000 workers in the streets. Three days later, on May 4, an unidentified assailant threw a dynamite bomb at police during a rally at Haymarket Square. Seven officers and at least four workers died. Eight labor organizers were convicted of conspiracy on evidence that has since been widely repudiated — several had not even been present at the May 4 rally.

The Haymarket affair became a global symbol of state repression of labor organizing. In 1889, the Second International designated May 1 as International Workers' Day in its memory. Today, May Day is a public holiday in more than 80 countries. The United States is one of the few industrialized democracies that does not formally recognize it — a deliberate choice. In 1894, President Grover Cleveland signed legislation establishing the first Monday in September as Labor Day, in part to displace the radical associations of May 1.

The suppression worked, mostly. For most of the 20th century, May Day in the United States was associated with Cold War communism and largely abandoned by mainstream labor. The AFL-CIO moved away from it. May 1 became just another workday.

Two moments revived it. In 2006, an estimated 1.5 million immigrants and their supporters marched on May 1 under the slogan "A Day Without Immigrants," in response to House-passed legislation that would have made undocumented presence a felony. The action was the largest single-day demonstration in American history at that point. Organizers called for a work stoppage and consumer boycott alongside marches — the same three-pillar structure being used in 2026. It worked as political demonstration even if it did not stop the legislation.

The second revival was 2017, when May Day actions returned following the first months of the Trump administration's first term, drawing hundreds of thousands in cities across the country.

May Day 2026 is the third act.

### The 2026 Democracy Crisis Context

What makes 2026 different from prior May Days is the specific character of the emergency being responded to.

The 2025-2026 period has produced an accelerating set of executive actions that have functionally challenged the separation of powers: mass federal workforce reduction through DOGE, deportation of legal residents without due process, refusal to comply with court orders (the Boasberg contempt probe was terminated by the DC Circuit on April 14 through a ruling that limits courts' ability to hold executive officials in criminal contempt), and sustained pressure on independent federal agencies. Courts have continued to rule against the administration on multiple fronts — the Supreme Court unanimously ordered the administration to facilitate the return of Kilmar Abrego Garcia in April 2026, and SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariff authority — but the distance between winning in court and prevailing in fact has grown.

The research literature on democratic backsliding (Levitsky and Ziblatt, Chenoweth and Stephan) is explicit on this point: when institutional safeguards erode faster than civil society can activate, the question is whether civil society can produce the sustained pressure that courts and legislatures cannot. May Day 2026 is an explicit attempt to test that capacity.

Erica Chenoweth's research on 323 mass mobilizations from 1900 to 2006 found that every nonviolent campaign that mobilized at least 3.5 percent of a country's population was uniformly successful in achieving its stated goals — and that nonviolent campaigns were more than twice as likely to succeed as violent ones. For the United States in 2026, 3.5 percent is roughly 11.5 million people. May Day 2026 is not going to hit that number on a single day. But that is not the point. The point is whether one day of action becomes the kind of organizational infrastructure — rosters, relationships, tested capacity — that can sustain pressure over time.

### What Makes This Different From Past May Days

Three things distinguish May Day 2026 from prior recent iterations:

**Scale of the pre-existing coalition.** The 200+ organization coalition was not assembled specifically for May Day. It grew out of the No Kings day-of-action organizing that preceded it. The NEA, National Nurses United, and Chicago Teachers Union represent millions of people with existing organizational capacity — meeting schedules, communication channels, membership rolls — not just social media followings.

**The explicit three-pillar structure.** "No School. No Work. No Shopping." This is not a march. It is an attempt at an economic action with three distinct levers: labor market disruption (work stoppage), educational disruption (school walkout), and consumer market disruption (boycott). Each pillar reaches a different population and creates a different kind of pressure on a different set of institutions.

**The explicit multi-year framing.** Organizers are not treating May 1, 2026 as an endpoint. The UAW's Shawn Fain has called for strikes across the labor movement on May Day 2028. DSA's post-May-Day analysis framework explicitly treats 2026 as a capacity test for 2027 and 2028. The question being asked is not "can we have a big day?" but "can we build the organizational depth to sustain escalation over years?"

## 2. The Demands and the Coalition

### The Three Demands

May Day Strong has organized the action around three explicit political demands, not merely a procedural demand for "democracy":

**1. Tax the rich — workers over billionaires**
Redirect economic policy from wealth concentration toward working families. The specific policy ask is not defined with legislative precision; the demand is directional.

**2. No ICE. No war. No private army serving authoritarian power**
Halt mass deportation operations, end the use of military-adjacent private security assets against domestic populations, and oppose executive actions that circumvent congressional war powers.

**3. Expand democracy and protect voting rights**
Protect electoral access, oppose executive interference with elections and election administration, and resist rollback of voting rights.

These demands are broader and more politically explicit than standard labor bargaining demands. This is intentional. May Day Strong is framing the action as a civic coalition action, not a labor dispute — which has implications for who can participate and under what legal frameworks.

**What is NOT confirmed:**

The AFL-CIO's participation is through constituent bodies — NC AFL-CIO, Milwaukee Labor Council — and implicit alignment, but the federation has not made a federation-wide strike call. This is the key distinction between what is happening on May 1, 2026 and what labor historians would call a "general strike" in the classic industrial sense.

This distinction matters practically: without a formal AFL-CIO national strike call, unionized workers do not have federation backing for a work stoppage, and individual unions without explicit local authorization are acting on their own or their members' initiative. It affects risk calculations, especially for workers in no-strike-clause contracts. See Section 4 (Know Before You Go) for the legal analysis.

### The Three-Pillar Structure

**Pillar 1: Work Stoppage**
The core ask is to not go to work on May 1. This takes different forms depending on employment situation:
- Union workers may strike, use personal days, or call in sick depending on their contract and local leadership's position
- Non-union workers face a different risk calculus (see Section 4)
- Teachers in participating districts may have organized school-level actions
- Students are asked not to attend school

**Pillar 2: Consumer Boycott**
Do not spend money at large retail or corporate establishments on May 1. If purchases are necessary, direct them to locally owned small businesses. The boycott is explicitly not "buy nothing" — it is "buy differently." The economic theory is that a concentrated one-day absence of consumer spending at large retailers is measurable and visible, and creates an organizational demonstration of economic leverage.

**Pillar 3: Demonstrations**
Attend a local rally, march, teach-in, or demonstration on May 1. These are the visible public manifestation of the action. Hundreds of events are planned nationally. The demonstration component is accessible to people who cannot participate in the work stoppage or boycott.

### General Strike vs. Civic Coalition Action — Why the Distinction Matters

A general strike, in the labor-history sense, is an action called by formal union federations or councils that stops economic activity across industries. The 1926 UK General Strike, the 1936 French general strike, the 1946 Oakland General Strike — these were called by union bodies with existing contracts and strike funds, and they stopped production across sectors.

May Day 2026 is a civic coalition action with a work stoppage element. This is a meaningful distinction, not a dismissal:

- It is accessible to people without union membership (the vast majority of American workers — union density in the U.S. is currently around 10%)
- It does not require formal labor law procedures (notice periods, authorization votes) that a traditional strike does
- It can include retirees, students, unemployed people, and community members who have no employment relationship to leverage
- It is legally more complex for employers to suppress, because it is not clearly a "strike" in the NLRA sense

The practical result is that May Day 2026 can involve a much larger population than a traditional labor action while providing somewhat weaker protections for individual workers who participate. Both things are true simultaneously.

## 3. How to Participate

The premise of this section is that not everyone can do everything, and that is fine. The goal is for you to make an informed choice about what level of participation makes sense for your situation — not to maximize any single action.

### Work Stoppage Options

**Option 1: Use PTO or a personal/vacation day**
The lowest-risk path for most workers. Request time off in advance, use accrued paid leave, and you have no employment exposure. The limitation: not everyone has PTO, and for some workers a paid day off feels like it defeats the purpose. It does not — removing your labor from the economy for a day, regardless of how you accomplish it, registers the same in payroll and productivity data.

**Option 2: Call in sick**
Higher risk than using PTO because sick day policies vary, and some employers investigate patterns. Coordinated sick-outs across a workplace are harder for employers to discipline than individual absences — this is the West Virginia teachers' model (2018 wildcat strike). If several coworkers are considering this, the risk is distributed. If you are the only person in your workplace participating, your exposure is higher.

**Option 3: Negotiate with your employer in advance**
Some employers — particularly smaller ones or those with sympathetic management — may agree to close for the day, allow flexible scheduling, or permit staff to attend demonstrations. Some businesses have publicly announced they will close on May 1. This is worth asking, especially if you frame it around worker and community wellbeing rather than political opposition.

**Option 4: Attend a rally or march before or after your shift**
If you cannot miss work, this is still participation. Morning demonstrations, lunch-hour rallies, and evening marches are available in many cities. The consumer boycott can be observed regardless of work status — just don't spend money at large corporate establishments during the day.

**What to do when negotiating with your employer:**
- Frame the conversation around your rights as an employee to engage in lawful civic activity
- You are not required to tell your employer you are participating in May Day specifically
- If your employer threatens you for discussing participation with coworkers, that may be an NLRA violation (see Section 4)

### Consumer Boycott — What to Avoid, What to Do Instead

**Avoid on May 1:**
- Amazon (including Amazon Fresh, Whole Foods owned by Amazon, and Amazon delivery)
- Walmart and Sam's Club
- Target
- Major chain grocery stores
- Large fast food chains (McDonald's, Starbucks corporate stores, Chick-fil-A)
- Online retail generally
- Major e-commerce platforms

**The intent is not to go without — it is to redirect:**
- If you need groceries, buy from a locally owned grocery or co-op the day before
- If you want coffee, find a locally owned cafe
- For any other purchase, check whether there is an independently owned alternative
- Local food co-ops, community-supported agriculture (CSA) pickup, and farmer's markets are affirmative alternatives

**The economic logic:**
Consumer boycotts work when they are concentrated, visible, and measurable. A one-day 20% drop in sales at major retailers is statistically detectable and generates internal management concern, even if it does not hit quarterly earnings materially. The signal matters as much as the dollar amount. For every $100 spent at a locally owned store, approximately $68 stays in the community; for chain stores, approximately $43. The boycott is also an affirmative investment in local economic infrastructure.

**What if you have no local alternatives?**
Buying nothing is an acceptable substitute. Stock up on May 30 (April 30) if needed. The goal is a measurable single-day reduction in corporate retail activity.

Demonstrators are photographed by press, by law enforcement, and by other participants. You have no legal right to prevent this in a public space. If you prefer not to be identifiable, wearing a mask is legal in most jurisdictions (pandemic-era anti-mask laws have mostly been repealed or narrowed, though check your specific state). Do not assume you are not being recorded.

Not everyone can be physically present or can afford to take a day off work. Digital participation is real participation if it amplifies the action and extends its reach:

- Donate to participating organizations' strike funds or mutual aid funds

**Online-only visibility actions:**

**For those outside the U.S.:**

- Even a coordinated social media post in solidarity at a specific time registers as international attention

## 4. Know Before You Go

This section covers legal realities, not reassurances. Read it before you decide what level of participation makes sense.

### Legal Rights at Demonstrations

**First Amendment baseline:**
The First Amendment protects peaceful assembly and speech on public property — sidewalks, parks, public plazas. You have the right to protest in these spaces without government permission, though gatherings above a certain size often require a permit for march routes. Check whether your local event has a permit; permitted marches have somewhat stronger legal standing if police attempt to disperse them.

**What police can do:**
- Enforce lawful permit conditions (route, time limits, noise ordinances)
- Order a crowd to disperse if they declare an unlawful assembly — this must be a specific, audible order
- Arrest individuals engaging in illegal conduct (blocking traffic without authorization, vandalism, assault)
- Move protesters out of specific areas for legitimate public safety reasons

**What police cannot do:**
- Arrest you for the content of your speech (viewpoint discrimination is unconstitutional)
- Disperse a peaceful assembly without specific cause
- Prohibit you from recording their actions in public spaces
- Search you without probable cause or consent (you can refuse a search; say clearly "I do not consent to this search")

**Your right to record:**
You have the right to record police performing their duties in public spaces, provided you do not physically interfere with their actions. Do not grab an officer's arm, block their path, or get within arm's reach while recording. Verbal questions and documentation from a safe distance are protected.

**If you are ordered to disperse:**
Comply promptly. A dispersal order, once given, makes remaining in the area subject to arrest regardless of whether you personally are doing anything wrong. The time to contest the order's legality is in court, not on the street.

**If you are detained:**
- Ask clearly: "Am I being detained or am I free to go?" If free to go, calmly leave.
- If detained: "I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer." Then say nothing further.
- You do not have to answer questions about your name in most states (some states have "stop and identify" laws; check your state). You do not have to answer questions about where you are going, where you have been, or what you are doing.
- Do not lie to police — lying to federal agents is a crime; false statements to local police may be as well. Silence is your right; use it.
- Write down badge numbers and officer names as soon as possible after any interaction.

**Legal observer resources:**
The National Lawyers Guild (NLG) provides legal observers at many demonstrations — they wear bright green hats. If arrested, NLG operates a jail support hotline in many cities. Find your local NLG chapter at nlg.org before May 1 and save their number.

### Undocumented Participants & ICE Enforcement Risk

This subsection is written directly for undocumented community members and their families. The risks here are different in kind from those facing documented participants — not just higher in degree — and they deserve honest, specific treatment.

The watch brief for this period explicitly flags ICE enforcement at or near demonstrations as a likely intimidation tactic given the "No ICE" demand at the center of May Day 2026. There is documented precedent in 2026: protesters in multiple cities reported being called by name by federal agents and followed home; a Marshall Project investigation confirmed federal retaliatory surveillance of demonstration observers. This is the threat environment you are operating in.

**Before you go**

Assess your local enforcement environment before deciding to attend in person. ICE operational tempo has been elevated in most major metros since January 2026. Check with local rapid-response networks (see resources below) for current enforcement patterns in your area.

Attending a public demonstration creates a record even if you are never arrested or detained. You may appear in press photographs, law enforcement surveillance footage, or participant-shared social media. Mixed-status families should make this decision together, accounting for the risk profile of every family member — not just the person considering attending. A parent's detention affects documented children; a spouse's immigration record can affect a partner's pending case.

If you decide not to attend in person, your participation through the consumer boycott and digital solidarity actions (Section 3) is real and carries none of the in-person enforcement risk.

**Your rights if encountered by ICE — the four absolutes:**

1. You do not have to answer questions. This applies regardless of your immigration status. Say: "I am exercising my right to remain silent."
2. You do not have to consent to a search. Say: "I do not consent to a search."
3. You do not have to sign any document. Do not sign anything — including what ICE may describe as "voluntary departure" paperwork — without speaking to a lawyer first. Signing can waive rights you do not know you have.
4. You have the right to speak to a lawyer. Say: "I want to speak to a lawyer." Then say nothing further until you have one.

These rights apply on a public street, at a demonstration, and inside a detention facility. They apply regardless of documentation status.

**At the action**

Use the buddy system — it is more important for undocumented participants than for anyone else at the demonstration. Your buddy should know your legal name (the name that will appear in any detention record), your emergency contact outside the country if applicable, and the name of an immigration attorney or legal aid organization you have contacted in advance. Buddy pairs with mixed language capacity are especially valuable: identify who in your group can communicate clearly in English with law enforcement if an encounter occurs.

Know how you will leave before you arrive. Identify two exit routes from the assembly point. If police or ICE personnel move toward the crowd, do not wait to see what happens — move to the exit immediately and calmly. The time to assess the situation is from a distance, not inside it.

Understand the distinction between a local police stop and an ICE encounter. Local police, in many sanctuary cities, are restricted from inquiring about immigration status or cooperating with ICE civil detainers. ICE agents are not restricted. If you are unsure who is detaining you, ask clearly: "Are you ICE or local police?" The answer determines your immediate legal situation.

On what to carry: do not bring documents that would confirm your immigration status and that you cannot afford to have seized — this includes foreign passports, consular ID cards, or immigration paperwork you are not legally required to carry. A state-issued ID (if you have one) is generally safer to carry than documents showing your country of origin.

**If someone is detained at the demonstration**

Do not attempt to physically intervene in a detention. Document what you see: badge numbers, vehicle plates, time, location, and the name of the person detained if you know it. Call the immigration rapid-response hotline for your area immediately (see resources below). Call Freedom for Immigrants' National Immigration Detention Hotline: 1-209-757-3733 (Monday through Friday, 8 a.m. to 8 p.m. Pacific). Detained individuals can reach the hotline from inside detention facilities at 9233#.

Notify the detainee's family and their designated immigration attorney as soon as possible. The first hours after a detention are the most critical for initiating legal response.

**After the action**

If you were detained and released, contact an immigration attorney before taking any other action. An arrest record — even one that results in no criminal charges — can surface in future immigration proceedings, including green card applications, naturalization, and removal proceedings. This is true even if you were acquitted or charges were dropped. The immigration consequences of a May Day arrest are not resolved by the criminal outcome.

Do not sign any document you receive after release without legal review. This includes offers of "voluntary departure," which forfeit your right to a hearing before an immigration judge.

If you were not detained but had any contact with ICE agents — being questioned, followed, or photographed — document it and report it to your local rapid-response network. This information helps communities track enforcement patterns.

### Employment Protections and Risks

**The core question: is participating protected concerted activity under the NLRA?**

Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act protects employees' right to engage in "concerted activity for mutual aid or protection." This includes strikes. The NLRA applies to most private-sector workers; it does not apply to agricultural workers, domestic workers, supervisors, or independent contractors.

**When a May Day walkout is more likely protected:**
- If the action is connected to workplace conditions — wages, hours, safety, working conditions — rather than purely political concerns
- If multiple coworkers participate together (coordination is the heart of "concerted activity")
- If the action is a one-time walkout rather than a series of intermittent work stoppages
- If your employer has not explicitly prohibited strikes or work stoppages under your employment agreement

**When a May Day walkout is less protected or unprotected:**
- If the walkout is framed purely as a political protest with no connection to your own workplace conditions — courts and the NLRB have held that purely political activity is not "mutual aid or protection" under the NLRA
- If your union contract contains a no-strike clause — you may still have First Amendment protection but not NLRA protection, and your employer may be able to discipline you under the contract
- If you are an at-will employee and the action is deemed unprotected political activity — at-will employees can be terminated for any non-prohibited reason; an unprotected walkout is grounds for termination in most states
- If your walkout is part of a pattern of "intermittent strikes" — courts have held that repeated short stoppages can be characterized as intermittent strikes and lose NLRA protection

**Union vs. non-union workers — practical risk difference:**

*Union workers with a no-strike clause in their contract:* Your union contract likely provides for grievance and arbitration if you are disciplined. Your employer must follow the disciplinary procedures in the contract. However, participating in a strike that violates a no-strike clause can be grounds for termination even under a union contract, and the union may not be able to defend you if you acted in violation of the clause. Ask your shop steward or union representative for your local union's specific position on May Day participation before you decide.

*Union workers without a no-strike clause, or with local union authorization:* You have the strongest position. A strike authorized by your local union, connected to workplace conditions, is clearly protected.

*Non-union at-will workers:* Your risk is highest. If your employer determines your absence was an unprotected political walkout rather than protected concerted activity, they may be able to terminate you. The risk is mitigated by scale (mass discipline is operationally difficult and generates adverse publicity), by using PTO or sick leave rather than a declared walkout, and by framing any communications with your employer around legitimate workplace or civic concerns rather than the May Day brand specifically.

*Government workers (federal and state):* Federal employees do not have the right to strike under federal law (5 U.S.C. § 7311). State employees' rights vary by state. If you are a federal employee, the work stoppage component carries genuine legal risk. Use PTO, personal leave, or sick leave if you want to participate without legal exposure.

*Healthcare workers and other essential services:* May have specific state or federal restrictions on work stoppages. Check your professional licensing board's guidance and your employer's emergency staffing policies.

**The practical reality about retaliation:**
Mass discipline after a large coordinated action is operationally and reputationally expensive. Employers that fire dozens of workers for May Day participation generate significant adverse press, potential NLRB complaints, and organizing momentum. This does not mean you have no risk — individual workers have been fired for participating in prior labor actions — but the aggregate risk calculus improves significantly with the number of participants.

### Safety Protocols

**The buddy system:**
Go with at least one other person and designate a check-in protocol: text your buddy at the start of the event, at regular intervals, and when you leave. Establish a meeting point if you get separated. Your buddy should know your legal name, your emergency contact, and any medical conditions that might be relevant.

**What to do if you are separated from your group:**
- Move to the edge of the crowd, not into the interior
- Text your buddy with your location
- Identify the nearest legal observer (green hat) or marshals (usually vest or armband)
- If there is a dispersal, move calmly in the direction indicated and exit rather than re-entering

**If there is an escalation or police action:**
- The safest place is away from the point of conflict
- Do not record from within a crowd that is being dispersed — you will not be able to exit quickly
- Link arms with nearby demonstrators if you are in the front of a march and there is pressure from behind — it reduces the risk of a crowd crush
- If someone near you is injured, alert a marshal or call 911; do not leave them alone

**Medical considerations:**
- If you have asthma, carry an inhaler — crowds and vehicle exhaust are triggers
- If you are on medications, carry them with you in their labeled containers
- Know the address of the nearest urgent care facility to your demonstration location before you go

**For parents bringing children:**
- Write your cell phone number on your child's arm in permanent marker
- Have a designated meeting point if separated
- March at the edges, not the center of dense crowds
- Smaller children in carriers are more mobile than strollers in crowd conditions

## 5. For Different Situations

This section addresses specific populations who face different constraints and opportunities.

### Essential Workers Who Cannot Strike

Healthcare workers, firefighters, transit operators, power grid workers, food supply chain workers — a significant portion of the economy cannot stop without genuine harm to public safety. The demand is not that you put patients or infrastructure at risk.

**Solidarity options that do not require leaving work:**

- Wear a solidarity pin, button, or color associated with the action (check your workplace dress code)
- Display solidarity messaging visible to patients, customers, or the public
- Post on social media from your personal account during off-hours
- Organize a brief pre-shift or post-shift gathering with willing coworkers
- Observe the consumer boycott during your off-hours on May 1 (don't buy from corporate chains on your lunch break)
- Donate to a strike fund for workers who are walking out
- Attend a demonstration before your shift begins or after it ends — many cities have early-morning and evening events scheduled
- Sign the May Day Strong pledge at maydaystrong.org

The symbolic value of essential workers expressing solidarity — even while working — is not nothing. The 2020 healthcare worker demonstrations during the pandemic, conducted by workers in scrubs still wearing their badges, carried specific weight precisely because those workers were not leaving their posts.

### Students

**K-12 students:**

Participating in a walkout as a minor involves your parents' or guardians' decision, not only yours. Indivisible's guidance is explicit: students under 18 should obtain parental consent. An unexcused absence is recorded; parents should communicate directly with schools if their child will not attend.

Many school districts with participating unions may have organized school-level actions. Check with your student government or administration whether your school has any official position.

If you attend, treat it with the seriousness of any civic engagement. Bring your own food and water. Tell a trusted adult where you are going. Nonviolent discipline is not optional.

**College and university students:**

You are an adult. You can make this decision for yourself, though you should understand the academic consequences of missing class if your absence is unexcused. Many faculty — particularly those affiliated with Higher Education Labor United or with union membership themselves — have signaled they will not penalize students who miss class for May Day.

If you are a graduate student worker or a student employee, your NLRA analysis is similar to non-union workers generally. Graduate student unions at many institutions have formally authorized May Day participation.

Universities with significant May Day organizing include most major public institutions and many private research universities. Student government associations at dozens of schools have passed resolutions supporting the action.

### Remote Workers

Remote workers have a particular advantage: the "No School. No Work. No Shopping." demand is fully observable without leaving home. The risk of participating in a work stoppage is similar to in-person workers; the experience of a day off is different.

**Options specific to remote workers:**

- Turn off work communications for the day — if you are using PTO, you are not obligated to be reachable
- If you do work on May 1, sign the pledge and observe the consumer boycott
- Use freed-up commute time (if any) to attend a local demonstration in the afternoon
- For digital participation, remote workers often have more bandwidth — coordinate social media amplification with your networks

**If your employer uses monitoring software:**
Some employers use activity-monitoring software on company devices. If you are taking a planned PTO day, this is not relevant. If you are logged in and attempting to not appear to be working, you are creating a different kind of risk — do not falsify your work activity.

### Unemployed Workers

You are not exempt from May Day and your participation is not less meaningful. The demands include economic conditions — inequality, worker rights, democracy — that affect you regardless of employment status.

- The consumer boycott is fully available to you
- Demonstrations are fully available to you
- If you are receiving unemployment benefits, there is no restriction on participating in lawful demonstrations
- Consider volunteering with local event organizers before May 1 — marshaling and logistics often need support
- The digital solidarity actions in Section 3 are available to you

### Retirees

The historical labor movement was built by people who are now retirees. Your participation is appropriate and your presence at demonstrations carries its own significance — particularly if you are a retired union member.

- No employment risk applies to you
- The consumer boycott is available to you
- Consider accompanying younger family members or neighbors to demonstrations, particularly if mobility or crowd navigation is a concern for them
- If you have grandchildren attending public schools, this is a conversation worth having with their parents

### People With Disabilities

Demonstration accessibility varies by event. When looking up your local event on mobilize.us/mayday or maydaystrong.org:

- Check whether the route and assembly point are wheelchair-accessible
- Note whether there is accessible seating or rest areas
- Contact local organizers directly through the Mobilize platform to ask about specific accommodations
- Some events have designated accessible viewing areas away from the main crowd
- Digital participation and the consumer boycott are fully accessible regardless of mobility or other disability status

## 6. The April 29 Mass Call

### What It Is

On Wednesday, April 29, 2026 at 7:30pm (Eastern time), May Day Strong is hosting a national Zoom call — the May Day Mass Call — as the final national coordination event before May 1.

This is not a protest. It is a logistics and solidarity call: a chance for participants from across the country to hear from coalition leaders, learn about local action logistics, ask questions, and feel connected to a national movement before the decentralized day of action.

These calls matter for a specific reason: decentralized national actions succeed or fail partly based on whether participants feel they are part of something coherent rather than isolated local events happening in parallel. The Mass Call is the mechanism for building that shared sense of scale.

### How to Join

Register and get connection details at maydaystrong.org. The call will be on Zoom; registration typically requires an email address. Expect the call to be recorded and posted afterward for those who cannot join live.

The 50501 Movement's "This Week in Action" newsletter (the50501movement.org) has also been coordinating event promotion, including the Mass Call.

### What to Expect

Based on how these coalition calls have been organized in the 2025-2026 cycle:

- Coalition leaders from participating organizations will speak briefly — expect NEA, NNU, Indivisible, and labor union voices
- Local organizers may share information about specific city events
- There will be a Q&A or breakout component for questions
- You will hear about the worker's hotline, legal resources, and safety protocols
- Turnout will be logged and may be referenced in post-May-Day reporting

### Why April 29 Specifically

The three-day sequence is intentional. April 28 is Workers Memorial Day — the AFL-CIO's annual day of remembrance for workers killed or injured on the job. It is a day of commemoration, not action; it sets the emotional and historical context. April 29 is the Mass Call — the coordination layer. May 1 is the action itself.

Organizations that have aligned Workers Memorial Day events on April 28 are using the day to build momentum and to remind participants why labor rights exist. If you have the capacity, participating in a Workers Memorial Day event on April 28 and then the Mass Call on April 29 creates continuity into May 1.

### Why It Matters for Post-May-Day Momentum

The Mass Call creates a roster. Everyone who registers is a potential future contact point for coalition outreach. If May Day's goal is to convert one-day participants into sustained organizers, the Mass Call is the first mechanism for capturing that contact information with consent.

Organizers know this. The ask after the call will not just be "show up Friday." It will include future engagement steps: join a local chapter, sign up for the next action, attend a follow-up meeting. If you want to stay connected after May Day, say yes to those next steps during or after the call.

## 7. What Outcomes to Watch

How you interpret May Day's success or failure will shape how you engage afterward. This section offers a framework grounded in what movements actually need, not what a single news cycle will report.

### Turnout: What the Numbers Mean

**What organizers are aiming for:**
Hundreds of thousands to low millions in demonstrations nationally. 600 actions planned across the country as of late April; the number will likely grow. A mid-range success would be demonstration turnout comparable to the 2017 May Day (large in major cities, thinner elsewhere) plus measurable work stoppage and boycott participation.

**What the media will report:**
Crowd counts at major cities (NYC, Chicago, LA, Portland). These numbers will be contested — police counts systematically undercount; organizer counts systematically overcount. Use multiple sources and middle estimates.

**What matters for movement purposes:**
Whether the organizational infrastructure — the rosters, the relationships, the local chapter meetings — grows out of May Day organizing. A relatively smaller demonstration that generates 200,000 new email sign-ups for participating organizations is more valuable than a larger demonstration that generates no follow-up contact.

### Employer and Government Response

**Employer response indicators:**

- Are there any widely publicized terminations of workers for May Day participation? Mass discipline is the administration's strongest counter-move and also its most costly in terms of public opinion and organizing momentum.
- Do any major employers announce preemptive closures or flexibility policies before May 1? This is a concession and a signal that the economic pressure is being taken seriously.
- Are there any labor injunction attempts against participating unions? An injunction would be an extraordinary escalation and would itself generate organizing response.

**Government response indicators (as of April 23, none confirmed):**

No executive orders, DOJ actions, DHS enforcement actions, or other government responses targeting May Day organizing have been confirmed. Watch for:

- ICE enforcement actions at or near demonstration sites — this is the most likely intimidation tactic given the "No ICE" demand
- Any executive order targeting union contracts or nonprofit status of participating organizations
- Any attempt to deny permits or restrict march routes at the last minute in major cities

**Absence of government response is also an outcome.** If May Day occurs without significant government interference, that confirms the administration's calculation that visible suppression of peaceful civic action is counterproductive. That calculation is worth noting.

### Media Framing

The immediate news cycle will focus on disruption (if any), crowd size, and the most visually striking images. This is predictable and should not define your assessment.

**What to track over the following week:**

- Are elected officials responding to May Day? Statements from senators, governors, and congressional representatives indicate whether the political establishment is feeling pressure
- Does the AFL-CIO change its posture? If May Day generates more visible labor participation than expected, AFL-CIO may move toward a stronger national endorsement for future actions
- Does employer behavior shift? Any major employer announcing wage increases, flexibility policies, or public statements acknowledging worker concerns in the week following May Day should be tracked as a potential downstream response

### The 3.5% Threshold in Context

Chenoweth's research establishes 3.5% of population as the threshold at which nonviolent movements have uniformly succeeded. For the United States, that is approximately 11.5 million active participants. May Day 2026 will not hit that number in a single day.

But Chenoweth's research also shows that movements that ultimately reach the 3.5% threshold typically do so through sustained escalation — a series of actions over months or years, each larger than the last, each building organizational capacity. The 3.5% is not a one-day requirement. It is a cumulative organizing target.

By that standard, a May Day 2026 that falls short of 11.5 million but generates genuine organizational growth — new members, new local chapters, tested logistics, expanded coalitions — can be a success on the terms that matter for sustained resistance.

**What a successful May Day looks like:**
- Participation substantially larger than prior May Days in the current cycle (2024, 2025)
- Documented disruption to economic activity in at least several major metros
- No mass government repression (which would indicate strategic miscalculation)
- Organizational growth — more roster sign-ups, more local chapter membership, more labor union members active in civic organizations
- A credible commitment to May Day 2027 and May Day 2028 escalation

**What falling short looks like:**
- Demonstration turnout smaller than No Kings day-of-action (suggesting mobilization is declining, not growing)
- Significant government or employer retaliation without corresponding organizing response
- Coalition fracture — participating organizations publicly splitting over tactics or framing
- No meaningful work stoppage component — only demonstrations (would suggest the economic pressure pillar failed to activate)

## 8. After May Day

### The First 48 Hours

The immediate aftermath of May Day matters as much as the day itself. What organizers do in the 48 hours after May 1 shapes whether participation converts to sustained engagement or evaporates.

**What to expect from organizing infrastructure:**

- Coalition organizations will send follow-up emails to participants — open them and follow the links
- Local chapters will schedule "debrief" or "next steps" meetings — attend if you can
- Social media will fill with participant photos, video, and testimonials — share them, especially if they counter inaccurate framing
- Organizers will release participation estimates — compare them to media reports and use the discrepancy to understand the framing battle

**What to do as an individual participant:**

- If you experienced anything legally notable at a demonstration — police contact, threats, employer retaliation — document it and contact the NLG or your local ACLU chapter
- If you want to stay engaged, say yes to the next ask in any follow-up email you receive
- Talk to the people you went with about what they want to do next — the strongest organizing asset is existing relationships

### Sustained Organizing — Where to Plug In

May Day Strong is not a permanent organization. It is a coalition vehicle for May 1. The sustained organizing happens in the constituent organizations. Here is where different participants should direct their energy:

**If you are a teacher or education worker:**
- NEA's member action network is the primary channel; nea.org
- If you are not yet a union member, contact your local teachers' union about joining
- Higher Education Labor United for campus workers; higheredlaborunited.org

**If you are a healthcare worker:**
- National Nurses United; nationalnursesunited.org
- SEIU (Service Employees International Union) represents a broad range of healthcare workers; seiu.org

**If you are a private-sector worker without a union:**
- Contact the AFL-CIO about unions in your industry; aflcio.org
- The Emergency Workplace Organizing Committee (EWOC) provides organizing support for workers trying to unionize; workerorganizing.org

**If you want civic (non-labor) involvement:**
- Indivisible local chapters are the primary civic organizing infrastructure; indivisible.org
- 50501 Movement for ongoing weekly action coordination; the50501movement.org
- DSA chapters if your politics align with democratic socialist organizing; dsausa.org

**The midterm election framing:**

Organizers are explicitly framing May Day 2026 as preparation for 2026 midterm election organizing. Several sources reference that May Day 2026 is intended to build the voter mobilization and campaign infrastructure for November 2026. If electoral organizing is your primary interest, the organizations above all have electoral components — voter registration, canvassing, get-out-the-vote.

### May Day 2027 and 2028 — The Long Arc

The UAW's Shawn Fain has called for strikes across the labor movement on May Day 2028. DSA's 2026 framing explicitly asks: what can we do now so that May Day 2027 is bigger than 2026 and May Day 2028 is bigger still?

This is the question worth holding after May 1. The research on what moves democratic resistance from demonstration to change is consistent: it requires organizational depth (not just turnout), nonviolent discipline (which holds coalition breadth), and sustained escalation over time (which requires infrastructure that survives between actions).

A one-day action is a test. It tests logistics, coalition discipline, geographic reach, and the capacity to activate participants who are not already organizers. May Day 2026's primary contribution to the longer arc is whatever it reveals about what is and is not working — and whether the organizations involved use that information to build toward 2027.

**The honest assessment:**

Demonstrations matter and are necessary. They are not sufficient on their own. The research that Chenoweth's 3.5% finding rests on is research about sustained mass campaigns — months to years of nonviolent action that builds economic costs and defection from the opponent's support base. May Day 2026 is one day. What it produces beyond that one day depends on choices that individual participants, local organizers, and coalition organizations make in the weeks and months that follow.

If you participate on May 1, the most useful thing you can do on May 2 is identify one sustained organizational commitment — a chapter meeting, a union membership application, a canvassing shift — and make it.
