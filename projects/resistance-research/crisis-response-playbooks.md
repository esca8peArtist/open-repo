# Democratic Crisis Response Playbooks
## Operational Guides for Institutions Defending Democratic Governance — 2026

**Version**: 1.0 — Session 549  
**Framework reference**: 35-Domain Diagnostic Framework (democratic-renewal-proposal.md)  
**Companion guides**: crisis-response-legislative.md, crisis-response-ag-coalition.md, crisis-response-advocacy.md, crisis-response-academic.md, crisis-response-civil-service.md  
**Purpose**: Execution-ready response sequences for the five highest-probability democratic crises in 2026. These are operational documents, not educational overviews.

---

## How to Use This Document

Each playbook covers one crisis scenario in three time horizons: 72-hour emergency response, 30-day consolidation, and 90-day institutional positioning. Each includes coordination requirements, success metrics, and failure modes.

The five sector-specific companion guides translate each playbook into institution-specific action sequences for: state legislatures and congressional staff, state attorneys general coalitions, advocacy organizations, universities and research institutions, and federal/state civil service defenders.

**The coordination premise**: No single institution can defend democratic governance alone. These playbooks are designed to interlock — when the AG coalition files suit, the legislative guide tells congressional staff what bill to introduce that week; when civil service defenders invoke whistleblower protection, the advocacy guide tells coalitions how to amplify the disclosure. Cross-references in each playbook indicate which companion guide covers each action.

**Domain references**: Each playbook cites the relevant domains from the 35-domain framework. These citations are not decorative — the domain files contain the specific legal arguments, precedent citations, and evidence that the 72-hour response actors will need. Before a crisis hits, relevant staff should have read the domain file, not just this playbook.

## Playbook 1: Federal Election Interference

**Crisis definition**: A federal actor (executive order, DHS directive, DOJ instruction) takes action to restrict, delay, or invalidate voting before or during the 2026 midterms. This includes: ICE operations near polling sites, voter roll purge orders, mail ballot rejection directives, or federal interference with state election administration.

**Domain references**: Domain 1 (Voting Rights and Elections), Domain 9 (Federalism), Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms), Domain 37b (State Election Security Coordination)

**Probability assessment**: High. The SAVE Act's Senate failure (48-50, April 2026) closed the legislative pathway for mail ballot restriction, but the executive pathway remains open. The administration has demonstrated willingness to use enforcement agencies as electoral tools (Operation Metro Surge, Hussen v. Noem). Domain 37b documents that CISA's destruction has left a state-level security coordination gap that has not been filled.

---

### 72-Hour Response

**Hour 1-4: Legal hold and documentation**

The first action is not a press release. It is a legal hold.

Democracy Docket (democracydocket.com/cases) should receive a case intake form within the first hour of any credible report. Marc Elias's litigation team has standing preparation for election interference scenarios — they need: (1) the specific federal action, with the authorizing document if available; (2) the specific state or jurisdiction affected; (3) the identifiable plaintiffs (voters who are being disenfranchised, election officials being interfered with, civil rights organizations with members in the affected area). Democracy Docket's case intake team can assess TRO eligibility within hours if this information is ready.

ACLU Voting Rights Project (aclu.org/voting-rights) operates a parallel intake process. For the 2026 election cycle, the ACLU has pre-positioned legal teams in every state with a competitive Senate or House race. Contact the relevant state ACLU chapter simultaneously with the national office.

State election directors (Secretary of State offices in states with contested races) should receive a factual summary of the federal action within four hours. They need to know: what federal actors are doing, what legal authority is claimed, what the response options are, and who else has been notified. In states with Democratic Secretaries of State — Michigan, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia, Nevada, New Hampshire — the election director's office should already have an emergency contact protocol with the state AG's office. If it does not, establishing that protocol before any crisis is the highest-priority pre-crisis action.

**Hour 4-12: AG coalition notification**

The 22-state AG coalition that has litigated birthright citizenship, IRS-ICE data sharing, and warrantless arrest cases (see litigation-tracker-2026.md, Category 4) has an informal coordination structure. The states with the strongest track record of rapid filing are: Massachusetts (AG Andrea Campbell), New York (AG Letitia James), Washington (AG Nick Brown), California (AG Rob Bonta), Illinois (AG Kwame Raoul), and Maryland (AG Anthony Brown). Contact these six first — their offices have litigation infrastructure and emergency filing capacity.

The AG coalition's standing theory for election interference challenges: states have sovereign authority over their own election administration under Article I, Section 4 of the Constitution. A federal executive action that overrides state election administration without congressional authorization violates this structural principle. This standing theory is well-established through Bush v. Gore and subsequent election administration cases. The companion guide for AGs (crisis-response-ag-coalition.md) has the full standing memo structure.

**Hour 12-48: Federal court filing**

The filing should seek a TRO on the specific federal action. Target circuits: for ICE-at-polls or enforcement operations, file in the same circuits where warrantless arrest injunctions are already in place (D.D.C., 9th, 10th, 4th). If the federal action affects a specific geographic area, file in that district's court where possible.

The TRO motion should identify: (1) the specific constitutional violation (14th Amendment for voter suppression; Article I Section 4 for federal interference with state election administration; First Amendment if the interference chills speech); (2) irreparable harm — votes that are not cast on Election Day cannot be recovered; (3) balance of equities — the government has no legitimate interest in interfering with lawful voting; (4) public interest — free and fair elections are the clearest public interest available.

Congress must be notified simultaneously. Senate Judiciary Committee Democratic staff director, House Administration Committee Democratic staff director, and the House and Senate minority leaders' offices should receive factual summaries within 24 hours. Their response is not primarily legal — it is political cover for the court. Floor statements by senior Democratic leaders within 48 hours of a filing create political cost for the administration's interference and put Republican institutionalists on record.

**Hour 48-72: Public framing**

The public narrative must be established before the administration establishes its own. Two frames that have worked in analogous cases:

First: election integrity. Do not concede the "election integrity" frame to the administration. The administration's action is the election integrity threat — it is preventing legitimate voters from participating. This reframe is documented in the Brennan Center's messaging research and should be adopted immediately.

Second: federalism. The administration is overriding state election authority. This framing recruits Republican state officials who support state sovereignty, including some who may not oppose the underlying policy goal.

**Success condition for 72 hours**: A TRO is in place restraining the specific federal action. At least six state AGs have filed or joined the action. Congressional leaders have made floor statements. Media has the factual record rather than the administration's framing.

### 30-Day Response

**Days 1-14: Preliminary injunction**

TROs last 14 days. The preliminary injunction motion must be filed within that window. The motion requires the same four elements as the TRO but with a fuller record. During the TRO period, the litigation team should: take depositions of election officials documenting the harm, gather declarations from affected voters, and secure expert declarations on the specific constitutional questions.

Congressional action within the first 14 days: Democratic leaders should convene an emergency hearing in the relevant committee (Judiciary for constitutional violations, Administration for election operations). The hearing record creates political cost and documentary support for the legal challenge. Republican members of Collins/Murkowski/Tillis's institutionalist bloc should be invited to testify — putting them on record as either supporting or opposing federal interference with state elections.

**Days 15-30: Coalition expansion**

If the preliminary injunction is issued, the coalition must expand to prevent the administration from forum-shopping or appealing to the Supreme Court's shadow docket for a stay. The counter-strategy: build so wide a coalition of plaintiffs (state governments, election officials, civil rights organizations, affected individual voters) that a shadow docket stay would visibly affect millions of people. The Supreme Court's shadow docket is most vulnerable to political pressure when its consequences are immediately visible to the public.

State legislatures in affected states should introduce legislation explicitly asserting state sovereignty over election administration. This creates a second legal basis for the AGs' challenge and puts the state's legislature on record. The companion guide for legislatures (crisis-response-legislative.md) has draft findings language.

**Success condition for 30 days**: Preliminary injunction in place. At least 10 states actively litigating. Congressional hearing record complete. At least one Republican state official on record opposing the federal interference.

---

### 90-Day Response

**Days 31-90: Integration into midterm strategy**

If the federal interference has been stopped by injunction, the 30-90 day period is about converting legal victory into electoral infrastructure. The specific mechanisms that were threatened should be hardened: if mail voting was the target, conduct a public education campaign on mail voting procedures in the affected states; if polling site access was threatened, recruit additional poll workers and establish community networks to monitor access on Election Day.

The longer-term institutional response: Domain 37b identifies the state election security coordination gap created by CISA's dismantlement. Fill it now. At least 12 states have begun bilateral or multilateral information-sharing agreements to replace the federal function. Connect the crisis response to this infrastructure-building.

**Success condition for 90 days**: The specific interference mechanism has been permanently enjoined or reversed. State election security coordination agreements are in place. The crisis has become a documented case study that strengthens pre-crisis preparation for other states.

### Success Metrics

- TRO issued within 72 hours of identified interference
- 6+ state AGs filed within 48 hours
- Congressional floor statements within 48 hours
- Preliminary injunction maintained through election
- Voter turnout in affected areas within 5% of projected baseline
- No confirmed voter disenfranchisement traceable to federal interference

### Coordination Requirements

This playbook only works if federal judges simultaneously act. The litigation strategy must be filed in courts where injunctions against this administration have already been issued — judges who have already found the administration's actions illegal are more likely to grant emergency relief quickly. Filing in hostile circuits invites a shadow docket reversal that could arrive faster than a hearing.

The AG coalition and Democracy Docket must move in parallel, not in sequence. Parallel filing in multiple circuits is not duplicative — it builds a national record and makes a Supreme Court shadow docket stay more politically costly.

---

### Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1**: The administration defies the injunction. This is documented in CASA v. DHS (warrantless arrests continuing after D.D.C. injunction) and Ramirez Ovando v. Noem (continued non-compliance, ICE supervisors unable to explain what orders applied). Prepare contempt motions in advance. Designate the specific officials responsible for compliance and name them in contempt motions if they do not comply. The companion guide for AGs covers contempt enforcement procedure.

**Failure mode 2**: The Supreme Court issues a shadow docket stay. Counter-strategy: make the stay's consequences immediately visible. If the stay allows ICE at polls, organize legal observers at every affected polling site to document the effect. Visual documentation of the stay's real-world consequences creates political cost even when the legal remedy has been removed.

**Failure mode 3**: The crisis emerges within 30 days of Election Day, leaving insufficient time for litigation. Pre-crisis mitigation: Democracy Docket and ACLU should have draft TRO motions pre-written for each identified interference scenario, requiring only the factual details to be filled in. Preparation time is the only remedy for timing failure.

## Playbook 2: Judicial Capture

**Crisis definition**: A SCOTUS appointment consolidates the 6-3 supermajority further, or the Court issues a ruling that eliminates key institutional protections (most likely: Trump v. Slaughter overruling Humphrey's Executor; Watson v. RNC invalidating mail ballot grace periods; Louisiana v. Callais eliminating majority-minority districts). This playbook also covers the ongoing judicial capture vectors documented in Domain 6: impeachment pressure on district judges, shadow docket normalization, and immigration court dismantlement.

**Domain references**: Domain 6 (Judicial Independence), Domain 35 (Supreme Court 2026 Term Preview), Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse), Domain 13 (Constitutional Amendments)

**Probability assessment**: High for at least one major adverse ruling. Trump v. Slaughter (Humphrey's Executor overruling) and Watson v. RNC (mail ballot grace periods) are both expected by late June 2026, both with majorities signaled at oral argument. A new SCOTUS vacancy in 2026 — a contingent but real possibility — would trigger a confirmation crisis.

---

### 72-Hour Response to Adverse SCOTUS Ruling

**Hour 1-12: Rapid legal triage**

Within 12 hours of a SCOTUS ruling that eliminates a structural protection, the legal community needs a precise damage assessment: what specifically has changed, what statutes or precedents remain viable, and what new doctrinal arguments are now available.

For a Humphrey's Executor overruling (Trump v. Slaughter): the immediate question is which agencies are now at-will and which, if any, are protected by the Federal Reserve carve-out logic. The Brennan Center for Justice, Constitutional Accountability Center, and Public Citizen Litigation Group have standing doctrine and constitutional amendment resources ready. Within 12 hours, their legal teams should publish a joint legal memo identifying: (1) which agencies retain statutory independence under the ruling's specific holding; (2) which agency functions can be replicated through private rights of action (Domain 6, Section 3.2); (3) which state analog laws can substitute for federal enforcement; (4) what constitutional amendment language would restore the protection.

For a Watson v. RNC mail ballot ruling: Democracy Docket and state Secretaries of State need an immediate compliance matrix — which states' laws are preempted by the ruling, what the state-specific remedy timeline is (states must amend election law before November 2026), and which ballot-receipt deadlines remain lawful.

**Hour 12-48: Congressional response**

An adverse SCOTUS ruling that eliminates institutional protections is a prompt for congressional action — not because legislation can override a constitutional ruling (it cannot) but because Congress controls the structural remedies that remain available.

For a Humphrey's Executor overruling: Congressional Democrats should introduce within 48 hours: (1) a package of private rights of action amendments to every major regulatory statute whose agency has lost independence (NLRA, FTC Act, CFPB/Dodd-Frank, FEC/FECA) — this creates distributed enforcement that survives agency capture; (2) a constitutional amendment establishing independent agency tenure protection explicitly in Article II. Neither will pass the current Congress, but both create the legislative record that the next Congress can use as pre-drafted bills.

For a Watson v. RNC ruling: Congressional Democrats should introduce a federal mail voting baseline bill that restores grace periods as a federal statutory right. This also will not pass the current Congress, but it creates the midterm contrast.

**Hour 48-72: State legislative activation**

The states are the backstop when federal institutional protections are stripped. Contact the 22+ state legislatures with Democratic governors and legislative chambers within 48 hours of an adverse ruling. The message is specific and actionable:

For Humphrey's Executor overruling: pass state analog laws that replicate the federal protections now lost (state labor relations boards with independent tenure protection, state consumer protection agencies with equivalent powers).

For Watson v. RNC: ensure your state election law conforms with the ruling's scope and, where possible, expand the alternatives (in-person voting access, ballot drop boxes, early voting hours). States that already have strong mail voting infrastructure (Colorado, Oregon, Washington, Hawaii, Utah) should make their model legislation available immediately.

### 30-Day Response

**Days 1-30: Build the amendment campaign**

Judicial capture at the Supreme Court level requires a constitutional response, not a statutory one. The Domain 6 and Domain 13 files document the constitutional amendment pathways. Within 30 days of a major adverse ruling, the amendment campaign should be publicly launched with: (1) a specific amendment text — not a general call for "reform" but a specific 12-year term limit for SCOTUS justices with phased implementation; (2) a sponsorship roll-out from Senate Democratic leadership and sympathetic Republicans (the Collins/Murkowski/Tillis bloc); (3) an academic coalition backing the amendment with a white paper (the companion guide for academic institutions covers this role).

The 30-day period is also when the replacement infrastructure is built. If Humphrey's Executor is overruled: the private rights of action model (Domain 6, Section 3.2) must be activated. This means unions, consumer advocacy groups, and civil rights organizations filing cases directly — not waiting for the now-captured agency. The advocacy companion guide covers this activation sequence.

---

### 90-Day Response

**Days 31-90: Midterm integration**

Every major adverse SCOTUS ruling in 2026 should be integrated into the midterm political campaign. The specific mechanism: the ruling was the direct consequence of judicial appointments made by a specific president with the votes of specific senators. Those senators are identifiable. In competitive 2026 Senate races, the ruling's real-world consequences — NLRB defunct, mail voting restricted, minority representation eliminated — can be directly attributed to named, vote-able incumbents.

The companion guide for advocacy organizations covers the voter education and mobilization sequence. The companion guide for legislatures covers how to use state legislative platforms to create contrast.

### Impeachment Pressure Response (Judicial Intimidation Crisis)

If the crisis is judicial intimidation rather than a specific adverse ruling — articles of impeachment introduced against district judges (as Ted Cruz proposed against Judges Boasberg and Boardman), threats, or actual impeachment proceedings — the response is different.

**72 hours**: The ABA (American Bar Association) Judicial Intimidation Hotline (documented in Domain 6) should receive a case report within hours. ABA President's emergency statement capability is established. Constitutional law professors from at least five law schools should be on record within 24 hours documenting why impeachment for lawful judicial acts violates the constitutional standard (high crimes and misdemeanors does not include correct legal rulings the executive dislikes). The companion guide for academic institutions covers the rapid scholarly response protocol.

**30 days**: Federal Judicial Safety Act legislative introduction — a bill creating felony penalties for threats against federal judges, with enhanced enforcement. This has bipartisan support on paper (multiple Republican senators have condemned judge threats in principle) and can be used to test the Collins/Murkowski/Tillis bloc.

**90 days**: If impeachment proceedings actually begin in the House, the Senate becomes the battlefield. Two-thirds conviction requires 67 senators; Democrats hold 47. No judge who has made correct legal rulings can be convicted. Focus energy on ensuring Republican institutionalists are publicly committed to the constitutional standard before articles are transmitted to the Senate, so that their vote is locked in by prior public statements.

---

### Success Metrics

- Amendment introduced with bipartisan cosponsors within 30 days of adverse ruling
- State analog legislation introduced in 15+ states within 30 days
- Private rights of action filed in 10+ cases within 60 days of Humphrey's Executor overruling (if it occurs)
- Judge impeachment attempt fails (standard: Senate vote to convict falls below 40 senators)
- Mail voting baseline in all states within 60 days of Watson ruling (if it occurs)

---

### Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1**: The amendment campaign fragments into competing amendment proposals. Counter-strategy: designate a lead organization (Brennan Center) to hold the amendment text stable and resist pressure to negotiate it into ineffectiveness before it has any political momentum.

**Failure mode 2**: State analog legislation passes but is immediately preempted by federal court ruling under the post-Loper Bright interpretive framework. Pre-mitigation: draft state statutes with explicit private rights of action that are distinct from agency authority, so that preemption of the agency does not preempt the private enforcement mechanism.

## Playbook 3: Executive Overreach — Emergency EO

**Crisis definition**: The president issues an executive order that (a) purports to act without congressional authorization in an area where Congress has not delegated authority, or (b) purports to supersede existing law by executive unilateralism. Highest-probability scenarios for 2026: emergency declaration used to postpone or interfere with midterm elections; dissolution or significant restructuring of a federal agency without statutory authority; invocation of Section 3 enforcement without congressional authorization.

**Domain references**: Domain 2 (Executive Accountability), Domain 9 (Federalism), Domain 34 (Congressional Power of the Purse), Domain 28 (War Powers and Venezuela), Domain 19f (War Powers Reform)

**Probability assessment**: Moderate-high. 164 executive orders in the first five months of the administration established the pattern. The Venezuela "arrest operation" (Domain 28) demonstrated willingness to take major executive action with only retrospective congressional notification. Emergency declaration authority under the NEA is the most likely vehicle.

---

### 72-Hour Response

**Hour 1-6: Standing identification**

Not everyone can challenge an executive order in court. Identify standing within hours. For most EOs, the viable plaintiffs are: affected states (through AGs), affected federal employees (through unions — AFGE, NTEU), organizations whose members are directly harmed, and Congress (where statutory authority has been violated).

For an EO purporting to postpone elections: states have standing as election administrators (Article I Section 4). Congress has standing under the Elections Clause. Individual voters have standing through established voting rights doctrine. File in D.D.C. or the circuit with the most established track record of granting emergency relief against this administration.

For an EO dissolving or restructuring an agency without statutory authority: affected employees and their unions have standing. Organizations that rely on the agency's functions have standing. This is the standing theory that AFSCME used in the DOGE/SSA litigation (litigation-tracker-2026.md, Category 3).

**Hour 6-24: Constitutional framing**

The constitutional argument should be established and simplified within 24 hours. Post-Loper Bright, courts have independent interpretive authority over statutory questions — they do not defer to executive interpretation of statutes. This means a court will genuinely decide whether the EO's claimed statutory authority exists. Prepare the statutory argument: identify the specific statute the EO claims to act under, quote the statute's actual text, and show that the claimed authority is not there. This is a straightforward textual argument after Loper Bright, not a deference question.

If the EO asserts emergency authority, identify which emergency declaration statute is being invoked. The National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.) requires a declaration and permits congressional termination by joint resolution. If the NEA is invoked, congressional leadership should introduce the termination resolution within 24 hours.

**Hour 24-72: Parallel tracks**

Legal challenge and congressional response must run simultaneously. The legal track (TRO filing) is covered above. The congressional track:

Senate Democratic leadership introduces NEA termination resolution within 24 hours. This will not pass, but it forces Republicans to vote either for or against the emergency — a politically costly vote in many cases. The Collins/Murkowski/Tillis bloc has demonstrated willingness to oppose executive unilateralism on procedural grounds. An emergency declaration to interfere with elections is precisely the kind of institutional-integrity question that may peel two or three Republican votes.

House minority leadership should convene an emergency press conference within 24 hours, clearly naming the EO as unconstitutional and identifying the specific statutory text it violates.

---

### 30-Day Response

**Days 1-30: Appropriations pressure**

Congress's most durable power against executive overreach is the power of the purse (Domain 34). Even if the current House Republican majority will not pass legislation stopping the EO, appropriations riders can restrict funding for its implementation. Democratic appropriators should draft specific language prohibiting use of appropriated funds to implement the specific EO provisions that exceed statutory authority. This creates a negotiating lever in any omnibus spending discussion.

State legislatures should pass resolutions formally opposing the EO and asserting state authority in the affected area. These resolutions do not have direct legal effect, but they create political record, generate media coverage, and demonstrate the breadth of opposition.

---

### 90-Day Response

**Days 31-90: Long-term architecture**

An executive order that reveals a gap in statutory authority is a legislative opportunity. Identify the specific statutory language needed to prevent this or similar EOs from claiming that authority in the future. Draft the legislation. Do not wait for the current Congress to pass it — the next Congress will need pre-drafted bills if the political environment shifts.

The companion guide for academic institutions covers the role of legal scholars in creating the doctrinal record that the next litigation round will use. Assign scholars from at least three law schools to publish peer-reviewed analysis of the EO's specific statutory overreach within 30 days.

---

### Success Metrics

- TRO issued within 72 hours
- NEA termination resolution introduced within 24 hours (if applicable)
- Appropriations rider drafted within 30 days
- Academic record (2+ law review analyses) complete within 30 days
- EO enjoined or rescinded before implementation date

---

### Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1**: Post-Loper Bright, courts defer to executive statutory interpretation anyway. Counter-strategy: Loper Bright explicitly eliminated that deference. If a court does defer, it is creating an independent appellate issue that may succeed on appeal even if it fails at the district level.

**Failure mode 2**: The administration implements the EO during the TRO litigation window. Counter-strategy: identify the specific implementation steps and challenge each one as it occurs, building an escalating contempt record.

## Playbook 4: Civil Service Targeting — Schedule F Equivalent

**Crisis definition**: A Schedule F-equivalent executive order reclassifies career civil servants as Schedule F or similar category, removing civil service protections and making them at-will employees subject to political removal. Mass firings of career staff in specific agencies. Or the equivalent through attrition pressure: creating conditions so hostile that career staff are constructively discharged.

**Domain references**: Domain 2 (Executive Accountability / Civil Service), Domain 26 (Government Accountability and Institutional Resilience), Domain 6 (Judicial Independence — immigration court capture as model)

**Probability assessment**: Active. DOGE mass firings (71,981+ documented), Schedule Policy/Career EO (Domain 2), immigration court dismantlement (100+ immigration judges fired, Domain 6 Section 2.2) — this crisis is already in progress. The playbook covers both ongoing response and response to an escalatory new action.

**Status note**: The AFGE/BOP preliminary injunction hearing in D. Conn. (April 30, 2026) is a live moment in this playbook's arc. Monitor the outcome in monitoring/2026-04-30 when results are available.

---

### 72-Hour Response to Escalatory Mass Firing

**Hour 1-12: Individual rights triage**

Fired civil servants have individual rights that must be exercised within specific statutory deadlines. The Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB) is the administrative review body for federal employee terminations — but the MSPB itself has been compromised (Wilcox firing, MSPB without quorum, Domain 6 Section 1.1). The backup is direct federal court filing.

Federal Employee Legal Services (contact: NTEU Legal Services, AFGE Legal Defense Fund, Federal Employee Advocacy Group) should have intake capacity established before any mass firing. Within 12 hours of a mass firing announcement, federal employee unions (AFGE, NTEU, NFFE, NWSEO, NFFE-IAM) should be notified and should activate their legal defense protocols.

The threshold question for each affected employee: are they a career civil servant (competitive service) or a Schedule F/Schedule Policy/Career employee? Career competitive service employees retain MSPB appeal rights even after a Schedule F EO if their specific position was lawfully in competitive service before the EO. This distinction requires individualized legal assessment and is why the legal triage step is first.

**Hour 12-48: Litigation filing**

The AFGE model in the BOP/D.Conn. case is the template. The legal theory: (1) the EO reclassification violates the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, which Congress passed specifically to establish merit-based civil service protection; (2) the firing violates due process (property interest in continued employment established by statute); (3) the mass firing without cause violates the rulemaking requirements of the APA (you cannot change agency employment rules without notice-and-comment).

State AGs have a role here that is underappreciated: they can challenge mass federal civil service firings on the grounds that the agencies' reduced capacity to implement federal programs harms state interests. Maryland v. Noem (the Williamsport ICE warehouse NEPA case) demonstrates Maryland AG Brown's willingness to bring this kind of state-interest challenge. AGs in states that administer federal programs through cooperative agreements — Medicaid, education, environmental enforcement — have concrete standing when the federal agency partner loses capacity.

**Hour 48-72: Whistleblower protection activation**

Career civil servants who document political interference in agency decision-making, illegal orders, or data misuse have whistleblower protection under the Whistleblower Protection Act (5 U.S.C. 2302). The Office of Special Counsel (OSC) handles complaints, but the OSC director is a presidential appointee and may be compromised. The backup whistleblower protection channel: inspector general offices, which retain some independence; congressional oversight (direct contact with congressional committee staff is legally protected whistleblowing); and federal court (qui tam and False Claims Act for financial fraud).

Document everything before leaving. Any civil servant who anticipates removal should create a record of: illegal orders received, data accessed outside normal authorization, political interference in civil service processes, any instruction to violate law or regulation. This documentation protects individual legal claims and creates the evidentiary record for later accountability.

---

### 30-Day Response

**Days 1-30: Congressional investigation**

The Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee (and House counterpart) has jurisdiction over civil service matters. Democratic staff directors should request immediate hearings with affected agency heads and DOGE officials. Even without majority control of the committees, minority members can convene unofficial hearings, take testimony for the record, and publish the findings.

The Government Accountability Office (GAO) can investigate whether firings comply with applicable law. A GAO investigation request from ranking minority members creates an official record-building mechanism that does not require majority committee support. GAO investigations are a documented tool in Domain 26 (Government Accountability).

State legislatures in states with significant federal workforce populations (Virginia, Maryland, California, Texas, Colorado, Washington) should hold hearings on the economic and functional impacts of federal workforce reduction. This creates political cost and supplements the federal record.

**Days 15-30: Replacement infrastructure**

When federal agencies lose capacity, state governments and civil society organizations sometimes absorb those functions. This is the parallel institutions strategy documented in parallel-institutions.md. Identify specifically which federal functions are being lost in the mass firing and connect state agencies and civil society organizations that can partially substitute.

Examples: if NOAA's climate data capacity is gutted, connect state environmental agencies and university research centers that can partially substitute. If CFPB's consumer protection enforcement is gutted, connect state AG consumer protection divisions and PIRG organizations that can expand their work.

---

### 90-Day Response

**Days 31-90: Civil service restoration legislation**

Draft and introduce legislation that: (1) explicitly prohibits reclassification of competitive service employees to Schedule F or equivalent categories without congressional authorization; (2) creates a private right of action for wrongfully fired civil servants with fee-shifting; (3) strengthens OSC independence by requiring Senate confirmation with a fixed term and for-cause removal protection.

This legislation will not pass the current Congress. It is pre-positioned for a Democratic Congress. The drafting work done now reduces the time to enactment from months to weeks if the political environment shifts after the 2026 midterms.

---

### Success Metrics

- Legal representation secured for all affected civil servants within 72 hours
- Preliminary injunction filed within 48 hours
- GAO investigation request filed within 7 days
- Congressional hearing record (even unofficial) complete within 30 days
- State analog capacity identified for every lost federal function within 60 days
- Civil service restoration bill introduced with cosponsors within 30 days

---

### Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1**: The MSPB quorum problem prevents administrative appeals from proceeding. The backup is direct federal court filing (APA and constitutional claims), not MSPB. Unions should have this backup filed in advance — do not wait for MSPB to fail.

**Failure mode 2**: Mass firing creates a chilling effect on remaining civil servants who self-censor to avoid being next. This is the most operationally dangerous failure mode because it achieves the political goal without requiring further action. Counter-strategy: the whistleblower protection system and union legal support must be visibly functional. If civil servants see that their colleagues who have been fired are represented, that protections are being enforced, and that those who document wrongdoing are protected, the chilling effect is reduced.

## Playbook 5: Prosecutorial Weaponization

**Crisis definition**: The DOJ initiates a criminal investigation or prosecution of a political opposition figure, civil rights organization, journalist, or civil society actor using the criminal charging power for political rather than law enforcement purposes. The SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026, documented in full in Domain 29) is the template. This playbook addresses: what happens when a civil society organization you are affiliated with, or a political ally, is indicted.

**Domain references**: Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization and DOJ Capture), Domain 7 (First Amendment / Civil Liberties), Domain 6 (Judicial Independence), Domain 26 (Government Accountability)

**Probability assessment**: Active and ongoing. 22 documented retaliatory prosecutorial actions (Domain 29, Section 2). The SPLC indictment is the largest target so far. The pattern analysis in Domain 29 identifies four target categories: political opposition, Democratic officials, protest suppression, and civil society organizations. Each category has active cases.

---

### 72-Hour Response

**Hour 1-6: Legal representation**

Any civil society organization that receives a federal grand jury subpoena, target letter, or criminal complaint should contact criminal defense counsel specialized in First Amendment and civil rights matters within hours. Firms with documented track records: Paul Weiss (before its capitulation), Covington & Burling (represented CASA in the warrantless arrest cases, litigation-tracker-2026.md Section 1.3), Wilmer Hale, Davis Polk's public interest practice, ACLU National, and specialized nonprofit defense organizations like the Institute for Constitutional Advocacy and Protection (Georgetown Law).

Do not speak to federal investigators without counsel present. This is not obstruction — it is the constitutional right to counsel under the Sixth Amendment and the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination. Any civil society organization that anticipates being targeted should brief its senior staff on this before a crisis, not during one.

Within 6 hours, activate the mutual defense network. After the SPLC indictment, over 100 civil rights groups, labor unions, and religious organizations signed a mutual defense pact. That network exists. Contact it. Organizations in the network have committed to collective response — press statements, legal solidarity, and shared defense strategy.

**Hour 6-24: Public response**

The public response to a politically motivated prosecution has two goals: establish the narrative before the government does, and signal to the prosecutors that the political cost of the prosecution is higher than they calculated.

The SPLC's response within 24 hours of the indictment was instructive: CEO Bryan Fair immediately connected the indictment to the civil rights legacy the SPLC defends, identified the specific legal flaws (the Thompson v. United States bank fraud contradiction, the absence of victim donors), and called the indictment what it was — political weaponization. Former prosecutors (Weissmann, Zelinsky, Boynton) published assessments within 24 hours calling the legal theory defective.

Replicate this pattern: (1) immediate CEO/executive director public statement framing the prosecution as political; (2) former prosecutor assessment published within 24 hours identifying specific legal defects; (3) civil rights coalition solidarity statement; (4) donor reassurance (as the SPLC's donors demonstrated, the prosecution may actually increase donations from aligned supporters).

**Hour 24-72: Institutional defense**

File a motion to dismiss at the earliest procedurally available opportunity — this begins to create the judicial record on the legal defects. In the SPLC case, the bank fraud counts almost certainly fail under Thompson v. United States; a motion to dismiss on that ground alone creates a record. Courts do sometimes dismiss politically motivated prosecutions at the pleading stage, particularly when the legal theory is clearly defective.

Contact congressional oversight immediately. Senate and House Judiciary Committee Democratic staff directors should receive a factual summary of the indictment and its documented legal defects within 48 hours. A congressional hearing request on DOJ political prosecution patterns — including the SPLC case and the 22 documented retaliatory cases in Domain 29, Section 2 — creates political cost and generates an oversight record.

### 30-Day Response

**Days 1-30: Pattern documentation and publication**

The prosecutorial weaponization campaign becomes harder to sustain when the pattern is documented and widely published. Domain 29's Section 2 (22 documented retaliatory cases) provides the starting point. Within 30 days of a new case, publish an updated pattern analysis that includes the new case, with specific documentation of: (1) the temporal correlation between the public political activity and the prosecution initiation; (2) the procedural anomalies (did the FBI director personally announce the charges? was the indictment sealed or unsealed? how does the charging volume compare to analogous non-political cases?); (3) the legal defects (assessed by former prosecutors, not political advocates).

Publication in: Just Security (which published Weissmann's SPLC analysis), Lawfare, law review rapid-response journals (Harvard Law Review Forum, Yale Law Journal's online companion, Virginia Law Review In Brief). These are the venues that reach federal judges, law clerks, and the appellate court community.

**Days 15-30: International pressure**

Political prosecutions in a democracy are subject to international human rights scrutiny. The UN Special Rapporteur on the promotion and protection of human rights and fundamental freedoms while countering terrorism, and the UN Special Rapporteur on the independence of judges and lawyers, both have mandates that cover prosecutorial weaponization. Filing communications with these Special Rapporteurs within 30 days creates an international record that generates political cost for the administration in bilateral and multilateral diplomatic contexts.

The International Federation for Human Rights (FIDH) and Frontline Defenders (for human rights defenders at risk) have emergency protection mechanisms for civil society organizations under government attack.

### 90-Day Response

**Days 31-90: Reform legislation and structural accountability**

Domain 29, Section 4 documents the reform pathways: statutory special prosecutor independence, charging-decision transparency requirements, and congressional oversight restoration. Within 90 days of a documented politically motivated prosecution, introduce the relevant legislation as a concrete, specific bill — not a general statement of principle.

The most actionable: the DOJ Transparency in Charging Decisions Act (draft language in Domain 29, Section 4.3) would require the DOJ to report quarterly to Congress on all criminal investigations of public figures with political affiliations, including the initiating evidence, the charging decision rationale, and the reviewing official's identity. This does not prevent prosecution — it creates accountability for the decision.

### Failure Modes

**Failure mode 1**: The prosecution achieves its intimidation goal even if it ultimately fails in court. A multi-year prosecution depletes the target organization's resources, distracts its leadership, and chills similar organizations whether or not a conviction is ever obtained. The SPLC will spend years and millions in legal defense regardless of the outcome. Counter-strategy: the mutual defense network must provide direct financial support (legal defense fund contributions), public solidarity (reducing the isolation that amplifies intimidation), and rapid public assessment of the legal defects (reducing the reputational harm from news coverage of the indictment).

**Failure mode 2**: The judicial record is built in a hostile circuit where the legal theory, however defective, receives deference. Counter-strategy: appellate planning begins at indictment. Identify the circuit, research the panel composition, and prepare the appellate brief's structure before the district court rules.

**Failure mode 3**: Organizations self-censor to avoid becoming the next target. This is the intended effect of prosecutorial intimidation campaigns. Counter-strategy: public and visible mutual defense commitments — organizations publicly stating they will continue their missions and defend any member of the network that is targeted — create collective security that is harder to pick off individually than isolated organizations.

## Cross-Playbook Coordination Matrix

The five crises described above are most likely to occur in combination, not isolation. The administration's 2025-2026 pattern has been to apply multiple pressure vectors simultaneously — election interference and prosecutorial weaponization; civil service targeting and judicial intimidation; emergency EOs and agency dissolution. The following combinations require specific multi-playbook coordination:

**Election Interference + Prosecutorial Weaponization**: If election lawyers or election officials are personally targeted with prosecution while simultaneously defending an election interference challenge, their personal legal exposure creates a conflict that must be managed. Personal defense counsel must be independent of the election defense litigation team.

**Judicial Capture + Civil Service Targeting**: If the judges who would hear civil service appeals are simultaneously under impeachment pressure, the likelihood of early relief in the civil service cases decreases. Pre-file contempt motions naming specific officials. Build the record at every step. Accept that some first-instance rulings will be adverse and plan for the appellate timeline.

**Executive Overreach EO + Election Interference**: If an emergency declaration is used as the vehicle for election interference, the Playbook 1 (election interference) legal strategy and the Playbook 3 (executive overreach) legal strategy must be pursued together, with a unified litigation team. The constitutional arguments are distinct (Article I Section 4 vs. statutory authority) and both should be pled.

## Pre-Crisis Preparation Priorities

The playbooks above describe what to do when a crisis arrives. The following preparation actions should be completed before any crisis:

1. **Democracy Docket retainer**: Every state AG office and every major civil rights litigation organization should have an engagement letter with Democracy Docket (or the relevant election litigation firm) that can be activated immediately without negotiating a new representation agreement.

2. **AG coalition standing memo**: The 22-state AG coalition should have a pre-written standing memo for each of the five crisis scenarios, requiring only the factual details to be inserted. Constitutional Accountability Center and National Association of Attorneys General's public law section can support this.

3. **Whistleblower intake protocol**: Every federal agency with significant civil service defenders should have an established, secure intake protocol for whistleblower reports — separate from MSPB (which may be compromised), connected to union legal defense, and with a direct congressional oversight contact.

4. **Mutual defense network activation procedures**: The 100+ organization mutual defense pact (formed after the SPLC indictment) needs an activation protocol — who calls whom, in what order, with what message — so that the first 12 hours of a new prosecution target don't require figuring out the network from scratch.

5. **Academic rapid response capacity**: At least five law schools should have standing rapid-response teams that can publish legal analysis within 24 hours of a major executive action. This is the complement to the former-prosecutor network — it provides doctrinal analysis alongside prosecutorial experience assessment.
