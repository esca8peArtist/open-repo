## 1. Unique Angle (200 words)

Domain 40's defining contribution is the framework's first systematic analysis of commercial surveillance infrastructure as electoral attack infrastructure. Two adjacent domains — Domain 43 (Epistemic Infrastructure/Deepfakes) and Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms) — address deepfakes and government-side election manipulation respectively. Neither addresses the commercial supply chain that makes both possible at scale.

The unique angle is the supply chain argument: voter manipulation in 2026 runs on a commercial data-broker foundation. Thomson Reuters CLEAR, LexisNexis Accurint, and hundreds of specialized political data firms aggregate voter rolls against consumer behavior, location records, court filings, and social media to build behavioral voter profiles. Those profiles power geofencing operations that target suppression messaging at specific demographic communities without leaving a statutory footprint. The PNAS 2026 study's finding — a 1.9% voter turnout reduction from targeted digital suppression ads, with non-white voters in battleground counties receiving such ads nearly 10 times more often than white voters in non-battleground white-majority counties — quantifies the democratic harm.

The domain also provides the first cross-domain synthesis connecting the data broker problem (present in Domain 25 as the FISA loophole, in Domain 36 as ImmigrationOS inputs, in corporate accountability research as ICE contractor infrastructure) to the electoral democracy problem specifically. Commercial surveillance infrastructure does not discriminate between use cases — and that non-discrimination is the democratic threat.

## 2. Key Themes and Evidence (300 words)

**The data broker supply chain.** Thomson Reuters CLEAR holds six ICE contracts (total potential value ~$54 million) while marketing the same aggregated data products to campaigns. LexisNexis Accurint holds records on 276+ million US residents; its PowerView Score incorporates Equifax credit data for economic messaging targeting. Specialized political data firms — L2 (220 million voters), TargetSmart (171 million cell phone numbers matched to voter files), i360 — build partisan voter profiles from these inputs, enriched with dark web data and consumer transaction records. A single Palantir query can combine Thomson Reuters CLEAR public records with commercial consumer data and law enforcement investigative tools. CLEAR is integrated system-to-system with Palantir's analytical platform.

**DOJ voter file collection as government-commercial synthesis.** The DOJ demanded voter files (including driver's license numbers and partial Social Security numbers) from at least 33 states, filing suit against 29 states and D.C. Thirteen states complied — Alaska, Arkansas, Indiana, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nebraska, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming. The Brennan Center obtained the confidential MOU: DOJ will share collected voter data with contractors with no binding encryption requirements, no audit logs, and no restrictions on contractor data retention or resale. Seven federal courts in seven states have dismissed the DOJ suits; the thirteen compliant states' data has already been integrated into contractor databases.

**2026 deepfakes as campaign infrastructure.** The NRSC-Talarico ad (March 11, 2026) — first national party committee deployment of a lifelike synthetic candidate video for over 60 seconds — operated in technical compliance with Texas disclosure law while remaining an intentional deception. Collins-Ossoff (Georgia Senate, November 2025) and Virginia Spanberger (February and April 2026, one with no disclosure at all) demonstrate that disclosure-only frameworks are compliance-as-cover. The PNAS 2026 study estimates targeted digital suppression ads may have prevented approximately 4.7 million people from voting in the 2016 election extrapolated from a 1.9% measured suppression effect.

**Regulatory vacuum.** FEC has been in 3-3 partisan deadlock since at least 2023; its September 2024 interpretive rule on AI in campaign ads is not a regulation and has produced no enforcement. FCC's AI robocall ruling closes only the robocall channel while all digital advertising remains unregulated. The December 2025 AI preemption executive order targets the 28-state disclosure patchwork — the only existing accountability layer — without replacing it with any federal standard.

**EU benchmark.** DSA prohibits political microtargeting using sensitive data categories. EU Regulation 2024/900 bans microtargeting using political opinions, religious beliefs, racial origin, or sexual orientation. EU AI Act Article 50 (enforcement August 2, 2026) requires machine-readable synthetic content marking and user disclosure. Meta exited European political advertising entirely rather than attempt compliance. The same company deploys the same tools with no regulation in US elections.
