# Phase 3 Playbook: State Legislators

**Audience**: Democratic state legislative leaders, trifecta caucus policy directors, voting rights committee chairs, NCSL democratic caucus coordinators, state legislative electoral campaign directors

---

## Executive Summary

The post-*Callais* redistricting cascade is the single most urgent state legislative emergency in 2026. State legislatures in Alabama, Tennessee, Louisiana, and South Carolina are redrawing maps in special sessions specifically designed to eliminate or dilute Black congressional representation before the November midterm. As of May 18, South Carolina's House is voting on a bill that would eliminate Rep. James Clyburn's majority-Black District 6. In competitive states where Democrats have legislative influence, the next 30 days are the window to move protective legislation before the June 1 reconciliation passage deadline changes the political landscape. Beyond the immediate emergency, the structural opportunity is this: state legislatures enacted more than 200 election-related bills in the first four months of 2026 — the legislative capacity exists. The question is whether Democratic-controlled or split legislatures are using that capacity for access or suppression. The Illinois constitutional amendment model (explicitly requiring race-conscious redistricting in anticipation of federal VRA gutting) is the template every Democratic trifecta or near-trifecta state should be replicating right now. Any state that has not begun drafting such an amendment by July 1, 2026 will miss the window to get it on a 2026 ballot.

These 19 states can enact protective voting legislation immediately.

**Split-legislature states with Democratic governors (veto power + leverage)**: Arizona, Kentucky, Louisiana (re: redistricting), North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Wisconsin. These states cannot pass legislation but governors can veto suppressive bills and deploy executive authority for voter access.

## Strategic Opportunities

**Opportunity 1: Illinois-model constitutional amendment in 4-6 trifecta states (deadline: July 1 for 2026 ballot)**

The Illinois constitutional amendment requires race-conscious redistricting and was explicitly designed as a *Callais* contingency. After *Callais* was decided April 29, this contingency became operative. Six trifecta states with sufficient minority populations and redistricting stakes should replicate this amendment: California, Michigan, New York, Maryland, New Jersey, and Washington. The legal drafting is not complex — the Illinois language is the template. The political challenge is getting it through both chambers and onto the November 2026 ballot before adjournment deadlines. In most states, the ballot placement deadline is July-August 2026. The leadership asks: call a special session if necessary; this is the most consequential state legislative act available after *Callais*.

**Opportunity 2: State-level documentary proof of citizenship opposition**

Four states enacted proof-of-citizenship requirements in the first four months of 2026 (Florida, Mississippi, South Dakota, Utah). In every state where Democrats have veto power or any chamber leverage, this expansion must be stopped. The concrete legislative action: in split states, Democratic minority caucuses should demand hearings that document the disenfranchisement impact (percentage of eligible voters without passports or birth certificates by county, by income level, by race) before any bill advances. In states where a bill has already passed one chamber, the Democratic governor's veto is the backstop. Pre-stage the veto message with specific constitutional analysis — the NVRA conflict theory that the AG coalition has already briefed.

**Opportunity 3: State voting access omnibus in trifecta states (Minnesota model)**

The Minnesota 2023 Democracy for the People Act (automatic voter registration, pre-registration for 16-17, permanent absentee list, multilingual materials, voter intimidation protections) demonstrated that individual voting access provisions are easier to challenge legally and politically than a comprehensive omnibus. Any trifecta state that has not enacted a voting access omnibus should have one drafted by August 2026 for Day 1 of the 2027 session. The sequence matters: enact voting access before other reform because voting access creates the electoral conditions for subsequent reform.

**Opportunity 4: State Medicaid floor legislation as OBBBA counter**

The OBBBA reconciliation bill's Medicaid cuts, if enacted, will be implemented through state Medicaid programs. Democratic trifecta states can enact state Medicaid floor legislation — statutes that maintain existing Medicaid eligibility standards regardless of federal cuts, funding the gap from state general revenue. The federal-state cost-sharing structure means this is expensive; but for states with sufficient fiscal capacity (California, New York, Massachusetts, Illinois, Washington), it is operationally achievable and politically powerful. California and New York should have state Medicaid floor legislation drafted before the OBBBA passes the Senate.

## Implementation Pathway

**Weeks 1-2 (May 18-31): Illinois amendment replication and South Carolina monitoring**

Immediate: contact the Illinois Senate President's and House Speaker's offices for the constitutional amendment language. Distribute to the six target trifecta states' majority leaders with a cover memo explaining the *Callais* trigger and the July 1 ballot deadline. The ask to each: convene a leadership meeting this week to assess feasibility of a special session or accelerated floor vote.

South Carolina monitoring: the May 18 House vote on the Clyburn district elimination is the most immediate legislative crisis. If the bill passes, Democratic governors and legislators in states with similar configurations (where a single majority-minority district exists under a Democratic representative) should immediately audit whether their state's maps are vulnerable to the same maneuver. North Carolina, Georgia, and Louisiana Democratic caucuses should complete this audit by May 31.

**Weeks 3-8 (June 1-30): Constitutional amendment filings and Medicaid floor drafting**

Six trifecta states file constitutional amendments. In states where the legislative session is already closed, call a special session. The political cover for a special session is *Callais* — the Supreme Court eliminated 50 years of federal voting rights protection; the state must act. This is not an aggressive political posture; it is a direct response to a specific legal development.

State Medicaid floor legislation: California, New York, Massachusetts, and Illinois state legislative leaders receive the Medicaid floor statute template. Commit to a floor vote before the end of the year, contingent on OBBBA passage. If the reconciliation bill does not pass, the floor statute becomes a standing commitment regardless.

**Weeks 9-16 (July-August): 2026 election cycle prep and DLCC coordination**

The 2026 state legislative elections are as consequential as federal elections. 2030 redistricting control depends on which party holds state legislative chambers after 2026. The DLCC should have a targeted list of the 25 most consequential state legislative chambers — where flipping a chamber or breaking a supermajority directly affects 2030 redistricting — and those chambers should receive maximum DLCC investment.

Pre-election legislative actions: in every state with a competitive 2026 election, Democratic legislative leaders should force floor votes on popular voting access provisions (early voting expansion, automatic registration, mail ballot access) before adjournment. Even in states where the bill will fail, a recorded floor vote is an electoral accountability record.

**Months 4-12: Interstate compact architecture and Year 2-3 prep**

Interstate compacts on voting rights: a multistate compact under which signatory states commit to specific voting access standards regardless of federal minimum. AG coalition offices serve as the legal drafting resource; state legislative leaders are the authorizing bodies. Target 15 states in a voting rights compact, with a signing ceremony timed to the midterm election results.

Year 2 (2027 sessions): in every trifecta state, have pre-drafted omnibus legislation for Day 1: (1) voting access omnibus (Minnesota model), (2) state civil service protection statute, (3) state Medicaid floor legislation, (4) independent redistricting commission constitutional amendment if not already enacted. These are the four measures that constitute a complete democratic defense package at the state level.

## Risks and Contingencies

**Risk 1: Republican special session redistricting speed advantage.** Republican governors in Alabama, Tennessee, and Louisiana called and completed special sessions to redraw maps faster than Democratic states are responding with protective amendments. The Democratic trifecta response must match the speed. Constitutional amendment ballot deadlines (typically July-August) are the external constraint; the leadership decision to act must be made in May to meet those deadlines.

**Risk 2: State fiscal constraints on Medicaid floor legislation.** California, New York, and Massachusetts have sufficient fiscal capacity; smaller trifecta states (Rhode Island, Vermont, Delaware) do not. The Medicaid floor legislation ask should be calibrated to state fiscal capacity. Smaller states should focus on enacting the eligibility floor without funding gap commitments; larger states should fund the gap.

**Risk 3: Federal preemption challenges.** The administration has shown willingness to threaten preemption of state laws it opposes. State voting access legislation is on the strongest possible constitutional ground — the Elections Clause gives states primary authority over their own elections. State Medicaid floor legislation may be challenged as conflicting with the federal Medicaid cooperative structure. Pre-stage constitutional analysis with AG coalition offices before enacting either.

**Risk 4: 2026 electoral map uncertainty.** The Callais redistricting cascade is still incomplete; final congressional maps in six states are not settled. State legislative electoral strategies should not be finalized until the congressional map landscape is clear (target: September 2026). The DLCC's targeted chamber list may shift as congressional maps are finalized.
