## Email 3 — National Women's Law Center (NWLC)

**To**: info@nwlc.org (or Emily Martin, Economic Security Program)
**Subject**: Gendered democratic deficit research — CTC window and Senate Finance markup

---

Dear NWLC Economic Security team,

I am writing to share research relevant to your work on childcare policy and women's economic security, timed to the Senate Finance Committee CTC markup window open through June 30.

The document "Economic Precarity and the Civic Participation Crisis" directly addresses the democratic dimension of the economic barriers NWLC documents: it quantifies a gendered democratic deficit that flows directly from childcare inaccessibility and wage stagnation for women. American Time Use Survey data shows that full-time employed mothers spend 7 more hours per week on childcare and household work than fathers in equivalent employment. For the 21 million single mothers earning an average of $31,000 annually, childcare at the national $1,230/month median would consume 47-58% of gross income — effectively pricing them out of both labor force stability and civic engagement simultaneously.

The research documents that mothers with children under 5 participate in organized civic activities at rates 18-25% lower than childless women, and single mothers show the most severe exclusion. Survey data consistently identifies "cannot attend meetings or voting due to childcare" as the primary participation barrier for this demographic — not motivation, not registration, not access to information. The physical impossibility of attending meetings while managing undivided childcare responsibility is the barrier.

This analysis provides a democratic design frame for your CTC expansion and childcare subsidy advocacy: the policies NWLC supports on economic security grounds are simultaneously the policies that address the most severe civic participation exclusion in the US electorate. A fully refundable CTC and expanded childcare subsidy programs do not merely reduce poverty — they expand the electorate by making participation materially possible for the demographic most systematically excluded from it.
