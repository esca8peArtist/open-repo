## Why Courts Matter Right Now

The federal judiciary is the primary institution currently constraining the Trump administration's most aggressive executive actions. As of March 2026, plaintiffs have prevailed in the overwhelming majority of cases that have reached a decision: in immigration and civil liberties matters, challengers have won in approximately 350 of 362 contested federal district court rulings. Courts have blocked warrantless arrests, halted data-sharing agreements that violated federal privacy law, stopped construction of unauthorized detention facilities, and preserved oversight mechanisms that the administration tried to eliminate. These wins are real but fragile — the Supreme Court's 6-3 conservative supermajority has reversed several lower-court injunctions on emergency application, and the administration has openly defied court orders in multiple cases. Tracking these cases matters because the legal record being built now will shape how much authority the executive branch retains for years to come.

## Category 1: Immigration Enforcement — Warrantless Arrests and Racial Profiling

### 1.1 CASA v. DHS (Washington, D.C. Immigration Arrests)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Columbia — Judge Beryl Howell
- **Filed by**: CASA (national immigration organization) and four D.C. community members, on behalf of a class
- **What it challenges**: A pattern of warrantless immigration arrests and checkpoint operations in D.C. neighborhoods with large Latino populations, conducted without required probable cause determinations or flight-risk assessments
- **Current status**: Preliminary injunction in place, issued December 2025, prohibiting immigration agents from arresting individuals without a warrant or without probable cause of both unlawful immigration status and flight risk. As of February 19, 2026, plaintiffs filed a motion alleging active noncompliance — the government continues making warrantless arrests in defiance of the order.
- **Next deadline**: Compliance hearing pending; plaintiffs are seeking enforcement of the injunction
- **Why it matters**: D.C. is a test case for the constitutional floor on Fourth Amendment rights during immigration enforcement. If the government can ignore court orders here, in full view of the federal judiciary, it signals it will do so everywhere.
- **Where to track it**: [ACLU of DC](https://www.acludc.org/press-releases/federal-judge-blocks-unlawful-immigration-arrests-in-washington-d-c/)

### 1.2 Ramirez Ovando v. Noem (Colorado Warrantless Arrests)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Colorado — Judge R. Brooke Jackson
- **Filed by**: ACLU of Colorado
- **What it challenges**: ICE's practice of conducting warrantless arrests without probable cause or flight-risk determination in Colorado
- **Current status**: Preliminary injunction issued November 2025, ordering ICE to follow its own legal standards before making warrantless arrests. In February 2026, the judge ordered the administration back to court after evidence emerged of continued noncompliance. In March 2026 testimony, ICE supervisors admitted they were receiving conflicting guidance from Washington D.C. about when the injunction applied. The judge expressed significant frustration on the record.
- **Next deadline**: Ongoing compliance monitoring; contempt proceedings possible
- **Why it matters**: ICE agents testifying that they don't know whether to follow a federal court order is a direct window into how the administration is managing (or failing to manage) the boundary between executive enforcement and judicial authority.
- **Where to track it**: [ACLU-CO case page](https://www.aclu-co.org/cases/ramirez-ovando-et-al-v-noem-et-al/)

### 1.3 Hussen v. Noem (Minnesota Racial Profiling — Operation Metro Surge)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Minnesota — Judge Eric Tostrud
- **Case number**: 0:26-cv-00324
- **Filed by**: ACLU, ACLU of Minnesota, Covington & Burling, on behalf of three community members and a class
- **What it challenges**: ICE and CBP's policy of conducting stops and arrests in Minnesota based on race and ethnicity, without individualized reasonable suspicion — documented during "Operation Metro Surge"
- **Current status**: The court found in March 2026 that plaintiffs made a "clear showing" that federal agents detained people "solely on the basis of their race" on at least 23 occasions, and that the government had adopted a policy authorizing ethnicity-based stops without reasonable suspicion. Despite this finding, the court denied a preliminary injunction because the surge in agents had been drawn down, reducing immediate risk. The underlying case continues.
- **Next deadline**: Case proceeds on the merits; no injunction means ongoing risk if another surge is ordered
- **Why it matters**: A federal judge confirming that the U.S. government adopted a race-based arrest policy is significant — both for the legal record and as documentation for future enforcement accountability
- **Where to track it**: [ACLU case page](https://www.aclu.org/cases/hussen-v-noem) | [Just Security analysis](https://www.justsecurity.org/130745/minnesota-ice-racial-profiling/)

## Category 2: ICE Detention Facilities

### 2.1 Maryland v. Noem (Williamsport ICE Warehouse — NEPA/APA)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Maryland
- **Case number**: Case 26-733
- **Filed by**: Maryland Attorney General Anthony G. Brown
- **What it challenges**: DHS and ICE's purchase and $102.4 million renovation of an 825,000-square-foot warehouse in Williamsport (Washington County), Maryland, to house up to 1,500 immigration detainees — without conducting the required environmental review (NEPA) or public notice and comment (APA)
- **Current status**: Complaint filed February 23, 2026. A federal judge granted a 14-day TRO (reported around March 10, 2026), halting construction. The court found the administration "likely failed" to conduct required environmental review and that the renovation project — including security fencing, exterior lighting, a checkpoint, and sewer modifications — likely constitutes a "major Federal action" triggering a mandatory environmental impact study. The TRO period is being used to consider Maryland's broader challenge.
- **Next deadline**: Preliminary injunction hearing on the broader challenge; if court finds a NEPA violation, full environmental review could delay the facility by months
- **Why it matters**: This case establishes whether the administration can stand up large-scale immigration detention infrastructure by skipping federal environmental law. A win here would create precedent applicable to the Social Circle, Georgia facility and others.
- **Where to track it**: [Maryland AG case documents](https://oag.maryland.gov/News/pages/Attorney-General-Brown-Files-Lawsuit-to-Stop-Construction-of-Unlawful-ICE-Detention-Facility-in-Washington-County.aspx) | [Prism News](https://www.prismnews.com/news/maryland-sues-to-stop-conversion-of-1024m-warehouse-into-1500bed-ice-facility)

### 2.2 Alligator Alcatraz (South Florida Detention Facility — NEPA/Public Records)

- **Court**: U.S. Court of Appeals for the 11th Circuit; separate state court proceedings
- **Filed by**: Multiple environmental and civil rights groups; Friends of the Everglades; separate public records litigation
- **What it challenges**: Operation of the "Alligator Alcatraz" immigration detention camp in the Florida Everglades, including: (1) failure to conduct NEPA environmental review; (2) conditions inside the facility; (3) access to attorneys; (4) Florida's withholding of public records
- **Current status**: The 11th Circuit issued a 2-1 ruling allowing the facility to remain operational, staying a lower court injunction pending appeal. A divided appeals court reasoned that because the federal government had not yet reimbursed Florida's costs at the time, federal environmental law did not apply. Oral arguments on the NEPA question are scheduled for April 2026. In a parallel state proceeding, Circuit Judge Angela Dempsey ordered release of Florida state records in early March 2026 — released documents show the facility costs over $1 million per day to operate (with a peak "burn rate" of $3 million/day in its earliest weeks). An environmental assessment found carbon monoxide and particulate matter emissions from generators and vehicle use exceeding regulatory thresholds.
- **Next deadline**: 11th Circuit oral argument, April 2026
- **Why it matters**: The facility has become a symbol of the administration's detention expansion. The cost data — over $1M/day — and the environmental findings create political and legal pressure regardless of the NEPA ruling. The records fight also sets precedent for transparency around state-federal detention partnerships.

### 2.3 Social Circle, Georgia — Water Cutoff (Preemption Watch)

- **Court**: No federal lawsuit filed as of March 19, 2026 — but federal litigation anticipated
- **Filed by**: N/A (current posture is municipal resistance)
- **What it challenges**: DHS purchased a 1-million-square-foot warehouse in Social Circle, Georgia, to house up to 10,000 immigration detainees — nearly doubling the town's current population. The City of Social Circle's water permit allows 1 million gallons/day; its sewage plant is already at capacity (660,000 gal/day). The facility's projected demand would exceed both limits.
- **Current status**: City Manager cut off water service to the warehouse on March 17, 2026. The City Council voted on March 18 to back that decision. Legal observers have noted that the Trump administration could sue the city under federal preemption doctrine. One headline quoted a source warning "the city is going to get their pants sued off." No federal legal action filed as of the date of this report.
- **Next deadline**: Watch for a DHS/DOJ filing seeking to compel water service, likely under the Supremacy Clause
- **Why it matters**: The Social Circle situation tests whether local governments can use infrastructure and permitting authority as a brake on federal detention expansion. If the administration prevails in preemption, it removes a significant local resistance tool. If cities hold, it creates a template.

### 2.4 Immigration Indefinite Detention — Due Process Habeas Corpus (Washington State and National)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, Western District of Washington; over 160 federal district courts nationally
- **Filed by**: ACLU, National Immigrant Justice Center, individual habeas petitioners
- **What it challenges**: The administration's policy — adopted in July 2025 — that anyone present illegally is subject to "mandatory detention" with no eligibility for bond, regardless of length of residence, family ties, or circumstances. The Tacoma immigration court had separately been misclassifying detainees as mandatory holds.
- **Current status**: The Trump administration's detention policy has been challenged in at least 362 federal cases. Challengers have prevailed in 350 of those cases, before over 160 different judges in approximately 50 courts. More than 100 new habeas corpus petitions were filed in Washington State in January 2026 alone. The U.S. District Court for the Western District of Washington ruled in April 2025 that the Tacoma misclassification practice was likely illegal and ordered bond hearings for wrongfully detained individuals. The Board of Immigration Appeals issued a 2026 decision (Yajure Hurtado) eliminating bond hearings in certain indefinite detention contexts, which is itself now under legal challenge.
- **Next deadline**: Ongoing — habeas petitions filed continuously; the Yajure Hurtado BIA decision is likely headed to circuit review
- **Why it matters**: The administration's theory — that all undocumented people may be held indefinitely without individual bond determination — is being rejected by courts at a rate of roughly 97%. But each rejection requires a separate legal fight; there is no single injunction covering the class.
- **Where to track it**: [InvestigateWest](https://www.investigatewest.org/rulings-found-immigrant-detentions-flouted-due-process/) | [ACLU class action press release](https://www.aclu.org/press-releases/new-class-action-lawsuit-challenges-widespread-denial-of-due-process-in-immigration-courts)

## Category 3: DOGE and Government Data Access

### 3.1 AFSCME v. SSA (DOGE / Social Security Data Access)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Maryland (Judge Ellen Hollander) → 4th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals → U.S. Supreme Court
- **Filed by**: AFSCME (government workers' union) and allied plaintiffs
- **What it challenges**: DOGE's access to Social Security Administration systems of record containing personally identifiable information on tens of millions of Americans
- **Current status**: This case has traveled through all three levels of the federal courts. Judge Hollander issued a preliminary injunction blocking DOGE access and ordering deletion of non-anonymized data already accessed. The 4th Circuit kept the injunction in place. The Supreme Court, in a 6-3 unsigned order (June 2025 — SSA v. AFSCME, No. 24A1063), granted the administration's emergency application and allowed DOGE access to proceed while litigation continues. A subsequent DOJ filing revealed that DOGE had shared SSA data through a third-party Cloudflare server "not approved for storing SSA data," and that communications and data use were "potentially outside of SSA policy and/or noncompliant" with the court's own order. As of March 2026, the underlying litigation continues; the IRS CEO refused to fully answer congressional questions about data-sharing practices in March 2026 testimony.
- **Next deadline**: Merits proceedings ongoing; possible contempt/compliance motion based on the Cloudflare disclosure
- **Why it matters**: The Supreme Court's 6-3 decision to lift the injunction was a major loss — it allowed DOGE access to some of the most sensitive personal data the federal government holds, on precedent that could apply to IRS, Medicare, and other databases. The evidence of court order violations creates a contempt avenue.

### 3.2 IRS–ICE Data Sharing Injunctions (Multiple Cases)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Massachusetts (Judge Indira Talwani); U.S. District Court, D.C. Circuit (Judge Colleen Kollar-Kotelly); D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals
- **Filed by**: Multiple immigrant community organizations and nonprofits, including Centro de Trabajadores Unidos
- **What it challenges**: A Memorandum of Understanding signed April 2025 between Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, allowing ICE to submit names and addresses of undocumented immigrants to the IRS for cross-verification against confidential tax records
- **Current status**: Multiple overlapping proceedings with conflicting results:
  - **Judge Talwani (Massachusetts)**: Issued a preliminary injunction on February 5, 2026, blocking the MOU as likely violating federal tax confidentiality rules and the APA
  - **Judge Kollar-Kotelly (D.C.)**: Found that the IRS violated federal law approximately **42,695 times** by sharing confidential taxpayer addresses with ICE. Two court orders continue to block large-scale data transfers and prohibit ICE from acting on IRS information already in its possession
  - **D.C. Circuit**: Denied a preliminary injunction to Centro de Trabajadores Unidos, meaning some sharing continues pending full merits review
  - A separate ruling found the IRS had **broken the law** in erroneously sharing thousands of people's information with ICE over the summer under the MOU
- **Next deadline**: Merits briefing; possible interlocutory appeals
- **Why it matters**: Tax data is among the most sensitive information the government holds. People who filed taxes voluntarily — doing the right thing — are having that information weaponized against them. The 42,695 figure represents documented, adjudicated violations, not allegations.
- **Where to track it**: [FindLaw — injunction analysis](https://www.findlaw.com/legalblogs/law-and-life/federal-judges-injunction-halts-irss-taxpayer-data-sharing-with-dhs-and-ice/) | [Al Jazeera — 42,695 violations](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/27/us-tax-agency-broke-privacy-law-approximately-42695-times-judge-says/) | [FedScoop — ICE use blocked](https://fedscoop.com/judge-blocks-irs-ice-data-sharing-agreement/)

## Category 4: Birthright Citizenship

### 4.1 Trump v. Barbara (and consolidated cases — Birthright Citizenship EO)

- **Court**: U.S. Supreme Court (Case No. 25-365); consolidated with CASA v. Trump and related challenges
- **Filed by**: State AGs (22-state coalition led by Massachusetts), CASA, ACLU, NAACP LDF, and others
- **What it challenges**: Trump's January 20, 2025 executive order purporting to strip automatic citizenship from children born in the United States if their parents are present unlawfully or temporarily — in direct conflict with the 14th Amendment's plain text, over a century of Supreme Court precedent, and federal statute
- **Current status**: Every federal court to have ruled on the merits has blocked the order. The Supreme Court accepted the case and has scheduled oral arguments for April 1, 2026, as part of the March argument session (March 23–25, March 30–April 1). A decision is expected by late June or early July 2026. AGs from 22 states, the ABA, and numerous amicus groups have urged the Court to reject the order. The Trump administration argues the 14th Amendment was intended only to guarantee citizenship to formerly enslaved people, not to extend a general birthright to children of noncitizens.
- **Next deadline**: Oral argument April 1, 2026; decision expected June–July 2026
- **Why it matters**: This is potentially the most consequential immigration law case in over a century. The 14th Amendment has been understood to guarantee birthright citizenship since 1898 (United States v. Wong Kim Ark). A ruling in the administration's favor would strip citizenship from an estimated hundreds of thousands of people born in the United States each year and upend the legal status of an unknown number of adults who were born here.
- **Where to track it**: [SCOTUSblog case page](https://www.scotusblog.com/2026/01/supreme-court-will-hear-birthright-citizenship-case-on-april-1/) | [Constitutional Accountability Center](https://www.theusconstitution.org/litigation/trump-v-barbara/) | [ACLU analysis](https://www.aclu.org/news/immigrants-rights/trumps-birthright-citizenship-executive-order-what-happens-next)

## Category 5: Free Speech, Due Process, and Political Detention

### 5.1 Khalil v. Trump / Khalil v. Bondi (Mahmoud Khalil Detention)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court (New Jersey); 3rd U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals; immigration courts
- **Filed by**: Mahmoud Khalil, represented by the ACLU and partner counsel
- **What it challenges**: The Trump administration's arrest and attempted deportation of Mahmoud Khalil, a former Columbia University graduate student and legal permanent resident, detained on March 8, 2025, for his role in the 2024 pro-Palestinian protests on campus. The administration invoked an obscure provision of immigration law (the "zipper clause" / INA § 237(a)(4)) that allows deportation of LPRs on foreign policy grounds — without any criminal charges.
- **Current status**: Khalil was transferred from Columbia's residential building to the LaSalle Detention Center in Jena, Louisiana, shortly after arrest. He was released on bail on June 20, 2025. In January 2026, the 3rd Circuit ruled that Khalil must exhaust immigration proceedings before seeking federal habeas review. His legal team plans to appeal that ruling. As of March 10, 2026 — one year after his arrest — Khalil returned to Columbia University and stated publicly: "One year after, the government has not charged me with any crimes or presented any evidence that I committed wrongdoings whatsoever." His immigration case before an immigration judge is ongoing; a loss there could result in re-detention.
- **Next deadline**: Immigration court proceedings ongoing; possible 3rd Circuit appeal of January 2026 ruling
- **Why it matters**: Khalil's case is the administration's flagship attempt to use immigration law as a political speech suppression tool against noncitizen activists. If the "foreign policy" deportation ground holds, it creates a mechanism to remove any lawful permanent resident the administration disfavors without any criminal process.
- **Where to track it**: [NPR — one year status](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/10/nx-s1-5716101/mahmoud-khalil-legal-fight) | [CIS analysis of zipper clause](https://cis.org/Arthur/Case-Columbia-Activist-Mahmoud-Khalil-Gets-Caught-Zipper-Clause) | [Wikipedia — comprehensive timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detention_of_Mahmoud_Khalil)

## Category 6: Oversight Mechanisms

### 6.1 OIDO / CRCL / CIS Ombudsman Reinstatement

- **Court**: Not currently in active federal litigation (advocacy-driven reinstatement)
- **Filed by**: Advocacy coalition including CLINIC (Catholic Legal Immigration Network), immigrant rights organizations
- **What it challenges**: DHS's March 21, 2025 unilateral closure of three statutory oversight offices: the Office of Immigration Detention Ombudsman (OIDO), the Office of Civil Rights and Civil Liberties (CRCL), and the CIS Ombudsman
- **Current status**: After approximately two months and significant legal and public pressure, DHS restored all three offices. OIDO is currently operational with its case management, detention oversight, policy and standards, and other divisions functioning. However, advocates and litigants have raised ongoing concerns about whether the offices are fully staffed and whether their findings are being acted on. Litigation continues to shape their operational scope.
- **Next deadline**: Ongoing advocacy monitoring; no imminent court deadline
- **Why it matters**: OIDO is the primary civilian oversight mechanism for immigration detention conditions. Without it, the roughly 40,000+ people held in ICE detention facilities at any given time have no independent internal advocate. Its restoration — even if partial — matters for the humanitarian record and for ongoing litigation about detention conditions.
- **Where to track it**: [VisaVerge — reinstatement coverage](https://www.visaverge.com/immigration/dhs-reverses-decision-to-abolish-immigration-ombudsman-and-oversight-offices/) | [CLINIC action toolkit](https://www.cliniclegal.org/resources/clinics-action-toolkit-restore-ombudsman-and-civil-rights-offices)

## Category 7: Voting Rights and Election Integrity

### 7.1 DOJ Voter Data Demands — Multi-State Litigation

- **Court**: Multiple U.S. District Courts; several dismissed at district level; others pending
- **Filed by**: Department of Justice (plaintiff), suing states; state AGs defending; Brennan Center, state alliances intervening
- **What it challenges**: DOJ has demanded full, unredacted voter registration files — including partial Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, and birthdates — from at least 44 states and D.C., ostensibly to verify voter roll compliance with the National Voter Registration Act. States that refused have been sued.
- **Current status**: As of March 6, 2026, DOJ has filed suits against 29 states and D.C. New lawsuits include Utah, Oklahoma, Kentucky, West Virginia, and New Jersey. Federal district courts have dismissed DOJ's lawsuits against California, Michigan, and Oregon, holding that federal law does not require states to turn over sensitive voter data. Utah moved on March 18, 2026 to have its case thrown out. Most states are providing publicly available versions of voter rolls (without SSNs) and resisting the broader demand.
- **Next deadline**: Multiple simultaneous proceedings; Utah motion to dismiss pending; remaining circuit splits developing
- **Why it matters**: Critics argue the DOJ voter data push serves two goals: (1) building a pretext to challenge midterm results by claiming voter rolls are inaccurate, and (2) creating a federal voter database that could be cross-referenced with immigration enforcement data. The Stateline analysis from March 13, 2026 concluded it is "laying groundwork to undermine confidence in midterms."

### 7.2 Texas Redistricting — LULAC v. Abbott (Congressional Map Gerrymandering)

- **Court**: Three-judge U.S. District Court panel (Texas) → U.S. Supreme Court
- **Filed by**: League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC) and minority voter plaintiffs
- **What it challenges**: Texas's 2025 congressional map, which a three-judge federal panel found racially gerrymandered Black and Hispanic voters in violation of the Voting Rights Act — describing the evidence as showing that "Texas racially gerrymandered the 2025 Map"

- **Next deadline**: Merits proceedings continue; practical effect of the Supreme Court stay means the gerrymandered map will be used in the 2026 elections
- **Why it matters**: The Supreme Court's willingness to stay a district court finding of racial gerrymandering means minority voters in Texas are being forced to vote under a map that a federal court found discriminatory. This is the practical application of the Court's increasingly permissive approach to voting rights litigation.

### 7.4 DNC v. DOJ (Midterm Election Surveillance Records)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court
- **Filed by**: Democratic National Committee
- **Filed**: March 10, 2026
- **What it challenges**: Seeks to compel the Trump administration to produce records relating to any potential deployment of federal agents or troops for the 2026 midterm elections

## Context: The Shadow Docket Is Now the Main Docket

On April 9, 2026, Justice Sonia Sotomayor told an audience at the University of Alabama School of Law that the Trump administration's volume of emergency appeals to the Supreme Court is "unprecedented in the court's history." Since January 20, 2025, the Supreme Court has issued approximately 35 emergency orders on its shadow docket responding to challenges to administration actions (as of mid-March 2026, per Ballotpedia); the administration has prevailed in approximately 80 percent of these. The pattern is consistent: lower courts enjoin an administration action on the merits, the administration seeks emergency relief, and the Supreme Court grants a stay without full briefing. This has become the primary vehicle by which the administration's most contested actions are preserved while litigation continues. The trend changes the meaning of every lower-court win in this tracker — injunctions are now provisional until the Supreme Court weighs in, and on the emergency docket the government's win rate is roughly 80 percent.

### 1.6 Gibson v. ICE / DHS (Minnesota Warrantless Home Entries) — NEW CASE

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Minnesota
- **Filed**: April 2, 2026
- **Filed by**: Protect Democracy, ACLU of Minnesota, ACLU of the District of Columbia, on behalf of six Minnesotans. Lead plaintiffs: Garrison Gibson (a Liberian national) and his wife Teyana Gibson Brown.

This case opens a second and more serious front — forcible home entry — and aims squarely at the internal memo rather than the downstream conduct. If the court reaches the memo itself, the ruling could apply to every ICE field office in the country. The secrecy of the memo (verbal training only) is itself a discovery target: it suggests the agency knew the policy would not survive Fourth Amendment scrutiny if exposed.

### 1.7 Castañon Nava v. DHS (Chicago Warrantless Arrests) — NEW CASE

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, Northern District of Illinois — Judge Jeffrey Cummings
- **Filed by**: ACLU of Illinois, National Immigrant Justice Center, partner firms
- **What it challenges**: ICE warrantless arrests and vehicle stops in the Chicago Field Office jurisdiction, originally through a 2018 class action that produced a consent decree (approved February 2022, effective May 2022, originally set to expire February 2, 2026).

In February 2026, the court ordered the release of four people whose arrests violated the decree and required DHS to produce a master list of all warrantless arrests since June 2025. Second, the evidentiary record — 78% low-risk arrests, field warrants filled out at the scene — demonstrates that the warrantless arrest pattern is not an operational necessity but a policy choice. Third, this is the Seventh Circuit's entry point into the warrantless-arrest litigation, joining the D.C., Fourth, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits where similar cases are active.

## Category 3 Update: DOGE and Government Data Access

### 3.1 AFSCME v. SSA — Fourth Circuit Reverses the SSA Injunction

- **Update as of April 11, 2026**: In early April 2026 the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, deferring to a prior Supreme Court interim order, vacated the district court's preliminary injunction that had blocked DOGE personnel from accessing Social Security Administration data, and remanded. The majority held that plaintiffs had not shown a likelihood of irreparable harm sufficient to support injunctive relief.
- **The dissent is the story**: Judge Robert King, writing in partial dissent, argued that the majority was required to reassess irreparable harm on the basis of the corrected factual record that had emerged since the original proceedings — a record that includes the DOJ's own acknowledgment that DOGE associates improperly accessed SSA data, that a DOGE employee signed an agreement to share SSA data with "an unnamed political advocacy group that wanted to overturn election results in certain states," that much of DOGE's data access occurred outside official protocols, that an unauthorized server was used, and that SSA itself still does not know the full scope of what DOGE accessed or shared. King wrote that SSA and the other defendants had provided "patently false information to the district court in the preliminary injunction proceedings."
- **Why it matters**: Three things. First, this is the most explicit acknowledgment on the record so far that a federal agency provided false information to a federal court to obtain a favorable injunction posture — and was then rewarded with appellate deference to the Supreme Court's earlier stay. Second, the substantive revelation (DOGE data flowing to a group seeking to overturn election results) connects data-sharing litigation to the election integrity category in a way that has been suspected but not judicially confirmed until now. Third, the procedural move — lower courts treating an interim Supreme Court stay as binding on the merits even after the factual predicate collapses — is a pattern that will recur and deserves its own tracking thread.

### 3.2 DOGE/Treasury Data Sharing — D.C. District Court Grants Summary Judgment to Government — NEW

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Columbia
- **Filed by**: Alliance for Retired Americans, AFGE, SEIU
- **What it challenged**: Treasury Department's sharing of Bureau of the Fiscal Service (BFS) records — including names, SSNs, dates of birth, home addresses, bank account information — with DOGE personnel, alleging APA violations.
- **Ruling**: The D.C. District Court granted summary judgment to the Treasury Department, holding that the internal data sharing did not constitute "final agency action" under the APA and was therefore unreviewable.
- **Why it matters**: This is a significant procedural defeat for data-privacy litigation against DOGE. The "not final agency action" holding means that internal data sharing between agencies and DOGE — regardless of how sensitive the data or how unauthorized the access — may be unreviewable under the APA unless plaintiffs can identify a separate statutory basis. This creates a gap: the Privacy Act, the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, and agency-specific confidentiality statutes may provide alternative theories, but they have different standing requirements and remedies. The practical effect is that DOGE can continue accessing Treasury payment data — which touches nearly every American who receives federal payments — without APA judicial review of the access itself.

## Category 4 Update: Birthright Citizenship

### 4.1 Trump v. Barbara — Oral Arguments Held April 1, 2026

- **Update as of April 11, 2026**: The Supreme Court heard oral argument in Trump v. Barbara (No. 25-365) on April 1, 2026. President Trump attended oral arguments in person, which according to the Court's records is the first time a sitting president has attended oral argument in a case to which he is a named party. No decision has been issued as of April 11.
- **Substantive posture**: The government's central argument is that children of unauthorized or temporarily present noncitizens are not "subject to the jurisdiction" of the United States within the meaning of the Fourteenth Amendment because they do not owe "direct and immediate allegiance." The class respondents and amici — including 42 briefs filed after the February 19, 2026 respondents' brief — rely on the text of the Citizenship Clause and on *United States v. Wong Kim Ark* (1898). Supporting the government are legal scholars, Republican members of Congress, the Republican attorneys general of 25 states, and the Federation for American Immigration Reform.
- **Read on the arguments**: Multiple contemporaneous accounts describe the government's presentation as having gone poorly on the jurisdiction question, with justices across the ideological spectrum pressing counsel on how the government's theory could be reconciled with the text of the Fourteenth Amendment and the holding of Wong Kim Ark. This is not a prediction of outcome — the same Court has granted the administration emergency relief on closely related issues — but it is the first moment in the Barbara litigation where oral argument produced visible skepticism from justices not on the liberal wing.
- **Why it matters**: Birthright citizenship is the deepest-rooted constitutional commitment under direct executive challenge. A ruling either way will be definitional. A narrow merits ruling against EO 14160 is the single best available mechanism for restoring the principle that the Fourteenth Amendment means what it says; a ruling for the administration (or a procedural ruling clearing the way for EO 14160 to operate in the interim) would fundamentally change the relationship between citizenship and executive discretion.

### 1.8 Gonzalez v. CBP (Eastern District of California) — Court Finds Violation of Prior Injunction

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, Eastern District of California — Judge Jennifer Thurston
- **Background**: In April 2025, Judge Thurston found that CBP agents had engaged in a "pattern and practice of warrantless arrests" in the Eastern District and issued an injunction barring immigration stops without reasonable suspicion that the individual was unlawfully present and without probable cause of flight risk. The injunction required agents to document specific facts and reasoning for each stop and arrest.
- **April 2026 development**: In a 63-page order released approximately April 2, 2026, Thurston found that CBP violated her injunction when agents swarmed the parking lot of a Home Depot in Sacramento in July 2025, detaining a group of day laborers. Agents arrested 11 noncitizens and one U.S. citizen. The court found agents used "eleven, virtually identical" DHS-issued forms to support the detentions — identical forms indicating no individualized assessment was made. Thurston wrote that "agents detained these people, demanded to see their 'papers' and questioned them about their immigration status all without any legal basis for doing so," and that agents relied on "unsupported assumptions, hunches and generalizations about the relationship between a person's apparent status as a day laborer and their immigration status."
- **Remedy**: Thurston ordered Border Patrol agents throughout the Eastern District to document reasoning and circumstances surrounding future immigration stops in a manner that satisfies the original injunction.
- **Why it matters**: This is one of the clearest judicial records of the mechanics of how warrantless-arrest operations actually function on the ground — identical forms, no individualized reasoning, stops driven by appearance. The Sacramento operation adds granular evidentiary detail to what the cases in Colorado, D.C., Illinois, and Minnesota are establishing in the aggregate. Combined with Ramirez Ovando (Colorado) and Castañon Nava (Chicago), this is the third circuit where a court has found actual post-injunction noncompliance, not merely risk of it.

## Category 5 (New): Executive Actions on Elections — April 2026

### 5.1 Executive Order on Mail Voting (March 31, 2026) — 23-State Legal Challenge

- **Executive action**: On March 31, 2026, President Trump signed an executive order directing the Department of Homeland Security and the Social Security Administration to compile a list of eligible voters and share it with states. The order directs the U.S. Postal Service not to deliver mail ballots to any voter not appearing on that pre-approved list, and requires each authorized mail voter's envelope to carry a unique barcode. - **Legal challenge**: On approximately April 3, 2026, officials from 23 states and the District of Columbia filed suit in U.S. District Court in Massachusetts to block the order. ## Category 6 (New): Appropriations and Institutional Authority

### 6.1 National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA / Trump (White House Ballroom) — D.C. Circuit Stay

- **Court**: U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit (appeal of U.S. District Court Judge Richard Leon's ruling)
- **Background**: The National Trust for Historic Preservation filed suit in December 2025 challenging the administration's demolition of the White House East Wing and construction of a new $300+ million ballroom expected to seat 1,000 guests. U.S. District Judge Richard Leon (a George W. Bush appointee) ruled that the project required congressional authorization and ordered construction to halt by April 14, 2026.
- **April 11 ruling**: On April 11, 2026, a 2-1 D.C. Circuit panel stayed Judge Leon's order until April 17, allowing construction to continue temporarily while the administration seeks Supreme Court review. The majority credited the administration's argument that national security implications of halting construction — including a bomb shelter, military installation, and medical facility said to be located beneath the ballroom — required weighing before any halt.
- **Why it matters**: This case is unusual in the landscape of executive-judicial conflict because it involves not immigration or civil liberties but the appropriations clause: Congress controls federal spending, and a $300+ million construction project at a federal historic property arguably requires legislative authorization. Judge Leon's ruling, if upheld, would be a significant assertion of congressional spending authority against executive unilateralism in facility construction. ## Meta-Observations from the April 2026 Update Pass

Three patterns emerge from the cases that moved between March 19 and April 11, 2026:

1. **Compliance litigation is becoming a distinct category of its own.** Ramirez Ovando (Colorado) and CASA (D.C.) are no longer about whether warrantless arrests are lawful — the injunctions answer that — but about whether the government is obeying. The next phase of this category will be contempt, compliance monitoring, and evidentiary hearings about internal agency guidance, not fresh Fourth Amendment rulings. The evidentiary record being built by Judge Jackson is potentially historic.

2. **The Fourth Circuit's SSA ruling is a test of what "deference to the Supreme Court" means when the factual record underlying the Supreme Court's interim order has been discredited.** Judge King's dissent frames the question sharply: if lower courts cannot reassess injunctions after discovering that the government lied to them, then the emergency docket has become a one-way ratchet. Academic commentary on this question (see the Volokh/Reason piece in the sources) is starting to catch up to what plaintiffs' lawyers have been saying since late 2025.

3. **The administration is running two parallel strategies in the courts.** Visible, well-briefed cases like Trump v. Barbara receive full Supreme Court review and — at least at oral argument — sometimes face genuine skepticism. Simultaneously, a stream of emergency-docket applications lets the administration achieve practical wins on contested questions without ever having to defend them on a full record. The Sotomayor remarks about "unprecedented" volume are pointing at this gap.

These three patterns together suggest that for the next phase of litigation tracking, the single most useful thing to monitor is not new filings but rather **how courts below the Supreme Court are choosing to treat interim Supreme Court orders** in light of emerging factual records. That is where the constitutional floor is being set right now.

**4. The data exfiltration risk has materialized.** The AFSCME v. SSA litigation warned that DOGE access to federal databases would result in unauthorized use. The March 2026 whistleblower complaint — alleging that a DOGE associate copied the NUMIDENT database (SSNs, birth data, parents' names for nearly every living American) onto a thumb drive and planned to take it to a contractor job — is exactly the harm the original plaintiffs predicted. Combined with the earlier revelation that DOGE shared SSA data with an election-results advocacy group, and the D.C. District Court's ruling that Treasury data sharing is unreviewable under the APA, the picture is of a federal data protection regime that cannot keep pace with executive-branch access grants. The litigation is building the evidentiary record, but the courts are — so far — declining to act on it quickly enough to prevent the harms from compounding.

**5. Consent decree defiance is a new front.** The Castañon Nava case in Chicago — where DHS unilaterally declared a consent decree terminated by internal memo — represents a challenge to judicial authority that is distinct from non-compliance with injunctions. Injunctions are new orders; consent decrees are negotiated agreements entered as court orders. If the government can walk away from them by memo, the entire framework of court-supervised institutional reform (which underpins desegregation orders, prison reform, police reform, and environmental cleanup) is at risk. This deserves its own tracking thread going forward.

### Category 8 (New): Civil Service and Institutional Capacity

### 8.1 Schedule Policy/Career (formerly Schedule F) — Final Rule Takes Effect

- **What happened**: OPM published a final rule in February 2026 reclassifying approximately 50,000 career federal employees in "policy-influencing" roles into a new category called "Schedule Policy/Career." The rule took effect March 9, 2026. Employees reclassified into this category lose the due process protections that career civil servants have held since the Pendleton Act era: the right to advance notice of removal, the right to appeal disciplinary actions to the Merit Systems Protection Board, and — in a significant departure from prior Schedule F proposals — the agency, not the independent Office of Special Counsel, is now responsible for investigating whistleblower retaliation.
- **Scale**: 50,000 positions is the OPM estimate; some analysts put the actual number higher once agency-level reclassification is complete. The public comment period produced over 40,000 comments, approximately 94% opposed.
- **Legal challenge active**: AFGE, AFSCME, AFL-CIO, PEER, and Democracy Forward filed an amended complaint in the U.S. District Court for Maryland in March 2026. A separate AFGE lawsuit (National Council of Prison Locals v. BOP, D. Conn., No. 3:25-cv-01907) has a preliminary injunction hearing scheduled April 30, 2026. No court has issued an injunction halting Schedule Policy/Career as of April 12, 2026; implementation is proceeding.
- **Why it matters**: Schedule Policy/Career is the long-run threat to the civil service that underlies most of the individual executive actions tracked elsewhere in this document. ICE agents who ignore court orders, DOGE personnel who access unauthorized systems, and SSA officials who provide false information to courts all operate within an institutional environment. If the career officials who provide institutional memory, legal compliance functions, and pushback against unlawful directives can be dismissed without appeal, the institutional constraints on executive overreach become even weaker. The whistleblower-retaliation change is particularly consequential: it means that the person who might alert the Inspector General, Congress, or the press to the kind of conduct documented in the SSA/DOGE cases would be appealing to the same agency head who ordered the conduct.

### NEW ENTRY — NPR/PBS Defunding EO — Judge Moss Issues Permanent Injunction (March 31, 2026)

- **Court**: U.S. District Court, District of Columbia — Judge Randolph Moss
- **Background**: In 2025, President Trump signed an executive order titled "Ending Taxpayer Subsidization of Biased Media," directing federal agencies to terminate all direct and indirect funding of NPR and PBS. Congress separately rescinded the Corporation for Public Broadcasting's multi-year advance appropriation in July 2025, effectively shutting down CPB in January 2026.
- **Ruling**: On March 31, 2026, Judge Moss issued a **permanent injunction** blocking the executive order as unconstitutional. Moss held that the order's mechanism — targeting two specific organizations based on the president's dislike of their editorial viewpoint — constitutes viewpoint discrimination in violation of the First Amendment. Moss wrote: "It is difficult to conceive of clearer evidence that a government action is targeted at viewpoints that the President does not like and seeks to squelch." He found that the order "singles out two speakers and, on the basis of their speech, bars them from all federally funded programs" and that "the First Amendment does not tolerate viewpoint discrimination and retaliation of this type."
- **Practical limitation**: The permanent injunction blocks the executive order but does not restore the $1.1 billion in advance CPB funding that Congress rescinded. CPB has already shut down. The ruling's practical significance is therefore doctrinal rather than operational: it establishes a clear record that executive viewpoint-based defunding of press entities violates the First Amendment, which will matter in any future attempt to extend the tactic to other media organizations or grant recipients.
- **Why it matters**: The Moss ruling is one of the most direct judicial statements in recent memory that the president cannot use executive authority to punish media organizations for their editorial content. Combined with the first-term pattern of FCC pressure, the CPB defunding through congressional rescission (which the injunction cannot undo), and ongoing White House hostility to independent media, this ruling draws a clear constitutional line — while also illustrating the gap between that line and actual outcomes when Congress acts as the vehicle for the restriction.

### Category 8 Update — MSPB Relinquishes Jurisdiction Over Immigration Judge Firings; Federal Circuit Appeal Filed

- **Development (March 20, 2026)**: The Merit Systems Protection Board issued a ruling in the cases of two fired immigration judges — Megan Jackler and Brandon Jaroch, fired February 14, 2025 — holding that MSPB lacks jurisdiction to hear their appeals. The two-member MSPB (both Republican) concluded that immigration judges are "inferior officers" under Article II of the Constitution, and that the president's power to remove inferior officers precludes MSPB appeal rights, regardless of the Civil Service Reform Act's text. This is a significant departure from over a century of precedent treating immigration judges as career civil servants.
- **Immediate Federal Circuit appeal**: Jackler and Jaroch promptly petitioned the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit for review. Their petition argues the MSPB decision "contradicts more than a century of binding Supreme Court precedent, dating to the 1800s," and that the CSRA explicitly extends appeal rights to career employees regardless of their function. The Federal Circuit has jurisdiction over MSPB appeals.
- **Broader MSPB jurisdiction relinquishment**: The immigration judge ruling is one of several concurrent MSPB jurisdictional retreats. On March 9, 2026 — the same day Schedule Policy/Career took effect — MSPB revoked its authority to hear appeals from employees converted into Schedule Policy/Career positions. OPM has also proposed rules that would strip MSPB of jurisdiction over most RIF (reduction-in-force) appeals, potentially removing appeal rights from as much as 30% of MSPB's current caseload.
- **Why it matters**: The immigration judge ruling and the Schedule Policy/Career jurisdiction relinquishment together represent a coordinated dismantling of the independent appeals mechanism that has protected career civil servants since 1978. If the Federal Circuit affirms the MSPB holding on immigration judges, that precedent will be available to argue that any category of federal employee who exercises "policy-influencing" functions — exactly the same language used in Schedule Policy/Career — can be removed without MSPB review. The logical conclusion is that Schedule Policy/Career employees face not just weakened due process on termination, but no MSPB avenue at all, leaving only APA litigation as a remedy. The Federal Circuit appeal is therefore one of the most consequential pending civil-service cases in the country.

### CRITICAL — DC Circuit Terminates Boasberg Contempt Inquiry (April 15, 2026)

**Case**: In re Grand Jury Investigation (D.C. Circuit, re: Noem v. J.G.G. deportation flights)

**Ruling**: On April 15, 2026, the D.C. Circuit issued a 2-1 opinion ordering Chief Judge James Boasberg to terminate his criminal contempt investigation of the Trump administration for defying a temporary restraining order that blocked flights carrying Venezuelan migrants to El Salvador.

**Majority holding (Judge Neomi Rao)**: Criminal contempt requires an order that is "unmistakably clear and specific." The TRO at issue "did not clearly and specifically bar the government from transferring plaintiffs into Salvadoran custody" because it was ambiguous as to whether it applied to flights already in the air. Therefore the TRO did not satisfy the specificity standard required to support criminal contempt proceedings.

**Dissent (Judge J. Michelle Childs)**: The majority's ruling allows litigants to evade contempt by adopting their own creative interpretations of court orders. "Today's decision invites litigants to exploit even textually dubious interpretations of a court order to avoid being held in contempt."

**Direct relevance to Xinis/April 28**: DOJ will cite this ruling at the April 28 hearing as precedent that any civil contempt order Xinis issues must be drafted with exceptional specificity to survive DC Circuit review. If Xinis does issue contempt sanctions, the administration's first appellate move will be to argue her orders fail the Rao specificity test. This weakens the enforcement mechanism substantially. Lawyers for deportees are seeking full DC Circuit en banc review — not yet resolved.

**Broader significance**: This is the first appellate ruling that directly limits the contempt mechanism in immigration enforcement cases. Combined with the AFSCME v. SSA Fourth Circuit holding (lower courts cannot reassess interim orders even when the factual record shows government deception), it creates a pattern of appellate holdings that reduce the practical enforceability of injunctions against the executive.
