## Preliminary Evidence Claims (Anchors for the Domain)

These four claims will anchor Domain 59's argument. Each is supported by at least one causal or quasi-experimental study and should be treated as the domain's core evidentiary commitments.

**Claim 1: The income-participation gap is 26 points and widening.**
In 2024, 42 percent of Americans earning under $50,000 did not vote, versus 16 percent of those earning over $100,000. This gap widened from 2020. Source: PRRI 2024 post-election survey; Census Bureau CPS Voting and Registration Supplement, November 2024.

**Claim 2: Financial relief causally increases voter turnout.**
Mortgage payment reduction through HARP refinancing (2009-2012) causally increased voter turnout in 2012, concentrated among low-income and politically independent borrowers. This is the domain's strongest causal anchor. Source: Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Zhang, Zhang — Dallas Fed Working Paper 2517, revised March 2026.

**Claim 3: Eviction causally reduces voter turnout.**
Neighborhood eviction rates causally reduce voter turnout, with the effect concentrated in neighborhoods where eviction is less normalized. Induced residential mobility persistently depresses voting for nearly two decades among low-income Black and Hispanic adults. Sources: Slee and Desmond (Politics and Society 2023); Kuk, Hajnal, and Lajevardi (PNAS 2024).

**Claim 4: The OBBBA's five concurrent cuts to civic capacity are multiplicative, not additive.**
Medicaid loss + SNAP reduction + student loan default + childcare subsidy elimination + housing assistance cuts compound: each additional crisis exponentially increases cognitive load and time poverty. The affected population (households subject to 2+ simultaneous OBBBA cuts) is estimated at 15-25 million Americans. Sources: Urban Institute (2026); CBPP (2025-2026); Georgetown Center for Children and Families (2025-2026).

## Category A: Peer-Reviewed Empirical Studies (Economics, Political Science, Sociology)

These are the domain's causal backbone. All should be read in full before writing begins.

**A1. Liu, McCartney, Ramcharan, Calvin Zhang, Xiaohan Zhang (Dallas Fed Working Paper 2517, revised March 2026)**
"Relieving Financial Distress Increases Voter Turnout: Evidence from the Mortgage Market"
URL: https://www.dallasfed.org/research/papers/2025/wp2517 | Revised: https://www.dallasfed.org/~/media/documents/research/papers/2025/wp2517r1.pdf
Relevance: The domain's primary causal anchor. Uses quasi-experimental variation from HARP eligibility cutoffs to show mortgage relief causally increased 2012 voter turnout — effect concentrated among low-income and independent borrowers.
Key finding: Financial relief has a direct, causal effect on voter participation — not merely a correlation. This is the strongest existing evidence that reducing financial precarity increases civic participation.
Status: Open access, confirmed live March 2026.

**A2. Slee, Gillian and Desmond, Matthew (Politics and Society, Vol. 51, No. 1, 2023)**
"Eviction and Voter Turnout: The Political Consequences of Housing Instability"
URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/00323292211050716
Summary page: https://nlihc.org/resource/evictions-associated-lower-voter-turnout
Princeton repository: https://collaborate.princeton.edu/en/publications/eviction-and-voter-turnout-the-political-consequences-of-housing-/
Relevance: Establishes causal link between neighborhood eviction rates and voter turnout depression. Uses panel variation in eviction filing rates as quasi-experimental variation.
Key finding: Increases in neighborhood eviction rates lead to significant decreases in voter turnout, especially in lower-eviction neighborhoods where eviction is a more disruptive signal.
Status: Paywalled at Sage; Princeton repository may have working paper version. Access to confirm before June 16.

**A3. Kuk, John; Hajnal, Zoltan; Lajevardi, Nazita (PNAS, 2024)**
"Residential mobility and persistently depressed voting among disadvantaged adults in a large housing experiment"
URL: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2306287121 | PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11098094/
Relevance: Causal study using housing voucher lottery (Moving to Opportunity) as natural experiment. Induced residential mobility persistently depressed voting for nearly 20 years among low-income Black and Hispanic adults.
Key finding: Moving — even to better housing — causes a long-term, persistent reduction in civic participation among disadvantaged adults. Mechanism is social capital disruption and registration lapse, not housing quality.
Status: Open access via PMC, confirmed.

**A4. Rastogi, Ankit and Laurison, Daniel (Sociological Science, 2025)**
"Higher Turnout, Greater Inequality? A Precinct-Level Analysis of Income Inequality in U.S. Presidential Voting, 2016 to 2020"
URL: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/23780231251338441
Relevance: Documents the precinct-level relationship between income inequality and voter participation gaps. Confirms that the income-participation gap grew from 2016 to 2020 and characterizes the structural mechanism.
Key finding: Higher aggregate turnout does not close the income-participation gap; in fact, high-inequality precincts show participation gaps that widen as overall turnout rises (high-income voters mobilize more effectively than low-income voters under turnout-increasing conditions).
Status: Access to confirm; Sage paywalled.

**A5. Uslaner, Eric and Brown, Mitchell (Russell Sage Foundation)**
"Inequality, Trust, and Civic Engagement"
URL: https://www.russellsage.org/sites/default/files/u4/Uslaner%20and%20Brown.pdf
Relevance: Documents the relationship between income inequality and social/institutional trust, which mediates civic engagement. High inequality reduces interpersonal trust → reduces willingness to engage in collective political action.
Key finding: The participatory gap between rich and poor increases with higher economic inequality; the mechanism operates through trust, not merely resource constraints.
Status: Open access PDF, confirmed.

**A6. Childhood poverty and political participation (ScienceDirect, 2025)**
"Childhood poverty and political participation: The role of family, gender and economic mobility"
URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379425001350
Relevance: Establishes intergenerational pathway: childhood poverty reduces adult political participation, with the effect partially mediated by economic mobility. Women and girls in poverty face the largest participation suppression.
Key finding: Childhood poverty has lasting democratic effects — it is not merely a childhood welfare issue but a democratic design issue. Economic mobility partially reverses the effect.
Status: Access to confirm.

**A7. Scholars Strategy Network — Working-Class Political Engagement**
"Strengthening Political Engagement Among Working-Class and Low-Income Communities"
URL: https://scholars.org/contribution/strengthening-political-engagement-among
Relevance: Synthesizes research on barriers to political engagement for low-income workers and identifies evidence-based interventions. Covers contact mobilization, barrier removal, and relevance framing.
Key finding: Low-income workers are not contacted to participate in campaigns, cannot contribute monetarily, and face logistical barriers (job security fears, transportation, phone/computer access) — a multi-barrier environment that no single intervention addresses.
Status: Open access, confirmed.

## Category B: Behavioral/Psychological Framework

**B1. Mullainathan, Sendhil and Shafir, Eldar (Times Books/Henry Holt, 2013)**
Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much
Overview: https://behavioralscientist.org/scarcity-excerpt-mullainathan-shafir/
Harvard IQ analysis: https://www.iq.harvard.edu/news/mullainathan-and-shafir-explore-cognitive-effects-scarcity
APA summary: https://www.apa.org/monitor/2014/02/scarcity
Relevance: Foundational theoretical framework for the "bandwidth tax" concept. Scarcity — whether of money, time, or cognitive resources — consumes mental bandwidth, reducing effective intelligence and self-control by an estimated 13-14 IQ point equivalent. "Tunneling" directs attention to the immediate crisis, crowding out less urgent concerns including civic engagement.
Key finding (directly applicable): Financial precarity reduces cognitive capacity for political engagement not as a preference but as a resource allocation consequence. This converts the "apathy" narrative into a resource-constraint narrative.
Status: Book — library access. Chapter 2 (tunneling) and Chapter 3 (bandwidth) are the primary research reads.

**B2. Bhanot, Syon; Linos, Elizabeth; and colleagues (Behavioral Public Administration literature)**
Research on cognitive load and civic behavior.
Entry point: BFI Working Paper "Scarcity and Inattention" (2022): https://bfi.uchicago.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/BFI_WP_2022-76.pdf
Relevance: Applied behavioral research on how cognitive load from financial scarcity reduces attention to civic tasks. The BFI working paper updates the Mullainathan/Shafir framework with more recent experimental evidence.
Key finding: Inattention induced by scarcity reduces engagement with government communications and public programs — with direct implications for civic participation.
Status: Open access, confirmed.
