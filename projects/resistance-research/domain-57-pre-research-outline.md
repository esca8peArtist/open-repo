## Lead Finding

US withdrawal from multilateral trade and security frameworks between 2025 and 2026 has produced measurable domestic economic harm concentrated in the Midwest — the same regional bloc that delivered the electoral margins enabling the executive unilateralism this domain analyzes. US agricultural exports to China have fallen 73 percent since January 2025; 72,000 manufacturing jobs were lost between April and December 2025; and the Supreme Court's February 20, 2026 ruling striking down IEEPA tariff authority has created a constitutionally contested tariff regime that now operates under Section 122 of the Trade Act with a 150-day clock. The democratic harm is not merely economic: when trade policy is made through executive emergency declarations rather than congressional deliberation, the communities most exposed to retaliation — Midwest farmers, export-dependent manufacturers, agricultural commodity workers — lose both their economic stake and their political voice in shaping the policy that harms them. The five causal pathways documented here show how this loss of democratic deliberation compounds across trade, alliance, currency, institutional, and domestic coalition dimensions.

## Section 1: Analytical Frame — Five Causal Pathways from Multilateral Withdrawal to Democratic Harm

The May 17, 2026 institutional outline (DOMAIN_57_RESEARCH_OUTLINE.md) established the constitutional architecture argument: treaty withdrawal circumvents Senate ratification requirements, expands presidential discretion in Youngstown Zone 3, and removes external accountability mechanisms that function as domestic democratic infrastructure. This companion document maps five economic and geopolitical pathways that complement the constitutional argument. Each pathway has a democratic harm component that is distinct from its economic harm component — the two must be analytically separated and then reconnected.

**The five pathways**:

1. **Trade Decoupling** — US-China tariff escalation, WTO non-compliance, IEEPA constitutional crisis; democratic harm is the bypass of congressional trade authority and the silencing of economically exposed constituencies
2. **Alliance Fragmentation** — NATO withdrawal threat/functional weakening, Taiwan strategic ambiguity erosion, AUKUS review; democratic harm is the removal of allied consultation constraints on executive military action
3. **Dollar De-Dollarization** — BRICS+ currency initiatives, CIPS expansion, commodity market shifts; democratic harm is the long-term erosion of dollar privilege that subsidizes US debt and reduces fiscal constraints on executive spending
4. **Institutional Legitimacy Loss** — WTO Appellate Body paralysis, ICC sanctions precedent, UN Security Council veto abuse, WHO withdrawal; democratic harm is the normalization of unilateral executive override of rules-based frameworks that constrain presidential power
5. **Domestic Coalition Cascades** — Agricultural commodity market collapse, manufacturing job loss, foreign policy elite fracture; democratic harm is the political silencing of multilateralism's former coalition (Farm Belt, export manufacturers, Republican internationalists)

**Cross-domain bridges**: Domain 23 (Trade Policy/IEEPA tariff unilateralism) covers the statutory mechanism; Domain 37 (Executive Interference) covers the executive power concentration pattern; Domain 28 (War Powers) covers the alliance consultation and military unilateralism dimension; Domain 29 (DOJ Weaponization) covers the domestic enforcement of trade-adjacent sanctions regimes. Domain 57's unique contribution across all five pathways is the democratic accountability framing: these are not merely economic or foreign policy disruptions, they are mechanisms that remove legislative and public deliberation from decisions with major distributional consequences.

## Section 2: Pathway 1 — Trade Decoupling

### 2.1 The US-China Tariff Escalation: Current Status (May 2026)

The tariff trajectory from 2025 to 2026 has gone through three distinct phases, each with distinct democratic accountability implications:

**Phase 1: Liberation Day escalation (April 2025)**. The Trump administration's April 2, 2025 "Liberation Day" tariff proclamation imposed sweeping tariffs on most trading partners under IEEPA emergency authority. China retaliated with a 34 percent tariff, then escalated to 125 percent. The US escalated to 145 percent. This exchange bypassed congressional authorization; IEEPA's emergency power was invoked to characterize trade deficits as a national security emergency — an unprecedented expansion of emergency power into commercial economic territory.

**Phase 2: IEEPA constitutional crisis (February 20, 2026)**. The Supreme Court, in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (24-1287), struck down IEEPA tariff authority in a 6-3 decision. The Court held that IEEPA does not authorize the president to impose tariffs. The administration immediately issued a Section 122 (Trade Act of 1974) replacement tariff at 10 percent — scheduled to expire in 150 days, on July 24, 2026. This expiration creates a critical window for congressional action to either authorize continuation or reassert its constitutional trade authority (Article I, Section 8).

**Phase 3: May 2026 partial truce**. Following the Trump-Xi Beijing summit in May 2026, the US-China effective tariff rate settled at approximately 30 percent — still the highest of any US trading partner. Negotiations continue on the restoration of pre-IEEPA tariff rates and potential extension of the trade truce. Rare earth restrictions, tech export controls, and agricultural purchase commitments remain contested.

**Democratic accountability argument**: The constitutional significance of the IEEPA ruling is that it establishes the executive has limits when it uses emergency power to circumvent the constitutional assignment of trade authority to Congress. The Section 122 replacement is temporary and requires congressional extension or replacement action. This is the most important near-term window for democratic rebalancing of trade authority — and the 150-day clock (expiring July 24, 2026) falls precisely during this domain's production window.

## Section 3: Pathway 2 — Alliance Fragmentation

### 3.1 NATO: Functional Withdrawal Without Formal Exit

The May 17 institutional outline covered NATO's constitutional dimension (NDAA 2024 Section 1250A, CRS R48868, Youngstown Zone 3 application). This section extends that analysis to the functional fragmentation mechanism: the Trump administration uses withdrawal threats as coercion against allied criticism of US executive action, rather than executing formal withdrawal. This coercion model has several democratic accountability consequences:

**The Iran war test case**: Following the April 2026 US military action against Iran (details in Domain 28), Trump threatened NATO withdrawal after allied governments publicly criticized the operation. This demonstrates the coercion model directly — the NATO withdrawal threat is used to insulate executive military action from allied democratic accountability. Allies who criticize executive military action risk being told the US will exit the treaty framework that protects them.

**European institutional response**: By April 2026, European NATO members had increased defense spending by 62 percent between 2020 and 2025. The EU is developing Article 42.7 (EU mutual defense clause) as a contingency parallel to NATO's Article 5. The Al Jazeera (April 30, 2026) analysis "Can the EU's Article 42.7 Offer Europe NATO-Like Collective Defense?" documents the institutional hedging in progress. The Carnegie Endowment "Trump Turns NATO into a Tool of Coercion" (May 2026) names the mechanism precisely.

**Hegseth's Article 5 erosion**: The Modern Diplomacy (April 3, 2026) analysis "Trump and Hegseth Are Spending NATO's Credibility" documents Secretary Hegseth's refusal to reaffirm the Article 5 collective defense commitment — the most direct functional withdrawal from NATO's core obligation without invoking Article 13.

### 3.2 Taiwan: Strategic Ambiguity Monetization and Security Destabilization

The Trump administration's approach to Taiwan has moved from strategic ambiguity toward what analysts are calling "strategic ambiguity monetization" — using ambiguity about US defense commitments as leverage for economic concessions. This creates a democratic accountability problem: if Taiwan policy becomes an executive bargaining chip in trade negotiations rather than a treaty-grounded policy subject to congressional oversight, the War Powers implications are unaddressed (see Domain 28).

**Key empirical anchors (2026)**:
- China 2026 National People's Congress: 7 percent defense spending increase, $277 billion total; pledge to "resolutely combat Taiwanese separatist forces"
- CFR backgrounder: Taiwan strategic ambiguity in the Trump era (current, confirmed live)
- CRS Report R48044: Taiwan Defense Issues for Congress (confirmed live via Congress.gov)
- CRS IF10275: Taiwan — Background and US Relations (confirmed live)

**Democratic accountability argument**: If the executive treats Taiwan security commitments as trade-policy leverage, the constitutional assignment of war powers to Congress is bypassed through the back door of commercial negotiation.

### 3.3 AUKUS Under Review: Reliability Signals and Alliance Hedging

The Department of Defense June 2025 review of the AUKUS agreement — framed as applying "America First criteria" — sent a reliability signal across the Indo-Pacific alliance network. The Lowy Institute (Australia) documented the shock to Australian strategic planning; the CSIS analysis "The AUKUS Inflection" documented the risk that AUKUS becomes a deterrence failure rather than a deterrence success. A new 50-year UK-Australia bilateral defense treaty was under development as a "Plan B" should the US withdraw from AUKUS.

**Democratic accountability argument**: When US treaty commitments are subject to unilateral executive review without congressional involvement, allies cannot rely on long-term security commitments — the classic multilateral withdrawal mechanism of reducing the self-binding constraints that make US commitments credible.

## Section 4: Pathway 3 — Dollar De-Dollarization

### 4.1 BRICS+ Currency Initiatives: Status and Democratic Implications

The BRICS+ bloc (11 nations including Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iran as of 2024) is advancing de-dollarization through several parallel tracks:

**"The Unit" digital currency**: A gold-backed digital trade settlement unit based on a basket of BRICS member currencies (CNY, INR, BRL, RUB) is in development. Russia confirmed in January 2026 that a unified currency is not under active negotiation, but the Unit project continues as a trade settlement mechanism.

**CIPS expansion**: China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) processed $245 trillion equivalent in yuan-denominated transactions in 2025 — a SWIFT alternative for yuan-denominated settlements. BRICS nations now conduct approximately 90 percent of intra-BRICS settlements in national currencies.

**BRICS Grain Exchange**: The proposed BRICS commodity exchange would price agricultural commodities in local currencies rather than dollars — directly targeting the dollar's role as the pricing currency for agricultural exports. This is where the de-dollarization and Midwest agricultural impact pathways intersect: Midwest corn, soybean, and wheat producers depend on dollar-denominated commodity pricing as a structural advantage against competitors.

**What de-dollarization does NOT mean (yet)**: No BRICS common currency exists or is imminent. The dollar retains unmatched liquidity and the legal/institutional trust underpinning of US capital markets. The realistic near-term threat is not dollar replacement but dollar erosion at the margins — commodity pricing diversification, trade settlement alternatives, reserve diversification — that over a decade reduces the "exorbitant privilege" that allows the US to run large deficits at low cost.

**Democratic accountability argument**: The "exorbitant privilege" — the ability to issue the world's reserve currency — functions as a hidden subsidy that reduces the fiscal constraints on executive spending. Large military expenditures, emergency tariff regimes, and unilateral financial sanctions all depend partly on dollar dominance. The executive branch benefits most from this subsidy; the democratic constraints on deficit spending are reduced by it. Gradual de-dollarization would increase fiscal discipline pressure — but in the short term, the executive can invoke dollar threats as further justification for emergency executive action.

## Section 5: Pathway 4 — Institutional Legitimacy Loss

### 5.1 WTO Appellate Body Paralysis and the Death of Rules-Based Trade

The WTO Appellate Body has been functionally paralyzed since 2019 when the US blocked appointments to fill vacancies. By 2025-2026, the US accounted for nine of 24 total "appeals into the void" — cases appealed to a non-functioning body to avoid binding dispute resolution. USTR Greer's explicit statement that the US will use unilateral enforcement rather than WTO dispute resolution represents the formal abandonment of rules-based trade enforcement for power-based enforcement.

**The 14th Ministerial Conference (MC14, March 2026, Yaoundé, Cameroon)**: The WTO's March 2026 ministerial conference confronted the institutional future without meaningful US engagement. The US did not commit to Appellate Body reform or restoration of WTO contribution payments.

**Legitimacy cascade argument**: When the US — the most powerful state and the WTO's largest single contributor — refuses to participate in or comply with dispute resolution, it signals to all other members that the rules-based framework is optional for powerful states. This legitimacy cascade reduces the effectiveness of the WTO as a constraint on executive trade action for all members.

### 5.2 UN Security Council Veto Abuse and Accountability Voids

US veto use at the UN Security Council creates accountability voids that parallel the ICC sanctions mechanism. Key instances (2025-2026):
- US veto of UNSC resolution on humanitarian access in Gaza (multiple instances, 2025)
- US blocking of UNSC action on Venezuela (connecting to Domain 28 war powers)
- US veto of UNSC resolution condemning Israeli settlement expansion

**Democratic accountability argument**: UNSC veto use by the US executive bypasses any domestic democratic deliberation. The president can use the veto to insulate specific executive policy choices from international accountability without congressional authorization, public debate, or judicial review.

### 5.3 WHO Withdrawal and the Pandemic Preparedness Infrastructure

The US withdrew from WHO on January 22, 2026 — 15 days after the presidential memorandum. The US had contributed $893 million to WHO in 2023 (the largest single-country contribution). The democratic accountability harm: domestic public health legislation, EPA regulatory justifications, and pandemic preparedness planning all depend on WHO surveillance and standards infrastructure. Withdrawal removes the US from the cooperative infrastructure that detected and responded to COVID-19 — the most consequential public health emergency requiring multilateral coordination in a century.

**Cross-domain bridge to Domain 39** (Healthcare Access and Democratic Infrastructure): WHO withdrawal directly degrades the surveillance and standards infrastructure that domestic healthcare policy depends on. This is developed in Domain 39; Domain 57 notes the multilateral withdrawal mechanism.

## Section 6: Pathway 5 — Domestic Coalition Cascades

This pathway is the democratic heart of the domain: the political silencing of the constituencies that multilateralism most directly served — and who now face the economic consequences of withdrawal while losing their political voice in shaping the policy causing harm.

### 6.1 Agricultural Commodity Collapse: Zone 5 Midwest Regional Analysis

**The structural exposure**: The Midwest (USDA hardiness Zone 5 — Iowa, Indiana, Ohio, Wisconsin, Michigan, Illinois, Minnesota, South Dakota, Nebraska, North Dakota) is the epicenter of agricultural commodity export exposure to China. Soybeans, corn, wheat, pork, and cotton from these states represent the majority of US agricultural exports to China.

**The economic data (2025-2026)**:
- US agricultural exports to China: projected $9 billion in 2026, down from $26 billion in 2024 (a 65% decline) and from pre-trade-war highs in 2022 (more than 50% below)
- USDA projects $17 billion in 2025, falling to $9 billion in 2026 — the lowest since the 2018 trade war
- China has not purchased US corn, wheat, or sorghum in 2025-2026 at any meaningful scale
- China retaliatory 10 percent supplemental tariff on US soybeans remains in place, leaving US at competitive disadvantage relative to Brazil
- Chinese soybean purchase commitments (25 million metric tons per year for 2026-2028) are far below 2020 peak levels
- Overall US agricultural exports to China have dropped $6.8 billion (73 percent) since January 2025

**County-level political exposure**: Because soybean farming is concentrated in specific Midwest counties, the economic harm from Chinese retaliation is geographically concentrated in the same counties that delivered Trump's 2024 electoral margins. The Minneapolis Federal Reserve analysis (2025) documents that counties more exposed to Chinese retaliation suffered disproportionate job losses — the Midwest farming base is simultaneously the most economically harmed and the most electorally significant.

**Political representation gap**: When trade policy is made through executive emergency declarations (IEEPA, Section 232, Section 301) rather than congressional deliberation, Midwest farm constituencies lose the congressional representation mechanism they would otherwise have to influence trade policy. The farm lobby's preferred mechanism is congressional legislation; the executive emergency approach bypasses that mechanism entirely.

**Key organization responses**:
- American Farm Bureau Federation: sent letter to Trump expressing concern about tariff financial hardship; 2026 convention voted to analyze tariff impacts; urged administration post-IEEPA ruling to seek trade frameworks rather than alternative tariff authorities
- National Corn Growers Association: advocacy for WTO-compliant trade resolution
- American Soybean Association: direct market access restoration advocacy
- National Farmers Union: trade policy reform position; supports congressional authority over trade

### 6.2 Manufacturing Job Loss: Durable Goods and the Midwest Industrial Base

**The employment data (2025)**:
- 72,000 manufacturing jobs lost between April ("Liberation Day") and December 2025
- 83,000 manufacturing jobs lost during Trump's first year in office overall
- Michigan: net loss of 2,500 manufacturing jobs between April and November 2025
- PIIE analysis: US economy 0.4% smaller than baseline by 2026; 0.62 percentage point GDP growth reduction
- Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis: "two channels of job losses" — direct (tariff-exposed industries) and indirect (investment slowdown from reduced return to capital)
- PIIE: permanent structural shift from trade-exposed manufacturing, mining, and agriculture to service sector, at lower real wages for all workers

**Brookings analysis**: "Not Your Grandfather's Factory — Why Tariffs Won't Help Midwest Manufacturing" documents that modern Midwest manufacturing is advanced-manufacturing and intermediate-goods-oriented — the supply chains most disrupted by tariffs on inputs (steel, aluminum, components) rather than protected by tariffs on finished goods.

**Center for American Progress**: "Trump's Trade War Squeezes Middle-Class Manufacturing Employment" documents the specific middle-class wage impact in manufacturing communities.

**Whirlpool-Iowa case**: CBS News documented layoffs at the Amana factory in Iowa — a direct illustration of intermediate-goods supply chain disruption. IAM representative Kerry Waddell: "job losses will be devastating for the local economy."

**Labor union fracture**: The trade policy debate has produced a fracture within organized labor's political coalition. The IAM (machinists) and UAW have historically supported some domestic content protections but opposed tariff escalation that destroys export markets and raises input costs. The AFL-CIO position on trade has been split: steel and aluminum unions favor protection; auto, agricultural equipment, and export-oriented manufacturing unions oppose tariff escalation.

### 6.3 Foreign Policy Elite Fracture

**The Republican internationalist collapse**: The Council on Foreign Relations, Atlantic Council, and foreign policy research institutes represent a foreign policy establishment that was historically bipartisan and institutionalist. The Trump second term has produced a structural fracture in this establishment:

- CFR launched a "Council Special Report: America Revived — A Grand Strategy of Resolute Global Leadership" (2026) — explicitly counter-programming to the withdrawal posture
- CFR article "Congress Checks Out" (2026) documents congressional abdication of foreign policy oversight
- CFR "Election 2024: Are Republicans Turning Isolationist?" documents the political transformation of the Republican foreign policy coalition

**The isolationist/internationalist split**: Republican voters divide among three tendencies — internationalist, nationalist, and non-interventionist. The internationalist wing (CFR, Atlantic Council, Chamber of Commerce, Business Roundtable) has been systematically marginalized. Their organizations remain analytically productive but politically sidelined.
