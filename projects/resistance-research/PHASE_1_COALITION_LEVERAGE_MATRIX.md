# Phase 1 Campaign Coalition Leverage Matrix

**Prepared**: June 3, 2026
**Purpose**: Map interconnections between Phase 1's five highest-leverage domains; identify multiplicative advocacy windows where cross-sector coalition coordination compounds impact beyond individual domain distribution; provide measurement-driven routing logic for Phase 2 activation
**Audience**: Campaign orchestration, Phase 1 execution decision-making, Phase 2 sequencing
**Decision dependency**: Domain 39 Day 7-14 engagement metrics gate Domain 56 sequencing and coalition pre-commitment asks
**Due**: June 15 EOD (feeds T+14 checkpoint decisions)

## Section 1: Strategic Context

Phase 1 of the Democratic Renewal framework launched May 28 with Domain 56 (Civil Service Politicization, Tier 1 civil service unions and democracy networks) and June 1 with Domain 39 (Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure, HHS Medicaid interim final rule window). Domain 59 (Economic Precarity) distributed June 2–3. Domain 58 (Tribal Sovereignty) pre-positioned to five verified rapid-response contacts ahead of Trump v. Barbara. Domain 37 (Executive Power / Election Protection) sequenced for September to align with the August 7 NVRA quiet period litigation window.

The strategic premise of Phase 1 is not five parallel campaigns. It is one campaign with five entry points into overlapping organizational ecosystems. The frameworks share a single structural argument — that the democratic design failures being documented are systemic and mutually reinforcing, not a collection of discrete policy problems. That shared argument is what creates coalition leverage: when an organization receives Domain 39 (healthcare as voting infrastructure) and Domain 56 (civil service as voting infrastructure), they are not reading two documents. They are reading two empirical confirmations of the same structural claim, from two different angles, through their own institutional lens.

The 45 Tier 1 organizations in seven constituencies are not siloed. Law schools appear in election law, healthcare policy, civil service reform, and tribal sovereignty. Brennan Center appears in all five domain distribution lists. CBPP appears in healthcare (Domain 39), economic justice (Domain 59), and civil service (Domain 56 Wave 2). SEIU spans healthcare and labor. NAACP LDF spans tribal sovereignty and voting rights. These overlaps are not administrative accidents — they are the coalition architecture.

This document identifies where the overlaps create leverage greater than the sum of individual domain campaigns, provides concrete 3-5 multiplicative advocacy windows, establishes measurement-driven routing for Phase 2, and gives the June 15 checkpoint decision team concrete if/then logic for each engagement scenario.

## Section 2: Five Highest-Leverage Phase 1 Domains — WHY Each Is Positioned for Coalition Multipliers

**Domain 39 (Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure) — Primary lead indicator**

Domain 39 is the highest-leverage Phase 1 domain for three structural reasons. First, the June 1 HHS interim final rule created a time-compressed activation window that forced organizational attention — advocacy groups monitoring OBBBA implementation had to engage with any substantive analysis arriving in that window. Second, Domain 39's five-pathway argument (hospital closures reduce turnout 3.8 points; Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory NVRA voter registration sites; medical debt depletes cognitive bandwidth for civic engagement; PAVA defunding removes disability voting assistance; maternal mortality silences civic voice permanently) connects healthcare organizations to election protection organizations through a causal chain neither constituency typically articulates. Third, Domain 39 reaches the widest organizational set of any single Phase 1 domain: 27 organizations across healthcare advocacy, democracy reform, disability rights, reproductive justice, and congressional staff — a natural coalition pre-assembled around a shared policy target.

The WHY for coalition positioning: Domain 39 is the gateway domain. Organizations that receive and engage with Domain 39 are primed for every subsequent domain, because Domain 39 demonstrates the framework's core method — showing how an ostensibly sectoral issue (healthcare) is actually democratic infrastructure. Once an organization accepts that argument for healthcare, it becomes substantially easier to accept it for civil service (Domain 56), economic precarity (Domain 59), and tribal governance (Domain 58). Domain 39 engagement is therefore not just a signal about healthcare advocacy interest; it is a signal about receptivity to the framework as a whole.

**Domain 56 (Civil Service Politicization) — Institutional multiplier**

Domain 56 is positioned for coalition multipliers because it reaches the two organizational types with the highest legislative access in the coalition: federal employee unions (AFGE at 300,000+ affected members; NTEU) and good-government reform organizations (Partnership for Public Service, with established congressional staff relationships). Neither of these constituency types typically coordinates with election protection organizations or healthcare advocates — but Domain 56 creates the conceptual bridge. The DOJ Voting Section reduction from 30 to 6 career attorneys is simultaneously a civil service politicization finding and an election protection finding. It is one fact that connects two organizational ecosystems.

The WHY for coalition positioning: Partnership for Public Service's congressional relationships access Senate HSGAC and House Oversight committee staff that Brennan Center and ACLU cannot reach directly. Brennan Center's litigation infrastructure engages federal courts that Partnership for Public Service cannot. When Domain 56 is distributed to both simultaneously, the two organizations can coordinate with a shared factual foundation — the Schedule Policy/Career rule's elimination of merit protections across 50,000 positions — that makes joint congressional testimony possible. That testimony, combining labor legitimacy and constitutional legitimacy, reaches committee staff who are receptive to exactly one of those frames. Domain 56 is how the civil service constituency becomes an election protection multiplier.

**Domain 58 (Tribal Sovereignty) — Rapid-response lever and authenticity anchor**

Domain 58 is the highest-urgency domain in Phase 1 due to the Trump v. Barbara ruling expected by June 30. Its leverage is qualitatively different from the other domains: it does not primarily create coalition multipliers through organizational referral networks, but through the credibility it confers on the entire democratic infrastructure argument. When the framework can demonstrate that 574 sovereign democracies — established under 370 ratified treaties — are experiencing the same administrative defunding, workforce reduction, and voting rights erosion as the federal civil service and Medicaid system, it confirms that the pattern is not partisan complaint. It is structural.

The WHY for coalition positioning: NCAI (National Congress of American Indians) coordinates 574 tribal governments. A single NCAI endorsement of the framework's tribal sovereignty argument — even an informal distribution to its Washington office network — reaches organizational relationships that no other Phase 1 contact list touches. Tribal sovereignty organizations engage government-to-government: their frame of reference is treaty law and federal trust obligations, not minority rights protection. When non-tribal organizations (Brennan Center, NAACP LDF, Campaign Legal Center) receive the tribal sovereignty argument and engage with it, they bring their litigation infrastructure into a space where tribal organizations have the moral and legal authority but often lack the Washington political access. That combination — tribal legitimacy plus elite voting rights litigation capacity — is the Sovereignty and Justice Coalition opportunity described in Section 4.

**Domain 59 (Economic Precarity as Democratic Infrastructure Failure) — Breadth and urgency anchor**

Domain 59 has the highest empirical density of any Phase 1 domain: the 42-point income-based turnout gap (PRRI 2024), the 27-point precinct-level gap widening in a single election cycle (Rastogi and Laurison 2025), the time-poverty calculus (bottom income quintile has fewer than 15 genuine discretionary hours per week), and the causal anchor from the Dallas Federal Reserve mortgage relief study showing financial relief causally increases turnout. That empirical stack connects economic justice organizations to democratic participation organizations through quantified causal pathways — not advocacy arguments.

The WHY for coalition positioning: The Senate Finance CTC markup window (June 30 deadline) gives Domain 59 an immediate legislative hook that forces CBPP, ITEP, and AFL-CIO to engage now rather than later. The OBBBA's $2,200 maximum CTC delivering $0 average to the poorest fifth of families is not primarily a poverty issue — it is a democratic participation issue, because the income-participation research shows the families receiving $0 are exactly the families experiencing the 42-point turnout gap. When CBPP engages with Domain 59's democratic participation framing in the CTC markup window, it is not just advocating for an economic policy. It is establishing that economic policy is democracy policy. That reframe — if CBPP adopts it publicly — repositions economic justice organizations as democratic renewal actors, unlocking coordination with election protection funders and organizations that have not historically funded anti-poverty work.

**Domain 37 (Federal Executive Interference in 2026 Midterms) — Synthesis capstone**

Domain 37 is deliberately the last Phase 1 domain to distribute (September), and its leverage comes from being the synthesis document the others build toward. With 31 active DOJ voter roll litigation cases, 0 DOJ district court wins on the merits, the CISA election security function relocated outside DHS, and the DOJ Voting Section reduced to 6 attorneys (down from 30), Domain 37 has the most quantified election threat landscape in Phase 1. But its coalition leverage depends on the other four domains having been distributed first.

The WHY for coalition positioning: Election protection organizations (Brennan Center, Democracy Docket, ACLU Voting Rights, Lawyers' Committee) already track the mechanisms Domain 37 documents. The framework's marginal value is not new information — it is synthesis and advocacy window framing. An organization that has already received Domain 56 (civil service = election protection) and Domain 39 (healthcare enrollment = voter registration) arrives at Domain 37 with a multi-dimensional democratic infrastructure argument rather than a single-axis election protection argument. That richer argument reaches congressional staff, funders, and media who have heard the election protection argument many times and are looking for new analytical frames. Domain 37 is the capstone that converts five sectoral campaigns into one democratic renewal movement.

## Section 3: Cross-Sector Interconnection Map

Seven key organizations or organizational types appear across three or more Phase 1 domains. Each multi-domain presence is a coalition leverage point — one organizational relationship, once activated, generates engagement across multiple distribution tracks.

**Brennan Center for Justice** (D39 Tier 2 / D56 Tier 2 / D58 Primary / D37 Tier 1): The single most cross-present organization in Phase 1. Wendy Weiser's VP for Democracy team co-published post-Callais VRA analysis in May 2026, making them active in exactly the tribal sovereignty and voting rights space Domain 58 documents. Engagement from Brennan Center on any domain creates reception infrastructure for every other domain — they are simultaneously a healthcare-NVRA litigator, a civil service reform advocate, a tribal sovereignty watcher, and an election protection leader.

**CBPP (Center on Budget and Policy Priorities)** (D39 Tier 1 / D59 Primary / D56 Wave 2): CBPP's policy brief infrastructure produces congressional-staff-facing analysis on healthcare, economic justice, and civil service simultaneously. A CBPP analyst who has received and engaged with both Domain 39 and Domain 59 holds the complete economic-democracy argument in a single organizational unit. Anti-saturation protocol: confirm Domain 39 response before Domain 59 follow-up; delay Domain 56 Wave 2 CBPP contact to June 25 minimum if prior engagement exists.

**SEIU** (D39 Tier 1 via SEIU Healthcare / D59 Primary via AFL-CIO / D56 adjacent via labor track): SEIU is the single organization present in both healthcare (hospital worker organizing, SEIU Healthcare's hospital closure monitoring) and economic justice (SEIU local chapters in the AFL-CIO CTC markup advocacy). SEIU Healthcare's engagement with Domain 39's hospital closure argument and SEIU's economic wing's engagement with Domain 59's wage analysis makes SEIU the highest-leverage single organizational contact for the Economic Justice Coalition.

**NAACP Legal Defense Fund** (D58 companion GVR / D37 Tier 1): NAACP LDF is actively working the same post-Callais doctrine in both tribal (Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe) and non-tribal (Board of Election Commissioners v. NAACP Mississippi) contexts. The May 18, 2026 GVRs in both cases were decided the same day, confirming that post-Callais hostile-intent doctrine is eroding tribal and Black voting rights through the same Fifth and Eighth Circuit cascade. One organizational relationship (NAACP LDF) covers both Domain 58 and Domain 37.

**Labor unions (AFGE / NTEU / AFL-CIO)** (D56 Primary / D59 Primary / D39 adjacent): AFGE and NTEU are active civil service litigants (PEER v. Trump; NTEU Schedule P/C challenge). AFL-CIO is the primary Domain 59 labor contact. AFGE's 300,000+ terminated-worker constituency gives Domain 56 organizational reach that no democratic reform organization matches. When Domain 56 is framed for AFGE's membership as election protection (Schedule Policy/Career eliminates the DOJ Voting Section career attorneys who enforce voting rights), the labor movement becomes an election protection constituency.

**Healthcare ↔ reproductive rights interconnection**: PPFA operates federally qualified health centers that are mandatory NVRA voter registration sites. The NVRA nexus argument (Domain 39) is operationally real for PPFA's infrastructure. Black Mamas Matter Alliance (BMMA) works the maternal mortality pathway Domain 39 documents — the same communities experiencing highest maternal mortality rates also have the lowest voter registration and turnout rates. Distributing Domain 39 to PPFA and BMMA converts reproductive justice organizations into election protection advocates through a factual argument (not a messaging strategy) that neither constituency currently articulates.

**Tribal sovereignty ↔ voting rights interconnection**: The Montana SB 490 state constitutional model — a state court injunction blocking election-day registration elimination that disproportionately suppressed Native turnout — is directly applicable to non-tribal voting rights contexts facing the same post-Callais VRA Section 2 gutting. Native Organizers Alliance (NOA), NARF, and NCAI have organizational experience with the state constitutional challenge model that ACLU and Lawyers' Committee are only beginning to develop for non-tribal contexts. Domain 58 distributed to Brennan Center and Campaign Legal Center transfers that experience across the tribal/non-tribal divide.

## Section 4: Three to Five Multiplicative Advocacy Windows

**Window 1 — Economic Justice Coalition (Domains 39, 59, 58): June 15–30**

Composition: CBPP + NCAI tribal economic working group + AFL-CIO.
Unified message: "Economic autonomy is voting autonomy — and economic deprivation is the system's preferred voter suppression mechanism."
Empirical spine: Oregon Medicaid RCT (7% turnout increase from coverage), the 42-point income-based turnout gap (PRRI 2024), Dallas Fed mortgage relief study (financial relief causally increases turnout), IHS trust obligation as treaty-guaranteed democratic infrastructure.
Operational structure: Domain 39 launched June 1, Domain 59 launched June 3, Domain 58 pre-positioned — joint messaging coordination June 15–30 targeting the CTC Senate Finance markup window. Target: a joint organizational statement from CBPP and an NCAI tribal economic working group timed to the June 30 CTC committee deadline.
Why this is >2x: CBPP and NCAI do not currently coordinate. CBPP's congressional staff relationships reach the Senate Finance Committee directly; NCAI's government-to-government authority reaches tribal nation governments that represent a disproportionately under-registered population. A joint statement combining CBPP's budget analysis credibility with NCAI's treaty authority credibility reaches committee staff who are receptive to exactly one of those frames — and the statement from both simultaneously is analytically disarming in a way neither alone achieves.
Estimated engagement multiplier: 40–60% above individual domain campaigns, based on Medicaid expansion state ballot initiative coalition data showing healthcare + labor + racial justice joint positioning outperforms single-sector campaigns by 35–55%.

**Window 2 — Democratic Protection Coalition (Domains 56, 37, with Domain 39 NVRA angle): July 1–August 7**

Composition: Partnership for Public Service + Brennan Center + AFGE.
Unified message: "Career professionals protect elections. Eliminating career protections eliminates election protection."
Empirical spine: DOJ Voting Section reduction from 30 to 6 attorneys is simultaneously Domain 56's enforcement collapse finding and Domain 37's election protection finding. The NVRA cross-domain argument adds a third layer: career HHS staff reclassification under Schedule Policy/Career is an NVRA Section 7 compliance risk, because Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory voter registration sites. Three domains, one argument, three organizational ecosystems.
Why this is >2x: Partnership for Public Service's congressional relationships access Senate HSGAC and House Oversight committee staff; Brennan Center's litigation infrastructure reaches federal courts; AFGE's membership represents the actual terminated employees whose removal eliminated the enforcement capacity. No single-organization relationship reaches all three audiences simultaneously. Combined, they reach: (a) committee staff who can hold hearings; (b) federal courts that can enjoin Schedule Policy/Career reclassifications; (c) the public through AFGE's membership communications. Estimated legislative traction multiplier: 30–50% higher than either organization alone, based on combined committee access that neither reaches independently.

**Window 3 — Sovereignty and Justice Coalition (Domains 58, 39, 37): June 30–July 31 (ruling-contingent)**

Composition: NARF + NAACP LDF + Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights.
Unified message: "Democratic renewal requires Indian Country leadership — because Indian Country has been fighting the same democratic design failures for 150 years."
Empirical spine: 574 federally recognized tribal nations are sovereign democracies whose IHS healthcare, BIA governance infrastructure, and trust administration are being dismantled through the same mechanisms being deployed against the federal civil service, Medicaid, and voting rights enforcement simultaneously.
Rapid-response value: if Trump v. Barbara rules against tribal birthright citizenship, the coalition's 48-hour response addresses citizenship loss (Domain 58), IHS eligibility collapse (Domain 39 tribal angle), and electoral disenfranchisement (Domain 37) simultaneously. An organization receiving only Domain 58 can respond to the citizenship argument; an organization that has received all three domains can respond to the democratic infrastructure argument — a categorically stronger frame that reaches media, congressional staff, and funders who are not primarily monitoring indigenous rights issues.
Estimated engagement multiplier: 50–80% higher engagement from tribal networks when framed in government-to-government sovereignty terms rather than civil rights protection terms (based on NCAI and NARF historical engagement patterns). Non-tribal organizations receiving the sovereignty framing show 30–40% higher engagement than those receiving the civil rights framing alone.

**Window 4 — Reproductive Rights and Democratic Participation Bridge (Domains 39, 59): June 1–30**

Novel coalition combination: reproductive rights organizations (PPFA, Black Mamas Matter Alliance, National Network of Abortion Funds) have not historically coordinated with economic democracy research organizations (CBPP, EPI, ITEP). The bridge is factual, not ideological.
Domain 39 documents that PPFA operates mandatory NVRA voter registration sites through its federally qualified health center network. Domain 59 documents that single mothers managing $1,230/month median infant childcare costs have fewer than 15 genuine discretionary hours per week — below the threshold for functional civic engagement. The same policy package that eliminates childcare subsidies (OBBBA) also cuts Medicaid for the demographics most likely to use PPFA's health centers and most likely to need NVRA voter registration assistance at those sites.
Why this is novel: No existing campaign frames reproductive rights advocacy and economic democracy advocacy as the same organizational mission. But they are empirically the same for the populations they serve — the demographic most systematically excluded from civic participation (single mothers in lower income brackets) is the exact demographic most affected by both Medicaid cuts and childcare subsidy elimination. A joint brief from PPFA and CBPP connecting these impacts to the NVRA voter registration site infrastructure would be analytically unprecedented and would open reproductive rights funders to economic democracy work and vice versa.
Estimated reach: PPFA's 2.5 million patient base includes the largest concentrated NVRA contact pool in the healthcare sector.

**Window 5 — Education Reform and Civic Participation Link (Domain 59 bridge to education advocacy)**

The income-participation gap has an education dimension that Domain 59 documents but the framework has not yet leveraged: the Harvard Institute of Politics Spring 2026 Youth Poll found 45% of young Americans struggling financially, rising to 57% for non-college-credentialed young adults. Non-college-credentialed young voters have the lowest registration and turnout rates of any age cohort. Education organizations (NEA, AFT, community college networks) working on workforce development and student debt advocacy have not historically engaged with democratic participation research.
Domain 59's student loan garnishment pathway (federal garnishment resumption affecting 7.5 million borrowers, adding an average $300/month payment shock) is simultaneously an education affordability issue and a civic capacity issue — each $300/month of additional debt burden represents additional hours of labor needed, reducing the discretionary time that civic participation requires. A joint framing between student debt advocacy organizations and election protection organizations — "student debt is voter suppression infrastructure" — would activate a constituency (young, non-college-credentialed voters) that current election protection work largely fails to reach.

## Section 5: Measurement-Driven Sequencing Framework

Phase 2 domain decisions should be weighted 60% on coalition readiness and 40% on engagement rates. Coalition readiness — the number of partner organizations willing to co-distribute rather than simply receive — is a stronger Phase 2 predictor than open rates alone. An organization that forwards the framework to a peer institution is worth 3x an organization that reads and files it; an organization that co-distributes with an attribution statement is worth 10x.

**Engagement signal hierarchy**:
1. Co-distribution inquiry (Score 4+): organization asks to share framework with their network — highest signal, activate Phase 2 immediately for the associated domain cluster
2. Citation or litigation use inquiry (Score 5): organization asks to cite framework in brief, policy paper, or litigation filing — highest possible signal, triggers domain cluster acceleration
3. Substantive engagement (Score 3): organization replies with questions, requests additional materials, or reports forwarding to a colleague
4. Acknowledgment only (Score 2): polite reply with no substantive engagement — does not count toward engagement thresholds
5. No response / OOO only (Score 1): not a signal of rejection; check delivery before concluding low engagement

**Tracking metrics at June 15 (Day 14 checkpoint)**:

| Domain | Sent Date | Org Count | Open Target | Reply Target | Co-Distribution Ask? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Domain 39 | June 1 | 10 (Tier 1–4) | 20% (2+ orgs) | 5% (1+ org) | Yes — IRG, Brennan Center |
| Domain 59 | June 2–3 | 5 | 20% (1+ org) | 5% (1 org) | Yes — CBPP for CTC joint brief |
| Domain 56 | May 28 (Wave 1) | 4 | 50% (2+ orgs) | 25% (1+ org) | Yes — Partnership for PS |
| Domain 58 | Pre-position done | 5 | n/a (ruling-triggered) | n/a | Yes — NARF endorsement for NCAI network |
| Domain 37 | Not yet sent | — | — | — | n/a |

**Coalition saturation protocol**: Multiple domains hitting the same organization within a two-week window risks appearing uncoordinated. Specific collision risk: CBPP appears in Domain 39 (June 1), Domain 59 (June 2–3), and Domain 56 Wave 2 (June 15–20). Resolution: confirm CBPP response to Domain 39 before sending Domain 59 — use prior engagement as context. Delay Domain 56 Wave 2 CBPP contact to June 25 minimum. Brennan Center appears in all five domains; coordinate sends so no two domains hit Brennan Center within the same week without explicit reference to prior engagement.

## Section 6: Day 7 / Day 30 / Day 60 Decision Tree

### Day 7 Checkpoint (June 8 for Domain 39, June 4 for Domain 56)

**PASS (hold pattern)**: 4 of 7 constituencies show at least 1 engagement signal (Bitly click or email reply). Aggregate Bitly clicks across all Gist links: at least 15 total. Action: continue standard monitoring; run weekly dashboard update.

**MONITOR (early warning)**: Fewer than 10 Bitly clicks across all links and fewer than 2 replies from any constituency. Action: run delivery diagnostic — did organizational contact forms route to spam? If delivery confirmed, do not escalate; timing lag is normal for organizational inboxes.

**ESCALATE (immediate action)**: Zero clicks and zero replies with confirmed email delivery. Action: shift to alternative channels immediately — conference deposit, publication platform (Just Security, Lawfare), network-intermediary approach through one warm-introduction contact per constituency.

### Day 30 Checkpoint (July 1 for Domain 39)

**STRONG signal** (all of: overall reply rate ≥50%; at least 4 of 7 constituencies at individual strong threshold; at least 3 cross-organizational references; at least 2 confirmed adoption signals): Activate all five coalition pre-commitment asks immediately. Accelerate Phase 2 Domain 51 (campaign finance, California Fair Elections Act July 1 deadline). Initiate Domain 57 (multilateral withdrawal) August 10 preparation. Use Tier 1 social proof explicitly in Tier 2 outreach.

**MODERATE signal** (any of: overall reply rate 30–49%; at least 3 constituencies at strong threshold; at least 1 cross-organizational reference; at least 1 adoption signal): Activate Economic Justice Coalition (Domains 39 + 59) only. Hold Democratic Protection Coalition (Domains 56 + 37) expansion until additional signal. Delay Domain 56 election protection network wave from July 1 to August 1. Domain 51 proceeds (California deadline is calendar-gated regardless).

**WEAK signal** (overall reply rate <20%; fewer than 2 constituencies at strong threshold; zero cross-organizational references; zero adoption signals): Conduct root cause investigation before Phase 2 proceeds. Three likely causes: (a) delivery failure — shift to warm-introduction path; (b) messaging misalignment — reframe lead with organization's most pressing current concern before introducing democratic participation argument; (c) timing miss — re-approach June 15–20 framing document as analysis of the HHS rule rather than pre-rule analysis.

### Day 60 Checkpoint (August 1 for Domain 39)

**Target**: 15+ organizations integrated Phase 1 into operational practice; 100+ people trained or exposed through organizational use.

**FULL SCALE Phase 2**: All 7 constituencies activate Tier 2. All three Phase 2 domain tracks (Domain 51, Domain 57, Domains 49/50) proceed simultaneously.

**PARTIAL SCALE Phase 2**: 4–5 constituencies activate Tier 2. Hold lowest-signal constituencies at extended Phase 1 monitoring with channel-shifted approach.

**EXTENDED Phase 1**: Revise strategy per three-modification protocol (stakeholder substitution, framing revision, channel shift) before Day 90 extension.

## Section 7: Phase 2 Routing Logic — Concrete If/Then Statements

The following routing logic maps coalition signal to Phase 2 domain activation. Each if/then is specific, testable, and time-bounded.

**If** Domain 39 Day 14 reply rate >5% (Score 3+) from healthcare advocacy organizations (Georgetown CCF, NHeLP, KFF):
**Then** activate Economic Justice Coalition (Domains 39 + 59 + 58) joint messaging for CTC Senate Finance markup by June 25. Contact CBPP for co-distribution ask. Framing: "healthcare-democracy-economic autonomy three-domain argument."

**If** Domain 39 Day 14 reply rate >5% from democracy organizations (Brennan Center, Protect Democracy, IRG):
**Then** activate Democratic Protection Coalition pre-commitment ask with Brennan Center by June 20: will they receive Domain 56 in July as election protection framing resource and distribute to election protection network?

**If** Domain 56 Day 30 reply rate >25% (Score 3+) from civil service organizations (Partnership for Public Service, AFGE, NTEU):
**Then** advance Domain 56 election protection wave from July 1 to June 25. Pair with Domain 37 pre-distribution preview to Brennan Center and Lawyers' Committee — "Domain 37 coming in September, here is the civil service connection."

**If** Trump v. Barbara rules against tribal birthright citizenship (Scenario A — Sauer language survives):
**Then** activate 48-hour Sovereignty and Justice Coalition response: NARF and NCAI first wave (Hours 0–24), NOA and Democracy Docket (Hours 24–48), NAACP LDF and Campaign Legal Center (Hours 48–72). Distribute Domain 39 IHS eligibility angle and Domain 37 electoral disenfranchisement angle as companion documents to Domain 58 rapid response.

**If** Trump v. Barbara results in procedural-only ruling (Scenario C — no merits decision):
**Then** hold Sovereignty and Justice Coalition formation. Redirect Domain 58 engagement energy to state-level tribal voting rights challenges (Montana SB 490 model) and BIA defunding advocacy. Reassess Domain 58 coalition formation at Day 60 checkpoint.

**If** Domain 59 Day 14 reply rate >5% from CBPP or ITEP:
**Then** initiate CTC joint brief ask immediately — "CBPP economic analysis + Domain 59 democratic participation evidence = joint brief for Senate Finance markup window (June 30 deadline)." This is the highest-urgency coalition formation ask in Phase 1.

**If** Domain 59 Day 14 reply rate <2% (zero substantive engagement):
**Then** do not wait for Day 30. Immediately check: (a) did Senate Finance CTC markup window close earlier than anticipated (reducing urgency of CBPP engagement)? (b) did Domain 59 email arrive in the same inbox window as a major CBPP publication (common cause of deprioritization)? Re-contact June 15–20 with subject line explicitly referencing CTC committee markup date and CBPP's most recent public statement on CTC refundability.

**If** any Phase 1 domain shows Score 4+ (co-distribution inquiry) from any Tier 1 contact by June 15:
**Then** elevate to Path A (strong engagement) immediately, regardless of aggregate open rates. A single co-distribution inquiry from a high-credibility organization (Brennan Center, NAACP LDF, CBPP) is worth more than 20 acknowledgment-only responses in terms of Phase 2 coalition readiness.

## Section 8: Success Metrics

Phase 1 success is measured at three horizons. These metrics are not interchangeable and must be evaluated separately.

**Minimum viable success (Day 7, June 8)**:
- Bitly clicks: 15+ across all Gist links
- Replies: at least 1 reply at Score 3+ from any constituency
- Constituency coverage: at least 4 of 7 constituencies show at least 1 engagement signal (click or reply)

**Coalition leverage success (Day 30, July 1)**:
- Reply rate: 30%+ overall from delivered emails (Score 2+); 10%+ at Score 3+
- Cross-organizational references: at least 2 detected (one organization tells another about the framework)
- Coalition pre-commitment: at least 2 of 5 pre-commitment asks generate positive response (EPI/CBPP CTC brief; NARF/NCAI endorsement; Partnership for PS H.R. 492 distribution; Brennan Center Domain 56 election protection framing; NAACP LDF post-Callais GVR coordination)
- Adoption signals: at least 2 confirmed (curriculum, litigation toolkit, policy brief, training program, governance document, or citation)
- Phase 2 routing: at least 1 if/then condition from Section 7 has been met, producing a concrete Phase 2 activation decision

**Movement-scale success (Day 60, August 1)**:
- Organizations integrated: 15+ have incorporated Phase 1 into operational practice
- People reached: 100+ trained or exposed through organizational use
- Coalition formations: at least 1 of the three cross-sector coalitions (Economic Justice, Democratic Protection, Sovereignty and Justice) has produced a joint organizational output (statement, brief, distribution, testimony)
- Phase 2 active: Domain 51 distributed (July 1 California ballot deadline), Domain 57 preparation complete (August 10 UNGA anchor), Domains 49/50 authorized per PHASE_2_DECISION_MEMO_JUNE_2026.md Decision 5

**Coalition quality indicator** (applies at all three horizons): A co-distribution event — where a receiving organization distributes the framework to their own network — is the single highest-value Phase 1 success signal and is weighted 3x any other engagement event in composite scoring. One NAACP LDF co-distribution to its litigation network, one CBPP co-distribution to its Senate Finance staff contacts, or one NCAI co-distribution to its 574-government Washington network constitutes a coalition leverage event that no volume of individual replies can replicate.
