# Phase 2 Expansion: Domains 41–43 Research Outlines

*May 13, 2026. Three candidate domains evaluated and outlined for the next Phase 2 expansion batch.
Each was selected because it fills a structural gap that the current 40-domain expansion batch does not
address, and each carries a clear democratic-design argument — not merely a policy critique.*

---

## Why These Three Domains Extend Phase 1

The resistance-research framework's Phase 1 and Phase 2 core (Domains 1–55) covered the full arc of
democratic erosion: electoral architecture, institutional capture, judicial independence, rights suppression,
healthcare and civic capacity, information infrastructure, and economic power concentration through lobbying
and campaign finance (Domain 51, Domain 38 expansion batch). The Phase 2 expansion batch (Domains 38–40)
deepened three mechanism domains: lobbying transparency and antitrust (38), education as democratic
infrastructure (39), and healthcare as democratic determinant (40).

Three structural gaps persist in the economic and infrastructure architecture of democracy:

**Gap 1: Consumer financial architecture as democratic suppression.** Domain 38 (Financial Sector
Independence) analyzes central bank independence, the Federal Reserve siege, and banking regulatory capture
through corporate PAC spending. It does not analyze the consumer-facing financial architecture — predatory
lending, the racial wealth gap's documented suppression of civic participation, the CFPB's systematic
dismantling, and how debt traps function as democratic infrastructure problems rather than simply economic
ones. The domain needed is not a duplicate of Domain 38; it addresses a categorically different harm
pathway: financial precarity as a mechanism of political exclusion.

**Gap 2: Platform information architecture as democratic design problem.** Domain 48 (Surveillance
Capitalism) analyzes commercial data broker markets and government warrant-bypass purchase. Domain 36 (AI
Governance) analyzes government use of AI against democratic participation. Neither domain analyzes the
structural design of platform recommendation systems — algorithmic amplification, ad transparency failures,
and the platform monopoly's capture of the digital public square — as a democratic design problem equivalent
to the broadcast regulation crisis of the 1970s. The needed domain frames platform accountability as an
information architecture problem, not a speech regulation problem.

**Gap 3: Housing architecture (not instability) as spatial democratic suppression.** Domain 47 (Housing
Security, Displacement, and Democratic Participation) is comprehensive on eviction, homelessness, and
housing assistance collapse. It does not analyze the upstream architectural layer: exclusionary zoning as
deliberate spatial segregation of political constituencies, gentrification as neighborhood political
displacement, and housing cost burden as community organizing suppression. These are distinct from housing
instability; they operate on stably-housed populations through the design of the housing system itself.

The three domains below address these gaps. Each carries a clear democratic-design thesis, a 2026 advocacy
window, international precedent, and cross-references to existing domains that enable distribution pairing.

## Domain 41: Consumer Financial Architecture and Democratic Equity

### Central Argument

The U.S. consumer financial system operates as an infrastructure of political exclusion: predatory lending,
the racial wealth gap, and the systematic dismantling of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)
function not merely as economic injustices but as documented mechanisms suppressing the civic participation
of the communities most affected. Financial reform is democratic infrastructure, not economic policy.

### Problem Statement

**The racial wealth gap and civic participation.** White households held 84.2 percent of all U.S. wealth
in 2023 while comprising 66 percent of households. The median Black family holds $44,100 in net worth —
15.5 percent of the $282,310 median white family. Hispanic families hold 2.3 percent of total U.S. family
wealth while comprising 9.6 percent of households (Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, 2023). This
gap is not a static inequality; it is reproduced through specific financial mechanisms — predatory lending,
discriminatory credit access, and inherited debt — that are simultaneously mechanisms of civic exclusion.
In 2024, racial turnout gaps widened: youth voter turnout ranged from 58 percent for young white women to
25 percent for young Black men (CIRCLE, 2024 election analysis). Black unemployment stood at 7.2 percent
versus 3.7 percent for white workers in July 2025 (BLS). The economic precarity that the racial wealth gap
produces is not background noise to the political exclusion documented in Domains 1, 22, and 54 — it is
a primary mechanism.

**Predatory lending as democratic disenfranchisement.** Payday lending, which carries effective annual
interest rates of 400 percent, is geographically and demographically concentrated in Black and Hispanic
communities through targeted advertising and branch siting (ACLU, National Fair Housing). Black and
Hispanic borrowers receive a disproportionate share of subprime and predatory mortgage loans — a pattern
that transferred an estimated $25 billion in housing wealth from Black and Hispanic families to financial
institutions during the 2008 financial crisis (NAACP predatory lending analysis). Pew Research's December
2024 student loan default analysis found that higher default rates among Black and Hispanic borrowers
reflect "greater financial difficulties these borrowers face, including lower incomes, employment gaps,
and less wealth" — wealth gaps that are themselves the product of prior predatory lending cycles.

**CFPB dismantling as democratic design failure.** The CFPB was established under Dodd-Frank (2010) with
an insulated funding mechanism — drawn from Federal Reserve revenues rather than congressional
appropriations, upheld in CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association (2024). The Trump
administration's 2025 CFPB deregulatory shift has proceeded on multiple tracks: on March 28, 2025, two
days before its own payday lending rule took effect, the CFPB announced it would not prioritize enforcement
of the rule's Payment Withdrawal and Payment Disclosure provisions. The Spring 2025 regulatory agenda
signals a full rule rescission is planned. The Bureau has simultaneously shifted focus away from small
business lending and Buy Now, Pay Later loans, sectors where predatory practices disproportionately affect
the same communities that have the lowest civic participation rates. The consumer protection infrastructure
is being dismantled at the same moment the communities it protects are under maximum electoral pressure.

**Bank concentration and democratic accountability collapse.** The S.2155 rollback (2018) raised the
Dodd-Frank stress testing threshold from $50 billion to $250 billion, exempting 25 of the 38 largest
U.S. banks from heightened oversight. The March 2023 banking crisis — Silicon Valley Bank, Signature Bank,
First Republic — was a direct product of this deregulation: banks that would have faced stress testing
requirements under original Dodd-Frank failed in ways that required $500 billion in emergency Fed
backstops. Bank regulators "overly influenced by industry" failed to use available tools, allowed mergers
that further concentrated the sector, and declined to break up institutions that benefit from implicit
too-big-to-fail subsidies (American Prospect, 2023). When four to six institutions control the majority
of consumer deposits, lending decisions, and payment infrastructure, those institutions exercise a form of
financial sovereignty over the communities they serve — without democratic accountability.

### Democratic Angle

The mechanism connecting financial architecture to democratic exclusion operates through three pathways:

**Pathway 1: Time and cognitive resource depletion.** Research on scarcity (Mullainathan and Shafir,
2013) documents that material precarity consumes cognitive bandwidth in ways that reduce capacity for
unrelated complex tasks, including political participation. A family managing a 400-percent payday loan
rollover cycle, a medical debt collection lawsuit, or a predatory auto loan is not tracking legislative
session calendars. This is not an argument about voter motivation; it is a finding about cognitive capacity
under structural financial stress.

**Pathway 2: Wealth-based civic mobilization suppression.** Political organizing requires material
resources: time not spent on second jobs, money for childcare during canvassing, transportation to polling
locations, and the ability to absorb financial penalties for civic engagement (missed work to vote, costs
of registering a new address). The racial wealth gap functions as a structural subsidy for white civic
participation and a structural tax on Black and Hispanic civic participation.

**Pathway 3: Financial institution accountability without democratic remedy.** When JPMorgan Chase,
Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citigroup collectively hold over $10 trillion in assets and determine
credit access, mortgage approval, and consumer lending terms for the majority of American families, those
institutions exercise power that is more consequential to daily life than most elected governments —
without any democratic accountability mechanism. The CFPB was the single federal institution with both
the mandate and the structural insulation to provide that accountability. Its systematic dismantling
removes the democratic check on the most consequential private power over ordinary economic life.

### Reform Architecture

**Reform 1: CFPB structural restoration and expansion.** Restore full enforcement authority under the
existing funding mechanism upheld in CFPB v. CFSA (2024). Extend CFPB's mandate to explicitly cover
Buy Now, Pay Later, earned wage access products, and data broker financial profiling. Establish a
community reinvestment mandate requiring CFPB to publish annual reports on lending disparities by race,
income, and geography to enable civil rights enforcement.

**Reform 2: Usury rate cap.** Enact a federal 36-percent annual percentage rate cap on consumer loans,
modeled on the Military Lending Act (currently limited to active-duty service members). Eight states
have effective rate caps; Vermont's 18-percent cap and Arkansas's constitutional 17-percent limit
demonstrate viability. International precedent: the UK's Financial Conduct Authority imposed a 0.8
percent daily cap on payday loans in 2015, reducing payday lending volume by 50 percent without
eliminating credit access.

**Reform 3: Public banking and postal banking.** The postal banking model — restored briefly during
the 1911–1966 period in the U.S. — would provide basic savings and low-cost lending services through
the U.S. Postal Service to the 5.9 million unbanked households (FDIC, 2023). International precedent:
Japan Post Bank serves 128 million account holders; Germany's Sparkassen cooperative savings banks
(Sparkasse system) serve rural and low-income communities with a mandate to serve the underbanked
regardless of profit. The German Sparkasse model — 370 savings banks, publicly owned by local
governments, required by charter to serve the full community — demonstrates that non-predatory
consumer financial services are structurally achievable.

**Reform 4: Community Reinvestment Act modernization.** The CRA (1977) requires banks to meet the
credit needs of their entire service communities, including low-income areas. Trump-era OCC rules
weakened geographic requirements; restoration and expansion to cover fintech lenders and non-bank
mortgage companies would extend community reinvestment obligations to the fast-growing segments of
consumer finance that have replaced traditional banking in underserved communities.

**Reform 5: Student debt and civic participation.** Student debt restructuring — income-driven
repayment simplification and public service loan forgiveness restoration — is framed here as democratic
infrastructure rather than economic relief. A 2025 study in Political Research Quarterly (David
Macdonald) found that student debt correlates with higher voter participation, particularly among
degree-holders, but the distribution of the debt burden is highly racialized: Black borrowers default
at twice the rate of white borrowers (Pew, December 2024), and the debt burden that is supposed to
be a civic mobilizer in fact suppresses participation in the communities bearing the highest default
risk. The reform is not debt cancellation per se; it is structuring debt to remove the concentrated
racialized harm pathway.

**International Precedent:** Nordic cooperative finance (Denmark's Coop amba restructuring, 2024;
Swedish cooperative credit unions); German Sparkassen public savings banks; UK FCA payday loan
rate cap (2015, 50 percent volume reduction); EU consumer credit directive (2023 revision) requiring
standardized disclosure and 36-month repayment planning for high-cost loans.

## Domain 42-Expansion: Techno-Monopolies and Platform Accountability

### Central Argument

Tech platform monopolies (Meta, Google/Alphabet, Apple, Amazon, X) have captured the infrastructure of
democratic deliberation — the digital public square — and operate it without the democratic accountability
requirements that traditional communications infrastructure has always carried. Algorithmic amplification
functions as an editorial force reshaping political opinion at industrial scale; political ad opacity
enables dark-money targeting of voters without statutory transparency; antitrust enforcement failures
have allowed platform consolidation to compress the information ecosystem in ways that broadcast
regulation specifically sought to prevent. Platform accountability is a democratic infrastructure
problem, not a speech regulation problem.

### Problem Statement

**Algorithmic amplification as political architecture.** The most significant finding in recent platform
accountability research is structural: platform recommendation algorithms do not merely transmit political
content — they reshape political opinion. A 2026 Nature study on X's feed algorithm found that switching
from chronological to algorithmic feeds increased engagement and shifted political opinions toward more
conservative positions on policy priorities, perceptions of criminal investigations, and international
affairs. During the 2024 U.S. presidential campaign, a 10-day experiment with 1,256 volunteers found
that increased exposure to algorithmically amplified anti-democratic content reduced cross-partisan warmth
by 2.48 degrees on a 100-point scale (FAccT 2025, "Auditing Political Exposure Bias"). A separate 2025
FAccT conference paper auditing X during the 2024 election documented systematic amplification of
conservative-aligned accounts in algorithmic recommendations regardless of user preference signals. Sky
News's month-long experiment and separate Wall Street Journal and Washington Post audits all documented
X's algorithmic promotion of far-right and extreme content. Meta's engagement-driven algorithm similarly
shows documented right-leaning amplification bias: right-leaning pages garner disproportionate
interaction shares relative to post share (2021 internal Meta data, confirmed by external audits).

This is not a question of content moderation. It is a question of editorial architecture. A broadcaster
that systematically promoted one political direction's content while suppressing another's would face FCC
scrutiny under the fairness doctrine framework, must-carry rules, or equal time provisions. Platform
recommendation algorithms exercise the same editorial power with none of the same accountability.

**Political advertising opacity.** Online political ad spending in the 2024 election cycle reached
$1.35 billion on Google and Meta alone by election day (Brennan Center, 2025), with total digital
political advertising approaching $1.9 billion. Despite this scale, transparency frameworks are
voluntary: no federal law requires platforms to publish political ad data; platforms "collect and
publish political ad data" as a matter of discretion, not obligation. The Brennan Center's analysis
found that "the origins of much of the money funding online political advertising are not publicly
known, as spenders can use dark-money nonprofit organizations to keep donors hidden." An independent
audit of 11 major tech companies' ad transparency tools found that "none of the ad transparency
tools are operating as effectively as needed, leaving voters worldwide vulnerable to disinformation
and manipulation" (Storyboard18 / Accountability Lab, 2024).

The intersection with Domain 51 (Campaign Finance, Dark Money) is direct: the same dark-money
infrastructure documented in Domain 51 routes political spending through digital advertising on
platforms that voluntarily decline to require donor disclosure. The DISCLOSE Act, which would have
mandated disclosure for political digital advertising, has failed to advance in the Senate for
multiple sessions.

**Antitrust failure as democratic design collapse.** The DOJ and FTC antitrust cases against Google,
Meta, Apple, and Amazon (2023 filings) represent the most significant attempt to address platform
monopoly since the Microsoft case (2000). Judge Leonie Brinkema found Google monopolized publisher
ad servers and ad exchanges (April 2025 remedies trial); Judge Amit Mehta imposed behavioral remedies
in September 2025 on Google Search. But the Meta case was dismissed in November 2025 — Judge James
Boasberg concluded Meta lacks monopoly power when TikTok and YouTube are included in the relevant
market, a definitional choice that treats all attention-capture platforms as substitutes for the social
networking infrastructure that enables political organizing, civil society formation, and news
distribution. The Washington Post reported the pattern bluntly in November 2025: "The government failed
to break up Meta. It's becoming a pattern."

The democratic design consequence is not merely economic inefficiency. When Meta controls Facebook,
Instagram, and WhatsApp simultaneously — the three platforms through which most Americans organize
community groups, follow local news, and receive political information — and when that control operates
through algorithms that prioritize engagement over accuracy, the result is a structural capture of the
democratic information commons by a single unaccountable actor.

**The Romanian election case study.** In November 2024, the Romanian presidential election was
annulled after authorities documented TikTok's algorithmic amplification of disinformation linked to
a candidate, triggering DSA transparency and content-moderation requirements. The European Commission
opened a DSA investigation. This is the first documented case of a democratic election being
successfully contested on the grounds of platform algorithmic manipulation — and the legal framework
that enabled the challenge (the EU Digital Services Act) does not exist in the United States.

### Democratic Angle

The democratic design failure is structural rather than incidental. The broadcast regulation framework
(Fairness Doctrine, must-carry rules, equal time provisions) was premised on the recognition that
when a communications medium becomes the primary infrastructure of democratic deliberation, its
operators must accept accountability requirements commensurate with their power over democratic
information architecture. Television and radio stations accepted these requirements as a condition
of using public spectrum. Platforms have captured the equivalent of the public spectrum — the
attention of democratic populations — through private network effects rather than government license,
and they have specifically argued that private ownership insulates them from equivalent accountability.

The democratic design principle this domain develops: any communications infrastructure that functions
as the primary medium of democratic deliberation carries accountability obligations commensurate with
that function, regardless of the ownership structure through which it achieved that position. The
accountability design must match the democratic function, not the property form.

The specific mechanisms:

**Algorithmic editorial power without editorial accountability**: A newspaper that systematically
published articles designed to depress voter turnout among specific demographics would face civil
rights litigation. An algorithm that systematically amplifies content depressing turnout among
specific demographics (see Domain 48 PNAS study: 1.9 percent turnout reduction from targeted
digital ads) operates without equivalent accountability because it is framed as an engagement
optimization tool rather than an editorial choice.

**Platform concentration enabling democratic organization suppression**: When a single company
controls the primary platforms for community organizing (Facebook Groups), protest coordination
(WhatsApp, Instagram), and civic information sharing, decisions about which content to amplify,
which accounts to restrict, and which communities to defederate carry the force of democratic
infrastructure governance.

### Reform Architecture

**Reform 1: Algorithmic transparency requirements (DSA-modeled).** The EU Digital Services Act
(in force for very large online platforms since February 2024) requires Very Large Online Platforms
(VLOPs) to disclose algorithm parameters to researchers and regulators, conduct annual systemic
risk assessments for democratic process risks, and allow users to access non-algorithmic (chronological)
feeds. As of December 2025, the Commission had opened 14 investigations; X was fined €120 million in
December 2025 for deceptive design, ad transparency failures, and researcher data access obstruction.
A U.S. equivalent — the Platform Accountability and Transparency Act (PATA), introduced in the Senate
2023–2024 session) — would establish equivalent requirements under FTC jurisdiction.

**Reform 2: Mandatory political ad transparency.** Federal law requiring all political advertising on
digital platforms to disclose the ultimate source of funding, irrespective of PAC/nonprofit
intermediary structure. This is the digital analog of broadcast equal time requirements: any platform
accepting political advertising accepts the accountability obligation that political advertising requires.
The DISCLOSE Act framework should be extended to digital advertising with no nonprofit shell exemption.

**Reform 3: News media compensation (Australia model).** Australia's News Media Bargaining Code (2021)
required Google and Meta to pay for news content, generating $250 million annually for Australian
news organizations. When Meta withdrew in 2024 (triggering newsroom job cuts), Australia proposed
the News Bargaining Incentive — a 2.25 percent tax on large digital platforms whether or not they
carry news, eliminating the withdrawal workaround (enacted April 2026). The U.S. equivalent — the
Journalism Competition and Preservation Act — would allow news organizations to collectively negotiate
with platforms. The democratic design argument: local journalism is democratic infrastructure, and
platform extraction of advertising revenue from local news markets without compensation is an
infrastructure defunding.

**Reform 4: Structural antitrust — platforms as communications infrastructure.** The antitrust
framework should treat platforms that have achieved dominant positions in democratic deliberation
infrastructure as common carriers subject to structural remedies analogous to the 1984 AT&T breakup:
mandatory interoperability (allowing users to move their social graphs across platforms), data
portability, and prohibition on acquisition of competing platforms that could restore competitive
pressure. The Capitol Forum's 2026 prediction of Judge Brinkema breaking up Google's ad tech monopoly
represents the leading edge of structural remedy doctrine developing in current litigation.

**Reform 5: AI-generated political content disclosure.** Require disclosure of AI-generated political
advertising content, modeled on the EU AI Act's Article 50 (labeling requirements for AI-generated
content used to influence elections). The 2026 midterms are the first election cycle in which
AI-generated microtargeted political ads operate at documented industrial scale; the 2026 advocacy
window for federal labeling legislation is the period before these ads normalize without disclosure.

**International Precedent**:
1. EU Digital Services Act (2022/2065): VLOP accountability requirements, risk assessment mandates,
   researcher data access, X €120M fine (December 2025)
2. Australia News Bargaining Incentive (April 2026): platform taxation for news defunding
3. Romania DSA election investigation (November 2024): first election annulment on algorithmic
   manipulation grounds
4. UK Online Safety Act (2023): duty of care framework for user-generated harms
5. Canada Online News Act (Bill C-18, 2023): news compensation framework

## Domain 43: Spatial Democracy — Housing Architecture and Political Power

### Central Argument

Exclusionary zoning, gentrification-driven political displacement, and housing cost burden function as
spatial democracy suppression: they segregate political constituencies by design, erase neighborhood
political infrastructure through market-driven displacement, and impose a structural time-and-resource
tax on community organizing in cost-burdened communities. This domain extends Domain 47's analysis of
housing instability (eviction, homelessness) upstream to the architectural layer — the design of the
housing system itself — as a democratic design problem requiring spatial justice responses.

### Problem Statement

**Exclusionary zoning as designed political segregation.** Exclusionary zoning — single-family zoning,
minimum lot sizes, setback requirements, parking minimums, and height restrictions — was developed in
the 1920s–1960s explicitly to maintain racial and economic segregation after explicit racial deed
restrictions were struck down (Buchanan v. Warley, 1917). The Century Foundation documents that
exclusionary zoning "raises costs, making it less likely that lower-income groups will move in, with
development fees for variance, building permits, and other requirements raising prices to levels
inaccessible for lower income people." The democratic design consequence: by concentrating low-income,
predominantly Black and Hispanic residents in specific zones while protecting high-income predominantly
white zones from density and affordability, exclusionary zoning deliberately segregates the
constituencies with the highest barriers to civic participation into the neighborhoods with the least
political infrastructure.

In 2025, nearly half of all renters — 21 million families — are housing cost-burdened, spending over
30 percent of their income on housing. Over 12 million are severely cost-burdened (above 50 percent
of income on rent). This rent burden is the direct product of exclusionary zoning's supply restriction:
when zoning prohibits multifamily housing in most of a metropolitan area, housing costs in the
permitted zones rise to extract maximum rent from captive demand. The communities bearing this cost
burden are not distributed randomly; they correspond closely to the communities bearing the highest
civic participation barriers documented across the framework.

**Gentrification as neighborhood political displacement.** A 2025 American University study
("Gentrification and Racial Representation: A Comparative Analysis") documented that in DC's
Shaw/U Street and Atlanta neighborhoods, gentrification produced "a reduction in the presence of
Black elected officials, a diminished role in decision-making processes, and a noticeable erosion of
Black cultural representation." A 2024 Oxford Social Forces study of gentrification in small cities
found that incoming residents organize and engage at the neighborhood level around gentrification
issues but fail to bring displaced communities into civic structures. A 2024 Census Bureau working
paper ("Whose Neighborhood Now? Gentrification and Community Life in Low-Income Urban Neighborhoods")
confirmed the pattern: gentrification transforms neighborhood civic infrastructure in ways that
systematically reduce the political power of prior residents while increasing the civic engagement
of incoming higher-income residents.

This is political displacement in addition to residential displacement. Black majority-minority
neighborhoods that have sustained African American political power for generations — producing
congressional representation, city council seats, school board control, and community institution
networks — lose that political infrastructure when gentrification displaces the constituency base.
Municipal influence diminishes as long-time residents lose seats on city councils, county commissions,
and community boards to newcomers representing different agendas. The democratic design consequence
is irreversible within a single electoral cycle: once a neighborhood's demographic composition shifts,
the political representation shifts with it and does not recover when former residents are dispersed
to distant zip codes.

**Housing cost burden as community organizing suppression.** Site-by-site rezonings and public
planning hearings — the primary mechanisms through which housing policy is made at the local level —
have structural features that systematically underrepresent renters and cost-burdened residents.
Existing homeowners, worried about construction noise and property values, are geographically
concentrated and easily mobilized to attend hearings. The benefits of new affordable housing are
diffuse among renters across a region, making beneficiaries difficult to mobilize politically.
The National Civic League's 2024 analysis documents that "instability affects civic engagement, as
households who are residentially unstable tend to participate less in their communities and have fewer
ties to their neighbors or institutions, further diminishing political power and social capital." Cost-
burdened renters, working multiple jobs to cover rent, face a structural time deficit for civic
engagement that is functionally a political exclusion mechanism.

The tenant organizing infrastructure that has developed to resist this dynamic — the national Tenant
Union Federation (formed in 2024 from local tenant unions) — represents an emerging response, but
it is organizing against structural exclusion from the political process, not operating within it.

**State preemption as spatial democracy suppression.** Fifteen states have enacted preemption laws
prohibiting cities and counties from adopting tenant protections (rent stabilization, just cause
eviction requirements, source-of-income discrimination bans). This state preemption pattern is
directly documented in Domain 33 (State Legislative Autocratization) as a mechanism of local
democratic suppression. Domain 43 provides the housing-specific case study: state legislatures
controlled by rural and suburban homeowner majorities systematically preempt the housing policies
that urban tenant majorities have voted for through local democratic processes. The democratic design
failure is not merely bad policy — it is a structural bypass of local democratic outcomes by state
majorities that do not bear the costs of the policies they prohibit.

### Democratic Angle

Domain 47 analyzes housing instability as a mechanism of political exclusion: eviction, homelessness,
and displacement remove people from the voter rolls, break civic networks, and deplete the cognitive
and material resources needed for political participation. Domain 43 analyzes the upstream layer:
the spatial design of the housing system itself as a democratic architecture problem.

The specific democratic mechanisms that Domain 43 addresses that Domain 47 does not:

**Spatial segregation of political constituencies**: Exclusionary zoning functions as a democratic
gerrymandering tool operating through the private market rather than legislative line-drawing.
By concentrating low-income, high-barrier-to-participation constituencies in specific geographic
zones, it ensures those constituencies' political power is concentrated in the areas with the
fewest political resources and most diffuse geographic representation in single-member district
systems.

**Neighborhood political infrastructure as democratic commons**: African American political power
in urban areas — civil rights organizations, church networks, HBCU alumni associations, ward
political clubs — is a political infrastructure built over generations. Gentrification's displacement
of these communities is a destruction of political commons, not merely a market transaction. The
democratic design argument is that this political infrastructure deserves the same protection
analysis as other democratic infrastructure (voting rights, redistricting, civil society).

**Local democratic self-governance vs. state preemption**: When tenants in a city vote for rent
stabilization through a ballot initiative, and the state legislature preempts it, this is not a
policy dispute — it is a structural bypass of local democratic outcomes. Domain 43 provides the
housing policy case study for the state preemption suppression mechanism that Domain 33 documents
at the structural level.

### Reform Architecture

**Reform 1: Zoning for democracy.** Federal or state "pro-housing" requirements — modeled on
California's Housing Element law — that require municipalities to zone sufficient land for housing
affordability as a condition of receiving federal transportation, infrastructure, and community
development funds. This is not a federal takeover of local zoning; it is a conditionality framework
that aligns infrastructure investment with democratic access. The Biden administration's pilot
program in this direction (HUD competitive grants for zoning reform) should be codified as a
statutory framework.

**Reform 2: Restoration of Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing (AFFH).** The AFFH rule, rescinded
in January 2025, required communities receiving HUD funds to analyze and address residential
segregation. Restoration of AFFH — with explicit requirements to assess the civic participation
effects of housing segregation, not merely the housing effects — would establish the democratic
design angle in federal housing policy for the first time. The AFFH framework should be extended
to require Civic Participation Impact Assessments as part of HUD funding reviews.

**Reform 3: Anti-displacement protections.** Federal preemption protection for local tenant
protections: municipalities that have enacted rent stabilization, just cause eviction, or source-of-
income discrimination protections through democratic processes should be protected from state
preemption laws that negate local democratic outcomes. This is the housing-specific version of the
preemption reversal recommended in Domain 33.

**Reform 4: Tenant organizing infrastructure funding.** The Community Services Block Grant program
should be expanded to explicitly fund tenant organizing capacity in severely cost-burdened
communities, on the model of the labor movement's acknowledgment that collective bargaining
requires organizing infrastructure. The Tenant Union Federation (2024) and state-level tenant
organizing networks represent existing infrastructure that federal capacity-building could scale.

**Reform 5: Civic Participation Impact assessments for large-scale displacement.** Any development
project receiving federal subsidy or approvals that will displace 50 or more households should be
required to conduct a Civic Participation Impact Assessment documenting projected effects on voter
registration, community organization networks, and neighborhood political representation — analogous
to the Environmental Impact Statement framework under NEPA. This creates a legal hook for organizing
against displacement projects and a documentation requirement that makes the democratic consequences
of displacement visible in the approval record.

**International Precedent**:
1. Vienna social housing model: 60 percent of residents in subsidized or public housing, preventing
   the spatial concentration of low-income populations and maintaining economically integrated
   neighborhoods with high civic participation rates across income levels.
2. Singapore public housing (HDB): 80 percent homeownership rate through public development,
   demographic integration requirements preventing ethnic enclave concentration.
3. Netherlands anti-gentrification protections: municipal preemption rights on housing sales to
   protect neighborhood composition in designated areas.
4. Montreal Community Land Trust model: permanent affordability through land trust structure
   preventing gentrification displacement.

## Recommendation: Priority Domain and Why

**Domain 42-Expansion (Techno-Monopolies and Platform Accountability) is the production priority.**

Three factors converge:

First, the Brinkema remedy decision in the Google ad tech antitrust case is expected in the window
between now and mid-2026. This is the first structural antitrust remedy that could actually break
apart a tech monopoly component in the United States. The opportunity to distribute research framing
platform accountability as democratic infrastructure protection — rather than economic competition
restoration — closes when the decision issues and the framing locks.

Second, the Nature 2026 study on X's algorithmic political effects and the FAccT 2025 audit paper
provide newly mature empirical foundations for the democratic design argument. These studies were
not available when Domain 48 was written; Domain 42-Expansion can integrate them without duplicating
Domain 48's surveillance architecture analysis.

Third, the DSA precedent is live and actionable: X's €120 million fine (December 2025) and the
Romanian election annulment based on TikTok algorithmic manipulation create a concrete international
precedent that U.S. advocacy organizations can point to for a U.S. legislative equivalent. The
advocacy window for pre-midterm digital ad transparency legislation and PATA-equivalent platform
accountability requirements is 2026.

Domain 41 follows closely because the CFPB enforcement rollback is still in the formal rulemaking
stage, preserving an advocacy window through the comment period. Domain 43 is the deepest and most
structurally important extension of the framework's spatial justice analysis, but its advocacy
window is less acute — making it appropriately third in sequencing.
