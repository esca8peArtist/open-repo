# Domain 38–40 Strategic Analysis

**Prepared**: May 5, 2026  
**Scope**: Deep-dive analysis of the top 2–3 candidates per domain position (Domains 38, 39, and 40) for Phase 2 execution. Each section addresses: policy urgency window, coalition alignment with Phase 1 contacts, research prerequisites, Phase 2 execution timeline (weeks to produce), and impact potential across institutional adoption, media reach, and litigation catalysis.

This analysis covers the Domain 38 runner-up candidates from Item 12 that remain in the Phase 2 queue — specifically the Intelligence Oversight and Voting Systems candidates — which have been re-scored in the prioritization matrix as the most urgent unresearched domains in the full Phase 2 set.

### 38-A: Intelligence Oversight and Surveillance Reform

**One-Line Frame**: The intelligence community's Section 702 foreign collection authority has been operating under a 45-day congressional extension since April 30, 2026, with a June 12 hard deadline — and the commercial data broker loophole through which ICE, DHS, and now the FBI track Americans without a warrant remains open regardless of the FISA outcome.

#### Policy Urgency Window

The FISA Section 702 situation as of May 2026: the House passed a three-year reauthorization (235–191 on April 29) that included an unrelated provision banning a Federal Reserve digital currency, killing its Senate prospects. The Senate then passed a 45-day extension by unanimous consent after a deal to declassify a recent FISC opinion on 702 usage. The new deadline is **June 12, 2026**.

Lawmakers are scheduled to return from recess in mid-May to begin Senate negotiations on a three-year extension. The core unresolved disputes are the same as in April: whether to require a warrant for FBI queries of 702-collected data on US persons, and whether to close the commercial data broker loophole. The bipartisan civil libertarian coalition (Freedom Caucus + progressive Democrats) that blocked prior reauthorizations has not dissolved — it has simply been outmaneuvered by procedural timetabling.

The **June 12 deadline is the domain's production window.** If the framework can distribute an Intelligence Oversight domain document to its Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee contacts before June 12, it positions itself at the precise moment when senators are deciding whether to include warrant requirements and data broker provisions in the three-year deal. Even if the legislation passes without those provisions, the domain document becomes the post-passage accountability frame for why the fight continues.

**Adjacent urgency**: FBI Director Kash Patel's restructuring of the Domestic Terrorism Operations Section, his firing of senior agents tied to prior Trump investigations, and the FBI's use of immigration protest investigations as a "funding networks" framing represent a parallel domestic surveillance crisis that overlaps with the Domain 41-A (Domestic Intelligence Accountability) candidate. Intelligence Oversight (38-A) focuses on the statutory architecture of foreign collection; Domestic Intelligence (41-A) focuses on the Attorney General Guidelines architecture of domestic investigation. Both flow from the same post-Church-Committee framework. Treating them as complementary documents — distributed in the same wave to the same civil liberties audience — maximizes coverage.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

The Phase 1 distribution network includes:
- **Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee contacts** (Tier 1): FISA reform is directly in their jurisdiction. The June 12 deadline gives them a specific legislative action frame. Senator Wyden has called the current reform proposals "fake" on warrant requirements; Senator Rand Paul has blocked prior extensions. The domain document supports both.
- **ACLU, EFF, Brennan Center** (Tier 2 civil liberties contacts): These organizations have active FISA litigation and advocacy programs. The domain document would integrate their existing analysis into the framework's democratic-design frame.
- **Law school national security clinics** (Tier 2): Harvard, Yale, and Georgetown national security law programs assign FISA scholarship and have clinic clients with active NSL and 702-related cases.

**Cross-partisan element**: The Cato Institute, Reason Foundation, and libertarian-aligned Republican Senate offices (Paul, Lee) are natural secondary distribution nodes for Intelligence Oversight in a way that few other domains reach. This expands the Phase 1 network to the right in a politically safe way (civil liberties is a cross-partisan frame).

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Primary sources already identified and accessible**: Church Committee reports (public domain), Attorney General Guidelines (DOJ public documents), FISA statute text, Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act bill text, Brennan Center 702 resource page, EFF FISA analysis, ACLU FOIA litigation records, the declassified FISC opinion (now due under the Senate deal).
- **Key source gap**: The exact text of the FISC opinion to be declassified under the April 30 Senate deal is not yet public. This is the one primary source worth waiting for — it may reveal Section 702 usage that strengthens the warrant-requirement argument.
- **No specialized access required**: All primary sources are publicly accessible. PACER access would enhance ACLU litigation citations but is not required.
- **Dependencies**: None. This domain is research-ready now.

#### Phase 2 Execution Timeline

- **Hours to produce**: 12–15 hours (primary source synthesis + constitutional analysis + comparative models)
- **Recommended production window**: May 15–22, 2026 (before June 12 deadline)
- **Distribution timing**: Distribute the week of May 25 — gives Tier 1 Senate contacts 2.5 weeks before the deadline to engage with the framework's analysis
- **Weeks from start to distribution**: 2–3 weeks

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: Senate Judiciary and Intelligence Committee staff are the highest-priority adoption target. A well-framed domain document that synthesizes the warrant-requirement debate with the commercial data broker loophole and the FBI domestic investigation crisis gives Senate staff a single-document briefing source they currently lack.
- **Media reach**: The June 12 deadline creates a natural media hook. The Brennan Center, EFF, and Lawfare blog will all be running FISA coverage through June 12 — a domain document that speaks to the democratic-design problem (not just the technical legal question) can surface through their distribution networks.
- **Litigation catalysis**: The ACLU's active FOIA litigation for fusion center surveillance records and the EFF's commercial data broker challenge are the primary litigation vectors. The domain document can directly support both by providing the structural democratic-design argument that briefs need.

### 38-B: Voting Systems and Election Administration Reform

**One-Line Frame**: The Callais ruling (April 29, 2026) eliminated the discriminatory-effects standard under VRA Section 2, requiring plaintiffs to prove discriminatory intent — and states are already planning to dismantle majority-minority districts before November 3, 2026.

#### Policy Urgency Window

The urgency structure has three interlocking components:

**Component 1 — The Callais aftermath (immediate, ongoing)**: The 6-3 decision in *Louisiana v. Callais* (written by Alito, joined by Roberts, Thomas, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett) eliminates the Gingles test for VRA Section 2 challenges to redistricting and restores a discriminatory-intent requirement. The ACLU described it as "the worst Voting Rights Act decision in a century." The Campaign Legal Center's response noted that "legislatures in several southern states have already begun planning to dismantle districts that have protected voters of color from racial voting discrimination for generations." Louisiana Governor Landry issued an executive order purporting to halt state House primaries pending redrawn maps. The ruling is on immediate effect per a May 2026 SCOTUS order.

**Component 2 — John Lewis VRA (Senate, pre-midterm)**: The John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act (S.2523 / H.R.14, 119th Congress) was reintroduced July 2025. Following Callais, the Congressional Black Caucus demanded an immediate floor vote. The bill needs 60 Senate votes and does not have them. However, the Callais ruling transforms this from a generic advocacy ask to a specific post-ruling remediation bill — which changes the political framing in ways that could move 1–2 Republican senators, particularly those in states with significant minority voter constituencies.

**Component 3 — November 3, 2026 (hard deadline)**: The midterm election is the structural constraint that makes voting systems the most time-locked domain in the Phase 2 set. For domain research to be actionable for election protection organizations (Protect Democracy, Brennan Center's Elections Program, Democracy Docket, voting rights law clinics), it must arrive in their hands by **July 2026 at the latest** — that gives them 4 months to incorporate the framework's analysis into their pre-election advocacy, litigation filings, and coalition communications.

**CISA compounding factor**: CISA's election security workforce has been systematically gutted (documented in the phase-2-expansion-candidates.md Candidate E section). The March 2026 executive order on elections, which directs DHS and SSA to compile a nationwide State Citizenship List and requires USPS to deliver mail ballots only to voters on that list, has been partially enjoined by a D.C. district court but is still in litigation. The operational election security vacuum left by CISA's gutting means states that previously depended on federal technical assistance for cybersecurity of election infrastructure are now on their own.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

The Phase 1 Tier 1 distribution network's most election-relevant contacts:
- **Brennan Center Elections Program**: Direct contact; the Brennan Center's State Voting Laws tracker and Callais response are the best existing research. A domain document would integrate their work into the framework's constitutional-design frame.
- **NAACP LDF, ACLU Voting Rights Project**: Active Callais litigation; the domain document directly supports their remediation argument.
- **State AG offices** (Georgia, North Carolina, Wisconsin AGs who have existing voting rights litigation): These offices need a constitutional design document, not just technical legal briefings.
- **Law school election law clinics** (Harvard, Yale, NYU, University of Michigan): Election law is the most direct academic pipeline; these clinics will assign the domain document immediately.
- **Congressional Black Caucus** (institutional outreach target not yet in Tier 1 list): The Callais ruling creates a specific outreach opportunity. The framework's analysis would be immediately useful to CBC members drafting VRA restoration messaging.

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Primary sources**: Callais opinion (April 29, 2026 — SCOTUS website); John Lewis VRA bill text (Congress.gov H.R.14 and S.2523); Brennan Center State Voting Laws tracker (updated annually); Trump March 2026 election EO text; CISA organizational chart (before and after gutting); D.C. district court partial injunction order.
- **Key analytical challenge**: The Callais opinion is 68 pages and requires careful reading to accurately characterize the intent-vs-effects doctrinal shift. This is the domain's primary research complexity — not source access but doctrinal analysis.
- **No specialized access required**: All primary sources are publicly accessible.
- **Dependencies**: None. The April 29 Callais ruling has resolved the primary factual trigger.

#### Phase 2 Execution Timeline

- **Hours to produce**: 14–18 hours (Callais doctrinal analysis + VRA legislative history + CISA operational security + international election administration comparisons)
- **Recommended production window**: May 20 – June 10, 2026
- **Distribution timing**: Distribute to election protection organizations in early-to-mid June 2026, before the August congressional recess
- **Weeks from start to distribution**: 3–4 weeks

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: The Callais ruling has created immediate demand from voting rights organizations for analysis that goes beyond the purely legal — that connects the doctrinal shift to the structural democratic design problem. The framework's comparative angle (international election administration models, CISA independence, federal floor standards) is exactly what advocacy organizations need but do not currently have from their litigation-focused internal research.
- **Media reach**: Callais coverage will continue through summer 2026 as states implement and challenges mount. A domain document released in June 2026 can be placed as a guest post in Slate, Vox, or Talking Points Memo's legal coverage, or cited in Votebeat (which covers election administration with the best granularity).
- **Litigation catalysis**: This is the highest litigation-vector domain in the Phase 2 set. Every new redistricting challenge filed post-Callais will need analysis of what remedies remain available under the new intent standard. A framework document that maps the residual enforcement pathways (state VRAs, Section 2 non-redistricting applications, APA challenges to election executive orders) directly serves active litigation strategy.

### 39-A: Reproductive Rights and Democratic Authority

**One-Line Frame**: Post-Dobbs state law cascades in 21 states have reduced women's political equality by creating time, resource, and bodily autonomy constraints on civic participation — and the 2026 ballot measure cycle in Missouri and Nevada represents the most live vehicle for reversing that cascade.

#### Policy Urgency Window

The urgency structure is ballot-initiative-anchored rather than legislative or judicial. The most active 2026 windows:

**Ballot measures (certified for 2026)**: Missouri and Nevada both have abortion-related measures certified for November 2026 ballot. Missouri's measure would restore abortion rights at the state constitutional level (reversing the state's complete ban). Nevada's measure would codify abortion access in the state constitution. These ballot initiative campaigns need the democratic-authority framing (reproductive autonomy as a precondition for women's political equality) rather than only the health-care framing.

**Ongoing state-level litigation**: *Fischer v. South Carolina* (reproductive rights under state ERA) is the current leading state constitutional case. Several state supreme courts (Arizona, Florida, Kansas) are hearing challenges to newly enacted restrictions. The framework's constitutional-design analysis directly serves state constitutional litigation.

**SCOTUS watch**: The current SCOTUS term has no reproductive rights case at the merit-review stage, but *Skrmetti* (June 2025 — narrowing equal protection for healthcare) creates a precedential risk that abortion restrictions will receive only rationality-basis review going forward. The domain document should integrate the *Skrmetti* trajectory.

**HHS Medicaid work requirement guidance** (June 1, 2026): The OBBBA work requirement interim final rule is due June 1 and is explicitly exempted from notice-and-comment. The intersection of reproductive rights and Medicaid access (Domain 31/11b covers the broader Medicaid crisis) means this domain document needs a cross-reference to phase-2-domain-candidate-1-medicaid-crisis.md for the Medicaid-reproductive-healthcare intersection.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

The reproductive rights coalition maps directly onto the framework's most active Phase 1 distribution targets:
- **Planned Parenthood** (national and state AGs aligned with PP): Have active state AG coalition against OBBBA work requirements and Medicaid abortion funding restrictions
- **Center for Reproductive Rights, ACLU Reproductive Freedom Project**: Direct research and litigation contacts
- **Women's law journals** (Harvard Journal of Law and Gender, Yale Law Journal, Columbia Gender and Law): Domain document would be assigned as supplementary reading in reproductive rights law courses
- **State legislators in ballot-initiative target states** (Missouri, Nevada, Arizona): Coalition needs the democratic-authority frame to broaden the ballot-initiative campaign beyond the health-care frame

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Hours to produce**: 14–18 hours
- **Key dependency**: The OBBBA HHS work requirement rule (June 1, 2026) should inform the Medicaid-reproductive intersection section. Research should begin immediately but the Medicaid cross-reference section should be finalized after June 1.
- **Production window**: June 5–25, 2026

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: The ballot initiative campaigns in Missouri and Nevada are the direct adoption target. A domain document that frames abortion rights as a democratic participation issue (not only a health care issue) gives campaign organizers a new persuasion argument for coalition expansion.
- **Litigation catalysis**: State constitutional challenges to abortion bans have produced the most active reproductive rights litigation docket since Dobbs. The domain document's state-constitution analysis supports state AG and clinic strategies.
- **Media gap**: Dobbs coverage is extensive but predominantly focuses on healthcare impact rather than the political-equality and democratic-participation dimensions. The domain's media gap score reflects this.

### 39-B: Labor Rights and Democratic Participation

**One-Line Frame**: Union density has fallen to 10.1% — its lowest since 1948 — but active organizing campaigns at Case New Holland (UAW, May 2026 contract expiration), healthcare workers, and dockworkers represent the most active organizing moment in two decades; the framework's labor-democracy synthesis fills a gap that labor economists document but no democratic-design document addresses.

#### Policy Urgency Window

The 2026 contract battle calendar is the organizing hook:
- **Case New Holland (UAW, May 2026)**: Contract for 1,000 UAW members building agricultural machines in Iowa and Wisconsin expires May 2026. UAW is demanding the company commit to keeping the Iowa factory open — a manufacturing democracy and rural civic infrastructure question that maps onto Domain 39-B's core argument.
- **DHL drivers (Teamsters Local, March 2026)**: 3,000 Teamsters at DHL with contract up March 31, 2026.
- **First Student drivers (Teamsters, March 2026)**: 17,000 first student drivers and support staff contracts expired.

The Davis-Bacon Act repeal threat (proposed in the OBBBA; ultimately not enacted at full repeal but reduced prevailing wage requirements for certain federal contracts) directly affects construction union density and would reduce union strength in the sector where density remains highest (11–13%).

The NLRB composition is the structural urgency: Trump's April 2025 NLRB appointments reversed Biden-era pro-union decisions and reinstalled labor-hostile interpretation of protected organizing activity. Any organizing campaign that relies on NLRB certification processes faces a structurally hostile regulatory environment through 2028.

The **2026 ballot initiative window** in Arizona and Nevada targeting right-to-work repeal represents the most direct democratic vehicle for labor law reform. Both states' ballot initiative campaigns need the labor-democracy synthesis framing.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

- **AFL-CIO, SEIU, UAW** (direct Phase 1 distribution targets via labor coalition Tier 2 contacts): AFL-CIO's voter engagement program reaches 5M+ voters in swing states; the framework's labor-democracy connection directly supports their GOTV framing.
- **Economic Policy Institute**: Primary think-tank anchor for labor-democracy research; EPI will cite the domain document.
- **Labor law clinics** (Cornell ILR, Columbia Law School, Georgetown Law School Labor and Employment Law Program): Will adopt as course material; direct clinical-practice application.
- **State labor federations in right-to-work ballot states**: Arizona and Nevada state federations are direct distribution targets.

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Hours to produce**: 12–16 hours
- **No hard dependencies**: The NLRB appointment track record is documented; the UAW contract situation is current. The domain can be researched and produced immediately.
- **Production window**: June 25 – July 15, 2026 (after Reproductive Rights domain completes)

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: Labor unions are the largest organized member network of any social movement sector. AFL-CIO's voter contact capacity (5M+ voters in swing states) means this domain document, if adopted, has the widest potential electoral impact of any domain in the Phase 2 set.
- **Media gap**: The labor-democracy connection (unions as the organizational substrate for translating economic grievance into electoral power) is underreported in democratic-reform discourse. Economic coverage addresses wages; electoral coverage addresses turnout; neither synthesizes them in the way this domain does.
- **Litigation catalysis**: NLRB decision reversal cases (Biden-era decisions now being challenged or reversed by Trump appointees) are the most immediate litigation vectors. The domain document supports labor law clinics filing amicus briefs in those cases.

### 40-A: Constitutional Architecture and Amendment Process Reform

**One-Line Frame**: The Convention of States Project has passed applications in 20 of 34 required states, Michigan votes on the convention question in November 2026, and the Heritage-ALEC-Koch network has tens of millions committed to calling a convention that could rewrite the Bill of Rights — while the progressive case for Article V (campaign finance amendment, SCOTUS term limits) requires exactly the same mechanism.

#### Policy Urgency Window

Three 2026 trigger events:

**Michigan November 2026 ballot decision**: Michigan voters will decide in November 2026 whether to rescind all prior Michigan Article V applications. If the rescission fails (meaning Michigan does not rescind), the momentum for reaching 34 states accelerates. If it succeeds, the coalition demonstrably stops the convention count at the state ballot box — providing the proof-of-concept that state-level rescission campaigns can work.

**H.Con.Res.15 (119th Congress)**: The one-sentence bill calling for an Article V Convention for a "Fiscal Responsibility Amendment" is in committee in 2026. It is the template for how the convention could be called if Republicans maintain the House majority after November 2026.

**Current application count uncertainty**: 20 states have active Convention of States Project applications as of early 2026; 17 states have rescinded all prior applications. The legal dispute about whether applications from different periods can be aggregated means the true count toward 34 is contested — but that ambiguity is itself a crisis because the administration and its allies have every incentive to declare the threshold reached when politically convenient.

**Why Constitutional Architecture scores lower despite high strategic importance**: The implementation score (3 — requires constitutional amendment to enact the framework's most ambitious reforms) and reversibility score (2 — constitutional damage from a runaway convention cannot be undone without generational effort) drag the composite score significantly despite high urgency and coalition scores. The domain is critically important for the framework's theory of change but the near-term advocacy window is less acute than the voting rights or FISA domains.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

- **Common Cause, Brennan Center**: Leading the 700+ organization coalition opposing the convention
- **Law school constitutional law faculty** (Harvard/Lessig, Yale/Amar, Georgetown/Barnett): The academic split (Lessig supports a controlled convention; Amar supports it; Barnett supports it from the right; Tribe opposes it) makes this the most intellectually rich academic distribution target in Phase 2
- **AFL-CIO, NEA**: Both formally oppose the convention; both are in the framework's Tier 2 labor distribution network
- **Conservative constitutionalists** (Federalist Society-adjacent scholars who oppose a runaway convention risk): The bipartisan defensive coalition is unusually broad; this domain reaches audience segments no other domain reaches

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Hours to produce**: 12–16 hours (constitutional law complexity)
- **Key dependency**: The Michigan November 2026 ballot decision creates a post-midterm research trigger. The domain document can be produced before November 2026 and updated after.
- **Production window**: September–October 2026 (before Michigan vote to frame the stakes; update after vote for implications)

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: This is the highest-leverage domain for law school clinics and academic networks — it fills the constitutional implementation gap that sophisticated readers of the 40-domain framework will identify. Every proposal that requires a constitutional amendment (SCOTUS term limits, Senate structural reform, rights floors) flows through this analysis.
- **Media reach**: The Michigan ballot decision creates a November 2026 news hook. The domain document would be directly citable in coverage of the state vote.
- **Litigation catalysis**: The rescission-validity legal dispute is the most immediate litigation angle — the question of whether state rescissions are valid Article V actions is ripe for litigation if the convention is called with states contesting their application count.

### 40-B: Tribal Sovereignty and Indigenous Democratic Design

**One-Line Frame**: The Trump administration's FY2026 proposed $911 million (24%) cut to core tribal programs defunds governments — not civil rights beneficiaries — and the *Trump v. Barbara* SCOTUS ruling on birthright citizenship and tribal membership (expected June/July 2026) will either confirm or dramatically destabilize the Indian Citizenship Act of 1924 as constitutional.

#### Policy Urgency Window

Two interlocking hard deadlines:

***Trump v. Barbara* SCOTUS ruling (expected June/July 2026)**: The Supreme Court heard oral arguments April 1, 2026 on whether the Trump administration's DOJ argument that "children of tribal Indians are not birthright citizens" satisfies the 14th Amendment. Chief Justice Roberts described the government's argument as "quirky" — the Court appeared to reject the administration's position, but no ruling has issued. When it does, the ruling either confirms a century of settled law or creates a constitutional crisis for Indigenous citizenship. Either outcome activates the domain: confirmation validates the sovereignty framework; a narrow or ambiguous holding requires immediate legal analysis.

**FY2026 tribal program appropriations (fall 2026 congressional window)**: The presidential budget proposes $617 million in BIA self-governance program cuts and $107 million in BIA Public Safety and Justice program cuts. The Congressional Appropriations Committees have proposed higher funding levels than the presidential budget — meaning the fall 2026 appropriations cycle is the advocacy window to protect those higher levels. A domain document that frames tribal funding as government-to-government treaty obligation (not discretionary racial preference spending) provides the constitutional argument that appropriations advocates need.

**BIA office closure schedule**: A Wisconsin BIA office serving all 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes closes August 2026. This is a documented, scheduled harm with a specific date — making it a news hook that is not available for other domains.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

The tribal sovereignty coalition has low overlap with the current Phase 1 distribution network — which is precisely why it scores high on coalition diversification value:
- **NCAI (National Congress of American Indians)**: 574-nation membership; sophisticated Washington advocacy presence; direct distribution node
- **NARF (Native American Rights Fund)**: Constitutional legal representation for tribal governments; active litigation docket; domain document directly serves NARF briefs
- **Federal Indian Law clinics** (Arizona State, University of Montana, University of New Mexico): Academic pipeline with direct client application
- **Conservative constitutionalists supporting tribal sovereignty** (fiscal federalists who support pre-constitutional governmental authority over federal program discretion): This is a genuinely unusual cross-ideological coalition opportunity

**Phase 1 contact expansion**: The tribal sovereignty distribution round would explicitly target contacts not already in the Phase 1 network — specifically tribal governance journalists (Native News Online, Indian Country Today), tribal college presidents (AIHEC), and state-level tribal liaisons in 23 states with significant Indigenous populations.

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Hours to produce**: 10–14 hours
- **Key dependency**: The *Trump v. Barbara* ruling should be read before finalizing the constitutional analysis section. Research should begin now (producing Sections 1–2 and 4–7) and the SCOTUS analysis section should be completed after the ruling issues (June/July).
- **Production window**: Begin now; finalize and distribute within 2 weeks of the ruling

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: This domain fills the most complete gap in the Phase 1 network. Unlike other Phase 2 domains that deepen existing relationships, Tribal Sovereignty creates an entirely new distribution pathway to tribal governance stakeholders — a constituency not yet reached.
- **Media reach**: The *Trump v. Barbara* ruling is a significant SCOTUS case with national media coverage. Native News Online, the AP Native affairs desk, and NPR's indigenous-affairs coverage are natural placement venues. The framework's constitutional-design contribution is a different angle from pure legal coverage.
- **Litigation catalysis**: NARF's litigation docket (the Tribal Supreme Court Project) is directly served by a domain document that provides the democratic-design argument behind treaty-rights litigation. The appropriations advocacy angle provides a direct path into the 2026–2027 budget fight.

### 40-C: Congressional Fiscal Authority Restoration

**One-Line Frame**: The Trump administration has explicitly announced its intention to treat the Impoundment Control Act as a countdown-to-expiration mechanism rather than a compliance obligation — and H.R.1180 to repeal the ICA entirely is in committee in the 119th Congress.

#### Policy Urgency Window

Unlike the other Domain 40 candidates, Fiscal Authority does not have a single hard-deadline trigger — it is a persistent, ongoing crisis that is continuously worsening. The urgency frame is therefore programmatic rather than calendar-locked:

The DOGE-directed fund cancellations, HHS Medicaid reprogramming, and DEO budget actions are all live impoundment violations occurring on a rolling basis. The FY2027 appropriations cycle (fall 2026) is the most proximate advocacy window: if Congress builds ICA anti-impoundment provisions into FY2027 appropriations (as it did in FY2026), that represents a legislative holding action. The domain document provides the constitutional argument that appropriators need.

**Why this ranks sixth overall despite its strategic importance**: Fiscal Authority is the implementation infrastructure for all other domains — but this is also what makes it lower-urgency as a standalone research priority. It is not a domain that will generate coalition mobilization or voter outrage on its own; it is a document that policy lawyers, appropriations committee staff, and think-tank fiscal analysts will use as a reference. Those audiences adopt the framework through research quality, not crisis urgency. Fiscal Authority should therefore be produced quickly (its 8–10 hour research time is the lowest of all Tier 2 candidates) and distributed broadly as part of the think-tank and policy-organization outreach wave.

#### Coalition Alignment with Phase 1 Contacts

- **CBPP, Brookings, Bipartisan Policy Center, Taxpayers for Common Sense**: These organizations have produced the most current analysis of the impoundment crisis; the domain document integrates their work into the democratic-design frame
- **Appropriations Committee staff** (both chambers): The framework's most technically credible audience for this domain; appropriations staff need the constitutional law argument that their own documents often understate
- **Administrative law programs** (Cornell, Georgetown, GWU Administrative Law): Domain document will be cited in agency authority scholarship
- **Libertarian-right fiscal hawks** (Cato Institute, Heritage budget scholars who support congressional power of the purse against executive impoundment): Bipartisan framing is achievable on this domain

#### Research Prerequisites and Dependencies

- **Hours to produce**: 8–10 hours (lowest of all Tier 2 candidates)
- **No hard dependencies**: All primary sources (ICA text, CRS R48432, GAO enforcement, CBPP analysis, Brookings 50th anniversary analysis, H.R.1180 text) are accessible now.
- **Production window**: Can be produced in parallel with any Tier 1 domain; recommend completing it in July 2026 as a second-wave document that ships with the Reproductive Rights and Labor Rights domains

#### Impact Potential

- **Institutional adoption**: Lower grassroots adoption potential but highest adoption potential among the target audience (policy lawyers, appropriations staff, think-tank analysts). This domain will be cited in law review notes, CRS reports, and congressional testimony more than any other Phase 2 domain.
- **Media reach**: The DOGE fund cancellations and impoundment violations are regularly covered by Politico, The Hill, and Roll Call. A domain document that provides the constitutional architecture for why what DOGE is doing is illegal creates a shareable expert-quotation source for congressional reporters.
- **Litigation catalysis**: The Train v. City of New York precedent and the Appropriations Clause Project's scholarship create a litigation architecture that is underutilized by Democratic AGs and civil rights organizations. A domain document that explains the private-right-of-action pathways for Appropriations Clause enforcement could catalyze new litigation not currently being considered.
