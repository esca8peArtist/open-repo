# Phase 2 Domain Prioritization Matrix

**Prepared**: May 5, 2026  
**Purpose**: Systematic scoring of all Phase 2 domain candidates to support post-Phase-1-launch sequencing decisions. Phase 1 (35-domain framework) approved for launch as of Session 662. This document is independent of the Phase 1 path decision and can be implemented immediately post-launch.

---

## Part I: Scoring Framework

### The Eight Criteria

Phase 2 domain selection requires balancing urgency, feasibility, audience impact, and strategic leverage. Eight criteria are scored 0–10 for each candidate, with weighting factors reflecting the framework's primary distribution goals: institutional adoption, litigation catalysis, and midterm-cycle impact.

---

**Criterion 1: Urgency Score (weight: 1.5x)**  
*Does the policy window close within 12–24 months? Is there an active crisis, a hard external deadline, or an irreversible implementation timeline underway?*

- 9–10: Crisis actively in progress; external deadline within 6 months; irreversible if unaddressed  
- 7–8: Active policy window with 6–18 month horizon; high probability of permanent closure  
- 5–6: Important but the window is several years; action is valuable but not time-critical  
- 3–4: Long-cycle issue with no discrete trigger in the 2026–2028 window  
- 0–2: Background structural issue; no current crisis  

**Criterion 2: Coalition Strength (weight: 1.3x)**  
*Are there existing institutional partners ready to adopt and distribute the research? Is there cross-partisan potential? How many organizations are already organized on this issue?*

- 9–10: Institutionally dense coalition with cross-partisan reach; 50+ organized partners; immediate distribution node  
- 7–8: Established single-sector coalition with adjacent-sector potential; 20–50 partners  
- 5–6: Emerging coalition; primarily one ideological direction; 10–20 partners  
- 3–4: Fragmented coalition; primarily activist base; limited institutional reach  
- 0–2: Isolated issue; no organized institutional infrastructure  

**Criterion 3: Research Complexity (weight: 0.8x, higher score = more feasible)**  
*How many research-hours are required to produce a production-ready domain document? Are primary sources accessible? Is technical depth barrier high?*

- 9–10: 8–10 hours; primary sources organized and accessible; no specialized access required  
- 7–8: 10–14 hours; standard legal/policy sources; moderate synthesis required  
- 5–6: 14–18 hours; specialized legal terrain or technical literature; higher variance  
- 3–4: 18–24 hours; requires specialized databases or expert access  
- 0–2: 24+ hours; interdisciplinary synthesis across multiple specialized fields  

**Criterion 4: Policy Window Months (weight: 1.4x)**  
*When does the most important legislative, judicial, or executive action window close? Expressed as a calendar constraint rather than abstract urgency.*

- 9–10: Window closes within 1–3 months  
- 7–8: Window closes within 4–6 months (2026 midterm pre-election period)  
- 5–6: Window closes within 7–12 months  
- 3–4: Window closes within 13–24 months  
- 0–2: Window is 2+ years or indefinite  

**Criterion 5: Litigation Vector Strength (weight: 1.2x)**  
*Are courts already engaged on this issue? Does a well-timed domain document feed directly into active litigation or near-term filing decisions?*

- 9–10: Active cases at circuit level or SCOTUS; domain document directly supports briefing or amicus argument  
- 7–8: Active district court litigation; domain document supports litigation strategy development  
- 5–6: Pre-litigation stage; domain document would support standing or claim development  
- 3–4: Litigation pathway exists but no active cases; would take 2+ years to reach significance  
- 0–2: Non-justiciable or primarily legislative issue  

**Criterion 6: Media Narrative Gap (weight: 1.0x)**  
*Is this topic underreported relative to its democratic importance? Does a domain document fill a gap in the public discourse that no existing institution fills?*

- 9–10: Critical gap; no other institution produces the integrative democratic-design analysis; high placement potential  
- 7–8: Moderate gap; reporting exists but lacks the structural synthesis this domain would provide  
- 5–6: Competitive landscape; major publications covering the topic with adequate depth  
- 3–4: Oversaturated topic; domain document would add marginal narrative value  
- 0–2: Well-documented by multiple major sources; no narrative gap  

**Criterion 7: Implementation Difficulty (weight: 0.9x, higher score = easier to implement)**  
*How hard is it to actually execute the reform proposals? Does this domain have implementable pathways within the 2026–2030 window?*

- 9–10: Executive action or administrative reform sufficient; no legislation required  
- 7–8: State-level legislation achievable; federal pathway requires simple majority  
- 5–6: Federal legislation requiring 60 Senate votes; achievable with 2026 midterm gains  
- 3–4: Constitutional amendment required or structural change with 10+ year timeline  
- 0–2: Requires fundamental restructuring with no near-term pathway  

**Criterion 8: Reversibility (weight: 1.1x)**  
*Can the harms being documented be reversed? Is this a single-party reversal issue or does it require bipartisan coalition? Is the policy damage permanent?*

- 9–10: Highly reversible through executive action with next administration; policy harm is correctable  
- 7–8: Reversible through federal legislation with simple majority; some permanent harm already done  
- 5–6: Reversible with sustained coalition; permanent harm is partial; bipartisan cooperation needed  
- 3–4: Significant permanent harm already locked in; reversal requires extraordinary political conditions  
- 0–2: Structural/constitutional damage that cannot be undone without generational effort

## Part V: Composite Scoring Logic and Weighting Rationale

### Why These Weights?

The weighting scheme reflects the specific constraints and goals of post-Phase-1 distribution. Three factors drive the higher-weight criteria:

**Policy Window Months (1.4x) and Urgency (1.5x) carry the highest combined weight** because Phase 2 research that arrives after its policy window has closed has zero institutional leverage. The framework's primary value is not retrospective analysis — it is prospective positioning that reaches decision-makers before the decision is made. A domain that scores 9 on both Urgency and Policy Window represents a candidate where delay measurably reduces impact. Every week of delay on a June 12 FISA deadline or a November 3 midterm deadline is a week of lost institutional positioning.

**Coalition Strength (1.3x) is the second-highest category** because institutional adoption — the framework's Phase 2 success metric — is primarily determined by whether existing organizations can use the domain document in their ongoing work. A domain that scores high on urgency but has no pre-existing distribution infrastructure requires the project to build a new audience from scratch; that is not Phase 2 work, it is Phase 3. A domain that lands in a coalition's hands when they are already organized on the issue gets adopted immediately.

**Reversibility (1.1x) and Litigation Vector (1.2x) are elevated** because the framework is explicitly oriented toward democratic recovery, not only documentation. Domains where the harm is already permanent (e.g., Dobbs — the constitutional right to abortion is gone; it cannot be restored by a single-party legislative majority) score lower on Reversibility even if the urgency is high. Litigation-strong domains provide the framework with an immediate downstream use case: an amicus brief, a litigation strategy document, a clinic client intake packet. This direct-use pathway elevates domains that are already in active legal dispute.

**Research Complexity (0.8x) and Implementation Difficulty (0.9x) are downweighted** because they represent execution constraints, not strategic importance. A difficult-to-research domain that scores 9 on urgency and coalition should still be prioritized; the research complexity score simply informs resource allocation and timeline planning, not the ranking itself.

### Worked Example: Why 41-B (Disability Rights) Ranks First

Disability Rights scores 8 across four of the eight criteria and 7–9 across the others, producing no single standout criterion but the most consistent high-performance profile in the matrix. The urgency is high (SAVE Act Senate, SSA collapse) but not as acute as the June 12 FISA deadline. The coalition is strong but not as institutionally dense as voting rights. What pushes it to rank 1 is the combination of:

1. **Highest reversibility score (7)**: SSA staffing restoration is an appropriations item (FY2027); it can be done through budget legislation. ADA enforcement can be restored through executive action. These are not constitutional amendments — they are decisions that a reformed administration or a changed appropriations majority can make quickly.

2. **Highest research feasibility (8)**: At 10–14 hours, this domain can be produced faster than any Tier 1 competitor. In a constrained-resource Phase 2, the fastest domain to complete is the first one distributed — and early distribution captures coalition adoption momentum.

3. **Highest media narrative gap (9)**: No institution currently produces the four-layer exclusion architecture synthesis (polling barriers + ID requirements + NVRA/SSA + SSI disincentives). The domain is genuinely novel to the research landscape, not merely good synthesis.

4. **Specific NVRA-SSA litigation theory**: The office-closure NVRA enforcement angle identified in Item 44 is an underlitigated theory that creates a direct litigation vector — state P&As or civil rights organizations could file enforcement actions based on the domain document.

### Worked Example: Why 38-B (Voting Systems) Ranks Second

The Callais ruling (April 29, 2026 — 6-3, written by Alito, eliminates discriminatory-effects standard under VRA Section 2) created a hard legal development that is simultaneously the most acute democratic crisis and the most time-locked window in the matrix. States in the South are already planning to dismantle majority-minority districts. The November 3, 2026 midterm is 182 days away. For Domain 38-B to be actionable for election protection organizations before November 2026, it must be completed, distributed, and adopted by July 2026 at the latest. This is not a preference — it is a calendar constraint. The 2026 midterm result determines whether a Democratic House majority in 2027 can pass the John Lewis VRA; the urgency therefore compounds across elections.

The litigation vector score (9) reflects that multiple active circuit-level cases are live: the Callais implementation is already in district court in Louisiana; multiple states are now open to challenge under the new intent standard; the Campaign Legal Center, NAACP LDF, and ACLU all have cases pending that a domain document directly serves.

## Part VI: Cross-Domain Connections Affecting Phase 2 Priority

Several scoring interactions are not fully captured in the independent scores:

**38-B (Voting Systems) + 41-B (Disability Rights) are reinforcing, not competing.** The Callais ruling and the SAVE Act both operate on the same population — low-income voters, communities of color, rural voters — and the disability-specific disenfranchisement architecture and the racial disenfranchisement architecture overlap in geography and community. A Phase 2 that includes both domains in the same distribution wave creates a more complete voting rights narrative than either domain alone.

**38-A (Intel Oversight) + 41-A (Domestic Intelligence) are partially overlapping.** The June 12 FISA deadline (38-A) concerns foreign intelligence collection. The Patel FBI restructuring (41-A) concerns domestic intelligence. These are distinct legal authorities (FISA vs. Attorney General Guidelines vs. Fusion Center enabling statutes) but share the post-Church-Committee reform architecture as a unifying frame. A combined or cross-referenced treatment would serve the distribution audience more effectively than two entirely separate documents.

**40-B (Tribal Sovereignty) has the June/July 2026 SCOTUS decision as a hard trigger.** When *Trump v. Barbara* issues, the tribal sovereignty domain becomes immediately time-activated. This means Tribal Sovereignty should be research-ready before the ruling so it can be distributed immediately when the case resolves. The pre-ruling research period (now through June) is the production window.

Based on composite scores, policy window calendars, and cross-domain reinforcement effects, the following four-tier priority structure is recommended for Phase 2:

3. 38-A (Intel Oversight) — June 12 FISA deadline, live crisis
4. 40-B (Tribal Sovereignty) — Begin before *Trump v. Barbara* ruling issues

**Tier 2 — Research by August 2026:**
5. 41-A (Domestic Intelligence) — After June 12 FISA action is known; midterm-linked
6. 40-C (Fiscal Authority) — Fastest, lowest complexity; pairs with any distribution wave
7. 39-A (Reproductive Rights) — 2026 ballot measures in Missouri, Nevada need the frame
8. 39-B (Labor Rights) — Case New Holland UAW May 2026 expiration; 2026 contract battles

**Tier 3 — Research by December 2026 (post-midterm):**
9. 40-A (Constitutional Architecture) — Michigan Article V ballot decision November 2026
10. 41-C (Dark Money) — 2026 cycle complete, full data available
11. 38-D (Energy Infrastructure) — IRA clawback litigation resolved

12. 38-C (Property Rights) — Long-cycle, no 2026-2028 window
