# Post-Phase-1 Institutional Adaptation Patterns & Feedback Integration Roadmap

**May 6, 2026 — Version 1.0**

**Relationship to prior documents**: `feedback-integration-roadmap.md` (v2.0, April 30) provides the foundational feedback architecture, sector seeding sequence, and loop topology. `stakeholder-feedback-integration-protocol.md` (May 5) provides the Tier 1 async template and red flag interpretations. This document deepens both by: (1) documenting sector-specific adaptation patterns at the modification level — what each sector will actually change and why; (2) specifying the full feedback collection apparatus including passive monitoring, structured check-ins, and artifact analysis; (3) building the iterative refinement cycle with decision gates; (4) identifying domain-specific modification risk; and (5) establishing Phase 2 readiness signals grounded in historical adoption velocity precedent.

---

## Part 1: Expected Adaptation Patterns by Sector

The working premise: no institution adopts a policy framework wholesale. Selective extraction is the mechanism through which a research document becomes embedded in institutional practice. The question is not whether sectors will adapt the framework — they will — but whether those adaptations are structurally sound extensions or dilutive distortions. This section documents what each sector will change, what they will keep, and what adaptation patterns signal high-quality versus shallow engagement.

### 1.1 State Attorneys General

**Adoption mechanism**: Operational extraction into active litigation strategy. AGs do not formally adopt frameworks — they mine them for constitutional argument structures that can survive a preliminary injunction standard in federal court. The Model Penal Code's adoption history is instructive: after the ALI published in 1962, 36 states adopted revised criminal codes over the following 15–20 years (averaging roughly 2.4 states per year at peak), but no state adopted the MPC in its entirety. New Jersey and Oregon came closest; most states adopted the MPC's mental state hierarchy (purpose, knowledge, recklessness, negligence) while rejecting its more controversial provisions on obscenity and drug offenses. The pattern for the 35-domain framework will follow the same logic: extraction of analytically rigorous, legally citeable sections; rejection of comparative-law sections lacking U.S. domestic precedent.

**What state AGs will modify**:
- Strip all foreign-court citations (German Federal Constitutional Court, Hungarian Constitutional Court comparative analysis) — these are non-persuasive in U.S. federal circuits and create framing vulnerability
- Supplement Domain 33 and Domain 34 with state-specific constitutional text — each AG's office will add their state's elections clause language, anti-commandeering precedents, and state-specific standing arguments
- Convert the framework's analytical propositions into litigation-ready declarations — the framework argues analytically; AG briefs state conclusions as legal facts supported by the framework's evidence

**What signals high-quality AG adoption**:
- Domain analysis cited in a supplemental brief (structural integration, not surface acknowledgment)
- Framework's evidence used in a coalition memo shared across DAGA member offices (propagation signal)
- AG policy counsel requests domain-specific deeper research before a filing deadline (operational dependency signal)

**What signals shallow or misaligned AG adoption**:
- A press release uses framework language but the underlying brief does not reference domain analysis
- AG staff cites the executive summary rather than the domain-specific section (awareness, not integration)
- Framework is forwarded to a legislative affairs division rather than the litigation division (wrong channel — framework is not a legislative tool for AG offices)

**Domain relevance by AG function**: Domains 33, 34, 37, 1, and 6 are highest-value for litigation. Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization defense) and Domain 16 (immigration enforcement authority) are highest-value for defensive responses to federal overreach. Domains with primarily legislative application (2, 9, 10, 17) are lower-priority for AG adoption; do not interpret low AG pull on these domains as a content gap.

### 1.2 Law Schools

Law schools adopt frameworks through three distinct tracks operating on different timelines, each with different institutional implications.

**Clinic adoption (fastest, highest operational impact — 6–12 weeks)**:
Clinic directors integrate domain analysis as primary case material for active student work. The MacCrate Report (1992) and Carnegie Foundation's *Educating Lawyers* (2007) both pushed law schools toward experiential learning models that require real-world analytical frameworks as case inputs. The 35-domain framework arrives at a moment when Harvard Law's Democracy and Rule of Law Clinic, NYU's Brennan Center Clinic, and BU's Legislative Policy and Drafting Clinic are actively seeking materials on democracy-adjacent issues — including domain-specific live controversies (Domain 27's academic freedom analysis maps directly onto Harvard's $2.2B funding freeze case). When a clinic adopts a domain section as primary case material, the adaptation pattern is: simplify the analytical structure, focus on the most actionable doctrinal sections, create student-facing worksheets from the framework's evidence tables.

**Faculty adoption (medium speed, high legitimacy multiplier — 4–16 weeks, semester-dependent)**:
A professor incorporates a domain section into a seminar reading list or uses the framework's analytical structure as the basis for their own research project. Faculty adoption drives the most durable subsequent adoption signal — student scholarship. Law review notes and comments frequently originate in seminar assignments. The adaptation: faculty typically reframe the framework's advocacy-adjacent language into more neutral academic register, add doctrinal precision to policy claims, and cite the framework as "analytical scaffolding" rather than as a normative argument.

**Law review adoption (slowest, highest credibility signal — 12–24 months from submission to publication)**:
This is the lagging indicator, not the leading indicator. A law review article citing the framework as the analytical basis for a doctrinal argument is the highest external validation signal available, but it will not appear until well into Phase 2. The law review editing process also refines framework claims — editors will push for stronger citations, cleaner doctrinal categories, and removal of politically charged framing. The result is a published version that is analytically stronger than the original on its cited points but narrower in scope.

**Modification risk in law school context**: The academic editing process is constructive, not dilutive. The most significant modification risk in law schools is attribution drift — a law review article that uses the framework's analytical structure extensively in footnotes while attributing the conclusions to other sources, effectively incorporating the framework without crediting it. This is standard academic practice (scholars build on prior work without always explicitly crediting it as a framework). It is not a problem to solve — it is the mechanism of academic impact.

### 1.3 Think Tanks and Policy Organizations

Think tanks adopt frameworks by decomposing them into single-issue publications that fit their existing research agendas and funder priorities. The ALEC model legislation precedent is directly applicable: between 2010 and 2018, ALEC model legislation was introduced in state legislatures approximately 2,900 times, with over 600 bills enacted. ALEC's effectiveness depended not on holistic adoption of its legislative agenda but on making individual model bills easy to customize — each bill had fill-in-the-blank spaces for state-specific data. The 35-domain framework should expect the same pattern: think tanks extract individual domains, reformat them into their house style, add their own supplementary data, and publish them without necessarily crediting the original framework as the source.

**The attribution dilution dynamic**: When the Affordable Care Act was developed around the Heritage Foundation's market-based framework, the Heritage Foundation did not receive public credit for the structural architecture because the rebranding served both parties' political interests. The 35-domain framework should expect similar dynamics: Brennan Center extracts Domain 1's voter purge evidence and publishes it as a Brennan Center report; EPI extracts Domain 17's labor enforcement analysis and publishes it as an EPI brief. The framework's analytical categories become embedded in the policy conversation through those publications, regardless of attribution.

**Adoption quality signals**:
- Surface adoption: Think tank publication uses a term first coined in the framework (e.g., "appellate capture," "wave filter legislative sequence") — this is adoption of vocabulary
- Framework adoption: Publication applies the framework's cross-domain analytical structure (e.g., treating judicial independence and prosecutorial weaponization as linked, not separate) — this is adoption of method
- Strategic adoption: Think tank builds a multi-report research agenda around the framework's identified gaps — this is the highest-quality adoption signal and a direct Phase 2 indicator

**What signals misalignment vs. resonance**: A think tank that extracts a single domain and publishes it without the analytical connective tissue is partially adopting. A think tank that restructures its research agenda around the framework's domain map is deeply adopting. The latter is rare but consequential — it indicates the framework has become the organizing logic for their institutional work.

### 1.4 Journalists

Journalists use policy frameworks in a functionally distinct way from any institutional sector. Their adoption is non-linear and often untraceable through standard citation methods.

**Vocabulary adoption (fastest, hardest to track)**: Journalists internalize distinctive terminology from policy research and begin using it as shorthand in reporting without citing the source. When framework language like "shadow docket" (coined by law professor Steve Vladeck) entered general legal journalism, it spread through reporter-to-reporter vocabulary diffusion, not through citation chains. The same will happen with the framework's distinctive terms. This adoption pattern is positive for framework diffusion but creates attribution challenges.

**Domain-specific deepening**: When a journalist is covering a story in a domain the framework addresses (e.g., election security, prosecutorial weaponization), they may use the framework's domain analysis as background research that shapes which questions they ask and which sources they contact, without the framework appearing anywhere in the published story. This is the most common journalist adoption pattern. It generates downstream effects — sources cited in the framework become sources cited in journalism — without generating traceable attribution.

**Direct citation**: Rare, and typically occurs when (a) the framework has been cited by an institutional source the journalist is already tracking (e.g., a Brennan Center report that cites the framework leads a journalist to the framework directly), or (b) the journalist covers the framework itself as a news story.

**What to monitor**: Track distinctive framework phrases (not common political vocabulary) in Factiva, Google News, and ProPublica searches. If "wave filter legislative sequence" or "Domain 33 six-state simultaneous action" appears in a news story, that is a direct vocabulary adoption event. If a story's narrative structure mirrors the framework's domain analysis without using distinctive terms, that is likely indirect adoption.

### 1.5 Unions and Civil Society Organizations

Grassroots organizations adopt frameworks through horizontal extraction across multiple domains for a single current crisis, rather than vertical deepening into any single domain. The 1960s analogy from `feedback-integration-roadmap.md` applies: NAACP LDF, SCLC, SNCC, and CORE all shared the civil rights movement's underlying analytical framework but adapted it entirely differently based on institutional constraints. The 35-domain framework should expect:

**Training integration**: Organizations convert domain analysis into member education materials — two-page issue briefs, workshop outlines, talking points for member meetings. The adaptation involves radical simplification: the framework's nuanced analytical structures are compressed into direct action propositions ("Here is why this matters, here is what to do"). This is the appropriate adaptation for grassroots contexts; the analytical rigor is preserved at the organizational level even when it is not visible in member-facing materials.

**Direct action alignment**: Organizations identify which of their active campaigns align with framework domains and incorporate the domain's evidence and framing into campaign materials. This creates traceable adoption through organizational communications (newsletters, action alerts, press releases) that cite or reference specific framework domains.

**Coalition-level adoption**: The SPLC mutual defense pact across 100+ organizations creates a natural distribution network for Domain 29's mutual defense activation template. When one pact member activates using the framework's analysis, the pattern spreads within the network. Track SPLC pact activation events and compare to Domain 29's activation protocols.

## Part 2: Feedback Collection Mechanisms

### 2.1 Direct Channels

**Email reply monitoring**: Every Phase 1 outreach email generates a potential direct feedback channel. Substantive replies (questions about specific domains, requests for deeper analysis, routing confirmations) are the first-tier feedback signal. All substantive replies should be logged with: date, contact, organization, domain(s) referenced, nature of the reply (question / request / confirmation / critique), and routing action. The `stakeholder-feedback-integration-protocol.md` provides the Tier 1 protocol; this section covers the complete apparatus.

**Follow-up survey (Week 3–4 for substantive responders)**: See `adoption-feedback-template.md` (companion document to this roadmap) for the four-question email survey template. The survey is sent only to contacts who have engaged substantively — it is not a mass survey. Key questions: (1) which domain sections have you used or shared; (2) what modifications have you made; (3) what is most missing for your specific context; (4) what would accelerate your ability to act on this.

**Inbound requests**: When contacts request domain-specific deepening ("can you provide more analysis on the statutory text angle for Domain 34"), log this as a Phase 2 demand signal. Two independent requests on the same domain from Tier 1 contacts trigger Phase 2 research consideration per the trigger table in `feedback-integration-roadmap.md`.

---

### 2.2 Passive Monitoring

**Citation tracking**: Set up Google Scholar alerts for: the framework's title ("Democratic Renewal Proposal"), each domain title, and distinctive phrases (minimum five that would not appear in other policy literature). Check SSRN weekly for working papers citing the framework. The Overton Policy Citation Index operates on a 6–18 month lag — early results will not appear until late 2026, but setting up tracking infrastructure now prevents retrospective reconstruction.

**GitHub Gist analytics**: The framework's Gist URL provides view counts. Track: total views, view velocity (views per day in the first 7, 14, 30, 60, 90 days), and referral sources if visible. View velocity decay rate is a leading indicator of whether organic diffusion is sustaining or requires additional outreach waves.

**Social media mention monitoring**: Use TweetDeck or similar for searches on framework title and distinctive terms. Track: original posts vs. shares, institutional accounts vs. individual accounts, sector of poster (helps map diffusion pathways), and whether posts contain original commentary or just amplification.

**Secondary distribution detection**: When an institution that received the framework directly shares it with their network, that secondary distribution event is often detectable through sudden spikes in Gist views, new inbound email requests with referral context ("I saw this shared by [organization]"), or organizational newsletter archives. Secondary distribution is the strongest early diffusion signal — it means the receiving institution judged the framework credible enough to stake their own institutional reputation on sharing it.

**Legislative text monitoring**: Use LegiScan and NCSL bill tracking for bill language matching domain-specific reform proposals. Domain 1 RCV provisions, Domain 9 interstate compact language, and Domain 29 whistleblower protection architecture are the most text-searchable (they contain distinctive structural propositions unlikely to appear in identical form elsewhere).

**Litigation monitoring**: Democracy Docket, ACLU case tracker, and DAGA press releases. Monitor for language patterns matching Domain 37 NVRA quiet period analysis, Domain 33 constitutional elections clause arguments, and Domain 29 retaliatory prosecution doctrine. Weekly review is sufficient after Month 1; daily during the first 30 days given the active election EO litigation context.

### 2.3 Structured Check-ins

**Quarterly calls with early adopter institutions (Month 3, 6, 9, 12)**: Identify 5–10 institutions that showed the highest Phase 1 engagement signals (substantive reply, routing confirmation, domain request, or citation event). Schedule 20-minute calls using the institutional interview script in `adoption-feedback-template.md`. The call agenda: (1) what have they done with the framework, (2) what modifications have they made, (3) what is most useful for their current work, (4) what would they need for continued or deeper engagement.

**Call selection criteria**: Prioritize institutions that produce observable outputs (court filings, published reports, legislation) over those that only engage internally — outputs are verifiable adoption evidence; internal engagement is self-reported and harder to validate.

**Call timing**: Month 3 is the earliest at which most institutional adoption cycles have produced their first visible output. Month 6 is optimal for identifying whether initial adoption has deepened or stalled. Month 12 is the Phase 1 retrospective — what did the framework actually accomplish, and what should Phase 2 prioritize.

---

### 2.4 Sector-Specific Feedback Requests

**State AGs**: Request the specific domain sections that have been used in filings or coalition memos. Ask: which constitutional argument constructions have been most useful, and which have been superseded by more recent case law. This feedback directly informs domain update priorities.

**Law school clinics**: Request syllabi or case assignment sheets that reference framework domains. Ask: which sections are assigned as primary reading vs. supplementary, and what modifications have clinic directors made for pedagogical clarity. Faculty feedback on doctrinal precision is the highest-quality content improvement signal available.

**Think tanks**: Monitor publications for framework citations; when found, conduct a content audit of how the domain analysis was modified. Compare the published version to the original domain section to identify what was stripped, added, or reframed.

**Journalists**: Do not send structured feedback requests to journalists. Journalists are not institutional partners — they are amplifiers and investigators. Feedback from journalists comes in through story coverage and interview requests, not through structured surveys. Monitor coverage and make framework materials available for background research on request.

**Unions and civil society**: Request training materials that organizations have developed using the framework. The adaptation artifacts (two-page issue briefs, workshop outlines, action alerts) are the most direct evidence of how the framework has been operationalized for grassroots contexts.

---

### 2.5 Adoption Artifacts Analysis

When institutions publish work that uses the framework — a Brennan Center report, an AG coalition brief, a law review article, a union training packet — conduct a structured analysis of the artifact:

1. What domain(s) does it draw on?
2. What analytical elements were preserved vs. modified?
3. Were evidentiary caveats maintained or stripped?
4. Did the adaptation extend the framework's logic to new territory (constructive) or simplify it to its most politically convenient elements (dilutive)?
5. What audiences does the artifact reach that the original framework did not?

Log all artifacts in a running Artifacts Register (see `adoption-feedback-template.md` for format). At 60-day and 90-day review, analyze the artifact set for patterns: which domains are generating the most derivative work, and which adaptations are consistently producing framework drift.

## Part 3: Iterative Refinement Cycles

### 3.1 Feedback Triage: What to Act On

Not all feedback should trigger a framework update. The decision rule:

| Feedback type | Action | Rationale |
|---------------|--------|-----------|
| Two or more Tier 1 contacts identify same evidentiary gap in a domain | Update: add evidence to the domain section | Multiple independent experts identifying the same gap = genuine gap |
| Multiple AGs request domain section + statutory language | Phase 2 candidate: full research on statutory implementation | AG demand for statutory text = framework ready to move from diagnosis to remedy |
| Single think tank disputes a domain's policy conclusion | Accept divergence: add "contested interpretations" subsection | Policy conclusions are legitimately contested; evidence is not |
| Sector adaptation produces more effective framing for one audience | Accept: create sector-specific brief; base domain retains analytical structure | Both versions serve the framework's purpose |
| New court ruling updates a domain's factual predicate | Update: add Section [X] update notation with date and new facts | Factual currency is a credibility requirement |
| Law school faculty identify doctrinal imprecision | Update: add doctrinal qualification, do not change analytical conclusion | Faculty feedback on legal precision is the highest-quality content signal |
| Single organization wants a full rewrite of a domain | No action | Difference in emphasis, not a deficiency |
| Low pull on a domain after 90 days | No update; reassess target audience | Wrong audience, not wrong content |

The triage principle: volume of agreement matters more than strength of individual opinion. One Tier 1 contact with strong views is less informative than three Tier 2 contacts independently reaching the same conclusion.

---

### 3.2 Monthly Synthesis Cycle

**Month 1 (post-distribution)**: Collect all direct feedback signals (email replies, substantive questions, routing confirmations). Tally domain pull counts — which domains were referenced most frequently in inbound communications. Identify any red flags (see `stakeholder-feedback-integration-protocol.md` for the five-flag taxonomy). No framework updates in Month 1. This is a collection period only.

**Month 2**: Analyze the Month 1 dataset. Run the triage table above against aggregated feedback. Identify: (1) any domains with two or more Tier 1 evidentiary gap signals; (2) any Phase 2 domain candidates with two or more Tier 1 demand signals; (3) any adaptation artifacts showing quality degradation. Issue clarification notes for any quality degradation cases. Do not update base domain sections based on a single month's data.

**Month 3 and beyond (ongoing quarterly cadence)**: Quarterly synthesis aggregating 3 months of data. This cadence matches the timeline at which most institutional adoption cycles have produced their first verifiable output and feedback has moved from initial impressions to experience-based assessment. Apply triage table, update domains meeting the threshold, identify Phase 2 research to initiate.

---

### 3.3 Quarterly Decision Gates

Four decision gates govern the Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition:

**Gate 1 (Month 3)**: Has any domain received two or more Tier 1 demand signals for statutory implementation language? If yes, initiate Phase 2 research on that domain. Has any AG requested domain deepening for active litigation? If yes, prioritize that domain in Phase 2 sequencing.

**Gate 2 (Month 6)**: Is Phase 1 adoption velocity tracking toward the quantitative benchmarks established in Part 5? If below benchmark, diagnose cause: content gap, wrong audience, or messaging failure. Each diagnosis has a different correction. If at or above benchmark, maintain current approach and accelerate Phase 2 preparation.

**Gate 3 (Month 9)**: Are at least three sectors showing measurable adoption evidence (not just engagement)? If yes, Phase 2 distribution to broader institutional networks is supported. If no, extend Phase 1 with targeted re-engagement of highest-potential sectors before broadening.

**Gate 4 (Month 12)**: Retrospective assessment. What did Phase 1 accomplish against the benchmarks in Part 5? Which domains achieved the deepest institutional integration? Which sectors were most and least responsive? What does this tell us about Phase 2 domain selection and sector prioritization?

---

### 3.4 Version Control Without Confusion

The ABA's experience with the Model Rules provides the clearest precedent: the Ethics 2000 Commission update (2002) created a period of confusion when some states had adopted pre-update Model Rules and others adopted the updated version, creating a patchwork where the "Model Rules" meant materially different documents in different jurisdictions. The framework must avoid this dynamic.

**Version tagging protocol**:
- Base framework: Version 1.0 (distribution date)
- Any substantive domain update: Version 1.1, 1.2, etc. (increment the minor version)
- Phase 2 expansion (new domains added): Version 2.0
- Include a version date at the top of every domain section: "Last updated: [date], [specific change]"

**Communicating updates**: When a domain is updated, send a one-paragraph change notification to every contact who received that domain in Phase 1. Format: "Domain [X] — Update [Date]: [what changed and why]. The updated section is available at [link]. This update does not change the domain's core analysis; it adds [factual update / evidentiary strengthening / doctrinal precision] based on [triggering event]."

**Do not pull and replace**: Institutions that have already incorporated a domain section into their work should not receive a correction that implies their work is now outdated. Frame updates as additive: "This supplements the analysis you already have." Reserve the correction framing for cases of genuine factual error or quality degradation.

## Part 4: Sector-Specific Modification Protocols

### 4.1 High-Risk Domains for Modification

Certain domains carry elevated risk if modified without understanding the analytical load-bearing elements:

**Domain 1 (Voting Rights and Election Integrity)**: The highest-risk domain for dilutive modification. The domain's analytical power comes from the specific interaction of three elements: procedural disenfranchisement evidence, state constitutional authority analysis, and the connection to Domain 6 judicial independence. Organizations extracting only the procedural disenfranchisement evidence and presenting it without the judicial independence analysis produce a brief that is easier to attack — the strongest current defenses against voting rights challenges rely precisely on the court strategy that Domain 6 documents. Modification rule: if an institution uses Domain 1, they should be encouraged to reference Domain 6's circuit strategy analysis as a companion piece.

**Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization Defense)**: The Weissmann assessment framing is load-bearing. The domain explicitly documents the contested legal status of its core propositions. Any adaptation that strips the "contested" characterization and presents the domain's analysis as settled law creates factual exposure that can be used to discredit the organization citing it. Modification rule: the "contested legal status" qualifier in the Weissmann assessment section must survive any adaptation.

**Domain 37 (Federal Election Interference Diagnostic)**: Politically charged; any extraction of the most alarming statistics without the analytical framework that contextualizes them creates the kind of overstatement that attracts fact-checkers. The CISA funding cut figures and election denier appointment counts should always be paired with the framework's methodological notes (source: Domain 37 baseline metrics, May 4, 2026) that document how each figure was derived.

**Domain 33 (Ballot Initiative Suppression and State Election Authority)**: The six-state simultaneous action map is the domain's most operationally powerful element. Adaptations that extract only the legal argument and omit the strategic sequencing recommendations miss most of the domain's value for AG coalition coordination.

---

### 4.2 Stable Domains for Sector-Specific Adaptation

**Domain 17 (Labor Rights and Worker Protections)**: Sector-specific adaptation is low-risk. The domain's evidence base (NLRB enforcement data, wage theft statistics, union density trends) is well-documented and independently verifiable. Labor organizations adapting this domain for member education will simplify the analytical structure without affecting the core evidence claims.

**Domain 9 (Interstate Compacts and State Coordination)**: The interstate compact survey and model language are designed to be adapted to specific state contexts. The analytical structure is explicitly a framework for modification — organizations are expected to substitute their state's relevant compact history, constitutional text, and legislative calendar data.

**Domain 27 (Academic Freedom and Institutional Independence)**: Primarily a descriptive domain at this stage. Adaptations by universities and research institutions are likely to be supplementary (adding their specific institutional context) rather than modifying the core analysis. Adaptation risk is low.

**Domain 20 (Environmental Federalism)**: Legal argument structure is well-grounded in existing case law. Adaptations by environmental organizations will add sector-specific agency data; the core constitutional arguments are stable.

---

### 4.3 Modification Approval Process

A formal approval process is not appropriate for this framework's distribution context and scale. The framework is not a model code requiring official sanction; it is a research document that institutions use as they judge appropriate. The right approach is a recommendation protocol, not an approval gate:

1. **Preferred citation format**: Recommend that all derivative works cite the framework as "Democratic Renewal Framework, [Domain X], Version [X.X], [date]." Make this easy by including it at the top of each domain section.
2. **Version notice**: When an institution publishes a modified version, encourage them to note "adapted from" with the original version and date, so readers can access the unmodified original.
3. **Correction outreach**: When an adaptation introduces a material misrepresentation of the evidence, contact the publishing institution directly with a correction. Do not issue a public correction unless the misstatement reaches major media.

## Part 5: Phase 2 Readiness Signals

### 5.1 Adoption Velocity Benchmarks from Historical Precedent

Three precedents establish realistic expectations for Phase 1 → Phase 2 transition timelines:

**Model Penal Code (ALI, 1962–1980s)**: 36 states adopted revised criminal codes over roughly 20 years, averaging approximately 2.4 states per year at peak adoption velocity. However, the MPC was distributed to state legislators through the existing ALI network, which already had formal relationships with legislative drafting commissions. The 35-domain framework has no equivalent institutional preexistence. Calibration: the MPC precedent sets the ceiling for what top-tier model framework adoption looks like. Reaching 5+ states with documented framework-informed legislative or litigation activity within 24 months would put this framework in MPC territory.

**ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct (1983–2009)**: 49 states adopted by 2009 (26 years), with California holding out until 2018. Adoption proceeded through state bar associations' existing governance structures, which had formal model rules adoption processes. The key diffusion mechanism: once a critical mass of states adopted (approximately 25 states by the mid-1990s), the network effect made non-adoption increasingly costly for the remaining states. The 35-domain framework should watch for a similar network effect in the AG coalition context — once 10+ AGs are publicly citing the framework in active litigation, the remaining AGs face pressure to engage or be seen as behind.

**Automatic Voter Registration (Brennan Center, 2015–2024)**: Oregon adopted in March 2015; 24 states and DC had adopted by late 2025 — a 10-year diffusion cycle producing approximately 2.4 states per year at peak. Crucially, the Brennan Center's model legislation approach created a standard template that made adoption administratively easy: states could copy the template rather than draft from scratch. The framework's domain sections serve a similar function for policy advocates — pre-drafted analytical frameworks reduce the production cost of domain-adjacent policy work.

**Quantitative Phase 1 adoption thresholds**:
- Minimum viable: 5+ Tier 1 institutional contacts producing domain-citing output within 90 days
- Target: 3+ state AG offices citing framework analysis in active litigation within 6 months
- Stretch: 5+ law school programs incorporating domain sections into curriculum or clinic casework within 12 months

These are conservative benchmarks. The AVR precedent suggests that with a credible initiating institution (Oregon played this role for AVR; the AG coalition in the D. Mass. election EO case could play it here), the adoption curve accelerates significantly after the first few prominent adoptions.

### 5.2 Phase 2 Trigger Criteria

Phase 2 research should be initiated when the following conditions are met (per the full trigger table in `feedback-integration-roadmap.md`):

| Trigger condition | Phase 2 action |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Three or more Tier 1 contacts request domain X statutory implementation language | Initiate full research on Domain X statutory companion brief |
| Five or more institutions in different states have adopted the framework for active use | Geographic expansion: targeted outreach to adjacent-state networks |
| AG coalition uses Domain 33 or Domain 37 analysis in a major filing | Domain deepening brief for the specific legal theory used |
| Two or more law school clinics assign a domain as case material | Academic companion piece: cleaned-up version with full doctrinal apparatus for academic use |
| Identifiable framework drift in three or more published adaptations of the same domain | Clarification brief for that domain; distribute to all Phase 1 contacts who received it |
| Phase 2 hard-deadline domains (Domain 42 DEA June 29, Domain 39 November ballot measures) | Initiate regardless of Phase 1 feedback — deadline-driven, not demand-driven |

### 5.3 Leading Indicators of Phase 2 Success

Based on Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations framework (1962/1995) applied to institutional policy adoption, the following Phase 1 signals predict Phase 2 success:

**Early adopter quality over quantity**: If the first three adopters are marquee institutions (Just Security, Brennan Center, a state AG office), their adoption accelerates the credibility threshold for early majority institutions. The same number of lower-profile adoptions has less predictive value. Monitor: who adopted first, not how many.

**Cross-sector cascade activation**: When a Tier 1 institution in one sector cites the framework and that citation appears in the communications of a Tier 1 institution in a different sector within 30 days, the cross-sector cascade is activating. This is the strongest leading indicator of broad institutional penetration — it means the framework is spreading through existing professional networks rather than requiring continued direct outreach.

**Inbound requests from contacts not in the Phase 1 list**: When organizations that were not recipients of Phase 1 outreach contact the framework author because they encountered it through secondary distribution, that is a critical mass signal. At that point, the framework is spreading faster than it can be tracked through the distribution list.

**Domain-specific feedback coherence**: If feedback across multiple independent institutions converges on the same domain as most useful, that domain is functioning as a common analytical anchor — the framework equivalent of the MPC's mental state hierarchy, which became so widely adopted that practitioners from different jurisdictions could reason together using the same conceptual vocabulary. Domain-specific feedback coherence predicts that domain's longevity in the institutional literature.
