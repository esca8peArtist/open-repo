## 1. What Changed and Why It Matters

On May 18, 2026, the Supreme Court issued a GVR — Grant, Vacate, and Remand — in *Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians v. Howe* (No. 25-253), vacating the Eighth Circuit's ruling and remanding for reconsideration in light of *Louisiana v. Callais*. The same day, the Court issued an identical GVR in *State Board of Election Commissioners v. Mississippi State Conference of the NAACP*.

The prior Domain 58 litigation section (Section 6.1) characterized the GVR in language that was ambiguous and, on careful reading, optimistic: it suggested the GVR "signals that at least five justices may agree that private Section 2 enforcement survives Callais." That reading was wrong and needed correction before synthesis.

**Why the GVR is worse than cert denial, not a win:**

A cert denial leaves the lower court ruling in place — frozen, imperfect, but final. The Eighth Circuit's holding that private plaintiffs cannot enforce VRA Section 2 would have stood, and tribal plaintiffs would have sought relief through different channels. A GVR is an instruction to reconsider under a standard flagged by the Supreme Court. The standard flagged here — *Callais* — is hostile. The Eighth Circuit must now re-examine the private right of action question in light of a ruling that gutted Section 2's discriminatory-effects standard, making it structurally harder to preserve any favorable answer to the private enforcement question.

**The Jackson dissent reveals the deeper problem:**

Justice Jackson dissented in three sentences, noting that *Callais* "did not address" Section 2's private enforceability — the precise question *Turtle Mountain* raised. Her proposed disposition (summary affirmance) was legally correct: when a Supreme Court precedent does not touch the question presented, a GVR citing that precedent is incoherent. The conservative majority's willingness to GVR anyway, deploying a doctrinally inapposite precedent, signals a strategy: use *Callais* as a vehicle to return Section 2 victories to lower courts without the Court ever having to rule definitively on private enforceability. If cases repeatedly return to hostile circuits, age toward mootness as states redistrict, and fail on practical rather than legal grounds, private enforcement is functionally eliminated without a single definitive ruling saying so.

**Why the companion Mississippi GVR confirms a coordinated cascade:**

The identical GVR in *Board of Election Commissioners v. NAACP* — issued the same day, with Jackson dissenting in "substantially identical reasoning" — removes any doubt that this is a systematic pattern. Fifth Circuit action vacating a state redistricting order under *Callais* occurred May 11, eight days earlier. The sequence across two circuits within eight days, bracketed by two simultaneous Supreme Court GVRs, is a coordinated multi-circuit erosion of Section 2 enforcement. Distribution materials and advocacy partners must be told this explicitly: the *Callais* cascade is no longer a risk prediction. It is an active, documented, multi-circuit phenomenon.
