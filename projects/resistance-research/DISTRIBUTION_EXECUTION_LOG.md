## Section 1: Sequencing Logic

The 25 Tier 1 contacts are organized in three waves. The waves are not arbitrary batches — each wave builds the credibility conditions that make the next wave more effective.

**Wave 1 (Days 1-5): Credibility anchors**
Six contacts whose fastest possible engagement creates the institutional signal that all subsequent outreach references. Just Security can publish within days. Brennan Center staff review creates a credibility signal even without a public output. Senate staff contacts (Whitehouse, Klobuchar/Merkley) directly connect the framework to active committee work. Protect Democracy is a citation relationship, not cold outreach.

**Wave 2 (Days 6-14): Institutional depth**
Nine contacts who convert research into policy pipelines — the academic researchers whose comparative evidence analysis is most structurally aligned, the think tank directors whose citation infrastructure reaches legislative and advocacy audiences, and the law school clinic directors whose students and fellows turn research into litigation.

**Wave 3 (Days 15-21): Civil society leaders**
Ten contacts who need to see institutional traction before investing staff time. These organizations receive dozens of research documents per month; what separates engagement from discard is whether peer institutions have already signaled credibility. Wave 1 and 2 activity creates that signal. Do not move Wave 3 forward — the sequencing is load-bearing.

**Applies to**: Contacts 4, 5 (and any additional Senate staff contacts in Tier 2/3)

**Core orientation**: Senate staff are operational. They are not reading research to develop scholarly opinions; they are reading research to find usable material for hearings, briefings, and bill drafts. Every sentence should be answerable with "can we use this in committee work?" Lead with the specific legislative vehicle. Never lead with the theoretical framework.

**Structural formula**:
- Sentence 1: Name the specific committee work this is relevant to (not "democracy reform" — "the Senate Judiciary Courts Subcommittee hearing on SCOTUS accountability scheduled for [date]")
- Paragraph 1: What the domain covers in operational terms — what the staff member can excerpt for a hearing brief
- Paragraph 2: What the specific legislative vehicle is (bill number, sponsor, current status) — staff need to know you understand the legislative calendar
- Paragraph 3: One genuine question — the one thing you would most value their judgment on, stated clearly
- Close: Offer the one-page domain brief rather than the full document. Staff have 30 minutes, not 3 hours.

**Tone**: Direct, specific, brief. No academic hedging. No "I hope this might be useful." "This may be directly useful for your committee work on X" is the correct register.

**Length**: 200-250 words maximum. Senate staff route emails to colleagues; shorter emails get routed; longer emails get saved for "later" (which means never).

**Subject line formula**: "[Specific Domain X] + [Specific Bill or Hearing Name] — Brief for [Senator Name] Staff Review"

### Variant B: Think-Tank Director

**Applies to**: Contacts 7, 8, 9, and any additional think tank contacts

**Core orientation**: Think tank directors manage publication pipelines, not just reading lists. They are asking "can we publish something based on this?" or "can we cite this in our next brief?" The value proposition is the research as a production input for their own analytical output, not as a document to endorse.

**Structural formula**:
- Sentence 1: Name a specific recent publication or program of theirs (not "your organization's work" — "your November 2025 brief on Schedule F constitutional vulnerability")
- Paragraph 1: What the domain covers that extends or complements their existing publication — what new data or comparative evidence you are adding to a conversation they have already started
- Paragraph 2: What the specific co-publication opportunity looks like — a one-page issue brief adaptation, a guest contribution to their blog, a backgrounder for their next testimony
- Paragraph 3: The specific feedback ask — the one analytical gap you genuinely want their judgment on
- Close: CC 4.0 language, links to domain document and full proposal

**Tone**: Collegial, professional, research-to-research. "Your analysis of X in [publication name] identified the gap that this comparative evidence is designed to fill" is the correct register. Avoid framing the research as a competitor to their published work.

**Length**: 250-350 words. Think tank staff read more thoroughly than Senate staff, but do not confuse "reads more" with "has more time."

**Subject line formula**: "[Domain Topic] Research — Complementary to [Org's Program Name] — [Co-Publication/Issue Brief/Backgrounder] Inquiry"

### Variant C: Law School Dean

**Applies to**: Contact 13 (Chemerinsky), and any other law school deans or clinic directors in Tier 2/3

**Core orientation**: Law school deans control institutional resources — curriculum, clinic programs, faculty research support, speaking series — but they are not the operational users of the research. The goal of the outreach is to route the framework into an existing institutional initiative (a democracy education project, a clinic program, a faculty seminar series) rather than to ask the dean to read it personally. Lead with the institutional alignment.

**Structural formula**:
- Sentence 1: Name the specific institutional initiative you are aligning with — not "Berkeley Law's democracy work" but "the 'We Hold These Truths' democracy education project"
- Paragraph 1: What the framework provides to that specific initiative — the specific curricular gap it fills, the specific evidence base the initiative needs
- Paragraph 2: What domains are most directly relevant to the initiative's current focus — be specific (not "constitutional law" but "the federal statute theory for SCOTUS term limits and the post-Loper Bright administrative landscape")
- Paragraph 3: The specific request — curricular integration, faculty reading group, clinic research use, speaking invitation
- Close: CC 4.0, links, explicit offer that no attribution or formal partnership is required

**Tone**: Institutional and collegial, slightly more formal than think tank register. Deans receive a high volume of pitches and respond to clarity of fit between the proposal and their existing institutional commitments.

**Length**: 200-300 words. Deans route these emails to faculty or clinic directors; write the email in a form that the person they route it to can act on without additional explanation.

**Subject line formula**: "[Specific Institutional Initiative Name] — Research Alignment — [Domain X/Full Framework] — [Specific Request]"

## Section 8: Response Handling Protocol

This section documents what to do when responses arrive. Log all responses within 24 hours.

**Positive/interested response**: Reply within 24 hours. Do not delay — responsiveness signals professionalism. If they ask for materials, send the domain document(s) plus the executive summary. If they ask for a call, send 3 specific time slots (not "anytime") in your first reply. Log the response type and meeting status in the tracking table.

**Feedback or factual correction**: Thank them immediately and specifically ("Thank you — this is exactly the kind of practitioner feedback that improves the analysis"). Verify the correction independently before updating the document. If correct, update the relevant domain document, note the correction, and send a corrected version to anyone who has already received that domain. If incorrect, respond with the specific counter-analysis and invite further input. Never defend an error; never ignore a substantive objection.

**Referral offered**: Accept enthusiastically and ask for a warm introduction if possible ("If you're willing to make an introduction, that would be enormously helpful — even a brief note saying you've seen the research would help [contact's name] have context for my outreach"). Log the referral path in the tracking table.

**No response after 14 days**: Send one follow-up. One sentence: "Following up on my email from [date] — happy to send a shorter domain brief if the full document is more than you have time for right now." No re-pitch. No further follow-up after Day 14 follow-up.

**Negative response**: Log the reason. Do not reply defensively. If they cite a specific error, treat it as a feedback response (above). If they simply decline, reply: "Completely understood — thank you for letting me know. If circumstances change or a specific domain becomes relevant to your work, I'm happy to share." Then close the contact.
