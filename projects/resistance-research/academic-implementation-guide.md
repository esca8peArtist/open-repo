# Academic Implementation Guide
## For Universities and Research Institutions

**Who this is for**: Provosts, deans, department chairs, individual faculty, research center directors, and university government relations staff. Also relevant for law school clinic directors and policy school faculty.

**The core task**: Use the 35-domain framework to identify research gaps, structure curriculum around democratic renewal questions, create student research opportunities, and develop a publication strategy that extends the framework's evidentiary base.

---

## The Monday Morning Question

You are a department chair or research center director. A faculty member flags the framework and says it identifies a research gap in your department's area. What do you do with it?

Answer: run the alignment check, identify the specific knowledge gap the framework names, assign a research team, and build toward a publication that closes the gap.

---

## Five-Step Decision Tree

**Step 1: Run the faculty alignment check.**

Map your department or center's active faculty to the 35 domains. Every academic institution has some domain alignment — a political science department will have faculty working on Domains 1, 6, 7, and 9; a law school on Domains 6, 14, 16, 29; a public policy school on Domains 2, 4, 5, 18; a public health school on Domains 11, 15, 16. The question is not whether alignment exists — it is whether the faculty member is aware that their existing research connects to an active democratic renewal gap identified by the framework.

The tool: distribute the executive summary (democratic-renewal-executive-summary.md) to department faculty with a one-paragraph prompt: "Which domain does your current research most directly address? What does the proposal get right? What does it get wrong? Where is the evidentiary gap?" The responses map your department's research assets to the framework's needs.

**Step 2: Identify the knowledge gaps the framework explicitly flags.**

Each domain section includes implementation pathways that require empirical verification — questions the framework raises but cannot answer with existing evidence. These are your research opportunities. Examples: Domain 1 (Electoral Reform) raises the question of whether state-level RCV adoption changes candidate recruitment patterns — a question answerable by political scientists with access to state candidate filing data. Domain 14 (Criminal Justice) raises the question of long-term recidivism outcomes under community-based sentencing alternatives — answerable by criminologists with state corrections data access. Domain 11 (Healthcare) raises the question of whether community health center expansion during crisis periods reduces emergency department utilization — answerable by health economists with CMS data.

These are not invented research questions. They are questions where the framework says "the evidence suggests X, but there is no rigorous study of the U.S. analog." That is a publishable gap.

**Step 3: Connect to curriculum at three levels.**

The framework supports curriculum integration at three levels:

Level 1 (Undergraduate): Use the executive summary and individual domain sections as primary course readings in political science, public policy, law, and sociology courses. The framework's structure — problem diagnosis, reform proposal, evidence base, implementation pathway — models the structure of applied policy analysis. It is analytically teachable.

Level 2 (Graduate): Use the framework as a capstone problem. Assign students to select one domain, critique the framework's analysis using their disciplinary methods, and propose a research design to fill the identified knowledge gap. A political science PhD student critiquing Domain 1's proportional representation claims using comparative electoral data; a law student critiquing Domain 29's prosecutorial weaponization analysis using federal criminal procedure doctrine; a public health student critiquing Domain 11's all-payer rate-setting claims using state-level insurance data. These are substantive student research contributions.

Level 3 (Professional / Executive Education): Use the framework as a scenario planning tool in continuing education for government officials, nonprofit leaders, and advocates. The three-wave implementation sequence provides a planning horizon; the domain tracking template in the main toolkit file provides the scoring mechanism.

**Step 4: Develop the publication strategy.**

The framework's evidentiary gaps are publication opportunities. Prioritize gaps in your domain area where: (a) the framework's claim is based primarily on international evidence, and the U.S. analog is understudied; (b) the policy decision is time-sensitive (the 2026 midterm window, the Section 702 expiration, the OBBBA Medicaid work requirement effective date); and (c) your institution has unique data access that makes the research tractable.

Venue strategy: the framework's most urgent domain findings belong in policy journals with rapid publication timelines (the Journal of Democracy, Just Security, Health Affairs, the American Journal of Political Science, and high-impact law reviews with expedited review tracks). The framework explicitly connects to working papers and policy briefs from the Brennan Center, EPI, and CAP — those organizations accept contributed analyses and can distribute to their existing policy networks with much faster reach than traditional academic journals.

**Step 5: Engage the institution itself.**

The university is not just a venue for research about democratic renewal — it is itself an institution that can implement the framework's recommendations. Domain 27 (Higher Education and Academic Freedom) provides the most direct application: the framework documents the compound four-track assault on academic freedom and proposes specific statutory protections. Every university should have a response to each of those four tracks. Faculty governance bodies can formally adopt the framework's academic freedom analysis and use it to evaluate their institution's response to federal pressure.

At the institutional level: does your university have a clear policy on how it responds to visa revocation threats against international students (Domain 27)? Does it have a documented stance on federal funding leverage demands (Domain 27)? Does it have an accreditation independence plan if federal accreditation weaponization proceeds? The framework provides the diagnostic checklist; institutional leadership decides the response.

## Three Concrete Domain Applications

**Application 1: Using Domain 27 (Higher Education) to structure a faculty senate resolution.**

Domain 27 documents that the U.S. academic freedom institutional autonomy score fell from 3.3 (2019) to 1.7 (2025) — faster than Hungary's decline during Orban's university takeover. It documents the Sanders HELP Committee report finding 17 First Amendment court rulings against the administration. It proposes specific statutory responses: an academic freedom protection statute, INA Section 237(a)(4)(C) amendment to prohibit speech-based visa revocations, and accreditor independence codification. A faculty senate resolution can reference all three proposed statutory changes, call on the institution's government relations office to advocate for them, and commit the faculty governance body to monitor compliance with existing academic freedom standards. The framework provides the evidentiary basis the resolution needs.

**Application 2: Using Domain 4 (Digital Government) for a computer science or information science research program.**

Domain 4 documents the digital governance gap — 12,000+ legacy federal IT systems, $11.5 billion in annual IT spending on outdated systems, Estonia's X-Road model processing 1.5 billion queries annually as a counter-example. It flags the risk that DOGE's approach to federal digital infrastructure creates security vulnerabilities and accountability gaps. A computer science research center can build a research program specifically around federal digital infrastructure security assessment — using publicly available GSA data, federal IT spending reports (FISMA annual reports), and comparative analysis of peer-democracy digital government architectures. This is a high-impact applied research program that directly addresses a framework gap.

**Application 3: Using the comparative recovery research (Section 3.5) for a law school clinic or seminar.**

Section 3.5 integrates the four-variable framework (timing, elite defection, institutional anchors, blueprint) derived from South Korea, Spain, Uruguay, Hungary, Venezuela, and Turkey. A comparative constitutional law seminar can use this framework as the organizing structure — one country case per week, students analyzing how the four variables apply, then a synthesis session applying the framework to the U.S. case. The law school clinic analog: students work on actual cases from the litigation-tracker-2026.md, using the framework's institutional integrity analysis (Domain 2) and judicial independence analysis (Domain 6) to develop legal arguments. This is live-case clinical work grounded in the framework's comparative research.

## Common Mistakes in Academic Settings

**Treating the framework as a citation rather than a research agenda.** The most common academic engagement with policy frameworks is to cite them in a footnote. The framework's value for academic institutions is not its citability — it is the research agenda it implies. Every knowledge gap the framework flags is a potential publication. Treat it as a research agenda generator.

**Failing to bridge research and institutional engagement.** Universities face the Domain 27 crisis directly — federal funding leverage, visa revocations, accreditation weaponization. Academic researchers who study democratic backsliding but do not apply their analysis to their own institution's response are leaving institutional capacity on the table. Connect the research to the institution's own governance decisions.

**Ignoring the publication timeline problem.** Democratic crises move faster than academic publication timelines. The most important research outputs during a democracy crisis are policy briefs, op-eds in high-reach venues, and rapid-publication journal pieces — not monographs. Adjust your publication strategy accordingly. The Brennan Center, Just Security, and Health Affairs Policy Brief track all accept contributed research and publish within days to weeks, not the 12-24 months of traditional academic review.
