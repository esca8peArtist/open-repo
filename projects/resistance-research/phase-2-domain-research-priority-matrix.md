# Phase 2 Domain Research Priority Matrix

**Prepared: May 5, 2026**

This document answers one operational question: after Phase 1 launches, which domain research do we do next, and when do we start? It translates the candidate analysis in `ITEM50_DOMAINS42-50_CANDIDATES.md` and `phase-2-domain-prioritization-matrix.md` into a concrete sequencing plan with specific trigger conditions.

The core design principle: Phase 2 domain selection should be driven by Phase 1 demand signals, not by research convenience or prior assumptions about what institutions need. If the domain performance heatmap in `phase-1-measurement-dashboard-spec.md` shows strong demand for prosecutorial accountability but weak demand for epistemic infrastructure, the sequencing shifts accordingly — regardless of what this document initially predicted.

---

## Part I: Priority Framework — How Domains Are Ranked

### Four Selection Criteria

Each candidate domain is scored against four weighted criteria. The weighting reflects the framework's distribution strategy: institutional adoption and litigation catalysis are worth more than research convenience or abstract importance.

**Criterion A: Institutional Demand Signal (weight: 2.0x)**
Is there evidence from Phase 1 engagement — replies, routing, domain mentions, explicit requests — that Tier 1 or Tier 2 institutions want this domain? Demand signal is the most important criterion because it means the research output already has a waiting audience. A domain with a strong demand signal from a Tier 1 AG office or Brennan Center researcher is immediately distributable; a domain without a demand signal requires speculation about its eventual audience.

- Score 5: Explicit request from Tier 1 institution during Phase 1 outreach.
- Score 4: Multiple unsolicited domain mentions in Phase 1 replies.
- Score 3: Single domain mention, or implicit demand (contact works primarily on this issue).
- Score 2: No Phase 1 signal; domain fills a framework gap with clear audience.
- Score 1: No Phase 1 signal; speculative audience.

*Note: At Phase 1 launch, all domains score 2 or 1 on this criterion. As Phase 1 engagement data accumulates (Month 1–3), this is the criterion that shifts most rapidly and should dominate sequencing decisions.*

**Criterion B: Urgency Window (weight: 1.5x)**
Is there a specific legislative, judicial, or electoral deadline that makes this domain more valuable if completed before a particular date? Urgency is about policy windows, not general importance. A domain that is important in principle but has no hard deadline scores lower than a domain that feeds a specific Senate vote, SCOTUS oral argument, or state ballot measure.

- Score 5: Hard deadline within 60 days of potential research completion.
- Score 4: Hard deadline within 3–6 months of potential research completion.
- Score 3: Policy window open for 6–12 months; no specific hard deadline.
- Score 2: Window is 12–24 months or diffuse.
- Score 1: Long-cycle structural issue; no near-term window.

**Criterion C: Research Complexity / Feasibility (weight: 1.0x)**
How many research hours are required, and how accessible are the primary sources? Complexity is inverted for scoring — lower complexity = higher score, because faster research completion means faster availability to waiting audiences.

- Score 5: 8–10 hours; organized accessible sources; standard domain format.
- Score 4: 10–14 hours; moderate synthesis required.
- Score 3: 14–18 hours; specialized sources or technical terrain.
- Score 2: 18–24 hours; interdisciplinary or specialized.
- Score 1: 24+ hours; requires expert access or specialized databases.

**Criterion D: Framework Gap Severity (weight: 1.3x)**
How significantly does this domain fill a gap in the existing framework? A domain that partially overlaps with Domains already in the framework scores lower than one addressing a completely absent analytical category.

- Score 5: Critical gap — no existing domain addresses this question; institutional audience regularly faces this issue without a framework tool.
- Score 4: Significant gap — existing domains touch this topic but the dedicated treatment would substantially improve the framework's analytical coverage.
- Score 3: Moderate gap — existing domain coverage is adequate; additional domain adds depth but not structural coverage.
- Score 2: Minor gap — existing domain(s) adequately cover the territory; a new domain would primarily add detail.
- Score 1: Redundant — covered by existing domains at sufficient depth.

## Part II: Candidate Matrix — Ten Priority Domains

The ten candidates below reflect the most important expansion targets based on the gap analysis in `ITEM50_DOMAINS42-50_CANDIDATES.md`, the Phase 1 distribution infrastructure already in place, and the 2026–2028 policy window calendar.

Scoring is assessed at Day 0 (before Phase 1 demand signals are available). Criterion A scores will be updated as Phase 1 engagement accumulates. Scores in brackets are [Urgency A score at Day 0 → Expected score after Month 2 Phase 1 signals].

*(Research already complete per ITEM12 — reference for sequencing comparison only)*

**Demand predictors**: Brennan Center economic democracy researchers; academic economists on regulatory capture; Senate Banking Committee Democratic staff.

### Domain 39 — Reproductive Rights and Democratic Institutional Design *(Candidate A from ITEM50)*

**Gap it fills**: No existing domain frames reproductive rights as a democratic responsiveness failure — how 22 states removed a right supported by 60% of the public through SCOTUS capture, gerrymandering, and legislative supermajority manipulation. - **Electoral audience**: November 2026 ballot measures in Missouri, Nevada, and Virginia make this the highest-urgency electoral mobilization domain in the framework.
- **AG offices**: Democratic AG offices in Arizona, Michigan, Minnesota with active Dobbs-related enforcement conflicts.

**Phase 1 demand signal**: [Day 0: 2] → Expected to rise to 4–5 quickly if AG contacts or reproductive rights–adjacent think tanks engage during Phase 1.

**Research complexity**: 12–15 hours (Score: 3).

**Urgency window**: CRITICAL — November 2026 ballot measures and SCOTUS mifepristone docket make this the highest-urgency Phase 2 candidate (Score: 5).

**Framework gap severity**: HIGH — Democratic institutional design framing of reproductive rights is absent from current framework (Score: 4).

**Phase 1 demand signal**: [Day 0: 2] → Expected to rise if Domain 36 (AI governance) generates engagement from academic contacts.

**Research complexity**: 10–14 hours (Score: 4).

**Urgency window**: HIGH — EU AI Act deepfake labeling enforcement begins August 2026; 2026 midterm primary deepfakes already documented (Score: 4).

### Sequencing Tiers — When to Begin Each Domain

#### Immediate Post-Launch (Months 1–2): Begin Research Now

**Domain 42 — Drug Policy (DEA June 29 deadline)**

If Phase 1 launches in early May 2026, the DEA administrative hearing on cannabis rescheduling begins June 29 — approximately 50–60 days away. A domain document completed before June 29 can be distributed to ACLU Criminal Law Reform, The Sentencing Project, drug policy reform organizations, and relevant congressional staff in time to inform advocacy during the hearing. This is the most specific time-bounded opportunity in the entire Phase 2 candidate set.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 42 research immediately if Phase 1 launches before June 15, 2026 and the DEA hearing date of June 29, 2026 has not been postponed."

**Domain 39 — Reproductive Rights (August–November 2026 ballot measure window)**

Missouri, Nevada, and Virginia ballot measures make this the highest-urgency Phase 2 domain for electoral mobilization purposes. A domain document completed and distributed by August 2026 (primary season) reaches reproductive rights organizations in time to support ballot measure campaigns. The SCOTUS mifepristone docket reinforces urgency.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 39 research in Month 1 if Phase 1 engagement shows any of: (a) AG offices with active Dobbs enforcement conflicts have engaged; (b) reproductive rights organizations respond to Phase 1 outreach; or (c) Phase 1 launch is any path (immediate start regardless of demand signals given CRITICAL urgency)."

#### Month 2–3: Begin If Phase 1 Demand Signals Confirm

**Domain 40 — Epistemic Infrastructure**

Begin in Month 2–3 if Phase 1 domain performance heatmap shows strong engagement with Domain 36 (AI governance) or Domain 8 (media ecosystem). The EU AI Act enforcement (August 2026) and 2026 midterm primary deepfake documentation provide the external urgency anchor. A Month 2–3 research start produces a distributable domain document by Month 4, in time for distribution to Stanford Internet Observatory, Harvard Shorenstein Center, and Brennan Center election security researchers before the August 2026 primary season deepfake window.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 40 research in Month 2–3 if Phase 1 domain heatmap shows 2+ mentions of Domain 36 or Domain 8 in Phase 1 replies OR if any Tier 1 think tank contact mentions AI/disinformation as a gap in outreach replies."

**Domain 41 — Post-Insurrection Accountability**

Begin in Month 2–3 if Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization) generates strong engagement with civil litigation advocacy organizations or congressional Judiciary staff. The Section 3 litigation timeline and Georgia prosecution create a judicial window that compounds over 2026–2027.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 41 research in Month 2–3 if Phase 1 engagement shows 2+ routing events from Domain 29 to civil rights litigation organizations, OR if Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights or Democracy Docket contacts engage substantively with Domain 29 analysis."

#### Month 3–4: Begin If Path B or Hybrid Engagement Pattern Confirms

**Domain 44 — Labor Power**

The PRO Act window opens after the 2026 midterms. A Month 3–4 research start (if Phase 1 is performing well on its distribution timeline) produces a distributable domain document by Month 5–6, available for distribution to labor organizations as they prepare for post-midterm legislative advocacy.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 44 research in Month 3–4 if Phase 1 domain heatmap shows 2+ mentions of Domain 17 (social safety net) or Domain 4 (economic policy) in Phase 1 replies, OR if AFL-CIO, SEIU, or related labor contacts engage with Phase 1 outreach."

**Domain 43 — Civil Asset Forfeiture**

Low urgency but high feasibility (8–12 hours). This domain is a candidate for parallel research if another higher-priority domain is underway and production capacity permits. Its bipartisan coalition (ACLU + Cato + Institute for Justice + FAIR Act sponsors) makes it uniquely useful for the framework's distribution into libertarian-right audiences that the current 35-domain framework does not adequately serve.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 43 research in Month 3–4 if Phase 2 production capacity permits parallel domains, or if Phase 1 engagement shows interest from libertarian-right institutional contacts (Cato, Niskanen Center, R Street Institute — all in Tier 3 of Phase 1 distribution list)."

---

#### Month 5–6: Defer Until Phase 1 Evidence Base Justifies

**Domain 45 — Housing as Democratic Infrastructure**

Defer to Month 5–6 minimum. Housing is not a primary focus of Phase 1 Batch 1–3 contacts. The framework gap is real but moderate — Domain 13 provides adequate coverage for the initial distribution period. Begin if Phase 1 generates engagement from tenant organizing contacts, housing policy think tanks, or state-level policy organizations focused on housing-democracy intersections.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 45 research only if Phase 1 domain heatmap shows emerging engagement with Domain 13 (housing) AND a Phase 1 contact has explicitly requested housing-democracy framing, OR if user selects this as a priority independent of demand signals."

**Domain 46 — Tribal Sovereignty**

Defer to Month 6+. This is the highest-complexity candidate (14–18 hours), the most geographically concentrated audience, and the furthest from the primary Phase 1 distribution network. Meaningful research quality on tribal sovereignty requires the most careful sourcing and the most careful framing; rushing it to fill a production slot would be worse than waiting.

**Decision rule**: "Begin Domain 46 research only when: (a) NCAI or NARF contacts have engaged with Phase 1 outreach; OR (b) FY2027 tribal appropriations conflict has escalated to a high-profile legislative confrontation; AND (c) sufficient research time is available for the full 14–18 hour research requirement."

## Part V: How Phase 1 Data Updates This Ranking

The scoring in Part II will be materially different by Month 3 once Phase 1 demand signals accumulate. Here is the specific update protocol:

**After Week 2**: Review all Batch 1 replies for domain mentions. Any explicit domain request or domain-specific routing event updates the Demand Signal score for that domain.

**After Month 1**: Update the domain performance heatmap in `phase-1-measurement-dashboard-spec.md`. Any domain cluster with "Strong" demand signal (3+ mentions) moves its Criterion A score to 5 and recalculates composite. If the ranking shift puts a previously deferred domain into the top tier, adjust sequencing accordingly.

**After Month 2**: First full attribution review. Any domain where a Phase 1 citation event satisfies Tests 1 and 3 of the attribution framework signals active research use — this is the strongest possible demand signal and scores 5 on Criterion A for the corresponding Phase 2 domain.

**Standing decision rule for all sequencing adjustments**: "A Phase 2 domain moves to immediate research priority if its Criterion A (Institutional Demand) score reaches 5 at any measurement window, regardless of where it originally ranked in the Day 0 matrix."
