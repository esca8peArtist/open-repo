# Citizen Election Observer Guide 2026: Protecting the Vote from Polling Place to Certification

**Guide completed**: June 28, 2026  
**Crisis window**: ACUTE — The November 3, 2026 midterms face the most concentrated election integrity threat since Reconstruction. Federal election monitoring infrastructure has been systematically dismantled: CISA zeroed out, DOJ command center eliminated, the civil rights election observer program under defunding review, and the White House deploying election monitors to Democratic-majority California and New Jersey in what critics characterize as political intimidation. CREW identifies 35 election officials nationwide who have previously refused to certify and may be in a position to do so again. Arizona's legislature proposed HB 2440 to grant legal immunity to officials who refuse to certify. The ACLU has committed $50 million and 100 paid staff to election safeguarding — its largest-ever midterm investment — precisely because the need is unprecedented.  
**Priority designation**: Highest-urgency Phase 2 adjacent guide — fills the actionable gap between structural analysis (Domains 37, 37b, E) and citizen-level response. Existing domains document the threats; this guide answers "what do I do."  
**Format note**: This guide is structured for three audiences who will use it at different stages: (1) individuals deciding whether and how to volunteer; (2) trained observers preparing for specific duties; (3) organizations deploying observers at scale. All three audiences should read the legal basis sections before proceeding to the procedural ones.

## Executive Summary

The November 2026 midterms will be contested in an environment where the federal infrastructure designed to protect elections has been deliberately dismantled, where at least 53 election-denying candidates hold or are running for positions with certification authority, and where the pattern of local boards refusing or threatening to refuse certification — documented in eight states since 2020 — is expanding to include legislative efforts to legalize refusal (Arizona HB 2440, 2025).

Against this backdrop, citizen election observers — poll watchers, canvass monitors, certification board monitors, and audit observers — are among the most effective tools available to democratic defenders. They are legal, they are scalable, they are documented, and they work: in every instance of a county board refusing to certify since 2020, the election was ultimately certified, in part because organizations had observers present who documented the refusal, triggered legal action, and created a public record.

The central finding of this guide: **citizen observation is not passive witness-bearing. It is an active democratic accountability function that requires legal knowledge, tactical preparation, documentation discipline, and network integration to be effective.** An observer who does not know the legal limits on their own conduct, who does not document in real time, and who is not connected to a legal rapid-response team is significantly less effective than one who is.

This guide provides everything required to be the second kind of observer.

**The six critical functions of a trained citizen election observer**:
1. **Legal deterrence** — the visible presence of a trained observer with documentation equipment changes behavior by officials and unauthorized disruptors alike
2. **Real-time documentation** — creating a contemporaneous legal record that is usable in court
3. **Incident escalation** — connecting documented violations to legal teams capable of obtaining emergency relief
4. **Voter protection** — identifying and reporting voter intimidation, access barriers, and equipment failures before they affect large numbers of voters
5. **Canvass and certification monitoring** — the most undercovered function; the majority of subversion attempts have occurred after Election Day, not during it
6. **Post-election audit verification** — ensuring machine count accuracy is confirmed by independent paper record review

## Section 1: The Legal Landscape — What Authorizes Citizen Observation

### 1.1 Federal Constitutional and Statutory Basis

Citizen election observation in the United States operates within a layered legal framework. At the federal level, three statutory regimes are foundational:

**The Voting Rights Act of 1965 (52 U.S.C. § 10301 et seq.)** — particularly Section 11(b), 52 U.S.C. § 10307(b) — is the primary federal tool for citizen election protection. Section 11(b) prohibits any person from "intimidat[ing], threaten[ing], or coerc[ing]" any person "for voting or attempting to vote" or "for urging or aiding any person to vote or attempt to vote." This provision applies bidirectionally: it protects voters from intimidation by partisan observers, and it provides the legal basis for injunctive relief against mass ballot challenges conducted with intimidatory effect, even when no individual challenge is per se unlawful.[1]

The VRA's Section 8, 52 U.S.C. § 10305, also authorizes the appointment of federal examiners and observers — the historic Civil Rights Division program that at its peak deployed 1,000+ federal observers to over 100 counties. As documented in Section 5 of this guide, that program is now under defunding review, making citizen observation a substitute for federal capacity that no longer reliably exists.[2]

**The Help America Vote Act of 2002 (HAVA), 52 U.S.C. § 20901 et seq.** establishes federal minimum standards for election administration, including provisional voting requirements (§ 21082), voter information posting requirements (§ 21081), and accessible voting system requirements. HAVA does not create an express federal right to observe elections, but it creates enforceable minimum standards whose compliance citizen observers can document and report.[3]

**18 U.S.C. § 594** (Intimidation of voters) makes it a federal crime to "intimidate, threaten, coerce, or attempt to intimidate, threaten, or coerce any other person for the purpose of interfering with the right of such other person to vote or to vote as he may choose." Maximum penalty: one year federal imprisonment. This applies to bad-faith challengers whose conduct crosses from observation into intimidation.[4]

**18 U.S.C. § 595** prohibits federal employees from using their official authority to interfere with elections. This is relevant in 2026 given documented concerns about federal law enforcement (ICE, DHS) operating near polling places.[5]

**The National Voter Registration Act (NVRA), 52 U.S.C. § 20501 et seq.** establishes a 90-day quiet period before federal elections (beginning August 5, 2026) during which systematic voter roll purges are prohibited. Citizen observers documenting roll purge activity during this period have a federal statutory basis for reporting violations.[6]

### 1.2 The First Amendment Dimension

The First Amendment provides a protective overlay for election observation in multiple dimensions. Courts have recognized that attending open government proceedings — including canvassing board meetings, county election board certification sessions, and post-election audit processes — is protected expressive activity. The right to observe and document government proceedings is not absolute (it can be subject to reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions), but it cannot be eliminated for political observers when the same access is granted to others.[7]

The States United Democracy Center analyzed First Amendment limits on poll observer conduct in detail in a 2024 brief, finding that government authority to regulate observer conduct is narrowly constrained — restrictions must be content-neutral, must leave open adequate alternative channels, and cannot be applied selectively to silence political viewpoints.[8]

### 1.3 State Statutory Basis — The Primary Legal Architecture

Election observation rights are primarily creatures of state law. Every state allows some form of observation, but the scope, access, and procedural requirements differ dramatically. The critical distinction the Carter Center documents in its U.S. election observation guide is the difference between three categories:[9]

**Partisan watchers/challengers**: Appointed by political parties, candidates, or ballot measure committees. These roles have the broadest access in most states — they can challenge voter eligibility (subject to legal constraints), observe at close proximity to voting equipment, and in some states request specific documentation. The tradeoff: they must be formally credentialed through the appointing party or candidate, their number is limited per precinct, and their conduct is strictly regulated.

**Nonpartisan observers**: Available in approximately half of U.S. states. These may be credentialed through civic organizations, academic institutions, or designated professional associations. Access is typically more limited than partisan watchers — they may observe from public viewing areas rather than behind the voting counter — but they are not constrained to party affiliation.

**General public**: At the polling place and canvassing board level, most proceedings are open to the public in some capacity. Tabulation processes (ballot counting centers) may be accessible to the public with appropriate credentialing.

The practical implication for 2026 observers: **your state's framework determines which category you can occupy, and that category determines your access rights.** Attempting to exercise partisan watcher rights without proper credentials is grounds for removal; failing to claim the full access your credentials entitle you to is a missed accountability opportunity.

## Section 2: State-by-State Framework — Swing States and High-Risk Jurisdictions

### 2.1 The Seven Priority States

Based on the Domain 37 research (federal interference risk), Domain 37b analysis (election administration vulnerability), and the documented certification refusal pattern, seven states present the highest concentration of election integrity risk and the most acute need for trained observers in 2026:

**Arizona** — Observer rights: Partisan watchers may observe equipment testing, Election Day polling (one representative per party per precinct within 75-foot limit), early voting, and central counting. Physical access restrictions are specific: observers cannot interfere or impede ballot secrecy. The 2025 HB 2440 proposal (which would have granted legal immunity to county officials refusing to certify) failed but was introduced — signals legislative sympathy for certification refusal. Federal court blocked an Arizona voter intimidation rule from the 2024 cycle in September 2025 while affirming the mandatory nature of certification. County certification: canvass must be completed by the third Thursday after election (November 21, 2026); state certification follows.[10][11]

**Georgia** — Observer rights: Partisan watchers may observe before, during, and after polls close. One to two watchers permitted per precinct. Watchers are prohibited from talking to voters, checking elector lists, or using photographic or electronic monitoring equipment. A critical vulnerability: Georgia does not broadly permit nonpartisan observation, though special Fulton County arrangements allowed Carter Center observation in 2024. The 2024 Georgia State Election Board rules granting county officials authority to conduct "reasonable inquiry" before certifying — struck down by court order in October 2024 — remain a template for future certification disruption attempts. County certification: 5 p.m. on November 12, 2026 (17 days post-election, based on 2024 pattern). Fulton County, the state's largest and most scrutinized, requires particular attention.[12][13][14]

**Michigan** — Observer rights: Registered Michigan voters may serve as challengers; anyone may serve as a poll watcher from public viewing areas. Challengers can formally challenge voter eligibility and voting processes; poll watchers observe without challenge authority. Maximum two challengers per party per precinct. A 2024 ACLU Michigan lawsuit against a Kalamazoo County board member who preemptively vowed not to certify resulted in a sworn affidavit to certify within a week of filing — the litigation-as-deterrent model.[15] County certification: county election boards meet at 11 a.m. on November 15, 2026 (10 days post-election); state certification by December 2, 2026.[16]

**Nevada** — Observer rights: General public may observe with signed acknowledgment of prohibitions. Observers cannot photograph the conduct of voting or record on audiotape. Election processes broadly open to public observation including voting conduct, ballot handling, and post-election audits. County certification: county boards must complete canvass by November 15, 2026 (10th day post-election); secretary of state canvasses on November 26, 2026 (fourth Tuesday of November).[17]

**North Carolina** — Observer rights: Anyone may monitor an election from outside the voting place buffer zone. Inside the polling place, partisan representation requires credentialing. The state Board of Elections provides observer training through its website. Removal procedure is structured: chief judge must provide warning and opportunity to correct, then notify county board director if removal is required. Bipartisan Board structure has provided some protection against certification refusal. No separate canvass certification deadline provided in current research; monitor NCSBE website.[18][19]

**Pennsylvania** — Observer rights: Registered county voters may serve as watchers; candidates appoint two per district, parties appoint three per district. Watchers must show certificates from county board. May observe equipment preparation, polling locations, absentee/mail-in processing, and canvassing. County boards deliver unofficial returns by the Tuesday following the election; official certification to the commonwealth secretary by the third Monday following the election (approximately November 24, 2026).[20]

**Wisconsin** — Observer rights: Observers must wear a badge reading "Election Observer" and sign a form acknowledging rules. Limited specific state provisions for nonpartisan observers. County boards of canvassers convene by 9 a.m. the Tuesday after the election (November 10, 2026) and must deliver certified vote statements to the Wisconsin Elections Commission no later than November 19, 2026. WEC final canvass and certification on or before December 1, 2026.[21]

### 2.2 High-Risk Certification Jurisdictions Beyond the Seven

Based on CREW's "Election Certification Under Threat" analysis and the Voting Rights Lab's 2026 Election Risk Heat Map, additional jurisdictions warrant observation focus:

- **Cochise County, Arizona**: Two supervisors previously indicted for attempting to delay certification (charges pending; Arizona HB 2440 was partly designed to protect them); highest certification refusal risk jurisdiction in the country
- **Luzerne County, Pennsylvania**: Documented paper ballot shortage on Election Day 2022; ongoing administration fragility
- **Jefferson County, Georgia**: Previous certification concerns documented
- **Maricopa County, Arizona**: Largest county in a swing state; continues to face politically motivated process challenges

### 2.3 States With Expanded Observer Rights (Nonpartisan Opportunities)

The following states offer formal nonpartisan observer pathways useful for observers not affiliated with a political party:

**California** — "Nonpartisan observers (defined as a bona fide association of citizens or a media organization)" have access to "any and all phases of the election." The strongest nonpartisan observer statute in the country.[22]

**Illinois** — Nonpartisan civic organizations may observe polling locations if registered voters in the state.[23]

**Montana** — "Any group having an interest in the election" may request observer access; Montana Election Observation Initiative (University of Montana) provides training and deployment.[24]

**New Mexico** — Distinguishes formal categories of "election observers" (including academic and international) from partisan "poll watchers," with defined access levels for each.[25]

**Utah** — Any registered or preregistered voter may observe multiple phases including audits and recounts.[26]

## Section 3: The Observation Phases — What to Observe and When

### 3.1 Phase One: Pre-Election Setup and Equipment Testing

**What it is**: Before polls open, election officials conduct equipment testing — logic and accuracy tests (L&A tests) on voting machines, seal checks on ballot boxes, and setup verification. This phase is generally observable.

**What to look for**:
- Equipment serial numbers match certification documentation
- Seals on voting machines are intact and seal numbers are logged
- Zero count tapes (showing all candidates start at zero) are printed and posted
- All required voter information postings are in place (HAVA § 21081 requirements)
- Accessible voting equipment is functional and positioned appropriately
- Chain of custody logs are being maintained

**Documentation priority**: Log equipment serial numbers and seal numbers independently. These become significant if equipment irregularities are alleged post-election. Photograph seal tags if permitted by state law (check state regulations — some prohibit photography of equipment).

**Legal note**: Logic and accuracy tests in most states are public proceedings. Request the schedule from your county election office 30 days before the election. Brennan Center's "Election Certification Processes and Guardrails" documents that equipment testing access is one of the least contested observer access areas.[27]

### 3.2 Phase Two: Election Day Polling Place Observation

**What it is**: The most publicly visible phase. Observers are present at polling places during voting hours (typically 7 a.m. to 8 p.m., with state-specific variations).

**Legal framework**: Partisan watchers must have credentials from the appointing party or candidate and present these to the presiding officer upon arrival. Most states limit the number of partisan watchers per party to 1-3 per precinct. Nonpartisan observers operate under state-specific rules (see Section 2).

**What to look for**:
1. **Voter check-in process**: Is the process operating without unreasonable delay? Are voters being turned away improperly?
2. **ID requirements**: Are state-compliant ID requirements being applied correctly? Are provisional ballots being offered to all voters who cannot produce required ID?
3. **Provisional ballot handling**: Are provisional ballot envelopes available? Are voters receiving the correct reason code? Are envelope completion instructions being provided?
4. **Accessibility**: Are accessible voting machines functional? Are voters with disabilities receiving assistance?
5. **Electioneering violations**: Is anyone within the legal buffer zone displaying campaign materials? (Buffer zones vary: Arizona 75 feet, Georgia 150 feet, Pennsylvania within the polling place)
6. **Equipment malfunctions**: Are machine failures being logged? Are paper backup procedures being followed?
7. **Long lines**: Are voters in line at closing time being allowed to vote?
8. **Observer access**: Are all parties' observers being given equivalent access? Are any observers being treated differently?

**Conduct rules — the legal limits of observer authority**:
- Do not speak to voters about their votes, their eligibility, or the election process (this is the election workers' role)
- Do not handle any election materials, including ballots, envelopes, or pollbooks
- Do not photograph voters or ballots in states where this is prohibited (Georgia prohibits it entirely; many states prohibit identifying information)
- Do not position yourself in a way that creates line of sight to completed ballots
- Do not challenge voters without a good-faith factual basis — mass challenges without individualized basis may violate VRA Section 11(b)[28]
- Do not disrupt operations or raise your voice — a warning precedes removal in most states; if removed, document and call your legal team immediately

**The challenger role — specific authority and strict limits**: In states permitting challengers (Michigan, Maryland, North Carolina, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and others), challengers may formally object to a voter's eligibility. The legal standard in most states requires "good reason to believe" the voter is ineligible — meaning a specific, articulable factual basis, not general suspicion. The Brennan Center has documented that mass challenge campaigns — such as those using bulk voter roll comparisons to generate lists of thousands of "suspect" voters without individualized review — may constitute VRA Section 11(b) violations.[29] If you are being directed to file mass challenges without individualized review, consult your legal team before proceeding.

### 3.3 Phase Three: Ballot Drop Box and Early Voting Observation

**What it is**: In states with drop boxes and early voting, the chain of custody for those ballots is observable. This is a frequently overlooked observation opportunity.

**What to look for**:
- Are drop boxes under 24/7 surveillance as required?
- Are ballot retrieval procedures being followed with two-person bipartisan or nonpartisan teams?
- Are chain of custody logs being maintained for every collection?
- Are observers being kept at "a sufficient distance... so as not to interfere with the election official's duties or to compromise the integrity of the chain of custody" — but also not being excluded entirely?[30]
- During early voting, are the same procedures being followed as on Election Day?

**Warning**: Unofficial "drop box observers" deployed by partisan groups to record voters at drop boxes have been the subject of litigation. In 2022, a federal court in Arizona enjoined a drop box observation operation run by a political organization, finding that the conduct of approaching and photographing voters at drop boxes constituted voter intimidation under VRA Section 11(b).[31] If you are near a drop box, your role is to document official procedure compliance — not to surveil voters.

### 3.4 Phase Four: Mail Ballot Processing and Central Counting Observation

**What it is**: In states with significant mail voting, a separate processing operation handles ballot envelopes, signature verification, and tabulation. This is increasingly the site of partisan disputes.

**Key access notes by state**:
- **Pennsylvania**: Watchers may observe mail-in processing and canvassing
- **Arizona**: Watchers may observe central counting locations
- **Michigan**: Challengers may observe at central tabulation with proper credentialing
- **Nevada**: Public observation of ballot handling and counting is broadly permitted

**What to look for**:
- Signature verification process — are the standards being applied consistently and the basis for rejections documented?
- Cure notification — are voters whose ballots are rejected being notified in time to cure (fix) their ballot as state law requires?
- Tabulation chain of custody — are tabulation machines' input and output being logged?
- Bipartisan/multipartisan oversight of batch processing

**The "ballot curing" window**: Most states with mail voting permit voters whose ballots are rejected for signature mismatch or other curable defects to correct their ballots. The deadline varies. Observers who document cure notification failures create evidence for emergency legal intervention to extend curing periods. Know your state's cure deadline before Election Day.

### 3.5 Phase Five: Canvass Monitoring — The Most Critical Underobserved Phase

**What it is**: The canvass is the formal post-election process of verifying totals, reconciling discrepancies, reviewing provisional ballots, and resolving the official vote count before certification. This process, which unfolds over days to weeks after Election Day, is where most of the certification-related subversion attempts have occurred.

**Why this is the priority in 2026**: Every county board certification refusal since 2020 occurred during the canvass-and-certification phase, not on Election Day. In Washoe County, Nevada (2024), three of five board members initially refused to certify a recount during this phase. In Cochise County, Arizona (2022), the board voted against certifying during the canvass phase. The ACLU's $50 million midterm investment specifically includes deploying observers to monitor ballot counting and certification.[32]

**Legal basis for canvass access**: Canvassing proceedings are generally open government proceedings. Michigan's canvassing process is explicitly documented as publicly observable through michiganvoting.org. The Florida canvassing board process is open to observation by candidates, party officials, and authorized designees. The EAC's overview of election results, canvass, and certification confirms these processes are designed for public observation.[33]

**What to look for at canvass proceedings**:
1. **Provisional ballot resolution**: Each provisional ballot should be adjudicated on an individual basis. Document if large batches are being rejected without individualized review
2. **Missing returns**: If any precincts have discrepancies between poll closing tallies and tabulated results, these should be reconciled through documented process
3. **Board member conduct**: Is any board member signaling intent to delay or refuse certification? This should be documented verbatim and escalated immediately
4. **Equal access for parties**: Are all parties' observers being granted equivalent observation access?
5. **Timeline compliance**: Is the canvass proceeding on schedule to meet statutory deadlines? Document any stated intention to delay

**Certification deadlines for monitoring purposes**:
| State | County Canvass Deadline | State Certification |
|-------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Arizona | November 21, 2026 | November 25 |
| Georgia | November 12, 2026 (5 p.m.) | 17 days post-election |
| Michigan | November 15, 2026 (11 a.m.) | December 2 |
| Nevada | November 15, 2026 | November 26 |
| Pennsylvania | ~November 24 (third Monday) | By December |
| Wisconsin | November 19, 2026 | December 1 |

### 3.6 Phase Six: Post-Election Audit Observation

**What it is**: More than 44 states plus D.C. require some form of post-election tabulation audit. The gold standard is the risk-limiting audit (RLA) — a statistically rigorous method for confirming that the machine count is consistent with the paper record. Five states require RLAs: Colorado, Georgia, Nevada, Rhode Island, and Virginia. Thirty-four states require traditional fixed-percentage audits.[34]

**The Carter Center** publishes a specific guide for observing risk-limiting audits, which is the most technically demanding observation role.[35]

**What observers can do in audits**: "The public must be allowed to observe, verify, and point out procedural mistakes in all phases of the audit without interfering with the process." Observers should document: the randomization method used to select audit batches; whether all paper ballots are available and properly logged; the process for adjudicating ambiguous marks; and whether the audit result confirms or contradicts the machine count.[36]

**If an audit reveals discrepancies**: Document, escalate to your organization's legal team, and contact the EAC's election security programs. A significant audit discrepancy — particularly one that is unexplained and unreported — is the highest-priority escalation trigger.

## Section 4: Legal Rights When Things Go Wrong

### 4.1 When an Observer Is Removed

Removal from a polling place or counting center is not the end of the observation function — it is the beginning of a legal escalation process. The proper procedure when an observer is removed or denied access:

**Step 1 — In the moment**: Do not physically resist removal. Calmly ask the presiding officer (or board chair, for canvass proceedings) to state the specific basis for the removal and identify themselves. Request that your name and the stated basis be recorded in the official log. Do not argue — argue later, in writing, with counsel.

**Step 2 — Document immediately**: Write down every detail within minutes of removal — exact words spoken, names of all individuals present, time, location, and your position and activity at the time of removal. Photograph your credentials and the exterior of the polling place.

**Step 3 — Call your legal team**: Every trained observer should have a rapid-response legal contact before their shift begins. This is not optional. For Election Protection Coalition observers, the hotline is 866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683).[37] For Protect The Vote 2026 observers, contact your designated legal team lead.

**Step 4 — File a complaint**: Submit a formal written complaint to the county board of elections and the state secretary of state's office within 24 hours. Include all documentation from Step 2.

**Step 5 — Evaluate legal remedies**: If removal was improper — denial of access that should have been granted under state law — emergency injunctive relief is available in most states. The applicable standard is irreparable harm (the election will proceed without proper observation while litigation is pending), which courts have regularly found sufficient to grant emergency relief in election cases.

### 4.2 When a Board Refuses to Certify

This is the highest-priority escalation scenario in 2026. Documentation during canvass proceedings creates the evidentiary record for this emergency.

**The legal framework**: In every state, certification is a mandatory, ministerial duty — officials have no legal discretion to refuse. Multiple state supreme courts have so held. Courts in Arizona and New Mexico have granted writs of mandamus against county boards that refused to certify.[38] A Georgia superior court ruled in October 2024 that "no county board member may refuse to certify or abstain from certifying election results under any circumstance."[39]

**The writ of mandamus** is the primary legal remedy. A mandamus action asks a court to order an official to perform a ministerial duty required by law. Given the mandatory nature of certification, courts have consistently found mandamus appropriate and granted emergency relief. In some states — Michigan and Colorado — state officials have statutory authority to certify county results directly if the county refuses.[40]

**Who can seek mandamus**: Depending on state law, the following have standing: the secretary of state or other state election authority; candidates whose races are affected; and in some states, voters. Organizations with affected member-voters may also have organizational standing. Contact the Brennan Center's Democracy Program or Election Protection Coalition immediately if a board indicates intent to refuse certification.

**The contempt backstop**: If a board defies a mandamus order, courts can impose civil or criminal contempt sanctions. Courts have also appointed persons to complete certification at the disobedient official's expense in some states. The pattern since 2020: every refusal has ultimately been overridden, but the override requires days or weeks — and during that window, observer documentation of the refusal, the board members' statements, and their votes is the evidentiary foundation.

**The criminal dimension**: Knowing refusal to perform a statutory certification duty is a misdemeanor in Arizona (class 3) and carries penalties in multiple other states. The Arizona indictment of Cochise County supervisors (2022) for attempting to delay certification established prosecutorial precedent for using criminal law as an accountability tool.[41]

### 4.3 Voter Intimidation — Reporting and Response

VRA Section 11(b) prohibits intimidation by anyone, including other observers. If you witness conduct that you believe constitutes voter intimidation:

**In-person response**:
- Do not physically intervene
- Document the conduct — verbatim statements, descriptions of physical posturing or gestures, identity of individuals involved
- Alert polling place workers if the conduct is occurring inside or immediately outside the polling place
- Call 866-OUR-VOTE immediately

**After the incident**:
- Report to the county election board
- Report to the state attorney general
- Report to the DOJ Civil Rights Division's Election Day hotline at (800) 253-3931 — note: this hotline's status for 2026 is uncertain given DOJ command center dismantlement (see Section 5); the 866-OUR-VOTE coalition maintains a parallel intake system

**What constitutes voter intimidation**: Courts have found the following to constitute unlawful intimidation: physical threats; following or surveilling voters; improper threats of prosecution or arrest; posing intrusive questions about eligibility; deploying uniforms or tactical equipment near polling places; and conducting mass voter challenges without a good-faith individualized basis in a manner that communicates a threat to voters.[42]

**Mass challenge caution**: The Brennan Center documents that "lodging discriminatory voter challenges to voter eligibility or lodging frivolous challenges without a good faith basis" may constitute VRA Section 11(b) violations.[43] If you are working for an organization that uses bulk voter roll comparison data to generate challenge lists, consult legal counsel before executing any challenge that does not have a documented, individualized factual basis.

### 4.4 Observer Conduct — Removal Grounds and How to Avoid Them

The most common grounds for improper removal of legitimate observers are: speaking to voters; interfering with the voting process; handling election materials; photographing prohibited content; and being present in a location the observer was not credentialed to access. All are avoidable with proper training.

The most common grounds for appropriate removal of bad-faith observers are: disrupting the voting process; creating a hostile or intimidating environment for voters; challenging voters without individualized basis; violating state prohibitions on photography; and exceeding the numerical limits for observers per precinct.

The distinction matters for escalation: if an observer is removed without cause, that removal is potentially unlawful and subject to legal remedy. If an observer is removed for conduct that genuinely violates the rules, escalation is inappropriate — the organization deploying that observer should review its training.

## Section 5: The 2026 Context — Specific Threats Requiring Heightened Attention

### 5.1 DOJ Command Center Dismantlement

The DOJ's traditional Election Day command center — which served as a central hub for election officials, law enforcement, and the public to report voting irregularities and voting rights violations — was not established for 2026, according to reporting from the Daily Boulder and corroborated by election law practitioners.[44] This represents a structural gap: the federal reporting infrastructure designed to receive and escalate Election Day complaints has been eliminated.

The practical effect: citizen observers in 2026 must route incidents through state-level and organizational channels rather than assuming federal intake capacity exists. The 866-OUR-VOTE coalition and state attorneys general offices become the primary escalation pathways.

**Contact alternatives for federal-level violations**:
- DOJ Civil Rights Division, Voting Section: (202) 307-2767 (main line)
- FBI field offices for potential criminal activity (document and call after Election Day)
- State AG offices — see Section 8 for state-specific contacts

### 5.2 Federal Observer Program Under Review

The federal election observer program — authorized under the VRA and administered through OPM since 1966 — sent over 1,000 observers to 100+ counties at its peak. Following the *Shelby County v. Holder* ruling (2013), coverage collapsed to a handful of court-ordered jurisdictions. In March 2026, the White House began reviewing the program for defunding, with only three or four jurisdictions still covered under court orders (Union County NJ, Pawtucket RI, and two Alaska areas).[45]

The implication: the federal observation backstop that historically supplemented citizen observers in high-risk jurisdictions is no longer reliable. Citizen observer networks must treat themselves as the primary observation infrastructure, not a supplement.

### 5.3 Weaponized DOJ Election Monitoring

## Section 7: Documentation Standards and Templates

### 7.1 The Documentation Mandate

Documentation is not optional in election observation — it is the primary output of the observation function. An observer who witnesses a violation and does not document it contemporaneously provides minimal value to legal proceedings. Courts evaluating emergency election relief applications need: specific facts, identified individuals, timestamps, and corroboration. An observer's notebook or timestamped photographs provide exactly this.

**The five documentation standards**:
1. **Contemporaneous**: Write it down at the moment, not hours later. Memory is unreliable under stress.
2. **Specific**: Names, badge numbers, exact quotes (use quotation marks only for verbatim words), times to the minute, precise locations.
3. **Objective**: Describe behavior, not inferences. "Supervisor Jones told voter Smith she was not on the list and pointed at the door" is documentation. "Supervisor Jones intimidated voter Smith" is a legal conclusion — save that for your legal team's memo.
4. **Corroborated**: Two observers documenting the same incident is significantly stronger than one. Coordinate with your partner observer.
5. **Preserved**: Send your notes to your legal team lead and your organization's incident intake by 10 p.m. on Election Day, regardless of whether you believe anything significant occurred.

## Section 9: Specific Scenarios — Decision Trees

### 9.1 A Board Member Announces Intent to Refuse Certification

1. Document verbatim statement, time, who said it
2. Call legal team lead immediately — do not wait until the meeting ends
3. Note every other board member's response
4. Do not leave the meeting — continue documenting
5. After the meeting: submit incident report to your organization, state AG, and Brennan Center Democracy Program (brennancenter.org)
6. Legal team pursues mandamus relief immediately — courts have granted emergency certification orders in hours when properly documented

### 9.2 An Observer Is Prevented From Entering a Polling Place

1. Remain calm; do not attempt to force entry
2. Ask the person preventing entry to identify themselves and state the legal basis
3. Call your legal team lead immediately
4. Call 866-OUR-VOTE
5. Document: time, names, exact words used
6. Wait outside and document all activity visible from the public area
7. If the denial is clearly improper (you have valid credentials and the state law grants you access), your legal team can seek emergency injunctive relief

### 9.3 You Witness Apparent Voter Intimidation

1. Do not physically intervene
2. Document in detail — who, what, when, where, exact words or gestures
3. Call 866-OUR-VOTE
4. Alert polling place workers
5. For federal law enforcement presence near polling place without specific criminal response: additionally contact the ACLU immediately and document every identifying detail about the personnel and vehicles
6. Submit incident report within the hour; this is priority escalation

### 9.4 An Audit Reveals an Unexplained Discrepancy

1. Document the discrepancy — machine totals vs. paper totals, specific race, margin of discrepancy
2. Document whether election officials are acknowledging and investigating the discrepancy or dismissing it
3. Do not speculate about cause; document what you observe
4. Contact your organization's legal team and the Brennan Center's election security team
5. Contact the EAC election security program (eac.gov/election-officials/election-security)
6. Contact Verified Voting (verifiedvoting.org/election-protection) which maintains audit expertise resources

## Section 11: The Comparative Framework — Why Citizen Observation Works

### 11.1 The Historical Record

The effectiveness of citizen election observation as a democratic safeguard has been demonstrated across multiple contexts. The Carter Center's international election observation methodology — which has validated elections and identified fraud in dozens of countries — was adapted for domestic U.S. use beginning in 2020 and applied at scale in Fulton County, Georgia and in New Mexico and Montana in 2024.[65]

The domestic record since 2020 is instructive:
- **Michigan (2024)**: ACLU litigation against a board member who announced intent to refuse certification resulted in sworn affidavit to certify within one week of filing — deterrence without a court order
- **New Mexico (2022, 2024)**: Mandamus proceedings against certification-refusing county boards resulted in court orders within days; elections certified on schedule
- **Nevada Washoe County (2024)**: Three board members initially refused to certify a recount; after the Nevada Secretary of State sought court compulsion, two of the three relented and the election was certified[66]
- **Pennsylvania (2022)**: Lancaster, Fayette, and Berks Counties required court orders to compel certification — in each case, the court acted and certification occurred[67]

The pattern: **no certification refusal has succeeded in preventing or overturning an election result.** Observer presence, documentation, and the rapid-response legal infrastructure connected to observers has been essential to that record. The question for 2026 is not whether the legal tools work — they do — but whether the observer network is large enough, trained enough, and geographically distributed enough to create sufficient deterrence and documentation capacity across the highest-risk jurisdictions.

### 11.2 The 2026 Specific Stakes

The stakes of the November 2026 midterms are specifically high along three dimensions that make citizen observation uniquely important:

**Congressional control**: Multiple competitive House races will determine which party controls the House. If any race's certification is contested and delayed beyond the congressional seating deadline, the implications extend to the composition of Congress itself and the certification of the 2028 presidential election — which will be conducted by the Congress seated in January 2027.

**Secretaries of state and AGs**: Multiple swing states are electing the officials who will administer the 2028 presidential election. The election integrity of those races — including their certification — will affect the integrity of two election cycles.

**The certification precedent**: If a certification refusal succeeds in 2026 — even temporarily, even in a non-decisive race — it will establish that the threat is credible and can be operationalized. The legal record built by citizen observers in 2026 is the precedent record for 2028.
