## Section 2: Phase 2 Domain Prioritization Per Outcome

### Scenario A: Strong Outcome (>40% engagement)

**Strategic read**: Batch 1 constituencies have validated the framework. The credibility anchors are engaged. Phase 2 launches at full intensity immediately. The sequencing question is: which domains reach which audiences at maximum policy-window alignment?

**Domain 57 and Domain 59 — parallel tracks, June 15 research launch, dual August 10 completion target.**

With strong Phase 1 validation, the full 90–110 hour Phase 2 research budget activates on schedule. Domain 59 (Economic Precarity as Civic Exclusion) enters its research phase from June 15 — Sections 1–3 cover the income-participation gap, the scarcity framework (Mullainathan/Shafir applied to civic participation), and the housing instability cascade. Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal) enters pre-production from June 15 — confirming all 21 primary sources live, acquiring Ikenberry's "Liberal Leviathan," and completing the constitutional asymmetry analysis (CRS R48868 deep read). These two domains have zero substantive interdependency; parallel execution is viable.

Resource allocation for a Strong outcome: 55–65 hours to Domain 59 (higher research load because Section 5's OBBBA multiplicative compounding analysis is the most data-intensive single synthesis task in Phase 2); 45–51 hours to Domain 57. Domain 59's compounding argument — that simultaneous OBBBA cuts to Medicaid, SNAP, housing assistance, childcare subsidies, and student loan protections produce a multiplicative rather than additive reduction in civic participation capacity for 80 million Americans — requires sustained 8–12 hour synthesis blocks. Build protected writing days in Week 7 (July 6–12).

**Domains 38–40 — distribution-only under Strong, no additional production research required.**

Domains 38 (AI Regulatory Capture), 39 (Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure), and 40 (Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation) have production-ready outlines. Under a Strong outcome, the priority is distributing these to the constituencies that validated the framework — not diverting research bandwidth from Domain 57/59. The exception that holds regardless of outcome: Domain 39's June 1 HHS interim final rule deadline creates a one-time, non-negotiable distribution window. Pre-distribute Domain 39 to healthcare advocacy and voting rights contacts by June 1 using the existing outline.

**Tier 2 pre-contact activates Week 5 (June 15–21)**. Strong Phase 1 validation is the most important Tier 2 trigger — it provides the social proof framing ("strong initial response from law schools and policy organizations") that most persuades immigration legal aid, faith organizations, and labor contacts who respond to institutional momentum.

### Scenario B: Moderate Outcome (25–40% engagement)

**Strategic read**: The framework is landing but not igniting. At least one sector is engaged; others are watching. Sequencing shifts to: which domain reaches the most receptive new audience fastest, independent of Phase 1 momentum?

**Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal) — primary track, June 10 research launch, August 10 completion.**

Domain 57 is the primary domain under a Moderate outcome for a specific strategic reason: its primary audiences (international law organizations, Senate Foreign Relations Committee staff, climate advocacy networks affected by UNFCCC/IPCC withdrawal, democracy promotion organizations like NDI and IRI) are largely separate from the Phase 1 Batch 1 constituency. This means Domain 57 expands reach to new audiences regardless of Phase 1 performance — it does not depend on Phase 1 momentum to find its audience. Under a Moderate outcome, this independence from Phase 1 performance is the decisive advantage. Start June 10; target August 10 completion to capture the full six-week UNGA 81 pre-distribution window.

**Domain 59 (Economic Precarity) — secondary track, July 1 research start, August 1 distribution target.**

Under a Moderate outcome, Domain 59 research begins no later than July 1 (not June 15 as in the Strong scenario). This one-week slip absorbs zero buffer against the August 1 distribution target for voting rights and economic justice organizations. Do not push Domain 59 research start past July 1 regardless of Phase 1 signals. The November 3 midterm deadline does not respond to Phase 1 performance.

**Domains 38–40**: Domain 39 retains its June 1 pre-distribution priority regardless of outcome. Domains 38 and 40 are deferred to the production queue following Domain 57 completion (after August 10), unless specific constituency signals from the Phase 1 engagement data indicate a strong AI governance or electoral security audience interest.

**Tier 2 pre-contact activates Week 6 (June 22–28)**. One additional week of Phase 1 data from Domain 56 and Domain 58 distribution responses provides sharper engagement signals to inform Tier 2 customization for each sector.

### Scenario C: Weak Outcome (<25% engagement)

**Strategic read**: The framework is not cutting through. The critical diagnosis question is whether the problem is messaging/framing, contact list selection, or technical delivery failure. The diagnosis drives everything. Domains 38–40 become the immediate research priority because they are faster to produce (10–14 hours each vs. 45–65 hours for 57/59) and have the most time-sensitive independent external deadlines.

**Domains 38–40 — immediate priority, June 1 to July 15.**

Domain 39 (Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure) has the June 1 HHS deadline and the clearest immediate policy hook — its production outline is already complete and can go to pre-distribution within one 3-hour session. Domain 38 (AI Regulatory Capture) has the August 2 EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement event — a media-visible deadline that generates attention independent of Phase 1 momentum. Domain 40 (Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation) has the November 3 election hard constraint and must reach election protection organizations by July 15 at the latest.

Under a Weak outcome, produce Domains 39, 38, and 40 in that sequence, June 1–July 15. Total production estimate: 30–42 hours across six weeks. This is the fastest-turn, highest-leverage path given the constraint environment.

**Pivot assessment at Week 5 (June 15)**: Has Phase 1 remediation recovered to Moderate? If yes, shift to the Moderate scenario from Week 6. If still Weak, complete Domains 38–40 and defer Domains 57/59 to August, accepting reduced UNGA 81 lead time (3 weeks instead of 6) and no peer review buffer for Domain 59.

**Domain 57 and Domain 59 under sustained Weak**: Domain 57 research begins August 1, targeting September 1 completion (three weeks before UNGA 81 High-Level Week — reduced from six weeks but still actionable for Senate Foreign Relations Committee staff). Domain 59 research begins July 15, targeting August 31 completion. The August 31 Domain 59 completion still reaches voting rights and economic justice organizations before mid-September, which is sufficient for organizations with October advocacy deadlines.

**Tier 2 pre-contact holds until Week 7 (June 29–July 5) under a Weak outcome**. Do not activate Tier 2 while Phase 1 remediation is underway. The Domain 39 distribution results (Weeks 3–4) provide the first post-Wave-1 social proof data point that anchors Tier 2 outreach.

### Scenario D: Split Outcome (Strong in one sector, Weak in another)

**Strategic read**: Split outcomes are the most information-rich result. They reveal which framing is working and which needs revision before Tier 2. This is the most likely real-world outcome given the different response cycle timing across constituencies.

**How to read a split**: If law schools are Strong and think tanks are Weak, the academic framing is working but the policy-shop framing needs revision. If immigration legal aid is Strong but unions are Weak, the litigation-utility angle is landing but the worker-power angle needs adjustment. Use the signals from the strong sector to revise messaging for the weak sector before Tier 2.

**Domain sequencing for Split**: Follow the STRONG scenario for domains that serve performing constituencies; follow MODERATE or WEAK for domains serving underperforming ones. Example: if law schools are Strong and unions are Weak, launch Domain 57 (constitutional law angle — strong law school resonance) on the Strong timeline (June 15) and delay Domain 59 (economic precarity/worker power angle — union resonance) to the Moderate timeline (July 1 start).

## Section 3: Movement Partner Engagement Angles

Each constituency requires a distinct translation of the framework's core analysis into the operational language of their work. These angles are the connective tissue between Phase 1 and Phase 2 — they determine whether the framework becomes a reference document or a tool.

### Law Schools — Election Protection Infrastructure

**Primary Phase 2 domains**: Domain 57 (constitutional asymmetry in treaty withdrawal power — clinic-applicable constitutional design argument); Domain 39 (OBBBA/NVRA nexus — health law and election law clinics can use simultaneously); Domain 40 (electoral manipulation — election law clinics with active surveillance law work).

**Engagement angle**: Law school clinics are positioned to advance the specific advocacy gap that Domain 57 identifies: for 65 of the 66 organizations withdrawn from in January 2026, no equivalent to the 2024 NDAA Section 1250A NATO protection exists. The Treaty Withdrawal Notification Act — the legislative model Domain 57 proposes — is exactly the kind of constitutional design reform that law school election and constitutional clinics are positioned to develop, draft, and support through amicus work and congressional testimony.

The health law clinic angle on Domain 39 is operationally distinct from the constitutional law angle: the APSR rural hospital closure study (3.8 percentage-point voter turnout reduction) and the NVRA Section 7 enforcement theory give health law clinics a democratic participation argument for healthcare access that is not found in existing health law scholarship. The June 1 HHS interim final rule opens the comment and litigation window immediately.

**What law schools most want from Phase 2 outreach**: Analysis that holds up under peer review; constitutional arguments that can be cited in clinic work; a clear ask that matches clinic capacity (submit amicus, testify, write a law review note, draft model legislation). Do not ask law schools to organize or advocate — ask them to analyze and advise.

### Unions — Worker Power Amplification

**Primary Phase 2 domains**: Domain 59 (Economic Precarity as Civic Exclusion — the gig economy temporal exclusion mechanism and OBBBA multiplicative cuts are directly applicable to member education and legislative testimony); Domain 39 (Healthcare Access — SEIU represents healthcare workers; rural hospital closures in union-heavy communities are a member-impact argument, not an abstract democratic design point).

**Engagement angle**: The Domain 59 reform architecture includes a federal right to paid time off for voting explicitly extended to gig and independent contractor workers — a proposal that several unions are already advancing on the legislative front. This is not background research; it is a ready-made legislative argument. For AFL-CIO and SEIU legislative staff, the quantified compounding analysis of OBBBA cuts (Medicaid + SNAP + housing assistance + childcare subsidies + student loan changes = multiplicative, not additive, civic participation reduction for 80 million Americans) provides the data-dense testimony support that floor statement preparation requires.

The Domain 39 rural hospital closure finding is specifically powerful for unions in rural-heavy sectors (UAW, USW, UFCW): a measured 3.8 percentage-point voter turnout decline per APSR study connects healthcare infrastructure loss to union member political participation in a way that neither the health policy literature nor the voting rights literature makes explicit.

**What unions most want from Phase 2 outreach**: Research that can go into member education, legislative testimony, or floor statement support without requiring a policy analyst to translate it. Domain 59's quantitative framing and Domain 39's causal evidence are both ready to use in that format. Offer domain extracts formatted for non-specialist audiences.

### Immigration Legal Aid — Border Security and Multilateral Withdrawal Connections

**Primary Phase 2 domains**: Domain 57 (ICC sanctions chilling effect on US attorneys cooperating with international accountability mechanisms — a specific legal consequence with direct immigration attorney exposure); Domain 39 (OBBBA Medicaid work requirements and coverage losses disproportionately affecting documented and undocumented immigrant families; NVRA/Medicaid enrollment nexus).

**Engagement angle**: The ICC sanctions analysis in Domain 57 Section 3 documents a specific legal consequence that is underappreciated in immigration legal aid work: the February 2025 ICC sanctions make it a federal offense (up to 20 years) for any US person to provide material support to ICC investigations. This extends to American lawyers and civil society organizations cooperating with ICC investigations of potential atrocities — including potential future investigations in the context of mass deportations, prolonged detention, or use of force against protesters. The Coalition for the ICC's "Criminalising Accountability" report (2026) documents current chilling effects on civil society. Immigration legal aid organizations' capacity to invoke international accountability frameworks on behalf of clients may be directly constrained by this mechanism right now.

The Domain 39 OBBBA analysis is time-urgent: the HHS June 1 interim final rule covers Medicaid work requirements that project 11.8 million coverage losses (CBO), with disproportionate impact on immigrant families. The NVRA/Medicaid nexus is directly relevant: Medicaid enrollment offices are statutory voter registration sites under 52 U.S.C. § 20506, and administrative contraction under OBBBA degrades voter registration infrastructure for exactly the populations immigration legal aid serves.

**What immigration legal aid most wants from Phase 2 outreach**: Concrete legal consequences their clients and attorneys face; actionable research they can use in filings and client communications; analysis of time-sensitive windows (the June 1 HHS rule is the most urgent). Do not lead with abstract democratic design framing — lead with specific legal exposure and policy windows.

**Primary Phase 2 domains**: Domain 57 (analytically novel — makes an argument not found in existing foreign policy or constitutional law literature); Domain 59 (provides the quantitative compounding argument that existing poverty policy literature cannot make because it analyzes a single program at a time); Domain 39 (June 1 HHS deadline creates immediate policy relevance); Domain 40 (EU AI Act Article 50 August 2 deadline provides international comparison argument).

Domain 59's compounding argument is the synthesis contribution that economic think tanks are positioned to extend through their own data access and congressional testimony infrastructure.

The most persuasive Tier 2 approach for foreign policy think tanks (CFR, Carnegie, Brookings, Atlantic Council) is offering research-in-progress access to Domain 57's working outline before completion — the UNGA 81 High-Level Week (September 22–28) is the shared policy window, and coordination at the outline stage allows think tank researchers to incorporate the analysis into their own UNGA preparation materials.

The analytical novel contribution — quantifying multiplicative OBBBA compounding — is precisely the kind of structural argument that think tank researchers can extend into their own publications with access to program-specific datasets that the academic literature does not have.

**What think tanks most want from Phase 2 outreach**: Analytical novelty, timing alignment with their own publication pipelines, and a clear coordination pathway (shared source list, working draft access, co-publication conversation). They do not need a polished final document — they need to know the analysis is credible and timely enough to integrate into their own work. Offer early access at the outline stage.

### Messaging Frame by Outcome

**Strong outcome messaging**: Lead with social proof and momentum. Frame: "The framework has generated stronger initial engagement than anticipated from policy practitioners — [specific signal]. I'm now in Tier 2 outreach to [constituency] because [specific reason this constituency's perspective is needed next]." The social proof framing is most effective when the named signals are specific: not "strong response" but "practitioners at Protect Democracy and the Brennan Center asked to adapt domain analyses for active litigation filings." Specificity signals credibility and filters for high-quality responses.

**Moderate outcome messaging**: Lead with constituency-specific utility, not broad momentum. Frame: "Initial distribution has produced engagement from [strongest-performing sector]. I'm reaching out to [new constituency] now because the two most applicable domains to your work — [Domain X] and [Domain Y] — connect [specific issue] in ways that the existing literature doesn't address systematically." The utility-of-specific-domains framing works independently of social proof volume — it appeals to the reader's professional self-interest rather than institutional momentum.

**Weak outcome messaging**: Lead entirely with domain utility and time-urgency. Drop the social proof framing entirely. Frame: "I'm distributing a democratic reform framework to [constituency] specifically because [domain X] addresses [specific operational problem] with [specific time-sensitive window]. The research is available as a free resource with no institutional strings." Under a Weak outcome, the strongest framing is maximum specificity about what the research provides and minimum framing about how well it has been received. An honest offer of specific, timely, useful research is more persuasive than a thin social proof claim.

### Per-Outcome Pre-Contact Days

**If Strong**: Tier 2 pre-contact Days 20–35 (June 7–22). Messaging emphasizes Phase 1 momentum plus Phase 2 urgency — specifically the time-critical windows for Domain 57 (August 10 UNGA deadline) and Domain 39 (June 1 HHS deadline). The combination of momentum framing and deadline urgency creates the strongest call to action: the framework is demonstrably resonating, and the window for engagement is specific and closing.

**If Moderate**: Tier 2 pre-contact Week 6+ (June 22–28). Messaging emphasizes institutional adoption precedent — "practitioners at [specific org] are adapting this analysis" — and policy window specificity. The distinction from Strong: you are not claiming broad momentum; you are claiming specific precedent from the strongest-performing sector, and you are offering the new constituency an early position before the full distribution cycle.

**If Weak**: Tier 2 pre-contact delayed to Day 45+ (July 2 at earliest). Messaging pivots to slow-burn institutional adoption framing: law schools (emphasize methodological rigor and clinic applicability with zero social proof claim); unions (emphasize specific legislative utility of Domain 59's compounding analysis and the federal voting time-off proposal). The slow-burn frame is: "This is analysis that will be relevant to your work regardless of when you engage with it; I'm offering it now because the June 1 and November 3 deadlines create windows that are time-sensitive."
