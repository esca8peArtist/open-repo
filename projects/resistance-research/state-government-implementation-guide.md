# State Government Implementation Guide
## For State Executive and Administrative Agencies

**Who this is for**: Governors' policy staff, agency deputy secretaries and directors, state attorneys general and their staff, state election officials, and state budget office staff. Also relevant for state legislative fiscal offices and executive agency counsel.

**The core task**: Use the 35-domain framework to (a) implement framework recommendations within existing state executive authority, (b) build institutional resilience against federal pressure and potential hostile federal action, and (c) coordinate across states to fill gaps that the federal government has vacated.

---

## The Monday Morning Question

You are a state agency director or a governor's policy chief. Your federal counterpart agency has been defunded, reorganized, or captured. You have existing staff, an existing budget, and existing statutory authority — but no federal backup. What do you do?

Answer: identify which federal functions your state can absorb, which you cannot absorb alone but can absorb in coordination with peer states, and which require new state authority. Then sequence the actions using the framework's wave structure.

---

## Five-Step Decision Tree

**Step 1: Audit which federal functions your agency is now de facto responsible for.**

In every domain where federal capacity has been degraded — USAID effectively destroyed, CFPB targeted, EPA rolled back, USDA supplemental nutrition programs cut, DOJ civil rights enforcement hollowed — state agencies face a choice: absorb the function, coordinate with other states to share the load, or allow the gap. The framework's implementation roadmap (Section 3.4) documents the parallel institutional infrastructure the U.S. already has at the state level.

Conduct a domain-by-domain audit: for each of the 35 domains, what federal functions in your domain did your state rely on? Which of those functions have been degraded or eliminated? Which can your agency absorb with existing authority? Which require new legislation? Which can be shared with peer states through an interstate compact or MOU?

**Step 2: Identify your state's "democratic firewall" assets.**

The States United Democracy Center's 2025 analysis documented how 20+ state AGs recovered frozen federal funding through coordinated litigation and how 19 state AGs blocked the executive order threatening voter data security. Your state's democratic firewall assets include: (a) the AG office's litigation capacity and existing legal coalitions; (b) the Secretary of State's election administration capacity and independence; (c) the governor's executive order authority to protect state agency data, employees, and operations from federal demands; and (d) your state's existing interstate compact memberships.

Map these assets against the framework's Wave 1 domains. The framework's Wave 1 (Domains 1, 2, 6, 7, 8) identifies the minimum institutional floor that must be preserved. Your state's firewall assets are the tools for preserving that floor.

**Step 3: Engage the Governors Safeguarding Democracy coordination infrastructure.**

The GSD network (launched November 2024, co-chaired by Pritzker and Polis) develops playbooks for state executives to respond to democratic threats. GSD provides legal and policy resources, coordinates across state lines on bill language, and hosts briefings on emerging federal actions. For any state in the GSD network, the first step before implementing a framework domain recommendation is checking whether GSD has already developed a playbook for that domain and whether peer states are coordinating on the same action.

Even for states not in GSD, the coordination model is applicable: identify 3-5 peer states (similar size, similar institutional capacity, similar political alignment) and establish a standing coordination mechanism — a monthly call, a shared Slack or secure channel, a shared legal resource pool — before you need it. The 65 AG lawsuits in 2025 were effective because the coalition was pre-built before the federal actions they targeted.

**Step 4: Use the framework's interstate compact analysis (Domain 9) to identify coordination opportunities.**

Domain 9 (Federalism and Local Power) documents the RGGI model — 12 northeastern states coordinating on carbon emissions through an interstate compact, producing 50%+ emissions reduction and $7.4B in clean energy investment without federal legislation. The same compact mechanism is available for any domain where federal capacity has been vacated. Examples:

- A multistate data privacy compact (Domain 21) that establishes shared privacy standards and a joint enforcement mechanism in the absence of federal privacy legislation
- A multistate election security compact (Domain 37b, in scoping) that coordinates state-level cybersecurity for election infrastructure after CISA was defunded
- A multistate Medicaid coordination compact (Domain 11/31) that enables states to negotiate drug prices jointly when federal negotiation is blocked

For each domain where federal function has been vacated, the question is: is there an existing compact that covers this, and can we join or strengthen it? If not, which 5-7 states have the capacity to form one?

**Step 5: Protect your own institutional integrity against capture pressure.**

Domain 2 (Institutional Integrity) documents the federal Schedule F model and DOGE approach — mass reclassification of civil servants as at-will employees, followed by ideologically motivated firing. State governments face analogous pressure from federal threats to condition grants on compliance with federal ideological demands.

Before you face the demand, establish the institutional protections: (a) document your agency's current merit-based civil service protections and identify any gaps that create Schedule F-equivalent vulnerabilities; (b) establish clear written protocols for how your agency responds to federal information demands that exceed statutory authority; (c) ensure your agency counsel has reviewed the legal basis for refusing specific categories of federal demands; and (d) create secure backup systems for critical agency data that are not accessible to federal systems that may be compromised.

## Three Concrete Domain Applications

**Application 1: Using Domain 16 (Immigration) to implement community-based alternatives to ICE detention in your state.**

Domain 16 documents that community-based alternatives to detention cost $20/day versus $140-200/day for ICE detention, and that community alternatives maintain 99% compliance rates. States with significant immigrant populations can implement state-funded community monitoring programs that provide alternatives to federal detention when ICE capacity is unavailable or when federal detention conditions raise state law concerns. The legal basis: state welfare and public safety authority. The political basis: documented cost savings. The framework's Domain 16 analysis provides the evidence base for the state budget justification.

**Application 2: Using Domain 8 (Media and Information) to fund state-level local news infrastructure.**

Domain 8 documents that local news closures correlate with increased municipal corruption and lower voter turnout. New Jersey's Civic Information Consortium — a public-private partnership providing state matching grants for local news startups — is documented in Domain 8 as the most successful U.S. model. A governor can direct existing state arts and cultural funding toward a local news consortium pilot through executive action, without new legislation. The New Jersey model has been operating since 2018 with documented outcomes; the framework's Domain 8 analysis provides the cost-effectiveness evidence for the budget justification.

**Application 3: Using Domain 2 (Institutional Integrity) to issue an executive order protecting state civil servants.**

Domain 2 documents the federal Schedule F reclassification mechanism and the specific legal vulnerabilities that made it possible. The state-level response: a governor's executive order establishing that state merit civil service employees may not be reclassified as at-will on the basis of political activity or views, with a specific process requiring legislative approval for any such reclassification. This is within standard gubernatorial executive authority in most states. The order creates a documented legal record that can support litigation if the order is challenged. Several states issued such protective orders in 2025 following the federal Schedule F final rule.

## Common Mistakes in State Government Settings

**Waiting for federal leadership that is not coming.** Agencies that are accustomed to federal guidance before acting are structurally disadvantaged in an environment where federal guidance has been weaponized or eliminated. The framework's Wave 2 analysis is explicit: state capacity must be built on the assumption that federal support is unavailable, not as a supplement to federal support.

**Treating coalition-building as a soft, optional activity.** The 2025 AG coalition lawsuits recovered billions in frozen federal funding. That result was only possible because the coalition existed before the freeze — AGs had established working relationships, shared legal analysis, and coordinated litigation strategy before they needed to deploy it. Coalition-building is state capacity infrastructure, not relationship maintenance.

**Underestimating data sovereignty.** Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Surveillance) documents the risk that federal systems compromised by DOGE data access create spillover vulnerabilities for state systems connected to federal databases. Every state agency should have a data sovereignty audit: which state databases connect to federal systems that may have been compromised? What data does your agency share with federal systems that could be used against your state's residents? What is your protocol for disconnecting from compromised federal data feeds?

## Implementation Tracking Checklist

- [ ] Domain-by-domain audit of vacated federal functions completed
- [ ] Democratic firewall assets mapped (AG, SoS, governor EO authority, compacts)
- [ ] GSD coordination check completed for each priority domain
- [ ] Interstate compact opportunities identified per Domain 9 analysis
- [ ] Schedule F vulnerability audit completed for state civil service
- [ ] Federal information demand response protocols established in writing
- [ ] Agency data sovereignty audit completed
- [ ] Peer state coordination mechanism established
- [ ] State budget justifications drafted using framework fiscal evidence
