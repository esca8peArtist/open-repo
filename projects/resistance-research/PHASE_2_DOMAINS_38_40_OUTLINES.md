## Domain 38: Regulatory Capture in AI/Technology Governance

### Title and Core Argument

**Title**: Regulatory Capture in AI Governance: How Industry Shapes the Rules That Were Supposed to Govern It

**Core argument**: The United States has no statutory framework for AI governance — the only federal accountability architecture was Biden's Executive Order 14110, revoked by Trump on January 20, 2025. In that vacuum, the rules that govern AI development and deployment are being written by the industry being governed: through voluntary standards (NIST AI RMF), revolving-door placement in regulatory agencies, industry-led standards bodies, and a December 2025 executive order directing four federal agencies to preempt state AI laws that would impose binding accountability requirements. This is regulatory capture with a specific structural feature: the captured institutions have no statutory authority to be captured — the capture is happening at the standard-setting layer before binding law is even enacted. The EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement deadline (August 2, 2026) will create a transatlantic accountability divergence that puts US markets and democratic institutions at structural disadvantage. Reform requires statutory AI governance authority, independent audit mechanisms, and capture-resistant standards processes — the same four-layer reform architecture as corporate regulatory capture, applied specifically to the AI governance context.

### Priority Research Areas

**1. The Statutory Vacuum — What the Regulatory Capture Is Filling**

The US has no general federal AI statute. This is the architectural fact that makes regulatory capture structurally possible. The Biden EO 14110 (Oct. 2023) created a chief AI officer requirement, impact assessments for high-impact AI, and transparency mandates — all revocable by executive action, confirmed by Trump's Jan. 20, 2025 revocation within hours of taking office. Trump's replacement (EO 14179, Jan. 23, 2025) removed accountability requirements and directed agencies to review all policies inconsistent with "minimally burdensome" AI development. The July 23, 2025 "Winning the Race" Action Plan extended this into an affirmative deregulatory posture. The resulting governance architecture is entirely voluntary: NIST AI RMF (self-administered), OMB M-25-21/M-25-22 (guidance memos, not regulations, no enforcement), agency AI use case inventories (self-reported, no independent audit).

*Source categories*: White House EO texts (whitehouse.gov), GAO reports on federal AI acquisition (GAO-25-107933, GAO-26-107859), OMB guidance memos (M-25-21, M-25-22), NIST AI RMF documentation, International AI Safety Report 2026.

**2. The Four Capture Mechanisms in AI Governance Specifically**

The domain should apply a four-layer capture analysis (parallel to the corporate regulatory capture framework) to AI governance specifically:

- *Revolving door*: Research documented 24% of high-profile AI regulatory incidents involve revolving door placements (arxiv.org/html/2605.06806 — "Big AI's Regulatory Capture"). Specific instances: [needs verification from revolving door databases] in NIST AI standards working groups, OMB AI policy positions, and DHS AI acquisition leadership.
- *Standards body capture*: NIST AI RMF workshops and working groups are heavily industry-attended. NIST's AISCWG (Interagency Committee on Standards Policy AI Standards Coordination Working Group) coordinates government positions on international AI standards — primarily through participation by industry-affiliated academic researchers and company employees. The "AI Agent Standards Initiative" explicitly seeks industry-led protocol development.
- *Coalition lobbying*: The Partnership on AI (PAI), technically a civil society / industry hybrid, shapes the vocabulary of AI governance norms. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and Meta are all PAI members. The AI Alliance (launched by IBM and Meta, December 2023) advocates for "open-source AI" norms that oppose binding safety requirements.
- *Legal preemption as capture tool*: The December 11, 2025 EO directing DOJ, Commerce, FTC, and FCC to challenge state AI accountability laws is a novel capture mechanism: using federal regulatory authority preemptively to eliminate state-level accountability requirements before federal binding rules exist. This creates a governance vacuum by design.

*Source categories*: arxiv.org big AI capture paper (2605.06806), EO texts, law firm analyses of Dec. 2025 preemption EO (Sidley, Hogan Lovells, Paul Hastings), state AI law tracker (Baker Botts Jan. 2026 report), NIST workshop attendance records.

**3. Industry-Led Standards as De Facto Policy**

NIST AI RMF is voluntary but functions as de facto binding through three mechanisms: (1) OMB guidance incorporates it by reference for federal contractors, (2) state AI legislation uses it as a floor reference, (3) private litigation increasingly cites RMF compliance/non-compliance as a duty-of-care standard. This creates a situation where the entity that effectively shapes AI accountability norms is NIST — an agency whose standards development process is predominantly industry-attended — without any democratic authorization, public comment requirement, or enforcement authority.

The International AI Safety Report 2026 (Yoshua Bengio et al., 100+ experts, 30+ nations) specifically called out this dynamic: current governance is "fragmented, largely voluntary, and difficult to evaluate due to limited incident reporting and transparency," and recommended independent auditing and pre-deployment testing requirements. The contrast between the international expert consensus and the US policy posture is the core of the reform argument.

*Source categories*: NIST AI RMF documentation, International AI Safety Report 2026 (internationalaisafetyreport.org), OMB M-24-10 (Biden-era incorporation by reference), state AI legislation tracker.

**4. EU AI Act as External Accountability Pressure — and the Regulatory Arbitrage Risk**

EU AI Act Article 50 enforcement begins August 2, 2026. Article 50 requires: machine-readable marking and detectability of AI-generated synthetic content; disclosure by deployers when AI creates realistic deepfakes. The EU AI Office's Code of Practice on AI-Generated Content Transparency (first draft December 17, 2025; finalized May-June 2026) operationalizes this. US companies that operate in EU markets must comply; US companies that operate only domestically face no equivalent requirement. This creates a "regulatory arbitrage" structure in which the least accountable AI deployments are channeled into US domestic political contexts (elections, voter targeting, benefits determinations) where enforcement is weakest.

The December 2025 US-EU regulatory tension: Trump threatened "immediate and substantial retaliation" against EU enforcement of the Digital Markets Act and DSA, which collectively target Apple, Google, Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft for potential fines exceeding €100 billion. This posture of attacking EU accountability frameworks while imposing no domestic equivalents is the most concrete expression of the regulatory capture problem at the international level.

*Source categories*: EU AI Act Article 50 text (artificialintelligenceact.eu), EU Code of Practice drafts (digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu), EU AI Act enforcement timeline analysis (resemble.ai, Jones Day), US-EU trade tension reporting (European Business Magazine).

**5. Reform Pathways — What Statutory AI Governance Would Require**

This section is the domain's policy contribution. Four reform elements:

- *Federal AI Accountability Act* [no currently enacted legislation — identify best bill text]: Statutory mandate for pre-deployment impact assessments, human review requirements for high-stakes decisions, algorithmic transparency, and independent audit authority.
- *NIST reform*: Require that AI standards development processes include mandatory public comment periods, prohibit participation in standard-setting for products subject to the standard (conflict of interest rule), and publish attendance records.
- *State law floor protection*: Federal legislation should set a floor (minimum standards), not a ceiling — preserving state authority to go further. The December 2025 preemption EO should be reversed; the framework should recommend the inverse architecture.
- *Independent AI oversight body*: Following the International AI Safety Report 2026 model — an independent, multi-stakeholder body with auditing authority, distinct from NIST (which is a standards body, not a regulator).

*Source categories*: Congressional AI legislation tracker, CAIP policy recommendations, Center for AI Safety recommendations, International AI Safety Report governance chapter.

## Domain 39: Healthcare Access as Democratic Infrastructure

### Title and Core Argument

**Title**: Healthcare as Democratic Infrastructure: How Medical Exclusion Functions as Civic Exclusion

**Core argument**: Healthcare access is a prerequisite for democratic participation, not a parallel or secondary concern. The framework documents five empirically grounded causal pathways through which healthcare deprivation produces civic exclusion: (1) rural hospital closures reduce local voter turnout by a measured 3.8 percentage points per study published in the American Political Science Review; (2) medical debt — borne by 100 million Americans, disproportionately Black and low-income — creates economic precarity that depresses civic engagement and blocks the time, resources, and stability that political participation requires; (3) Medicaid enrollment offices are mandatory voter registration sites under the NVRA, and administrative cuts to those offices mechanically reduce voter registration among low-income and majority-minority populations; (4) maternal mortality racial disparities (Black women 2-4x more likely to die in childbirth than white women) concentrate political powerlessness in the communities most dependent on organized state power for survival; (5) disability disenfranchisement — guardianship laws stripping voting rights, P&A program defunding, and Medicaid work requirements imposing participation barriers on disability beneficiaries — reduces the most politically marginalized population's access to the franchise. The OBBBA (P.L. 119-21) accelerates all five pathways simultaneously: 11.8 million projected coverage losses (CBO), 1.2-1.8 million work requirement disenrollments by January 2028 (Georgetown CCF), rural hospital infrastructure further stressed by Medicaid revenue reduction, and P&A program defunding proposed for FY2026.

### Priority Research Areas

**1. The Causal Chain: Rural Hospital Closures and Voter Turnout**

The most empirically specific finding in the domain: a study published in the American Political Science Review found that communities affected by a rural hospital closure experience a 3.8 percentage-point reduction in voter turnout. This is direct, peer-reviewed causal evidence that healthcare infrastructure loss produces democratic participation loss — not through attitudinal or motivational pathways but through concrete capacity pathways (loss of healthcare workers who often anchor civic infrastructure, loss of community anchor institution, economic destabilization).

Current context: 18 rural hospitals closed or converted to limited services in 2025 alone. Since 2020, 117 rural hospitals have eliminated or plan to eliminate labor and delivery units (11% reduction). OBBBA Medicaid cuts reduce rural hospital Medicaid revenue — rural hospitals serve disproportionately Medicaid-dependent populations. The Rural Health Transformation Fund ($50 billion over 5 years) is projected to offset only 37% of rural Medicaid cuts and 5% of total federal Medicaid reductions (Chartis 2025 analysis).

*Source categories*: American Political Science Review (hospital closure voting study — Cambridge Core), Chartis 2025 Rural Health State of the State, SPLC "Critical Condition" report on HR 1 and rural healthcare, BU SPH rural hospital analysis, World Socialist Web Site rural healthcare collapse tracker (WSWS May 12, 2026), National Rural Health Association 2025 policy analysis.

**2. The NVRA Nexus: Medicaid Offices as Voter Registration Infrastructure**

The National Voter Registration Act (52 U.S.C. § 20506) requires public assistance agencies to offer voter registration. This makes Medicaid enrollment offices mandatory voter registration sites. The Institute for Responsive Government has documented that Medicaid automatic voter registration (AVR) has dramatically outperformed traditional NVRA implementation: in states with AVR, voter registration rates at public assistance offices approached 85-90% of eligible contacts; without AVR, NVRA compliance produces approximately 1% registration rates at those same offices.

Current crisis: Only 5 states (Colorado, Oregon, California, Rhode Island, and one additional) have AVR implemented through Medicaid. The OBBBA enrollment infrastructure cuts — administrative pressure from 6-month recertification cycles, Medicaid agency budget compression, state matching fund uncertainty — reduce the capacity to offer meaningful voter registration at enrollment offices exactly as enrollment volume increases due to coverage churn.

The Poynter fact-check (2026) clarifies a key nuance: Medicaid does not automatically register people to vote (Dr. Oz's claim was false). The NVRA requires offer, not automatic registration. This distinction matters for accurate framing: the democratic harm is not that Medicaid = automatic voter registration; it is that Medicaid enrollment offices are the statutory voter registration access point for low-income populations, and degrading those offices degrades voter registration infrastructure for a specific constituency.

*Source categories*: Institute for Responsive Government (responsivegov.org Medicaid AVR research), NPR Biden administration Medicaid voter registration analysis, APHA Access to Voting policy brief, County Health Rankings (voter turnout as health indicator), Poynter fact-check on Medicaid voter registration.

**3. Medical Debt as Civic Exclusion**

100 million Americans carry some form of healthcare debt; 14 million owe $1,000 or more (CFPB 2022). The burden is disproportionately carried by Black Americans and low-to-middle income households. Medical debt forces reallocation of time, money, and cognitive bandwidth that political participation requires — particularly for working-class voters who cannot afford the time cost of registration, polling place travel, and civic engagement activities without material support.

The CFPB's 2024 rule removing medical debt from credit reports — which has now been blocked by the Trump administration — illustrates the policy leverage point. OBBBA's projection of 11.8 million coverage losses and the associated premium tax credit expiration will increase the pool of Americans carrying uninsured healthcare debt over the 2026-2028 period.

*Source categories*: CFPB Medical Debt Burden report, KFF "Burden of Medical Debt in the United States," Undue Medical Debt bipartisan survey findings, NPR medical debt state law pivot analysis.

**4. Maternal Mortality and Racial Civic Participation Disparities**

Black women are 2-4 times more likely to die in childbirth than white women. American Indian/Alaska Native women face similar disparities. Both 2025 Congressional resolutions (S.Res.67 and H.Res.119) declared racism a public health crisis and cited these figures. The democratic participation argument: maternal mortality concentrates loss of life in communities whose political participation is already most constrained by structural barriers. Women who die in childbirth cannot vote. Communities whose maternal mortality rates are highest are communities whose civic engagement resources are most depleted by grief, medical trauma, and the economic consequences of family loss.

The Momnibus framework (Black Maternal Health Momnibus Act, 119th Congress) provides the legislative vehicle. The domain should frame maternal mortality prevention not only as a health equity demand but as a democratic equity demand: protecting the lives of Black and Indigenous women is protecting the civic voice of their communities.

*Source categories*: S.Res.67 and H.Res.119 text (congress.gov), Ann Fam Med health disparities and voter engagement (annfammed.org), PMC article on expanding voter registration to clinical settings, Black Mamas Matter Alliance policy documents.

**5. Disability Disenfranchisement — Guardianship, P&A Defunding, and Work Requirements**

Three distinct disability-democracy pathways:

- *Guardianship and voting rights*: At least 52 bills across 20 states have addressed guardianship-based voting rights disenfranchisement since 2023 (per NDRN). State laws continue to allow courts to strip voting rights from people under guardianship based on "capacity" determinations that are often procedurally inadequate and ableist. This is the most direct legal form of disability-based civic exclusion.
- *P&A program defunding*: The Protection and Advocacy for Voting Accessibility program has distributed $130 million since 2003 to help disabled voters overcome participation barriers. The Trump administration proposed eliminating P&A program funding for FY2026. Without P&A, the primary legal and technical assistance program for disabled voters at the state level disappears.
- *Work requirements as disability exclusion*: OBBBA work requirements apply to "non-disabled" adults, but the exception definition is inadequate — many people with disabilities that affect work capacity (episodic conditions, mental health conditions, chronic pain) do not qualify for the formal disability exemption and will be caught in the work requirement's coverage loss mechanism.

*Source categories*: CAP "Democracy Denied for Disabled Americans" (americanprogress.org), NDRN voting rights page, ADA.gov voter rights fact sheet, Disability Belongs Medicaid 2026 update, Disability Rights in 2026 analysis (abilities.com), progressivelifestylesinc.org 2026 federal policy shifts.

**6. FQHCs as the Underutilized Democratic Infrastructure**

Federally Qualified Health Centers serve 32 million patients annually, disproportionately low-income, uninsured, and minority populations. HRSA guidance explicitly encourages FQHCs to support nonpartisan voter registration efforts. The Healthy Democracy Kit research (PMC 10213595) documented a voter registration toolkit that FQHCs can implement. The AAMC Center for Health Justice has published a nonpartisan voter registration fact sheet for healthcare institutions.

The gap: FQHC underutilization for voter registration is structural, not attitudinal. FQHCs lack the staffing, training, and reimbursement incentives to operationalize NVRA obligations. Expanding FQHC voter registration capacity is a high-leverage, low-cost democracy investment that directly serves the populations facing the most structural civic exclusion.

*Source categories*: HRSA FQHC guidance, Healthy Democracy Kit (PMC 10213595), AAMC voter registration fact sheet, Big Cities Health Coalition CMS HealthCare.gov voter participation analysis, Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (RWJF) March 2026 civic participation and health analysis.

## Domain 40: Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation

### Title and Core Argument

**Title**: Surveillance Capitalism and Electoral Manipulation: How Commercial Data Infrastructure Became Democratic Attack Infrastructure

**Core argument**: The electoral manipulation threat of 2026 is not primarily from foreign interference or lone bad actors — it is from the systematic integration of commercial surveillance infrastructure (data brokers, social media platforms, AI generation tools) with political targeting operations, in an environment with no federal regulation of the result. Four components form the infrastructure: (1) Data brokers — Thomson Reuters CLEAR, LexisNexis, Equifax — aggregate voter rolls, consumer data, location records, court filings, and social media into profiles usable for voter targeting at a scale and specificity that no prior campaign technology achieved; (2) AI-generated synthetic content (deepfakes, voice clones, synthetic text) deployed at industrial scale in the 2026 midterm cycle — 50% of voters report deepfakes influenced their election decisions; (3) Geofencing and microtargeting match these profiles to hyper-localized advertising systems that create distinct information environments for different voter segments; (4) A regulatory vacuum — the FEC deadlocked and cannot issue AI deepfake guidance, the FCC's robocall prohibition doesn't extend to digital advertising, no federal law bans political deepfakes, and the December 2025 AI preemption EO blocks the 28 states that had enacted disclosure requirements from going further. The EU's DSA (political microtargeting prohibitions on sensitive data) and AI Act Article 50 (deepfake labeling enforcement, August 2, 2026) create a US-EU accountability divergence in which the same tech companies comply with substantive EU standards while deploying unregulated manipulation infrastructure in US elections. The domain proposes an integrated reform architecture: data broker regulation as election law (voter data as specially protected category), AI-generated political content prohibition or mandatory provenance (going beyond disclosure), FEC mandate to issue binding guidance, and federal floor standards that supersede the current preemption-toward-deregulation EO.

### Priority Research Areas

**1. The Data Broker Architecture: From Consumer Surveillance to Voter Targeting**

Thomson Reuters CLEAR aggregates public records including voter registration rolls, court filings, driving records, and social media, combined with proprietary data. LexisNexis and Equifax collaborate: LexisNexis' PowerView Score incorporates Equifax credit bureau data. Political campaigns begin with voter lists purchased from state election authorities, then use data brokers to append consumer behavior data, creating behavioral voter profiles.

The AFSC Investigate database (investigate.afsc.org) documents Thomson Reuters' contracts with ICE — demonstrating that the same commercial data infrastructure serves both immigration enforcement and political targeting. Data broker lists now include 500+ companies (GhostVault 2026 estimate); the political targeting sector includes specialized firms that compile partisan voter profiles using dark web data, social media scraping, and consumer transaction records.

The DOJ demand for voter files from at least 27 states (Brennan Center documented) represents a government-private data broker synthesis: federally collected voter data (under threat of enforcement) being integrated with commercial surveillance infrastructure. The EFF's analysis of political campaign data use (deeplinks/2024/04/how-political-campaigns-use-your-data-target-you) provides the operational framework.

**2. AI-Generated Voter Manipulation: Industrial-Scale Deployment in 2026**

The 2026 midterms are the first US electoral cycle with political deepfakes deployed as standard campaign practice:

- NRSC released a video of Texas Democratic candidate James Talarico making statements he never made (CNN, March 13, 2026) — the first case of a national party committee deploying a synthesized video deepfake of a federal candidate.
- Rep. Mike Collins (R-GA) created a deepfake of incumbent Democratic Senator Jon Ossoff.
- Republican Committee for Loudoun County, Virginia released three AI-generated ads attacking Governor Abigail Spanberger.
- Biden deepfake robocalls in New Hampshire primary (2024) cost $1 and took under 20 minutes to create — the production cost threshold for electoral manipulation has effectively gone to zero.

Survey data: 50% of 2026 voters report deepfakes had "some influence" on their election decisions. WEF Global Risks Report 2026: "Deepfakes have crossed a critical threshold in 2026, have improved and eliminated earlier tell-tale glitches and are now accessible to anyone with a smartphone."

*Source categories*: CNN Talarico deepfake story (March 13, 2026), American Prospect "American Politics Is Already Inundated With AI Deepfakes" (April 17, 2026), Animal House "The 2026 Midterms: The Deepfake Election," Senator Warner press release on deepfakes (March 2026), Brennan Center "Gauging the AI Threat to Free and Fair Elections," Brennan Center "Preparing to Fight AI-Backed Voter Suppression," campaignnow.com "Regulators Scramble" report, truescreen.io deepfake certification analysis.

**3. The Regulatory Vacuum: FEC, FCC, and the 28-State Patchwork**

The federal regulatory architecture has three failure modes:

- *FEC deadlock*: On September 19, 2024, the FEC voted not to open a rulemaking on AI in campaign ads. Instead, it adopted a non-binding interpretive rule explaining how existing regulations on fraudulent misrepresentation "may apply." This is not a regulation; it has no enforcement teeth. The FEC has been in partisan deadlock (3-3 split) and cannot reach consensus. No federal law bans political deepfakes in any medium.
- *FCC/TCPA gap*: The FCC ruled in February 2024 that AI-generated voices in robocalls violate the Telephone Consumer Protection Act. This closes the robocall channel but leaves all digital, social media, and television advertising completely unregulated.
- *28-state disclosure-only patchwork*: Twenty-eight states have enacted AI disclosure requirements for political content. Most require small "AI Generated" labels — the NRSC's Talarico deepfake complied with Texas disclosure law while remaining an intentional deception. Disclosure-without-prohibition is compliance-as-cover.

The December 2025 preemption EO (directing DOJ, FTC, and FCC to challenge state AI laws) is directly relevant: if the EO's preemption theory succeeds in court, the 28-state disclosure patchwork — the only existing federal-or-state accountability — disappears. This is not a marginal risk; Davis Wright Tremaine's legal analysis of the EO concludes that "the order signals a serious preemption strategy" and that state AI laws requiring model alterations may be vulnerable.

*Source categories*: FEC record on AI interpretive rule (fec.gov), Federal Register AI in campaign ads (2024-21979), campaignnow.com FEC analysis, Brookings policy framework for AI in political ads, CAP "Protecting Democracy Online in 2024 and Beyond," EPIC deepfake elections analysis, Davis Wright Tremaine preemption EO analysis.

**4. EU DSA and AI Act as Accountability Benchmarks**

The EU Digital Services Act prohibits political microtargeting using special categories of sensitive data (GDPR Article 9(1): sexual orientation, ethnicity, religious beliefs, political opinions — though note, DSA prohibits the use of sensitive categories for targeting, but has not yet enacted the full political targeting ban that civil society has advocated). The European Data Protection Board has recommended a total ban on microtargeting using sensitive data for political purposes.

EU AI Act Article 50 (enforcement August 2, 2026): requires machine-readable marking of AI-generated synthetic content; disclosure to users when AI creates realistic deepfakes. EU Code of Practice on AI-Generated Content (finalized May-June 2026) operationalizes this.

The US-EU divergence creates a "regulatory arbitrage" in which US companies comply with substantive accountability requirements for European political markets while deploying unregulated synthetic content in US elections. This is not a hypothetical: Meta, Google, and Twitter/X all maintain different content moderation and political advertising standards for EU markets (DSA compliance) than for US markets (voluntary standards only).

*Source categories*: DSA Wikipedia article, EU DSA Q&A (digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu), AlgorithmWatch DSA explained, International IDEA EU Digital Regulatory Framework and Electoral Processes, EU AI Act Article 50 text, EU 2026 enforcement escalation (European Business Magazine).

**5. Reform Architecture: What Structural Regulation Would Require**

The domain's policy contribution is a four-element reform architecture that matches the scale of the manipulation infrastructure:

- *Voter Data Protection Act*: Designate voter registration data as a specially protected category under federal privacy law — prohibiting data brokers from including voter roll information in commercial behavioral profiles without explicit statutory authorization. - *Federal Deepfake Prohibition in Political Advertising*: Move beyond disclosure-only requirements to a categorical prohibition on photorealistic AI-generated video and audio depicting real candidates in political ads, with a safe harbor for clearly satirical content. - *FEC Mandatory AI Rulemaking*: Congressional mandate requiring the FEC to complete a rulemaking on AI-generated political content within 180 days, with specific requirements for synthetic content prohibition or provenance disclosure, and a tie-breaking mechanism when commissioners are deadlocked.
- *Federal Floor + State Authority Preservation*: The December 2025 preemption EO should be reversed for this domain. Federal law should set a minimum floor that the 28 states with AI disclosure requirements exceed; states should retain authority to go further, consistent with the anti-preemption model.
