## 1. White House Ballroom — SCOTUS Application Not Yet Filed; 96-Hour Clock

**Status as of evening pass**: No SCOTUS emergency application has appeared on the public docket. No new order from Judge Leon. The situation is identical to the afternoon pass.

**What the D.C. Circuit actually ordered (April 11)**: The three-judge panel (Millett and Garcia in majority, Rao dissenting) did two things simultaneously. First, it extended the stay on Leon's injunction through April 17 — construction can continue through that date. Second, it remanded to Leon with a specific instruction: Leon must clarify whether and how his injunction affects the underground security infrastructure (bomb shelters, hospital, Top Secret Military installations). The panel held it cannot properly rule on the emergency stay without knowing whether Leon's order reaches or leaves untouched the underground work. This is why the administration's SCOTUS narrative — built on the specificity of the April 9 security filing — may be complicated before any application lands at the Court: if Leon issues a narrow clarification excluding underground work from his injunction's scope, the "stopping security construction" argument loses its force.

**No clarification from Leon as of evening pass**: Leon has not issued the remand response. This is now a 96-hour window (April 13 evening through April 17) in which two separate events could break: (a) Leon issues the clarification, potentially narrowing the administration's SCOTUS hook; (b) DOJ files SCOTUS application directly without waiting for the clarification, betting the Court will grant the stay regardless.

**SCOTUS routing**: If filed, the application goes to Chief Justice Roberts as Circuit Justice for the D.C. Circuit. Roberts could rule unilaterally, refer to the full Court, or request a response from the National Trust before acting. Given the administration's security framing, a solo Roberts order granting a stay pending full consideration is the most likely scenario if the application is filed at all.

**National Trust's position**: Their April 13 filing characterized the project as having "no national security emergency" — but this framing does not engage the underground infrastructure specifics. The Trust's stronger argument is separability: that above-ground ballroom work and underground security work are legally distinct, and that Leon's injunction could and should be read to cover only the above-ground phase.

## 3. CORRECTION AND UPDATE — CIT/IEEPA Track (Tomorrow's Conference)

**Critical correction to prior tracking**: The litigation tracker's Section 6.2 framed the CIT/IEEPA case as a pending tariff legality challenge — but the underlying legal question was resolved on February 20, 2026, when the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*. Chief Justice Roberts wrote for the Court that IEEPA does not give the president authority to impose tariffs: "Those words cannot bear such weight." The ruling was 6-3 along the lines of statutory interpretation, not constitutional avoidance.

**What the April 14 conference is actually about**: Following the SCOTUS ruling, the Trump administration announced a new 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 (the separate track being litigated before the CIT three-judge panel, argued April 10). In the meanwhile, the CIT has been managing the administrative process of refunding IEEPA duties already collected. The April 14 conference before Judge Eaton is specifically about whether CBP is on track to launch Phase 1 of the refund mechanism by April 20:

- **CBP status report due**: April 14 at 12 PM EDT — CBP must file a declaration describing progress on the four components of the refund system (between 60–85% complete as of April 1)
- **Closed conference**: April 14 at 3 PM EDT before Senior Judge Richard Eaton
- **Phase 1 rollout**: April 20 — automated refund process covering approximately 63% of affected entries (unliquidated, within 90-day voluntary reliquidation window, or in "Suspended/Extended/Under Review" status)
- **Phase 2**: Finally-liquidated entries, timeline TBD

**The Section 122 case (separate track)**: The three-judge CIT panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) argued the Section 122 10% global tariff on April 10. The panel was sharply skeptical — challenging whether the statutory conditions for Section 122 (balance-of-payments crisis) had been met and whether the authority extends to the president's claimed scope. No ruling has been issued. This is the active legal challenge; the IEEPA refund proceedings are administrative implementation of an already-decided question.

**Why this matters for resistance tracking**: The Section 122 case is arguably more consequential than the IEEPA case was, because Section 122 is the administration's backup legal authority. If the CIT invalidates Section 122 tariffs and the Federal Circuit or SCOTUS affirms, the administration has exhausted its statutory tariff authorities. The April 10 argument posture suggests the CIT panel may be heading toward another invalidation.

**AFL-CIO posture**: Liz Shuler has made no statement specifically endorsing the May Day general strike. The federation's most recent public communication (April 8 "What Working People Are Doing This Week" post) made no reference to May 1 action. The legal exposure rationale — the NLRA's prohibition on sympathy strikes, and the AFL-CIO's fiduciary obligation not to expose affiliates to secondary boycott liability — remains the most plausible explanation. Individual affiliated unions can participate; the federation cannot formally call a general strike without potentially violating its own obligations under the Act.

**What would move the needle**: The AFL-CIO national endorsement would require either (a) Shuler calculating that the legal risk is worth the political moment, or (b) a dramatic escalation event before May 1 that makes neutrality politically untenable. The most plausible trigger would be a SCOTUS shadow docket order that directly strips federal workers of remaining protections — e.g., an adverse ruling in the MSPB/NLRB removal-power cases before May 1.

**CPS Chicago board watch**: No update on Chicago Board of Education action. The CTU endorsement and Mayor Johnson's support create institutional momentum, but the board has not voted.
