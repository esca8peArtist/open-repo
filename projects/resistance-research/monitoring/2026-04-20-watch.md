## 1. CAPE Phase 1 Launch

### What Is CAPE

CAPE (Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries) is U.S. Customs and Border Protection's new automated system within the Automated Commercial Environment (ACE) portal for processing refunds of duties collected under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act before the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs on February 20, 2026.

The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* that IEEPA does not authorize tariff imposition — specifically that the statute's power to "regulate importation" does not encompass the taxing power to levy tariffs, which is constitutionally reserved to Congress. This invalidated approximately $165–179 billion in duties collected under two categories: (1) Trafficking and Immigration Tariffs on Canadian, Mexican, and Chinese imports beginning February 2025; and (2) Reciprocal Tariffs imposed from April 2025 on imports from virtually all trading partners. President Trump signed an executive order the same day directing agencies to cease collection and then shifted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 as the tariff replacement vehicle.

CAPE is the government's technical answer to the refund obligation. Rather than processing 53 million entries one at a time — which CBP acknowledged would cause "substantial manual effort and potential disruption" — CAPE consolidates refunds by liquidation date and importer of record, batching entries up to 9,999 per submission. It operates through a new tab in the ACE portal.

### The 26,664 Enrolled Importers

As of March 26, 2026, 26,664 importers of record had completed ACH enrollment to receive electronic refunds. This figure represents approximately 78% of entries subject to IEEPA duty payments, with a principal duty amount of approximately $120 billion. However, the total universe of IEEPA-affected importers is approximately 333,000 — meaning roughly 307,000 importers who paid IEEPA duties have not yet enrolled for electronic refunds. The $120 billion figure covers the enrolled 78%; the full universe represents approximately $165–166 billion. The unenrolled importers face a structural problem: CBP has confirmed refunds will only be issued electronically, and as of the March 31 CBP declaration to the CIT, more than 12,300 certified refunds had already been rejected since February 6 because recipients had not set up ACH banking information in their ACE accounts.

Phase 1 is structured to cover approximately 63% of all IEEPA-paid entries — specifically unliquidated entries and entries within 80 days of the standard liquidation date. This corresponds roughly to the enrolled-importer universe. Phase 2 will address finally-liquidated entries and more complex cases; no Phase 2 timeline has been confirmed.

### What Section 232 and 301 Tariffs Have to Do With It

Critically: nothing in terms of refund eligibility. CAPE covers only IEEPA-based duties. Section 232 tariffs (steel and aluminum, now including copper after the April 6, 2026 proclamation) and Section 301 tariffs (China-specific goods) remain fully in effect. The SCOTUS IEEPA ruling explicitly did not reach Section 232 or 301 authority, which rest on different statutory bases. This means importers of steel, aluminum, copper, or China-sourced goods who paid both IEEPA and Section 232/301 duties on the same entries face a complex calculation: the CAPE Declaration removes the IEEPA component, the system recalculates duties as if IEEPA had never been declared, but the Section 232/301 layer stays intact. For derivative products — goods containing steel or aluminum content — the Section 232 "derivative content value calculation" problem intersects with the IEEPA refund in ways that require entry-by-entry analysis of the metal-content percentage and the applicable HTSUS classification. CBP issued separate guidance on this, but the intersection remains technically complex.

### Phase 1 Mechanics

The process requires:

1. The importer of record or licensed customs broker (not a third-party, not ABI filers generally) logs into the ACE portal.
2. A new CAPE tab appears in the portal on April 20.
3. The filer uploads a CSV file listing entry summary numbers for which they are requesting IEEPA duty refunds, up to 9,999 entries per declaration. Multiple submissions are permitted but amendments are not.
4. CBP's mass-processing engine automatically removes applicable IEEPA HTS codes from accepted entries, recalculates duties as if IEEPA were never declared, and updates entry summaries.
5. Refunds are consolidated by liquidation date and importer and issued via ACH to the bank account registered in ACE within 60–90 days of Declaration acceptance, absent a compliance concern requiring further review.
6. CIT Judge Eaton pre-endorsed the Phase 1 framework prior to rollout, providing judicial sanction.

Phase 1 excludes: entries flagged for reconciliation (Entry Type 09); entries designated on drawback claims; entries covered by open protests; entries not filed in ACE or lacking liquidation status; and entries subject to antidumping or countervailing duties (AD/CVD) for which the Department of Commerce has issued liquidation instructions pending under 19 U.S.C. § 1504(d). AD/CVD entries with suspended liquidation can be included.

### Implementation Complications to Watch On April 20

Four categories of risk:

**ACE system stability.** As of March 30, 2026, the four CAPE components were at varying completion levels: Claim Portal 85%, Mass Processing 60–75%, Review/Reliquidation 80%, Refund 75%. A federal court filing described the system as 75% complete overall. While CBP reported "satisfactory progress" to the CIT, a system processing tens of thousands of simultaneous entries on day one carries material technical risk. Any announcement of a delay, scope reduction, or ACE outage on April 20 is a significant signal.

**ACH non-enrollment.** The majority of IEEPA-affected importers remain not set up for electronic refunds. The 12,300 rejected refunds already in the record is a concrete leading indicator. On April 20, many importers may discover their declarations are accepted but refunds cannot be disbursed until they complete ACH setup — creating a bottleneck that extends the de facto refund timeline beyond the stated 60–90 days.

**Broker filing bottleneck.** Only the importer of record or the licensed customs broker who originally filed the entries can submit a CAPE Declaration. A single broker handling 9,999-entry declarations on behalf of multiple importers faces a significant data-compilation task. Large-volume brokers who have not completed data preparation may find April 20 is a start-of-queue date, not a filing date.

**Government appeal risk.** The administration has until approximately late May to appeal the IEEPA ruling. A pending appeal would not automatically stay the refund obligation, but it creates uncertainty about whether the CAPE process could be halted or modified mid-stream. CIT oversight of the CAPE process through Judge Eaton's endorsement provides some procedural backstop.

**The $46 billion gap.** The enrolled 26,664 importers represent $120 billion in duty value. The full IEEPA refund obligation is approximately $165–166 billion. The gap — approximately $45–46 billion — represents entries from importers who have not enrolled for ACH refunds. These importers are not in Phase 1's processing queue. Their refund path is either enrollment before Phase 2, the protest mechanism under 19 U.S.C. § 1514, or being swept in when Phase 2 launches.

## 2. Abrego Garcia — DOJ April 20 Brief

### Thread State

Kilmar Abrego Garcia is a Maryland man wrongfully deported to El Salvador in March 2025. SCOTUS unanimously upheld the return obligation in April 2025. He was released from immigration detention in December 2025 by prior Xinis order and has remained in the United States under court protection. Judge Paula Xinis (D. Maryland) has been managing DOJ compliance since then. She has a "bad faith" finding in the record, has indicated she is weighing contempt, rebuked DOJ for attempting to "dictate the Court's schedule," and called DOJ's suggestion that Abrego Garcia could "remove himself" to Costa Rica — while the DOJ is simultaneously prosecuting him in Tennessee — "a fantasy."

As of April 8, 2026, DHS reaffirmed its intent to deport Abrego Garcia to Liberia, a country he has no connection to, despite a U.S. agreement with Costa Rica to accept deportees in precisely his situation. The DOJ's March 21 filing asked Xinis to dissolve the injunction preventing re-deportation by April 17. Xinis rejected that demand and set her own schedule: briefs April 20, hearing April 28.

In parallel, the Nashville criminal case is before Judge Waverly Crenshaw (M.D. Tennessee) on a vindictive prosecution motion. Acting AG Todd Blanche publicly linked the Maryland civil case to the Nashville prosecution — confirming the DOJ's own political motivation in statements that are now in Crenshaw's record. Xinis noted the structural impossibility of the administration's position: the same DOJ that is prosecuting Abrego Garcia in Tennessee is simultaneously trying to deport him from the country, which would end the prosecution and defeat the requirement for his domestic presence at criminal hearings.

### What the Brief Must Address

The April 20 DOJ brief responds to Xinis's pending motion regarding compliance with the "facilitation" order and the injunction against re-deportation. Based on the established record, the brief must address four issues:

1. **Whether DOJ maintains the Liberia deportation demand.** The Liberia position is structurally incoherent on its face: (a) Abrego Garcia has no connection to Liberia; (b) the U.S. has an operative agreement with Costa Rica for deportees in his situation; (c) active Tennessee prosecution requires his domestic presence; (d) SCOTUS's unanimous return order remains in effect. If DOJ doubles down, it hands Xinis a clean factual predicate for a contempt finding.

2. **Whether DOJ concedes anything on the return obligation.** Xinis's facilitation order requires affirmative action; "not deporting him" is not compliance. Prior DOJ briefs have tried to reframe "facilitation" as passive non-interference rather than active return effort, an argument Xinis has explicitly rejected. Any new theory along those lines will be received with demonstrated skepticism.

3. **Whether DOJ files a simultaneous Fourth Circuit emergency motion.** The Fourth Circuit previously called the DOJ's attempt to appeal Xinis's expedited discovery order "extraordinary and premature." A new Fourth Circuit emergency filing to stay Xinis's injunction or transfer the case would be a code-red escalation — it would mean the administration is attempting to remove the case from a judge whose factual record it cannot overcome. This possibility is live given the administration's pattern of appellate forum-shopping.

4. **Whether the newly unsealed documents are addressed.** Xinis ordered a tranche of documents unsealed. Their contents are not yet fully indexed in public reporting. If those documents contain new evidence of coordination between the Tennessee prosecution and the Maryland civil case, the brief's treatment of them (or silence about them) is significant.

### Four Possible Brief Scenarios

**Scenario A — Retreat from Liberia.** DOJ quietly abandons the Liberia demand, pivots to arguing the injunction against re-deportation is now moot or improperly framed, and asks Xinis to vacate on changed-circumstances grounds. This scenario is inconsistent with the April 8 DHS reaffirmation but would be the legally rational move. Probability: low given recent posture.

**Scenario B — Maintain Liberia, argue constitutional limits on judicial authority.** DOJ doubles down on Liberia as the third-country designation, reasserts the president's foreign affairs prerogative, and argues Xinis lacks authority to dictate the destination of removal — a structural executive power argument that has failed at every prior appellate level. This is the administration's comfort zone. It sets up a contempt posture. Probability: high based on prior filings.

**Scenario C — State secrets reassertion.** DOJ invokes state secrets or executive privilege over the diplomatic-relations details of the Liberia or Costa Rica negotiations, arguing Xinis cannot order disclosure of foreign policy communications. Xinis has already dismissed this argument once as "vague and unsubstantiated." A repeat invocation would be treated harshly. Probability: moderate as a supplementary argument.

**Scenario D — Fourth Circuit emergency filing on the same day.** DOJ simultaneously files an emergency stay application in the Fourth Circuit arguing Xinis's injunction interferes with executive foreign-affairs authority, requesting a stay pending appellate review. This would be designed to freeze Xinis's contempt proceedings while an appellate court takes up the structural question. Given the Fourth Circuit's prior rejection of an "extraordinary and premature" DOJ application, success odds are low — but the filing itself would create weeks of delay. Probability: possible; watch for same-day PACER entries in the Fourth Circuit.

### What a Contempt Finding Would Require

If DOJ's brief provides no path to compliance and the April 28 hearing confirms the impasse, Xinis's contempt analysis requires:

- A clear and unambiguous court order (the facilitation order and injunction against re-deportation qualify)
- The respondent's knowledge of the order (DOJ has been operating under it for months)
- Noncompliance (the Liberia demand, the Tennessee prosecution contradiction, and the "remove himself" suggestion all indicate active non-compliance with the facilitation standard)
- The ability to comply (DOJ has argued impossibility; Xinis has rejected that framing; the Costa Rica agreement undermines the impossibility claim)

A contempt finding before May would put the administration in the position of either complying or escalating to a SCOTUS application seeking to vacate the contempt — a politically costly posture in a case with extensive public documentation of the administration's conduct.

## 3. White House Ballroom — Post-Expiry Status

### The April 17 Deadline: What Happened

As of the preparation of this brief (April 13, 2026), the April 17 midnight deadline for the D.C. Circuit stay has not yet passed. This section synthesizes what the record established before April 13 and identifies the three outcome scenarios for April 20 monitoring.

**Confirmed pre-expiry facts:**

- The D.C. Circuit 2-1 panel (Millett, Garcia; Rao dissenting) stayed Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction through April 17 and remanded to Leon for clarification: whether the "safety and security" exception in his order adequately addresses the administration's national-security claims about below-ground infrastructure.
- As of April 13, Judge Leon had issued no clarification order — four full days after the remand and with 96 hours remaining to the deadline.
- As of April 13, no SCOTUS application appeared on the public docket for *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA*.
- The National Trust argued in an April 13 filing that the above-ground ballroom work and below-ground security infrastructure are "operationally and contractually separable" — which, if accepted by Leon, would eliminate the administration's primary national-security hook for a SCOTUS application.
- The D.C. Circuit majority found the administration had "not yet shown how any national security concerns were not covered by the original order's exemptions" — an appellate factual finding unfavorable to a cold SCOTUS application.

**The Rao dissent as the administration's SCOTUS roadmap:** The Rao dissent (D.C. Circuit, April 11) argued the majority should have vacated Leon's injunction outright rather than remanding, writing that the national-security implications had been inadequately weighed and the record before the court was sufficient to resolve the case. If the administration filed cold at SCOTUS, the Rao dissent would be the primary framework offered to Roberts — giving him a sitting circuit judge's analysis rather than relying solely on the government brief.

**Leon's six-day silence entering April 17:** A judge who was completing a straightforward clarification order on a tight circuit-set deadline would typically act promptly. Leon's silence is either (a) a comprehensive opinion being drafted with care, in which case it may appear April 16 or the morning of April 17; or (b) deliberate non-action before the deadline, placing the burden squarely on the administration to go cold to SCOTUS without the clarification the D.C. Circuit requested.

### Three Post-Expiry Branches

**Branch A — Leon acted before midnight April 17.** Leon narrows his injunction to above-ground work only, expressly permitting the below-ground security infrastructure. The administration files at SCOTUS with a weaker national-security argument. Roberts decides whether to administratively stay the narrowed order pending full briefing. The administration's strongest remaining argument is inherent presidential authority over the White House as executive mansion — a claim Leon explicitly rejected. Roberts' IEEPA oral-argument scepticism about arguments that "prove too much" is a warning signal for this framing.

**Branch B — Administration filed cold at SCOTUS on April 16–17 without Leon's clarification.** Procedurally permissible but awkward. Roberts would need to grant an administrative stay to preserve status quo while the full Court deliberates. Roberts can act unilaterally as circuit justice for the D.C. Circuit within hours of an application filing. The Rao dissent provides cover. The key question is whether Roberts reads a transparent "let us keep building while the legal theory is sorted out" application favorably. The IEEPA ruling's textualist restraint is not encouraging for a broad inherent-authority application.

**Branch C — Neither Leon nor the administration acted; stay expired at midnight.** Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction reinstates automatically. Under this branch:

- National Trust files an emergency contempt motion with Leon within hours of midnight or at court opening April 18.
- Contempt here is photographic. The East Wing construction site is visible from Pennsylvania Avenue and monitored by press continuously. A crane operating on April 18 morning is unambiguous, unlike the "facilitation" definitional games in the Maryland case.
- Leon's response options: Order to Show Cause, formal contempt finding, or incremental coercive sanctions (fines per day of continued construction).
- An administration that continues construction under a reinstated Leon injunction is making a deliberate public choice to defy a federal court order at the most symbolically loaded site in American government. This is qualitatively different from the immigration compliance ambiguity.

**The April 20 monitoring question:** Depending on which branch materialized, April 20 may find the thread in one of three states:

1. A SCOTUS stay is in place (Branch A or B successful), construction continues, and the East Wing site is active. In this state, the case moves to merits briefing before SCOTUS.
2. Leon's order is reinstated and the administration has complied — construction paused, contempt proceedings not necessary. Leon issues a formal contempt notice as a prophylactic record regardless.
3. Branch C materialized and the administration chose defiance — construction visible April 18, National Trust contempt motion filed, Leon entering contempt proceedings, and the administration either actively defending or going emergency to SCOTUS to stay the contempt.

**Why the ballroom matters for April 20:** The ballroom's post-April-17 status shapes the broader judicial defiance narrative entering the Abrego Garcia April 28 hearing. If the administration is simultaneously in contempt posture before Leon in D.C. and before Xinis in Maryland, the institutional dimension of the confrontation is qualitatively different than if either thread is in a compliance or appeal posture. Xinis knows what Leon's courtroom is doing. Leon knows what Xinis's courtroom is doing. The simultaneous compression of judicial authority tests is not coincidental — and the administration has been running a managed-attrition strategy across all threads.

## 4. Secondary Threads

### 4a. Section 122 / CIT — Barnett Panel Deliberating

**Current status:** No ruling issued. Three-judge panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) deliberated through at least April 13 following the April 10 oral argument.

**The record entering deliberation:** DOJ attorney Brett Shumate "repeatedly admitted he cannot say what the balance-of-payments deficit is right now and could not even give an estimate" (International Trade Today, April 10). Section 122 requires the president to find "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits" as a condition precedent for invoking the statute. The government's lead attorney could not identify the condition precedent. At least two judges told the government its theory "proves too much" — that under the administration's reading, the president could invoke Section 122 virtually any time. The panel expressed uncertainty about translating the 1974 Bretton-Woods-era "balance-of-payments" concept into a 2026 floating-exchange-rate context.

**Two cases, two plaintiff theories:** The Liberty Justice Center case (small businesses) argues Section 122 requires a balance-of-payments deficit in the traditional sense, which does not exist. The 24-state AG case adds structural arguments about the unprecedented scope of the tariff regime. The panel could rule on one theory, the other, or jointly.

**What a ruling would mean:** Section 122 is the administration's primary remaining tariff vehicle after the IEEPA ruling. An invalidation would eliminate both the legal and political basis for the current 10% universal surcharge regime, forcing a third tariff legal theory. Given the $166 billion IEEPA refund obligation already underway, a Section 122 ruling would open a second major refund stream — this one potentially larger in ongoing economic impact since the Section 122 tariffs are currently being collected on new imports.

**July 24 hard deadline:** Section 122 is a 150-day emergency measure effective February 24, 2026. It expires July 24. A ruling before late June preserves emergency appellate posture. A ruling after expiry risks mootness arguments before the Federal Circuit and SCOTUS.

**Watch for:** Any CIT panel order — preliminary injunction, summary judgment, or procedural ruling — should be flagged immediately. The more detailed the written opinion, the longer the drafting; the panel's awareness that Federal Circuit and SCOTUS review is coming means the opinion will be written with care. Silence through April 20 is consistent with normal deliberation pace.

### 4b. Nashville / Crenshaw — Dismissal Still Expected "At Any Time"

**Current status:** Judge Waverly Crenshaw (M.D. Tennessee) has issued no ruling as of April 13. Silence extends past three weeks since the February 2026 hearing.

**What is established:** Crenshaw has already found "realistic likelihood" of vindictive prosecution. The government's own witness testified at the February hearing that the charges were "extraordinary" and would "likely be seen as vindictive." Acting AG Blanche publicly linked the Maryland civil case to the Nashville prosecution — CNN confirmed this April 11. This is as close to a self-incriminating statement of vindictive purpose as appears in the case law. A judge who is finding dismissal has all the factual predicates in the record.

**The two interpretations remain:** (1) Crenshaw is drafting a durable dismissal opinion built to survive Fourth Circuit and SCOTUS review — the more airtight, the longer the drafting. (2) Crenshaw is considering a Blanche subpoena compelling the Acting AG to testify under oath in Nashville before ruling — an extraordinary step that requires procedural scaffolding and would dominate both the Nashville and Maryland proceedings.

**Connection to April 28:** A Crenshaw dismissal before April 28 eliminates the government's primary criminal leverage framing in the Maryland hearing. The Tennessee prosecution has been the structural justification for maintaining Abrego Garcia domestically against his will while simultaneously threatening deportation — a logical impossibility Xinis has called "a fantasy." A dismissal removes the prosecution and clarifies that the only remaining issue is the civil return obligation. A Blanche subpoena before April 28 would dominate both courts and the news cycle simultaneously.

### 4c. Humphrey's Executor / Trump v. Slaughter

**Status:** No SCOTUS decision. Oral argument December 8, 2025. Expected June 2026. SCOTUSblog: "Opinion: TBD / Vote: TBD."

**The likely outcome:** Roberts ("dried husk"), Barrett ("eroding"), and Gorsuch ("poorly reasoned") collectively signal the conservative majority is aligned against retaining Humphrey's Executor in its current form. Legal analysis from Constitutional Accountability Center, Ward & Smith, Lawfare, and SCOTUSblog converge on the same conclusion. The operative question is overruling vs. narrowing. Narrowing to adjudicatory functions (as opposed to policymaking functions) achieves the operative result — Slaughter and Bedoya are removed — without the institutional cost of explicitly overruling a 90-year unanimous precedent. Most post-argument analysis favors narrowing as the most likely vehicle, though both outcomes are substantively equivalent for practical independence of the agencies affected.

**The institutional stakes:** An overruling or substantial narrowing gives the president at-will removal authority over FTC, NLRB, CFPB, FEC, MSPB, EEOC, FERC, and all other independent regulatory agencies. Combined with the IEEPA tariff ruling — which constrained executive economic emergency power — the Court's current term is simultaneously limiting the statutory power of the executive while expanding its structural control over the administrative apparatus. The throughline is formalist separation of powers: narrow the emergency authorities, tighten the personnel control. The administrative state loses insulation on both ends.

**No April 20 event.** Monitor for any Thursday or Monday SCOTUS opinion release through June 2026.

### 4d. May Day 2026 / No Kings General Strike

**Current organizing state:** The "May Day Strong" coalition has consolidated with the following confirmed institutional labor backing: National Education Association (NEA, published "May Day Strong" toolkit), SEIU 509, SEIU 2015, National Nurses United (200,000 members confirmed by President Mary Turner), AFA-CWA, Chicago Teachers Union (31,000 members), United Electrical Radio and Machine Workers (UE), United Teachers Los Angeles (UTLA) and other AFT locals, Socialist Alternative, Indivisible, and Higher Education Labor United. Chicago Mayor Brandon Johnson declared May 1 a city holiday. The AFL-CIO as an institution has not confirmed, though individual locals are leading.

**The framing shift that matters:** "No Work, No School, No Shopping" is qualitatively different from the March 28 No Kings marches. A march expresses opinion; a strike withdraws economic cooperation. The Minnesota general strike on January 23, 2026, which took over downtown Minneapolis, is explicitly the model organizers are working from. National Nurses United's Mary Turner's statement — "Nurses never back away from a fight" — is notable because nurses striking involves direct patient care risk calculations, not just employer-relation risk. The participation of healthcare workers in the general strike framing is a threshold cross.

**Legal exposure for participants:** Organizers are explicitly addressing the legal risk for non-union workers who walk out. Strike FAQ documents from Socialist Alternative and organizing guides from Higher Education Labor United both address termination exposure. The May Day Strong coalition is funding legal defense for workers who participate. This is a structural difference from prior mobilizations: the coalition is acknowledging the legal risk and providing cover, rather than pretending the legal risk doesn't exist.

**April 29 call to action:** A national lead-up organizing call and pre-strike events are scheduled for April 29, two days before May 1. This is the immediate precursor to the main action. Watch for: (a) final confirmed union/organization endorsement count; (b) administration pre-strike messaging framing strikers as unlawful; (c) any injunctive action against strike organizers; (d) size of the April 29 lead-up turnout as a preview indicator for May 1 scale.

**The resistance strategy connection:** May Day 2026 lands regardless of judicial outcomes. Whether Leon is in contempt proceedings, whether Xinis has found DOJ in contempt, whether Section 122 has been invalidated — the strike frame operates on a parallel track. The question for resistance strategy is whether a successful May Day amplifies judicial pressure and Congressional attention, or whether the administration successfully frames strikers as radicals to neutralize movement legitimacy entering the midterm cycle.
