## 1. Leon / White House Ballroom — Four Days of Silence, April 17 Incoming

### Confirmed as of April 13

**The D.C. Circuit order (April 11)**: A 2-1 panel (Millett, Garcia; Rao dissenting) stayed Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction through April 17 and remanded to Leon for factual clarification: specifically, whether his "safety and security" exception adequately addresses the administration's national-security claims regarding below-ground work. The panel wrote it could not "fairly determine whether and to what extent the district court's 'necessary for safety and security' exception addresses Defendants' claims of irreparable harm."

**Leon's silence**: As of April 13, Judge Leon has issued no clarification order. This is the fourth full day after the D.C. Circuit remand. No docket entry has surfaced in search-indexed reporting.

**No SCOTUS application confirmed**: Search-indexed results through April 13 show no public SCOTUS docket entry for *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA*. The D.C. Circuit order gave the administration until April 17 to "seek Supreme Court review." No application has been confirmed as filed.

**National Trust's separability argument** (confirmed, April 13 filing): The National Trust argues the above-ground ballroom work and below-ground security infrastructure are "operationally and contractually separable," which, if accepted by Leon in a clarification order, would eliminate the administration's primary national-security hook for a SCOTUS application. The administration would then have to argue presidential inherent authority over the White House itself — a significantly weaker posture.

**Administration's position**: The Trump administration has repeatedly argued that any pause in construction creates national security risk. The D.C. Circuit 2-1 majority explicitly found the administration had "not yet shown how any national security concerns were not covered by the original order's exemptions." This is a factual finding unfavorable to the administration's SCOTUS application narrative.

### What Changes as April 17 Approaches

The D.C. Circuit April 11 ruling has two operative effects:
1. It continues the administrative stay of Leon's injunction through April 17
2. It remands to Leon for clarification — without requiring Leon to act before April 17

Leon is not procedurally required to issue his clarification before the stay expires. If he does not act by midnight April 17, his March 31 preliminary injunction reinstates automatically. The construction halt kicks in. The contempt clock starts with the first visible construction activity thereafter.

**Connection to post-IEEPA landscape**: The SCOTUS IEEPA ruling (which struck down IEEPA-based tariffs) shifted the administration's tariff apparatus to Section 122 as a backup. A CIT ruling invalidating Section 122 tariffs would eliminate the primary remaining emergency tariff vehicle — a much larger economic and political consequence than the IEEPA ruling.

## 7. Humphrey's Executor (Trump v. Slaughter) — Argument Analysis Deepens, Decision Pending

### Confirmed Developments

**No decision issued**: Oral argument was December 8, 2025. As of April 13, 2026, no decision has been issued. SCOTUSblog records "Opinion: TBD / Vote: TBD." This is consistent with normal SCOTUS deliberation timing for a constitutionally significant case.

**Post-argument consensus**: Legal analysis from Constitutional Accountability Center, Ward & Smith, Lawfare, and SCOTUSblog all describe the Court's conservative majority as favorable to the government's position. Roberts described Humphrey's Executor as "a dried husk." Barrett noted "an eroding of Humphrey's Executor over the years." Gorsuch argued it was "poorly reasoned from the start."

**The operative question is not whether but how**: An outright overruling of Humphrey's Executor would give the president at-will removal authority over the heads of all independent agencies — FTC, NLRB, CFPB, FEC, MSPB, and all others. A "substantial narrowing" (the less dramatic option) would distinguish Humphrey's on its facts and apply removal protections only to adjudicatory rather than policymaking functions — still a significant weakening. Current analysis suggests the majority may opt for narrowing rather than outright overruling, both as a matter of judicial restraint and because narrowing achieves the operative result (Slaughter and Bedoya are removed) without the institutional cost of overruling a 90-year-old precedent.

**What this means for resistance work**: An overruling or substantial narrowing of Humphrey's Executor lands alongside the IEEPA tariff ruling as a structural transformation of the administrative state. The tariff ruling limits emergency economic power; the Humphrey's ruling expands removal power. These are not contradictory — the throughline is formalist separation of powers that concentrates authority in the president while stripping independent agency insulation. Combined effect: the president controls who runs every regulatory agency, but is limited in the emergency statutory powers those agencies can invoke. This leaves the regulatory state structurally weakened on both sides.

**Expected decision window**: Late May to late June 2026, consistent with the Court's typical end-of-term release of its most consequential opinions.

### Escalation Status: BACKGROUND — decision by June; flag immediately when issued

Xinis called this position a "fantasy." It has no judicial endorsement anywhere in the record and contradicts the simultaneously operative Costa Rica agreement. The April 20 DOJ brief will reveal whether the administration intends to maintain that fantasy at the cost of contempt, or retreat quietly. Given the administration's behavior on prior "facilitation" questions, the contempt-at-cost-of-defiance posture has precedent.

The May Day general strike organizing represents a parallel track that does not depend on any of the judicial outcomes. Whether or not Leon's injunction reinstates, whether or not Xinis finds contempt, the strike frame will land on May 1 regardless. The question for resistance strategy is whether a successful May Day — even partial — amplifies pressure on the courts and Congress, or whether the administration successfully frames strikers as lawbreakers to dilute the movement's institutional legitimacy.
