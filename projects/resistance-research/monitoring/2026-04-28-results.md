## New Developments Not in Pre-Brief (April 26 Update)

### 1. DC Circuit Terminates Boasberg Contempt Inquiry — April 15 (MATERIAL)

The DC Circuit issued a 2-1 ruling on April 15, 2026, ordering Judge Boasberg to end his criminal contempt investigation into the Trump administration's Venezuelan migrant deportation flights. Judge Neomi Rao's majority held that criminal contempt requires an order that is "unmistakably clear and specific" — and that the TRO in the Boasberg case did not meet that standard. Judge J. Michelle Childs dissented, warning that the ruling allows litigants to evade contempt by contesting orders based on their own interpretations.

**Why this matters for Xinis/April 28:** This ruling creates a direct precedent the DOJ will cite in the Maryland case if Xinis attempts to issue contempt sanctions. The administration will argue that Xinis's discovery orders, like the Boasberg TRO, were insufficiently "clear and specific" to support contempt. The strength of any contempt order Xinis issues on April 28 depends substantially on how precisely she drafts it. Lawyers for deported migrants are seeking full DC Circuit en banc review of the Boasberg ruling — but that request has not been resolved. This is the single most significant legal development that should have been in the pre-brief.

Source: [JURIST — US appellate court ends contempt inquiry over migrant deportations](https://www.jurist.org/news/2026/04/us-appellate-court-ends-contempt-inquiry-over-migrant-deportations/)

### 2. ICE Arrests Down 12% Since Minneapolis — April 25 Data (ENFORCEMENT POSTURE)

New Deportation Data Project analysis (published April 25) shows ICE arrests fell from an average of 8,347/week to 7,369/week — a 12% decline — in the five weeks following Tom Homan's February 4 drawdown announcement after the Minneapolis killings. However, the national decline is not uniform: arrests increased significantly in Kentucky, Indiana, North Carolina, and Florida during the same period. Collateral arrests (targeting people not originally intended as arrest targets) have also declined — from more than a quarter of all arrests at the start of the year to fewer than one-fifth by early March.

**Why this matters for May Day:** The moderated national tempo does not indicate enforcement withdrawal. The geographic inconsistency means May Day participants in Kentucky, Indiana, NC, and Florida face a more elevated enforcement environment than the national average suggests. The administration explicitly has not changed its enforcement goals; the change is tactical, not strategic.

Sources: [Washington Times — ICE arrests drop nearly 12% after immigration shake-up (April 25)](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/apr/25/ice-arrests-drop-nearly-12-minneapolis-killings-immigration-shake/); [Spectrum Local News — ICE arrests drop nearly 12%](https://spectrumlocalnews.com/snplus/politics/2026/04/25/ice-arrests-nationwide-data-minneapolis-immigration-shake-up-deportation-data)

## What We Know as of April 26 Pre-Hearing

### Abrego Garcia Case: Current State

**Short version**: The case remains partially under seal following an April 23 sealed-filing event. The April 28 hearing proceeds against a backdrop of unknown deposition outcomes and a hard April 30 discovery deadline. The case has produced a significant new whistleblower development and the Nashville criminal case has Todd Blanche's public statements threatening to blow it up entirely.

### Four Live Threads Entering the Hearing

**1. Sealed Discovery Stay (April 23 development)**

On April 23, both parties filed motions under seal. Judge Xinis issued a stay of all discovery until April 30 at 5:00 p.m. "with the agreement of the parties." The reason remains sealed. Abrego Garcia's attorney Simon Sandoval-Moshenberg said his team is "not at liberty to speak about the current status of the case at this time."

Analytical significance: Simultaneous sealed filings from both parties normally indicate negotiation rather than unilateral defiance. Whether the four court-ordered depositions actually occurred before the April 23 deadline passed is not in the public record.

**2. Contempt Exposure — Still Live**

Xinis has publicly flagged she is weighing contempt. The ABA Journal reported that even with Abrego Garcia physically present in the U.S., his attorneys' filing argues the government's discovery stonewalling "merits contempt inquiry." The record built through April 23 — 1,140 documents withheld under various privilege claims, only 32 produced in response to discovery — gives Xinis a documented factual basis for sanctions if she chooses to use it.

**3. Nashville / Crenshaw — Ruling Imminent, High Wildcard**

The Nashville human smuggling case before Judge Waverly Crenshaw has a ruling that is described in multiple sources as "imminent" or "at any time." As of April 26, no ruling has been issued. The trigger: Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche made public statements about the Abrego Garcia prosecution that prosecutors' own witnesses acknowledged on the record would be seen as vindicating the vindictive-prosecution claim. Crenshaw has two options — dismiss the charges, or summon Blanche to testify under oath. Either outcome reshapes the Maryland civil case: dismissal eliminates DOJ's rationale for keeping Abrego Garcia in the U.S. under criminal supervision, while compelled Blanche testimony under oath creates a new discovery vector directly into DOJ's political decision-making.

**4. Whistleblower: Erez Reuveni**

Former Acting Deputy Director for the Office of Immigration Litigation Erez Reuveni, in a June 2025 whistleblower complaint, stated he became aware of DOJ plans to resist court orders during deportation proceedings. He details three cases where he tried to argue for the government while refusing to violate laws. His complaint is now being cited in the Maryland civil case to argue the case should remain active and not be mooted. Significance: Reuveni's account provides internal DOJ documentation of deliberate non-compliance decisions — potentially usable as evidence in contempt proceedings.

## DHS Payroll Crisis: Hard May Deadline

**This is a material development for May Day logistics and post-May Day enforcement capacity.**

The DHS partial shutdown has now run 69 days. Emergency funds (from the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") will run dry after the first payroll in May — approximately the week of May 4-8. DHS spends approximately $1.6-1.7 billion on payroll every two weeks for 270,000 employees. As of April 19, fewer than $1.4 billion remained in the emergency fund.

The Senate passed a budget resolution 50-48 on April 23, clearing the path for $70 billion in ICE and CBP funding through reconciliation. The House must also pass the resolution before committees can draft actual legislation. Trump's deadline for the bill: June 1. The Homeland Security committees' draft legislation deadline: May 15.

**Practical implication**: ICE and DHS operations in the first week of May are proceeding under a known financial ceiling. Enforcement capacity does not necessarily decline (law enforcement-designated personnel have continued being paid throughout the shutdown), but the broader agency faces a structural constraint that may shape both operational tempo and political pressure around May Day.

### Safety and Legal Context

**Key new development — ProPublica / Frontline investigation (April 14-21 publication)**: "Caught in the Crackdown" documented more than 300 arrests of protesters and bystanders at anti-ICE protests in 2025-2026. In more than one third of cases, charges were dropped, refused by prosecutors, or lost at trial. Eight cases went to jury acquittal. The pattern: arresting officers' statements repeatedly contradicted by video evidence. This is significant for May Day participants because:

1. The arrest-to-conviction pipeline for political protest charges has a documented 30%+ failure rate at the federal level, where U.S. attorneys normally secure convictions in over 90% of cases.
2. The failure pattern correlates with video documentation — protests with high video coverage produced the highest charge-collapse rates.
3. The investigation establishes that federal agents made legally unsustainable arrests as a matter of pattern — not isolated incidents.

**ICE tracker injunction (April 18-23)**: U.S. District Judge Jorge L. Alonso (N.D. Illinois) granted a preliminary injunction protecting ICE-tracking apps and websites. The court found the Trump administration likely violated the First Amendment by strong-arming Apple and Facebook into removing both Eyes Up (ICE activity tracker) and ICE Sightings - Chicagoland. Both apps document publicly observable ICE operations. The injunction means these tools are now protected pending the full case — directly relevant to May Day participant safety planning.
