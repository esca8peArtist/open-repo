## 1. Leon Remand / April 17 Expiry — THE HARD DEADLINE

### Confirmed Pre-Expiry Facts

**What the D.C. Circuit actually ordered** (confirmed from multiple sources through April 15):
- The 2-1 majority (Millett and Garcia; Rao dissenting) stayed Leon's preliminary injunction through April 17, explicitly framing the extension as time for the administration "to seek Supreme Court review of this order remanding for factual development and clarification."
- The remand instruction was for Leon to clarify whether his injunction reaches the below-ground security infrastructure (bomb shelters, military hospital, missile-resistant structural elements, Top Secret military installations) or only the above-ground ballroom work.
- Leon's original March 31 ruling exempted "construction necessary to ensure the safety and security of the White House" but concluded, after reviewing classified material submitted ex parte, that his injunction did not jeopardize that work. The D.C. Circuit found the record insufficient to confirm this at the appellate level.

**What was NOT confirmed as of April 15**:
- No Leon clarification order appeared on the docket through the April 15 monitoring pass — six full days after the remand.
- No emergency SCOTUS application appeared on the public docket through April 15.
- No voluntary construction pause was announced by the administration.

**National Trust's factual position** (confirmed, April 13 filing):
The National Trust submitted that prior government representations established the above-ground and below-ground phases were "operationally and contractually separable." If accurate, a narrowed Leon order would halt above-ground ballroom work without touching security infrastructure, eliminating the administration's national security hook for a SCOTUS application.

### Decision Tree — Final State

**Branch A: Leon acted on April 16 or April 17 morning before expiry**

This is the orderly resolution path. Leon narrows his injunction to above-ground work only, the administration files cold at SCOTUS with a much weaker national-security argument (because the underground work is expressly permitted), and Roberts faces a choice between a routine shadow-docket stay on discretionary appropriations grounds or letting the above-ground halt stand pending full briefing. The administration's strongest argument on this branch is that the president has inherent constitutional authority over the White House as executive mansion. Roberts's question at the IEEPA oral argument — whether the administration's statutory reading "proves too much" — suggests he may apply the same scepticism to an overbroad executive-authority claim here. But the outcome on SCOTUS is genuinely uncertain even on Branch A.

**Branch B: Administration filed cold at SCOTUS on April 16 without Leon's clarification**

Procedurally permissible but awkward. The application would ask the Court to stay a remand the circuit court itself said was procedurally necessary. Roberts would have to grant an administrative stay to preserve status quo while the full Court deliberates — then issue a more permanent stay ruling. Roberts has granted administrative stays in compressed windows before (see the CFPB cases, the Title IX cases). The key variable is whether the factual record submitted to SCOTUS — without Leon's clarification — is sufficient for Roberts to find that the administration is likely to succeed on the merits. The Rao dissent gives Roberts a ready-made framework (Rao argued the majority should have vacated Leon outright on national security grounds rather than remanding). But Roberts also knows what a cold filing looks like. If the application is transparently thin — "let us keep building while we figure out the legal theory" — he may decline.

**Branch C: Neither Leon nor the administration acted — stay expired at midnight**

This is the branch where Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction reinstated automatically. Under this branch:
- The National Trust would file an emergency contempt motion with Leon within hours of midnight, or at court opening on April 18.
- The factual record for contempt is uniquely strong. Construction at the former East Wing site is physically visible from Pennsylvania Avenue and monitored continuously by press cameras. Unlike the Abrego Garcia contempt threads — which involve disputed testimony about what "facilitation" means — ballroom contempt would be photographic.
- Leon's response would determine whether the administration enters full contempt posture: he could issue an Order to Show Cause, a formal contempt finding, or apply incremental coercive sanctions (fines per day of noncompliance).
- An administration that continues construction against a reinstated Leon injunction would be making a deliberate choice to defy a federal court order in public view. This is qualitatively different from the Abrego Garcia compliance game, where "facilitation" is subject to interpretation. A crane on the East Wing site on April 18 morning is not subject to interpretation.

### Assessment: Most Likely Branch

The evidence pattern entering April 17 most strongly supports Branch C or a last-minute Branch B filing. The rationale:

Leon's six-day silence is not consistent with a judge who is completing a straightforward clarification order on a tight deadline set by his own circuit. Leon resisted earlier pressure in this case — the administration sought a stay from him directly after his March 31 ruling and he declined. His silence through April 15 suggests either (a) he is writing a careful, comprehensive clarification opinion that takes time, in which case it may appear April 16 or early April 17, or (b) he is deliberately not acting before the deadline, placing the burden on the administration to go to SCOTUS.

If Branch C materializes: the contempt filing and the administration's response to it become the dominant story. Watch for whether the administration argues that Leon's original order is void ab initio (executive prerogative theory), or whether it complies with the reinstated injunction while appealing. A compliance-with-appeal posture is legally conventional. A defiance posture is the constitutional confrontation scenario.

## 2. SCOTUS Application — No Filing Confirmed Through April 15

**Status**: No public SCOTUS docket entry for *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA* appears in indexed search results through April 15. This is the last confirmed data point.

**Why cold filing matters procedurally**: Roberts does not need to wait for the full Court. Under SCOTUS emergency practice, the Chief Justice (or the circuit justice, which for the D.C. Circuit is Roberts) can issue a unilateral administrative stay upon receipt of an application, typically within hours if time-sensitive. The administration knows this. If a filing occurred overnight April 16-17, Roberts had the procedural tools to act before midnight.

**The Rao dissent as SCOTUS roadmap**: The Rao dissent (D.C. Circuit, April 11) argued the majority should have vacated Leon's injunction outright rather than remanding. Rao wrote that the record before the court was sufficient to find that the national security implications had been inadequately weighed. If the administration filed at SCOTUS, it would attach the Rao dissent prominently. Roberts, deciding whether to administratively stay, would be reading an argument from a sitting circuit judge that the majority got this wrong. That is more persuasive than a government brief alone.

**If no SCOTUS filing occurred**: The government either accepted the construction halt (unlikely given the political investment in the project) or is operating in defiance. Both are monitored under Branch C above.

## 3. Section 122 CIT — Deliberating, No Ruling

**Status**: The three-judge CIT panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) has not issued a ruling as of April 17. The April 10 oral argument was seven days ago. The panel is deliberating.

**What remains confirmed from the record**:
- DOJ attorney Brett Shumate could not state the current balance-of-payments deficit when directly challenged by Judge Stanceu. This is on the record. Section 122 requires the president to find that "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits" exist before invoking the statute. The government's lead attorney could not identify the condition precedent.
- Judge Barnett's "proves too much" concern is consistent with the SCOTUS IEEPA majority's textualist approach — the Court refused to read sweeping emergency authority into narrower statutory text.
- The panel expressed uncertainty about translating the 1974 Bretton-Woods-era concept of "balance-of-payments" into a 2026 floating-exchange-rate context.

**Why silence is consistent with either outcome**: A panel ruling that invalidates a major tariff regime will be written carefully. CIT judges know the Federal Circuit and SCOTUS will review their work. The Volokh/Reason post-argument analysis characterized the panel as skeptical but noted that the form of the ruling — preliminary injunction vs. summary judgment vs. statutory narrowing — is genuinely uncertain.

**July 24 hard deadline**: Section 122 tariffs are 150-day emergency measures that expire July 24, 2026. A ruling before late June preserves the emergency appellate posture. A ruling after expiry risks mootness.

**Escalation status**: AMBER — monitoring daily. No change from April 15.

## 7. Background Threads

### 7a. Trump v. Slaughter (Humphrey's Executor) — Pending, Decision by June 2026

**Status**: Argument heard December 8, 2025. SCOTUSblog records "Opinion: TBD / Vote: TBD." No decision has been issued as of April 17.

Roberts described Humphrey's Executor as "a dried husk." Barrett said there has been "an eroding of Humphrey's Executor over the years." Gorsuch argued it was "poorly reasoned from the start." Post-argument analysis uniformly describes the government as in a strong position for an overruling or substantial narrowing.

**What an overruling means in practice**: At-will presidential removal authority over the heads of the FTC, NLRB, CFPB, FEC, MSPB, and all other independent regulatory agencies. This would be the most consequential expansion of unilateral executive power over the regulatory state since Humphrey's was decided in 1935. Combined with the IEEPA tariff ruling — where SCOTUS drew a line on emergency economic powers — the Court's current term is simultaneously constraining executive economic authority while expanding executive removal authority. These are not inconsistent: the Court's throughline is a formalist, text-based separation of powers that weakens the administrative state's independent institutional capacity while sharpening presidential control of what remains.

**Escalation status**: BACKGROUND — monitoring for decision.

## Analytical Note: The Pattern Being Established

The convergence of threads in the April 15-28 window is not coincidental. Three separate courts — Leon in D.C., Crenshaw in Nashville, Xinis in Maryland — are simultaneously holding decisions that could define the practical limits of judicial authority over the administration. The administration has been managing these threads individually: running out the clock on the ballroom, wearing down Nashville with process, and arguing definitional games ("facilitation") in Maryland. 

What changes if Branch C of the ballroom scenario materialized — if the stay simply expired and construction continued — is the visibility. Unlike the immigration compliance games, which require factual inquiry into what "facilitation" means, a construction crane operating against a reinstated court order is a public, photographic, unambiguous fact. That creates a different kind of contempt record than any of the immigration threads: one that is impossible to explain away as compliance-with-different-interpretation, and one that is happening at the most symbolically loaded site in American government.

The administration understands this. Which is why the Branch C outcome — deliberate defiance of the reinstated Leon injunction — would represent a qualitative escalation in the administration's relationship with the federal judiciary. It would also generate the clearest legal record of contempt since the cycle began. Whether that is the outcome being observed is what the post-indexing follow-up will determine.
