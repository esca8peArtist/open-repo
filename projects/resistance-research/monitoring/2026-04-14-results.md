## 4. Section 122 CIT Ruling — NO RULING, Panel Tracks Toward Invalidation

**Result**: The three-judge CIT panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) has not issued a ruling following the April 10 marathon oral argument.

**The April 10 argument posture — confirmed details**: The Volokh Conspiracy analysis (published the day of argument) and International Trade Today reporting document the following:

- Judge Stanceu repeatedly challenged DOJ attorney Brett Shumate on whether the statutory condition — "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits" — had been met. Shumate conceded he could not state what the balance of payments deficit is, could not even give an estimate. This is a fatal evidentiary gap: the statute requires the president to find a specific condition exists before invoking it, and the government's own lawyer cannot articulate what that condition is.

- Judge Barnett expressed concern that the administration's theory "proves too much" — that under the government's reading, the president could invoke Section 122 at any time based on any trade deficit, effectively giving him permanent, unlimited tariff authority without congressional reauthorization. Barnett's framing mirrors the SCOTUS majority's concern in the IEEPA case: the Court was unwilling to read such sweeping authority into statutory text.

- The panel's questions focused heavily on the definition of "balance-of-payments deficits." The administration's position (that the current account deficit satisfies the trigger) conflicts with the statute's apparent origin in the Bretton Woods fixed-exchange-rate era, when "balance of payments" had a specific, narrower meaning related to the capacity to defend a currency peg.

**Why no ruling yet is not surprising**: This is a three-judge panel with a complex, first-impression statutory question. Even a panel that is fully aligned on outcome will require weeks to draft an opinion that addresses all arguments, is internally consistent, and can withstand Federal Circuit review. The April 10 argument was a Friday; April 14 is the first full business week after the hearing. No ruling this quickly was expected.

**The July 24, 2026 expiry factor — new development**: The Section 122 tariffs are set to expire on July 24, 2026, after 150 days, unless Congress votes to extend them. Congress is unlikely to do so given current political dynamics. This creates a hard deadline for the economic significance of the CIT ruling: a ruling before July 24 forces a Federal Circuit or SCOTUS emergency resolution; a ruling after July 24 (if the tariffs have already expired) may moot the emergency posture even if the legal question remains.

**Monitoring posture**: Check CIT docket daily. Any ruling from this panel is a code-red event requiring same-day analysis and update to the litigation tracker.

## 7. New Developments Not in Watch Brief

### 7a. Section 122 July 24 Expiry — Hard Deadline for Economic Stakes

Not flagged in prior briefings: the Section 122 tariffs are statutory 150-day emergency measures that expire July 24, 2026 unless Congress votes to extend. Congress is politically unlikely to do so. This creates a ruling deadline: a CIT ruling before July 24 produces an emergency Federal Circuit/SCOTUS posture with live economic stakes; a ruling after July 24 with expired tariffs eliminates the emergency basis and may result in a moot appeal even if the legal question has enduring significance.

**Implication for tracking**: The CIT panel has approximately 100 days from oral argument before the tariffs expire. There is no formal deadline, but a ruling after June 1 begins to compress the Federal Circuit review window. Add July 24 to the hard-deadline calendar.

### 7b. Shumate Admissions — Evidentiary Record Confirmed

The April 10 oral argument produced a confirmed evidentiary gap: DOJ attorney Brett Shumate admitted on the record that he cannot state what the U.S. balance-of-payments deficit is, cannot even estimate it. This admission is now in the CIT record and is legally significant: Section 122 requires the president to find "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits" — if the government's own counsel cannot articulate the deficit, it weakens the statutory-findings basis for the invocation.

Trump's public statement that "the military is building a massive complex under the ballroom, and that's under construction" is now in the public record and presumably known to Leon. This voluntary presidential disclosure that the underground complex is military in character strengthens the D.C.
