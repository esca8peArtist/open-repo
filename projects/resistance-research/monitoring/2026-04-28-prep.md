## What the DC Circuit Boasberg Ruling Means for April 28

This is the most important legal development to understand before watching the April 28 hearing. It is not in the pre-brief and it directly shapes the hearing.

**The ruling**: On April 15, 2026, the DC Circuit issued a 2-1 decision (Rao-Walker majority, Childs dissenting) ordering Judge Boasberg to terminate his criminal contempt investigation into Trump administration officials for the Venezuelan deportation flights. The majority held that criminal contempt requires an order that is "unmistakably clear and specific" — and that the Boasberg TRO did not meet that standard. [Source: JURIST](https://www.jurist.org/news/2026/04/us-appellate-court-ends-contempt-inquiry-over-migrant-deportations/)

**What DOJ will do with it at April 28**: DOJ will almost certainly cite this ruling in any contempt proceeding before Xinis. The argument will be: just as Boasberg's TRO was insufficiently clear to support contempt, Xinis's discovery orders are insufficiently clear (or insufficiently complied-with in bad faith) to support contempt.

**Why this argument is weaker in the Xinis context than the Boasberg context**:

1. Xinis's contemplated contempt is *civil*, not criminal. The Boasberg ruling specifically addressed criminal contempt procedures. Civil contempt (coercive sanctions — daily fines, incarceration until compliance) is governed by a different legal standard: willful non-compliance with a valid court order. The DC Circuit's ruling does not foreclose civil contempt in the Fourth Circuit.

2. The Fourth Circuit is not bound by DC Circuit precedent. The Fourth Circuit covers Maryland; the DC Circuit covers D.C. They are coordinate circuits with independent authority.

3. SCOTUS's unanimous April 10 return order is the controlling authority above Xinis's orders. The Boasberg ruling was about a TRO. The Xinis contempt exposure arises from non-compliance with discovery orders entered after SCOTUS's return mandate. The posture is legally distinct.

4. Xinis has a documented factual basis that Boasberg did not: 1,140 documents withheld, only 32 produced, a "bad faith" finding already on the record, and four deposition orders whose completion status remains unknown.

**The counterargument the administration has**: The Rao majority's logic — that contempt requires unmistakably clear orders — can be extended by analogy to any sufficiently contested order. If the administration disputes the scope of the discovery orders, it can argue those orders are not "unmistakably clear" in what they require. This is a weaker argument against Xinis's explicit deposition orders than it was against Boasberg's more ambiguous TRO, but it is the argument that will be made.

**En banc watch**: Abrego Garcia's lawyers and civil libertarian groups are seeking full DC Circuit en banc review of the Boasberg ruling. If en banc review is granted and the ruling reversed, the precedent DOJ is trying to leverage evaporates. No ruling on the en banc petition as of April 26.

## The Sealed Record — Three Competing Theories

The April 23 sealed filings are the central unknown entering April 28. Understanding which theory is correct will shape what the hearing is actually about.

**Theory A — Negotiated partial compliance**: Both parties filed under seal because they are presenting the court with a negotiated offer. The government offered compliance on some subset of the deposition and document demands; the plaintiff's side accepted the pause to evaluate the offer. The April 28 hearing is a status conference to report on where negotiations stand. April 30 is the deadline for compliance terms to be confirmed or denied.

*Signal that this is correct*: If the hearing opens in public session with both sides' lawyers speaking calmly and Xinis scheduling a compliance review for April 30, this is Theory A.

**Theory B — Sealed refusal with preparation for Fourth Circuit appeal**: The government formally refused the depositions through sealed filings and both parties agreed to pause proceedings while DOJ prepares an emergency Fourth Circuit stay. The sealed motions are DOJ's formal notice of intent to seek circuit review. The April 28 hearing is a required status conference before the administration files upward.

*Signal that this is correct*: If the hearing is brief, produces no orders, and is followed within 2-6 hours by a Fourth Circuit emergency docket entry, this is Theory B.

**Theory C — Partial compliance under privilege assertion**: Officials sat for depositions but asserted executive privilege extensively. The sealed filing is a privilege log. Both parties agreed to litigate the privilege claims under seal. The April 28 hearing addresses the adequacy of privilege invocations rather than outright defiance.

*Signal that this is correct*: If Xinis is asking pointed questions about the content of specific privilege assertions, and if we hear references to "what was said versus what was withheld," this is Theory C.

Theory A is most consistent with the mutual agreement to the stay. Theories B and C are both consistent with DOJ's established pattern of delay through process rather than direct defiance. All three remain live.
