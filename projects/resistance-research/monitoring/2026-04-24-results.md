## Key Finding: Deposition Status

**The deposition completion status for April 23, 2026 is not confirmed in public reporting as of April 24.**

The AI-synthesized summaries in search results were conflating April 23, 2025 sealed-motion events with the current April 2026 proceedings. After cross-checking article dates, no confirmed public reporting on whether the four Trump administration officials (Mazzara, Cerna, and two others from DHS/State) complied with or defied the April 23, 2026 deposition deadline has been found.

This is analytically significant in its own right: if the depositions had been cleanly completed and the government fully complied, that would be newsworthy enough to generate clear reporting. A similar sealed-motion dynamic in 2026 cannot be ruled out.

The April 28 Xinis hearing remains the next confirmed public event where deposition outcomes will be on the record.

## May Day 2026 — April 23-24 Developments

Two confirmed new developments in the April 17-24 window that postdate the April 23 brief's framing:

### 1. Chicago/CPS-CTU Agreement (April 17 — confirmed)

Chicago Public Schools and the Chicago Teachers Union (31,000 members) reached an agreement on April 17 after days of tension. The CTU had pushed to cancel school entirely on May 1; CPS management initially refused. The agreement:

- Schools remain open for full instruction on May 1.
- CPS provides buses to an afternoon rally for students and educators who wish to attend.
- May 1, 2028 (next time May Day falls on a school day) is designated a teacher-directed professional day.

Mayor Brandon Johnson issued an official statement: "We are pleased all parties are working together to ensure school communities can participate in commemorating International Workers Day."

**Significance**: This resolves the sharp CTU-CPS conflict in a way that gives organized labor a partial win — public school infrastructure (buses) supporting the May Day rally — without a full school closure. It is a model for how institutional actors can partially accommodate general-strike pressure without formally endorsing it. The April 23 brief had described the CPS involvement as "subsidizing teacher participation" based on a Jonathan Turley op-ed; the actual outcome is more nuanced.

### 2. Strike Geography Expanding (confirmed as of April 23-24)

Payday Report tracking now shows **65-85 cities** hosting confirmed May Day actions (up from the coalition count framework in the April 23 brief). New additions include:

- Over 1,000 nurses at **University Medical Center New Orleans** beginning a five-day strike on May 1, tied to failed contract negotiations (not purely political — overlapping labor dispute and civic action).
- Informational pickets planned by nurses in Wichita, Kansas.
- Small business closures, particularly in immigrant-community businesses, being organized in solidarity.

### Government/Employer Reactions

**Federal government**: No confirmed executive action targeting May Day organizing as of April 24 — no executive order, no DOJ action, no ICE enforcement threat targeting demonstrations.

**Employers**: Fisher Phillips LLP (employer-side labor firm) published a guide for employers on May Day walkout rights and responsibilities, noting that many CBAs include no-strike clauses that would make coordinated walkouts contract violations. This is an indicator that large employers are actively preparing legal responses, not yet filing pre-emptive injunctions.

**Chicago (as above)**: Partial accommodation rather than opposition.

### What Remains Unchanged

No formal AFL-CIO national federation strike call has been issued. The limiting factor distinguishing this from an industrial general strike in the classic sense remains the same.

### Escalation Status: AMBER/GREEN — scale is growing (65-85 cities, nursing strike added), employer legal framework being activated but no injunctions filed, no federal enforcement action. April 29 Mass Call (7:30pm) and April 28 Workers Memorial Day are next lead-up markers.

## CIT Section 122 — Any Ruling?

No ruling confirmed as of April 24. The Barnett/Kelly/Stanceu panel has issued no decision, injunction, or supplemental briefing order in the fourteen days since the April 10 oral argument.

The states' motion included both a motion for summary judgment and a separate motion for preliminary injunction — the preliminary injunction motion is the higher-urgency vehicle. Oregon AG Rayfield publicly expressed hope for a ruling on the speed of the IEEPA CIT decision (approximately two weeks post-argument). That two-week window is now passing without a ruling.

**New contextual development**: One additional legal analysis (Yale Journal on Regulation) notes that the Supreme Court's IEEPA ruling threw out one of two tariff cases before it, and that the remaining case may affect how courts interpret statutory trade authority limits going forward. This does not alter the Section 122 analysis directly but reinforces the hostile legal climate for broad executive tariff authority.

**July 24 hard deadline** (150-day Section 122 expiry) remains the outer mootness boundary.

### Escalation Status: AMBER — no change, deliberation now at 14 days. The preliminary injunction motion is the watch item; an injunction would immediately halt the 10% surcharge.
