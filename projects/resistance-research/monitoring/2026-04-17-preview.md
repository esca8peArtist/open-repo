# Pre-April-17 Scenario Analysis
## What the Next Four Days Could Look Like

*Written April 13, 2026 — Session 79 analysis pass*
*This is not a monitoring pass. It is a forward-looking analytical document.*

---

## The Stakes

April 17 is not a single event. It is a collision point for at least three simultaneous judicial pressure fronts that have been building since March. At its center is the White House ballroom case — National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA — where a D.C. Circuit stay of Judge Leon's injunction expires at midnight. If the administration wants construction to continue past that date, it must either get Leon to narrow his order before the deadline, or file an emergency application at the Supreme Court. Neither has happened as of Sunday evening, April 13.

The broader significance is larger than the ballroom. This is the first time the administration faces a hard judicial deadline where its preferred path — shadow docket emergency application to SCOTUS — may not be available because it has not yet secured the factual predicate that would make the application plausible. Judge Leon has been instructed to resolve the factual question the administration needs resolved before SCOTUS can credibly act. Whether the administration waits for that answer or goes to SCOTUS cold is the central strategic question of the next four days.

## Part 1: The April 17 Ballroom Scenarios

### Background: How We Got Here

Judge Leon (U.S. District Court, D.C., George W. Bush appointee) issued a preliminary injunction in late March 2026, finding that the administration's $300-400 million White House East Wing demolition and ballroom construction project almost certainly required congressional authorization that was never sought. His ruling rested on the Appropriations Clause: Congress controls federal spending, and a landmark-demolishing construction project at a protected federal historic property is not within the president's inherent facility-management authority.

The D.C. Circuit stayed Leon's order twice — first through April 14, then through April 17 — while a 2-1 majority (Millett and Garcia; Rao dissenting) worked through the factual complexity. The majority's problem is this: the administration filed an April 9 brief detailing that below the ballroom footprint lies classified infrastructure including bomb shelters, a military hospital, missile-resistant structural elements, and Top Secret Military installations. The majority could not tell, from the record before it, whether Leon's injunction blocked only the above-ground ballroom work or also the below-ground security infrastructure. So it remanded to Leon for a factual clarification.

The National Trust responded on April 13 with a direct factual counter-claim: prior government representations had established that above-ground and below-ground phases were "operationally and contractually separable," meaning the above-ground ballroom work could be stopped without affecting the below-ground security installations. If that is true, there is no national security emergency — there is a ballroom emergency.

That is where things stand as of Sunday evening.

---

### Scenario A: Leon Narrows His Order Before April 17

**What this means:** Leon issues an order before April 17 clarifying that his injunction does NOT reach the below-ground security infrastructure — bomb shelters, military hospital, Top Secret installations. The injunction as narrowed would halt above-ground ballroom construction but expressly permit below-ground work to continue.

**Probability:** 40%. Leon is a methodical judge who resisted pressure to rule quickly at earlier stages. The four-day window (April 13-17) is tight but not impossible — the D.C. Circuit's remand instruction is effectively a directive to act fast.

**What happens to the SCOTUS application:**

If Leon narrows his order to above-ground work only, the administration's primary SCOTUS argument — "halting construction creates a national security emergency because the underground security work cannot proceed" — largely collapses. The National Trust would be right: the security work is separable, Leon's narrowed order does not touch it, and there is no emergency. The administration could still file at SCOTUS arguing that even the above-ground ballroom halt is constitutionally infirm (executive prerogative over White House facilities, etc.), but this is a much weaker argument without the national security overlay. DOJ would need to argue constitutional authority over ordinary facility construction — an area where Congress has explicit spending power — without the sympathetic "protecting the president" hook.

A Roberts Court facing a narrowed Leon order would have much less ideological cover to grant emergency relief. Roberts himself has shown at oral argument (in Trump v. Barbara, April 1, in the IEEPA tariff case) that he is willing to tell the administration its arguments don't work on the text. "The president wants a ballroom and Congress didn't appropriate the money" is not a shadow docket emergency.

**What it means for construction actually stopped:**

The above-ground ballroom work halts. This is significant because the administration has been racing construction — every day of above-ground work before April 17 is work that creates facts on the ground. A narrow Leon order freezes that work above ground while the merits are resolved, which could take months. Below-ground work continues, meaning the security infrastructure arguments become moot and cannot be relitigated at SCOTUS.

**Rule-of-law pattern:**

A narrow Leon order sustained by the D.C. Circuit (if SCOTUS declines to grant emergency relief above-ground) would be a significant affirmation that the Appropriations Clause means something — that the president cannot redirect hundreds of millions of dollars in construction at a federally protected historic site by executive fiat. The pattern it reinforces is that courts are willing to enforce structural constitutional constraints (spending, appropriations) even in politically charged contexts, so long as the emergency-override rhetoric (national security) can be separated from the actual legal question.

### Scenario B: No Leon Response + No SCOTUS Application — Stay Expires April 17

**What this means:** Leon does not issue a clarification before April 17. The administration does not file at SCOTUS. The D.C. Circuit stay expires. Leon's original injunction reinstates. Construction must halt.

**Probability:** 20%. This is the most overlooked scenario because the conventional assumption is that the administration will either get a ruling or file at SCOTUS. But the remand puts Leon in the driver's seat on timing, and the administration filing at SCOTUS without the factual record the D.C. Circuit requested would be unusual.

**The contempt problem:**

If the injunction reinstates and the administration continues construction, it is in contempt. This is not theoretical — the pattern across immigration cases (CASA v. DHS, Ramirez Ovando in Colorado, Gonzalez in Eastern California) is that the administration has continued conduct in apparent defiance of court orders. If it does so here — if cranes continue operating on April 18 in defiance of a reinstated Leon injunction — the National Trust would immediately file for an order to show cause. A contempt finding against the executive branch for ongoing physical construction at a federally protected site would be qualitatively different from the immigration contempt proceedings. It would be visible, physical, daily, and politically radioactive.

The administration's calculation is whether contempt proceedings at the district court level are more manageable than the political cost of acknowledging the court's authority. Given that it has already managed immigration contempt threats without SCOTUS intervention, it might make a similar calculation here. But the ballroom is visible in a way that ICE arrests are not — journalists can stand outside the White House and photograph whether cranes are running.

**Rule-of-law pattern:**

Scenario B produces the starkest rule-of-law test of the four scenarios. If the administration ignores the reinstated injunction, it has crossed into open defiance of a specific court order about a specific, photographable construction project. The question then becomes whether the D.C. Circuit or SCOTUS will accelerate contempt proceedings or allow them to drag. Historical precedent from the Nixon tapes case (U.S. v. Nixon, 1974) suggests that the Court will not allow open executive defiance of a specific order to stand indefinitely — but the timescale for that resolution can be measured in weeks, not hours.

### Scenario C: SCOTUS Application Filed Before April 17

**What this means:** DOJ files an emergency application at the Supreme Court — probably addressed to Roberts as Circuit Justice for the D.C. Circuit, possibly to Kavanaugh as the designated backup — before April 17. The application asks SCOTUS to stay Leon's injunction pending full review.

**Probability:** 50% (the most likely scenario if Leon does not act first; drops to 25% if Leon narrows).

**Routing and mechanics:**

D.C. Circuit emergency applications go first to the Chief Justice. Roberts can act unilaterally (grant a stay, deny it, or call for a response from the opposing party), or he can refer the matter to the full Court. Given the political salience and the factual complexity the D.C. Circuit has flagged, Roberts would almost certainly call for a response from the National Trust before acting — which would compress the timeline further but would also deny the administration an immediate unilateral win. A solo Roberts denial, at this stage, without a response, would be extraordinary and would almost certainly not happen.

**Realistic outcomes if filed:**

Three possibilities:

1. Roberts grants a temporary administrative stay pending response (60% probability if application is filed). This is the lowest-commitment move — it keeps construction going for another week or two while briefing happens, and does not constitute a ruling on the merits. It fits Roberts' documented preference for process over unilateral shadow docket action where he has written critically about the abuse of emergency applications.

2. Roberts refers to full Court; full Court grants a stay pending cert (20% probability if application is filed). This requires five votes. With a 6-3 conservative supermajority, the votes are conceivably there — Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett have all been willing to grant administration emergency applications. Roberts' posture in this case is uncertain; he has shown skepticism of administration overreach arguments on statutory grounds but has not broken with the conservative bloc on emergency applications about executive facility use. However, the administration's win rate on the shadow docket has been approximately 80% overall, and this case — with its "protecting the president" framing — would likely reach that threshold.

3. SCOTUS denies the application (20% probability if application is filed). This would be a genuinely significant signal — a refusal to grant emergency relief means the injunction reinstates and the Court has declined, at least for now, to protect the administration's construction from judicial oversight. A denial, if accompanied by any written statement from the majority, would be the clearest signal yet about the Court's tolerance for Appropriations Clause violations framed as security emergencies.

**Roberts' posture — what we actually know:**

In the IEEPA tariff oral argument (February 2026, Learning Resources), Roberts joined a 6-3 majority striking down tariffs on statutory grounds, writing that IEEPA's words "cannot bear such weight." This is Roberts in his statutory textualist mode — willing to rule against the administration when the legal text does not support the claim. His comment at the birthright citizenship oral argument ("It's the same Constitution") has been characterized as skeptical of the administration's argument that the 14th Amendment should be read to exclude unauthorized-presence births. In the MSPB/NLRB removal-power case, Roberts issued a solo stay of Harris and Wilcox's reinstatement on April 9 — consistent with the view that he likely believes the president has broader removal authority than the lower courts held.

The ballroom case is a different posture. It is not a statutory textualism question (unlike IEEPA). It is not a removal-power question. It is an Appropriations Clause question combined with a national security claim. The national security claim — if Leon's order genuinely stopped underground security work — would likely give Roberts cover to grant a stay. If Leon narrows his order before the application arrives, that cover disappears. Roberts granting emergency relief to protect a ballroom, without the security emergency, would be the most politically exposed shadow docket move of his tenure.

**Counter-arguments the National Trust will make:**

If SCOTUS calls for a response, the National Trust's brief will argue: (1) no genuine irreparable harm to national security — the security infrastructure is below ground and Leon's injunction can be narrowed to exclude it; (2) Congress controls appropriations for White House construction and no appropriation exists for this project; (3) the historic preservation interest is real and irreversible — demolition of the East Wing cannot be undone; (4) the administration's security framing was not raised in the original proceedings before Leon, was raised for the first time on emergency appeal, and is unsupported by the factual record the D.C. Circuit has now directed to be developed.

**Rule-of-law pattern:**

A SCOTUS grant here — particularly a grant without a full factual record — would extend the shadow docket dynamic the Court has already established across immigration, DOGE, and civil service cases. The pattern is: lower court enjoins executive overreach on merits; administration files at SCOTUS emergency docket; Court grants stay with minimal briefing; case proceeds on shadow docket timescale (months to years). If SCOTUS grants here, the Appropriations Clause gets added to the list of constitutional provisions the administration can effectively override on the emergency docket, pending eventual merits resolution.

A SCOTUS denial — particularly if Roberts writes or notes — would be a meaningful signal in the opposite direction: that the emergency docket has limits, that the Appropriations Clause is not subordinate to executive national security assertions, and that some categories of executive action require full merits review before courts will permit them to proceed.

### Scenario D: Leon EXPANDS His Order (Includes Underground/Bomb-Shelter Work)

**What this means:** Leon responds to the D.C. Circuit's remand by issuing a clarification that expressly states his original injunction DOES reach the underground security infrastructure — that all construction at the White House East Wing site, including below-ground work, is covered.

**Probability:** 15%. Leon's original ruling focused on the above-ground ballroom project; the security infrastructure was not the target of the lawsuit. An expansion would require Leon to make findings about the security work without a developed record. However, the National Trust's April 13 brief explicitly argued the phases are separable — if Leon accepts that framing, he might conclude his injunction as written already covers both phases.

**Effect on SCOTUS application:**

A Leon expansion would be the strongest possible trigger for a SCOTUS application. If above-ground AND below-ground construction are both halted, the administration's national security argument is at maximum force: a federal judge has ordered the halt of classified military infrastructure at the White House. This is the scenario where Roberts would face the most pressure to act solo and fast. Even a justice skeptical of the administration's position would find it difficult to allow classified underground construction to be halted without full briefing on the security implications.

**Probable SCOTUS response:**

If Leon expands, expect a SCOTUS administrative stay within 24-48 hours of application, almost certainly granted by Roberts unilaterally. The stay would cover all construction — above and below ground — while full briefing proceeds. This would be the administration's best case outcome: SCOTUS not only keeps construction going but does so in a posture where the "security emergency" framing appears most credible.

**Rule-of-law pattern:**

A Leon expansion followed by a SCOTUS stay would crystallize the tension in the cleanest possible way: a District Court judge applying the Appropriations Clause to block classified Executive Branch construction at the White House, and SCOTUS overriding that finding on the emergency docket. The question of congressional spending power over White House facility construction would then be litigated on the full shadow docket timeline — meaning construction continues for months while the merits resolve, potentially making the injunction moot through completion. This is the pattern visible in other areas: the administration wins the race against the injunction by winning on the emergency docket, even if it ultimately loses on the merits.

## Part 2: The SCOTUS Application Probability Assessment

### What We Know About Roberts' Posture

Roberts is not a simple conservative bloc vote. He is operating across three visible data points that are relevant here:

**IEEPA tariffs (February 20, 2026, 6-3):** Roberts wrote for the majority striking down IEEPA tariffs on statutory grounds. He was willing to deal the administration a major policy defeat in a case where the administration argued broad executive emergency power. The operative language — "cannot bear such weight" — is textualist, not ideological. It suggests Roberts draws lines on statutory authority claims when the text does not support them.

**Birthright citizenship oral argument (April 1, 2026):** Roberts' comment — "It's the same Constitution" — is widely read as skeptical of the administration's argument that a 14th Amendment understanding settled for 128 years (since Wong Kim Ark, 1898) should now be narrowed by executive order. This is Roberts in his institutionalist mode: the Court's precedents and the Constitution's settled meaning are not infinitely malleable to current executive preference.

**MSPB/NLRB solo stay (April 9, 2026):** Roberts unilaterally stayed the reinstatement orders for fired independent agency heads — a move that almost certainly signals the full Court will rule in the administration's favor on removal power in Trump v. Slaughter. This is Roberts in his executive-power-expansion mode: willing to grant emergency relief to protect the administration's ability to remove officials the president wants removed. The government's win rate on shadow docket applications where Roberts issued a solo order is very high.

**Roberts in the ballroom case:** Which Roberts shows up depends heavily on whether the security framing survives the Leon remand. If it does — if below-ground work is genuinely at risk — Roberts is more likely to act solo and fast (MSPB mode). If the security framing collapses because Leon narrowed the order — if it becomes purely a ballroom question — Roberts is more likely to call for briefing and possibly decline (IEEPA/birthright mode). The IEEPA ruling was 6-3; the birthright oral argument skepticism was from Roberts specifically. In both cases, Roberts was willing to say publicly that the administration's argument did not work. The ballroom case, stripped of its security overlay, is constitutionally a cleaner case for the National Trust than IEEPA was for the importers.

**Probability assessment — SCOTUS grants emergency stay if application filed:**

- If Leon narrows (security framing collapses): 30% chance SCOTUS grants stay
- If Leon expands or is silent (security framing intact): 70% chance SCOTUS grants stay
- Overall blended probability (weighted by scenario probabilities): approximately 50-55% chance SCOTUS would grant if an application is filed

This is notably lower than the administration's overall shadow docket win rate (~80%), reflecting the unusual posture of this case: a conservative judge (Leon, Bush appointee) applying the Appropriations Clause, no immigration or national security emergency in the primary framing, and Roberts' demonstrated willingness to rule against the administration on structural constitutional questions.

### Administration's Arguments If They File

The administration's SCOTUS application will likely be structured around three claims:

**1. Commander-in-chief and facility authority:** The president has inherent constitutional authority over White House operations and security, including physical construction necessary to protect the first family. No specific appropriation is required for security measures taken at the seat of executive government. This argument has not been tested directly at SCOTUS, but the administration has analogous emergency-power framing in cases like the travel ban and border wall cases.

**2. Irreparable harm to national security:** Halting construction while classified infrastructure is in progress creates operational gaps in presidential security. The specific items (missile-resistant structure, drone-proof roofing, blast-proof glass, bomb shelters, classified military installation) are tangible and verifiable. Even if the above-ground ballroom is separable, the administration will argue that construction sequencing means stopping above-ground work necessarily delays the completion of the integrated project, including below-ground elements.

**3. National Trust lacks Article III standing or has an inadequate legal basis:** The administration will argue that historic preservation law does not give a private organization the authority to dictate White House construction, and that the "no statute comes close" formulation Leon used reflects an overread of the Appropriations Clause as a check on inherent executive authority over executive facilities.

### National Trust Counter-Arguments

**1. The Appropriations Clause is unambiguous:** Congress controls spending. A $300-400 million construction project at a federally protected historic property requires a congressional appropriation. No appropriation has been sought or granted. Leon's formulation — "no statute comes close to giving the President the authority he claims" — is textualism, not activism. Roberts' own IEEPA opinion used the same reasoning structure.

**2. No genuine irreparable harm to national security:** Prior government representations established that above-ground and below-ground phases are separable. Leon's injunction, narrowed to above-ground work, does not stop security infrastructure. The administration's security claims were raised for the first time on emergency appeal, after the district court proceedings were complete — a maneuver that should not generate emergency relief.

**3. Historic preservation harm is irreversible:** Unlike most injunction postures where the question is which side faces irreversible harm, here the administration's position has the National Trust facing it: demolishing the East Wing and building a ballroom cannot be undone. The administration can wait for the merits to resolve; it cannot rebuild a demolished historic structure.

**4. Shadow docket abuse:** The National Trust will argue, if they have counsel willing to make this argument, that the shadow docket mechanism should not be used to override a developed district court record (Leon issued a 37-page opinion) on a question of first impression (Appropriations Clause, White House construction, historic preservation) without full briefing. This is the strongest version of Roberts' own public concerns about the shadow docket being used to make substantive law.

## Part 3: Concurrent April 14-17 Events

### CIT Conference on IEEPA Refund Implementation (April 14)

Two things happening in the same courthouse at different times on April 14:

**Noon: CBP files Phase 1 status report.** CBP must file a declaration showing progress toward the four components of the IEEPA tariff refund system (CAPE system), which CBP reported as 60-85% complete as of April 1. Phase 1 launches April 20 and covers approximately 63% of affected import entries — unliquidated entries, entries within the 90-day voluntary reliquidation window, and entries in "Suspended/Extended/Under Review" status. If the declaration shows material slippage, Senior Judge Eaton could modify the April 20 deadline or order a further compliance hearing.

**3 PM: Closed conference before Senior Judge Eaton.** This is not a public hearing. The conference will likely focus on whether the April 20 Phase 1 launch date is achievable, whether importers have adequate notice to submit refund applications, and whether the Phase 2 timeline (finally-liquidated entries) needs to be specified. The IEEPA refund process is an implementation track — the legality question was resolved 6-3 in February. What Eaton is managing is whether CBP actually executes.

**What a Phase 1 refund failure would mean:** If CBP misses the April 20 deadline, Eaton would likely hold a compliance hearing and possibly impose specific milestones with penalties. This is a less dramatic outcome than the Section 122 ruling but is important for the thousands of importers waiting for refunds on already-collected duties.

### Section 122 Ruling — When and What

The three-judge CIT panel (Barnett, Kelly, Stanceu) has not issued a ruling following the April 10 oral argument. The panel was sharply skeptical of the administration's position — the DOJ attorney could not state what the current balance-of-payments deficit is, which is the statutory predicate for Section 122 authority. Two of three judges suggested the government's interpretation "proves too much" — i.e., would authorize tariffs virtually any time the president wanted.

**If the ruling comes this week (April 14-17):** A ruling striking down the Section 122 10% global tariff would land simultaneously with the ballroom April 17 deadline. The combined effect would be: the administration faces a possible SCOTUS application on ballroom construction AND a second major tariff invalidation in less than two months. The administration would immediately seek a stay of any Section 122 ruling pending appeal to the Federal Circuit. The CIT panel may or may not grant a stay; if not, the administration goes to the Federal Circuit or SCOTUS for emergency relief.

**Probability of Section 122 ruling this week:** 25%. The panel heard argument April 10 and has had four days. Oral argument posture was strongly against the administration. However, trade court rulings on statutory authority questions often take weeks. The July 24, 2026 expiration date of the 150-day Section 122 window creates some urgency — if courts want to rule before the tariff expires on its own terms, they have roughly 100 days from now. There is no hard deadline compelling a ruling this week.

**What invalidation would mean:** If the CIT panel invalidates Section 122 tariffs, the administration has exhausted its primary tariff legal authorities. IEEPA: invalidated 6-3. Section 122: invalidated. The administration's remaining options would be Section 301 tariffs (which require a USTR determination of unfair trade practices and a formal investigation process), the Smoot-Hawley tariff schedules (which require legislative action), and targeted national-security tariffs under Section 232 (which have survived judicial review in prior cases but require a specific national security finding). None of these are as flexible or comprehensive as the IEEPA framework the administration had been using. The tariff enforcement architecture the administration built in the first half of 2025 would effectively need to be rebuilt on a narrower statutory foundation.

### Other Pending Rulings April 14-17

**Ramirez Ovando v. Noem (Colorado warrantless arrests):** Judge Jackson's compliance ruling following the March 10-11 evidentiary hearing is still pending. A contempt finding here — which Jackson has the record to support — could come any day. It would be the first contempt finding against the administration for defiance of a warrantless-arrest injunction, and would arrive in the same week as the ballroom deadline.

**Nashville (Crenshaw, Abrego Garcia criminal case):** No ruling yet. Dismissal described as "imminent" since April 11. If Crenshaw rules this week, it would likely be a dismissal — removing the last federal criminal exposure in Abrego Garcia's case and converting the entire matter to the civil injunction posture before Xinis in Maryland.

**Gibson v. ICE (Minnesota home entries):** The merits ruling that Garrison Gibson's arrest violated the Fourth Amendment (April 11) has been issued; no new proceedings scheduled this week. But the underlying class action continues and discovery on the May 2025 ICE home-entry directive is now actively contested.

## Part 4: Democratic Renewal Lens

### If SCOTUS Grants the Ballroom Application

A SCOTUS grant — particularly a solo Roberts order, or a full-Court grant without calling for response — would be the most significant expansion of the shadow docket's scope yet recorded. Prior emergency grants have been concentrated in immigration (CASA, warrantless arrests), civil service removal power (MSPB/NLRB), and DOGE data access. These categories all involved contested statutory interpretations of specific federal frameworks. The ballroom case, at its core, is about the Appropriations Clause — the most foundational structural constraint on executive spending authority in the Constitution, operative since the founding.

If SCOTUS grants emergency relief for ballroom construction on a "national security" framing, the practical precedent it establishes is: the president can claim national security to proceed with any construction at an executive facility without congressional appropriation, and courts will defer to that claim on an emergency basis. The "national security" label has already been used to justify immigration enforcement, data access, tariffs, and facility construction. Adding White House construction to that list removes one of the clearest limits — the spending power — from the category of things courts will enforce against the executive in real time.

The longer-run signal for democratic institutions is that the Court has chosen, repeatedly, to make law on the shadow docket in the administration's favor, without the briefing, record development, and deliberation that the Court's own rules contemplate for questions of first impression. The Appropriations Clause question — can a president spend hundreds of millions on White House construction without congressional authorization — has never been decided. If SCOTUS answers it on the shadow docket in 48 hours, the answer is effectively unreviewable: the construction will be largely complete before the merits question is resolved on a full record.

This is not a hypothetical structural concern. The AFSCME v. SSA Fourth Circuit ruling (April 2026) established the operational dynamic: lower courts granted injunctions on factual records that later proved to contain government misrepresentations; the Supreme Court's interim stays treated those records as valid; when the records collapsed, the lower courts were told they could not reassess irreparable harm. The system is not self-correcting in the administration's favor once the shadow docket grant issues.

### If SCOTUS Denies

A denial of an emergency application — particularly if Roberts writes — would be a qualitatively different moment. The shadow docket's asymmetry (administration wins ~80% of the time) has normalized the expectation that executive overreach, when challenged, will eventually be preserved at SCOTUS on emergency review. A denial disrupts that expectation.

More importantly: a denial would force the administration into one of two choices — comply with the Leon injunction (halt construction) or defy it openly. Given the administration's pattern on compliance in immigration cases, defiance is possible. But defying a SCOTUS-affirmed injunction is categorically more dangerous than defying a district court order in a case the administration is still litigating upward. There is no "above the Supreme Court" to appeal to. The only remaining tools would be Congressional action (emergency appropriations for the construction) or a new legal theory (which would take months to develop and litigate).

Is a SCOTUS denial a meaningful signal about court independence? Partly. It would signal that the Appropriations Clause has a floor that even this Court will not let the administration breach on an emergency application. But it would not reverse the structural asymmetry — 80% win rate on other cases — or undo the damage done by prior shadow docket grants. It would be one data point in a pattern that has consistently favored the administration, a single correction in a trend rather than a trend reversal.

The stronger independence signal would require not just a denial, but a denial accompanied by a statement addressing the shadow docket abuse more broadly — the kind of concern Sotomayor voiced at University of Alabama on April 9, now translated into a majority or concurrence. That is unlikely at this stage.

### What the Leon/D.C. Circuit/SCOTUS Interaction Tells Us About Structural Judicial Resilience

The procedural posture of the ballroom case is unusual and instructive. The D.C. Circuit majority — Millett and Garcia, both Obama appointees — found a middle path that neither capitulated to the administration nor simply denied its emergency motion. Instead, they created a factual-development requirement: before we can rule on your emergency claim, you must prove the facts underlying it before the district court. This is the D.C. Circuit operating as a procedural check on the administration's strategy of using national security framings to bypass factual development.

That is structurally significant because it mirrors what a healthy judicial system should do — refuse to resolve contested factual questions in favor of emergency applicants who have not developed the record below. The D.C. Circuit's remand is more institutionally correct behavior than a simple grant or denial would have been. The question is whether SCOTUS, if asked, honors that procedural structure (by declining to grant emergency relief before the record is developed) or overrides it (by treating the national security claim as sufficient on its face).

If SCOTUS overrides the D.C. Circuit's fact-finding structure on an emergency application, it is doing two things simultaneously: (1) removing the D.C. Circuit's factual check, and (2) establishing that sufficiently vivid national security framing can substitute for evidentiary development on the emergency docket. That is the functional equivalent of telling the administration: if you want emergency relief, just keep asserting national security loudly enough.

The three-court interaction pattern — district court builds record, circuit court requires factual development, SCOTUS grants emergency relief before development completes — has been the effective outcome in the SSA/DOGE litigation (district court found government lied; Fourth Circuit deferred to SCOTUS interim order anyway; record collapse did not trigger reassessment). The ballroom case is a cleaner test because the factual question is more discrete: is the above-ground ballroom work separable from below-ground security infrastructure, yes or no? If SCOTUS will not wait four to six weeks for that answer, the structural conclusion is clear: the emergency docket operates independently of factual truth.

## Part 5: Civic Action Implications

### How April 17 Affects the May Day Coalition's Strategic Planning

The May Day coalition as of April 13 evening has materialized as a genuine institutional labor force — NNU (200,000 members), AFT (1.7 million), NEA (3 million), CTU (31,000), UFCW Local 3000 (50,000), plus federal sector unions, AAUP, UE, Starbucks Workers United, and state labor councils. The coalition crossed the threshold from activist-driven protest infrastructure into established institutional labor endorsement.

The coalition's strategic problem is the same one it has had since January: institutional endorsement does not automatically translate into economic disruption. The difference between a very large protest and a general strike is that in a strike, workers do not show up. The 23% Minnesota participation figure (Blue Rose Research) from the January strike is encouraging but does not resolve the fundamental question for May 1: will workers in non-union, at-will employment risk their jobs to participate, knowing that NLRA secondary strike prohibitions and NLRB enforcement mechanisms may not protect them under the current administration?

The April 17 outcome matters to this question in the following way:

**If courts hold — Leon injunction reinstated, construction halts, SCOTUS denies or Roberts requires briefing:** The message for organizers is that judicial resistance is working, that structural legal constraints are being applied even against a politically aggressive executive, and that the institutional system has some self-correcting capacity. This tends to reinforce moderate-wing coalition strategy: use legal avenues, build electoral infrastructure, pressure through organizing rather than confrontation. The May Day ask becomes "make your voice heard through civic action" rather than "stop the collapse of the rule of law."

**If courts fold — SCOTUS grants emergency relief without factual development, construction continues, the Appropriations Clause becomes judicially unenforceable on the shadow docket:** The message for organizers is that legal channels have reached their effective ceiling in this administration's term. Courts can slow but not stop executive overreach when SCOTUS provides emergency cover. The practical implication for May Day is that the economic-disruption component — the general strike element — becomes more strategically urgent, because it is one of the few remaining mechanisms for exerting constraint on an executive that has found ways to manage or override judicial check.

This distinction matters for coalition messaging and for AFL-CIO national calculation. The AFL-CIO has remained silent on formal May Day endorsement partly because it is not clear whether legal channels have failed. If the April 17 outcome signals legal channels have failed — SCOTUS extends shadow docket protection to the Appropriations Clause — that changes the AFL-CIO's cost-benefit analysis on endorsement. The federation's legal exposure under the NLRA (secondary strike prohibitions) becomes relatively less important when the executive branch has effectively captured the emergency docket that would adjudicate those prohibitions.

### If Courts Hold the Line: Message for Organizers

The immediate message is: continued investment in legal organizing infrastructure pays. The warrantless arrest cases (CASA, Ramirez Ovando, Gibson) have won repeated injunctions and are building compliance records. Gibson v. ICE produced a merits Fourth Amendment ruling — not just an injunction — establishing that the home entry was unconstitutional. The IRS data-sharing cases established that the government violated the law 42,695 documented times. The IEEPA tariff ruling was 6-3, written by Roberts. These are real wins, built through sustained legal organizing and litigation.

The organizing framework that follows from courts holding: maintain the legal pressure infrastructure while building the electoral capacity to change the political composition that shapes SCOTUS and the executive branch. No Kings' June 14 mobilization, the electoral pivot town hall (April 6), and the NEA's published May Day toolkit are all consistent with this framework. They are building the civic infrastructure — documentation (Eyes on ICE), mobilization networks, voter registration pipelines — that would convert the legal wins into lasting political change.

The specific May Day message in a "courts hold" scenario: show up, demonstrate scale, make the November 2026 electoral priority visible, and reinforce the political cost to officeholders who have enabled executive overreach. This is a protest-plus-electoral-organizing model, not a general strike model.

### If Courts Fold: What Tools Remain

If the April 17 outcome signals that the emergency docket has absorbed the Appropriations Clause, the strategic inventory of remaining tools is:

**Legal tools that still function:** Individual habeas corpus petitions (still winning at 97% rates in immigration; require individual filings, not class-wide relief). State AG coalition lawsuits (the mail-voting EO challenge, the 23-state coalition in Massachusetts, is the current leading example). Privacy Act and agency-specific confidentiality statutes for data-sharing challenges (the APA route has been partly closed by the Treasury summary judgment ruling). Section 301 and Section 232 tariff challenges on the specific-statutory-requirement issues (still unresolved).

**Political tools:** The 2026 midterm elections remain the most significant lever. No midterm environment in recent history has been triggered by a legal crisis of this scope — courts have not yet been irretrievably captured, and the voting-rights cases (DOJ voter data, Texas redistricting, mail-voting EO) have not yet produced final adverse outcomes. The window for electoral correction still exists, but it narrows if the mail-voting EO is not enjoined before state election systems begin building 2026 procedures around it.

**Economic tools:** The general strike component of May Day is the most visible tool that does not depend on judicial validation. A successful general strike — one that produces measurable economic disruption, documented absences, economic data — generates political leverage independent of court outcomes. It is also the most uncertain tool: no general strike of national scope has occurred in the United States in the current era, and the legal infrastructure protecting strike participants is weaker under the current administration's NLRB posture than it has been at any point since the NLRA was enacted.

**International tools:** Less-tracked but relevant. The European Union and allied governments have been watching the IEEPA tariff litigation and the Constitutional structure cases. The administration's pattern of ignoring court orders has been noted in diplomatic contexts. The May Day general strike, if it achieves meaningful scale, would be closely watched internationally as a signal about domestic resistance capacity.

**The key message for organizers in a "courts fold" scenario:** Do not wait for the next court ruling to generate momentum. The legal channel is not exhausted — individual habeas petitions keep winning, state AG coalitions keep filing, new cases keep being litigated — but the emergency docket has become an executive veto on lower-court injunctions. May Day's function shifts from a democratic check to a statement of capacity: this many workers, this much economic disruption, this is what the political cost of continued overreach looks like before November.
