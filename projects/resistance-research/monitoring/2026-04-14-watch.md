## Scheduled Events

### 1. CBP Status Declaration — Noon EDT
**What it is**: The Court of International Trade (Senior Judge Richard Eaton) ordered CBP to file a declaration by noon Eastern Time describing the current state of Phase 1 IEEPA refund implementation. As of April 1, CBP reported the four technical components at 60–85% completion. Today's declaration is the next progress report.

**What to look for**:
- Is Phase 1 on track for April 20 rollout? CBP has been cooperative with the refund process following the February 20 SCOTUS ruling (*Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*) — the declaration should confirm or modify that timeline.
- Any indication of DOJ resistance to the refund process. The administration has complied instrumentally with the refund order to date; any shift in that posture would be significant.
- Technical specificity: the declaration should address automated systems (unliquidated entries, 90-day voluntary reliquidation window, entries in suspended/extended/review status). A declaration that is vague, incomplete, or requests additional time may prompt Eaton to press harder at the afternoon conference.

**What this is NOT**: This is not a ruling on Section 122 tariff legality. The IEEPA tariffs were already struck down 6-3 by SCOTUS on February 20. Today's proceeding is about refunding money already collected under a now-invalid authority.

**Escalation threshold**: If CBP's declaration reveals non-compliance, delay tactics, or material gaps in the refund system, Eaton may use the afternoon conference to impose a more specific compliance schedule or threaten sanctions. That would warrant a separate monitoring note, but would not change the Section 122 track.

### 2. Judge Eaton Conference — 3 PM EDT (Closed)
**What it is**: The closed session at which Eaton will review the CBP declaration and assess Phase 1 readiness. Likely outcomes range from "on track, no changes" to "timeline modification required."

**What to watch**: Whether any docket entry appears on the CIT docket in the IEEPA refund proceedings after 3 PM. A closed conference rarely produces a public order the same day unless Eaton is dissatisfied with the declaration. If nothing appears on the docket today, the most likely inference is that Phase 1 is proceeding as expected toward the April 20 rollout.

## Probabilistic Events (No Set Time — Monitor All Day)

### 3. Leon Remand Response — White House Ballroom
**Status entering April 14**: No clarification order issued as of April 13 evening. The D.C. Circuit's April 11 ruling (Millett and Garcia, majority; Rao dissenting) remanded to Leon with a specific question: does his injunction reach the underground security infrastructure (bomb shelters, military hospital, classified installations) or only the above-ground ballroom work?

**Why this is the highest-priority item of the week**: Leon's response determines the shape of the SCOTUS application if one is filed. A narrow order (injunction covers above-ground only) deflates the administration's national security framing. A broad order (covers both layers) maximizes the administration's SCOTUS hook and makes a Roberts emergency grant more likely. Silence — Leon does not respond before April 17 — reinstates the original injunction.

**What to monitor**: Any docket entry in *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA* (D.D.C.). PACER is the primary source; news outlets will pick it up within 1–2 hours of a significant order dropping.

**If Leon issues a narrow order (injunction covers above-ground only)**:
- The administration's national security argument is functionally neutralized for SCOTUS purposes
- A SCOTUS application becomes much harder to justify — Roberts would be asked to protect a ballroom construction project, not a classified security installation
- Below-ground construction continues; the factual dispute the D.C. Circuit was worried about disappears
- Above-ground construction halts; the National Trust's core objective is achieved pending merits litigation
- The probability that SCOTUS grants emergency relief drops to roughly 20–25%
- Roberts granting a stay under these circumstances would be the most politically exposed shadow-docket move of his tenure; it would require him to accept that a president can demolish a federally protected historic site by executive fiat with no appropriation

**If Leon issues a broad order (injunction covers both layers)**:
- SCOTUS application is near-certain within 24–48 hours
- The administration's security framing is at maximum strength — "halting construction is halting classified infrastructure"
- Roberts faces genuine pressure to act, likely via a solo administrative stay pending response from National Trust
- The National Trust will immediately counter-file arguing the above-ground and below-ground phases are separable (their April 13 filing explicitly establishes this factual predicate)
- Roberts solo administrative stay: approximately 60% probability within 24–48 hours of application

**If Leon issues no order before end of business April 14**:
- The window is compressing; Leon has approximately 72 hours remaining
- The administration must decide whether to file at SCOTUS cold (without the factual clarification the D.C. Circuit requested) or wait for Leon and risk running out of time
- Each day of Leon silence slightly increases the probability of a cold SCOTUS filing
- End-of-day April 14 with no Leon order is a meaningful signal: at that point, the administration either files at SCOTUS on April 15–16 or risks the stay expiring

### 4. SCOTUS Emergency Application — White House Ballroom
**Status entering April 14**: No application on the public docket as of April 13 evening. The administration has given no public indication of timing.

**What to monitor**: The SCOTUSblog emergency docket and the Brennan Center shadow docket tracker. Any entry in *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA* on those trackers is a code-red event. Applications go to Roberts as Circuit Justice for the D.C. Circuit; a solo Roberts administrative stay could issue within hours of an application dropping.

**Application timing logic**:
- Administration prefers to file after Leon narrows (gives them a clean posture at SCOTUS — "the Court of Appeals remanded, the district court complied, and we still need relief for the above-ground work")
- Filing before Leon acts is strategically awkward — the D.C. Circuit explicitly flagged the missing factual record, and SCOTUS would be receiving an application that the court below said was premature
- But if April 15 arrives with no Leon order, the administration faces a forced choice: file cold or accept expiration

**National Trust counter-strategy**: Their April 13 separability argument is designed to preempt the broadest SCOTUS framing regardless of when the application arrives. They have established on the record that above-ground and below-ground phases are operationally separable — meaning even if SCOTUS grants emergency relief for security construction, the above-ground ballroom halt should survive.

### 5. Section 122 Tariff Ruling — CIT Three-Judge Panel
**Status entering April 14**: No ruling from the panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) following April 10 oral argument. The panel is under no deadline.

**Why it could drop April 14**: The April 10 argument was sharply adversarial and apparently decisive — the panel did not appear to need additional briefing, and at least two judges signaled during argument that the administration's position "proves too much." Panels that are confident in their direction can issue quickly. April 14 is the first full business day after the weekend following argument.

**The legal question**: Whether Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 permits the president to impose a 10% global import surcharge based on a trade-deficit finding. Section 122 was written for the Bretton Woods era; it contemplated a fixed-exchange-rate balance-of-payments crisis mechanism. The challengers argue the statutory conditions have not been met and that the president's invocation is judicially reviewable. The administration argues presidential determinations under Section 122 are unreviewable and that trade deficits satisfy the trigger.

**If panel rules against the administration**:
- The administration's backup tariff authority — which it invoked specifically because IEEPA was struck down on February 20 — is invalidated
- Emergency stay application to the Federal Circuit is the immediate next step
- The economic stakes are large (10% global surcharge currently in effect) — a Federal Circuit denial could compel SCOTUS to take the case on emergency posture before the term ends
- This is a larger economic event than the IEEPA ruling in some respects, because it eliminates the administration's remaining major statutory tariff authority

**If panel rules for the administration**:
- Challengers appeal to the Federal Circuit
- Less likely given oral argument posture, but an upholding opinion would need to address the "proves too much" problem squarely
- The SCOTUSblog analysis (March 2026) noted the Court may grant cert regardless of Federal Circuit outcome given the economic stakes

**If no ruling today**: No change to existing tracking; monitor daily.

**Escalation threshold**: Any CIT docket entry in the consolidated Section 122 cases — check PACER under the *PrimeSource Building Products* or related consolidated captions.

### 6. Nashville — Crenshaw/Blanche (Abrego Garcia Criminal Track)
**Status entering April 14**: No ruling from Judge Crenshaw (M.D. Tenn.) on dismissal. The record closed weeks ago. Dismissal has been described as "imminent" since CNN's April 11 reporting. The extended silence is the main data point.

**The Blanche problem — why silence matters**: Crenshaw's prior written opinions have already established that Blanche's June 2025 public statements directly linked the Maryland civil case to the Nashville criminal investigation of Abrego Garcia, and that this linkage "suggests the administration's frustration with Abrego" — language indicating the prosecution was not motivated by ordinary law-enforcement criteria. The government put two local witnesses on the stand to rebut the vindictive-prosecution presumption; Blanche himself never appeared to explain his own statements. That testimonial gap is Crenshaw's primary unresolved factual issue.

**Two possible reasons for continuing silence**:
1. Crenshaw is drafting the dismissal order — complex opinions take time, especially when the judge wants a clean record that is resistant to the government's likely appeal
2. Crenshaw is weighing whether to subpoena Blanche before ruling — compelling the acting Attorney General to testify under oath about his own public statements would be a qualitative escalation; the fact that Crenshaw has not simply dismissed but is also not subpoenaing quickly suggests he may be moving toward this option

**Escalation threshold**: Any docket entry in the Middle District of Tennessee case is significant. A dismissal order is the expected outcome. A subpoena order for Blanche would require immediate monitoring and a separate note; it would be the most significant act of judicial resistance to the administration in the Abrego Garcia constellation.

**Downstream significance if dismissed**: The government's criminal leverage over Abrego Garcia disappears. The Maryland case (Xinis, hearing April 28) becomes the only live legal track. The "he's a criminal" political narrative loses its most legally credible support.

## Background Thread Statuses (as of April 13 EOD)

### Humphrey's Executor / Roberts Solo Stay (MSPB and NLRB Removal Power)
Roberts on April 9 unilaterally stayed the D.C. Circuit's en banc order reinstating MSPB chair Cathy Harris and NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox. The MSPB is now without a quorum; tens of thousands of federal worker appeals are frozen. Oral argument in *Trump v. Slaughter* is expected June 2026. The underlying question — whether *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935) survives as precedent allowing independent agency commissioners to be protected from at-will removal — has been building since the Selia Law and Collins cases of 2020-21. If the Court overrules *Humphrey's Executor* in whole or in part, the FTC, NLRB, MSPB, EEOC, SEC, FCC, and CFPB all lose structural independence guarantees. No April 14 action expected on this track, but it shapes the Roberts-at-SCOTUS analysis for the ballroom case: Roberts has already demonstrated he will use unilateral shadow docket power in executive-branch removal disputes.

**Monitoring posture**: Background. Watch for any new emergency filings or docket activity in the Harris or Wilcox reinstatement cases at the D.C. Circuit or SCOTUS.
