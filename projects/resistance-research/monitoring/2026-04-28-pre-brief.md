# April 28 Pre-Hearing Brief

*Prepared 2026-04-26. Two days before hearing. Companion to 2026-04-28-watch-brief.md.*
*Results to be filed same day as 2026-04-28-results.md.*

---

## Who

**Judge**: Paula Xinis, U.S. District Court for the District of Maryland.

**Subject**: Kilmar Abrego Garcia — Salvadoran national, Maryland resident, wrongly deported by ICE to El Salvador's CECOT prison in March 2025. The Trump administration admitted in court filings the deportation was an error. SCOTUS unanimously ordered the administration to "facilitate" his return in April 2026. He is currently free in the U.S. under a Xinis protective order barring ICE re-detention.

**Parties**: Abrego Garcia (represented by Simon Sandoval-Moshenberg); DOJ/DHS/ICE for the Trump administration.

---

## What Is Before the Court April 28

Four overlapping questions, all live simultaneously:

1. **Deposition compliance** — Xinis ordered four administration officials to sit for sworn depositions by April 23. Both parties filed sealed motions April 23 and Xinis paused all discovery until April 30, 5:00 p.m. Deposition completion status is not public. April 28 is the first public window into what occurred under seal.

2. **Contempt** — Xinis has publicly flagged she is "weighing contempt proceedings" after finding the administration acted in "bad faith" and "willfully" defied discovery orders. Civil contempt (daily fines, potential imprisonment of officials) is the coercive mechanism on the table.

3. **Return obligation** — SCOTUS's unanimous April 10 order requires the administration to "facilitate" Abrego Garcia's return from El Salvador. The administration has instead maintained a demand to deport him to Liberia, which Xinis has called a "fantasy" given the simultaneous criminal prosecution in Nashville requiring his U.S. presence.

4. **Liberia demand** — DOJ's April 20 brief maintained Liberia as the preferred removal destination. Costa Rica has a signed U.S. removal agreement. No legally coherent justification for the Liberia preference has been offered.

---

## Significance for the Broader Resistance/Immigration Movement

This case is the clearest live test of whether federal courts can compel executive compliance with their own orders. The administration has a documented pattern across multiple cases — Boasberg, Xinis, and others — of discovering procedural arguments, sealed maneuvers, and jurisdictional challenges rather than complying. What Xinis does April 28 will set the template.

If she issues civil contempt sanctions with real teeth (accumulating daily fines, threat of official imprisonment), she establishes a mechanism that other courts in other cases can replicate. If the administration successfully delays, appeals, or seals its way past the April 28 hearing, the pattern of "win in court, lose in fact" continues — which is the single most damaging dynamic for institutional resistance to the administration's immigration enforcement agenda.

The immigration movement is watching this case specifically because Abrego Garcia's situation is not unique — it is a documented instance of what advocates say is a pattern of wrongful deportations. A contempt mechanism that works here is a usable tool in future cases.

## Expected Range of Outcomes

**Most consequential**: Civil contempt order with sanctions — tests whether courts can actually coerce the executive branch.

**Most likely near-term**: Status conference with the April 30 discovery deadline taking precedence — hearing resolves little but the clock gets tighter.

**Wildcard that reshapes everything**: Crenshaw rules in Nashville before or concurrent with the April 28 hearing, dismissing the criminal prosecution on vindictive-prosecution grounds — eliminates DOJ's "criminal presence required" argument entirely.

## New Developments Since April 24 Watch Brief

**Senate $70B ICE reconciliation vote (April 23)**: The Senate passed a budget resolution 50-48 along party lines, clearing the path for $70 billion in ICE and Border Patrol funding via reconciliation — bypassing the 60-vote filibuster threshold. This ends the 10-week DHS partial shutdown through the reconciliation vehicle. The Homeland Security committees must produce legislation by May 15; Trump has set a June 1 deadline. Context: the DHS shutdown was triggered after Democrats demanded warrant requirements for ICE home entry following two U.S. citizens killed by immigration agents in Minneapolis. The reconciliation path drops those Democratic preconditions entirely. This is a material escalation of the legal environment around ICE enforcement capacity — directly relevant to both the Xinis hearing and May Day organizing context.

**DHS payroll cliff — first week of May (April 22-26)**: DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin confirmed that emergency funds used to pay DHS employees during the shutdown will run out after the first payroll in May (approximately the week of May 4-8). As of April 19, under $1.4 billion remained in the emergency fund against a $1.6-1.7 billion biweekly payroll. Law enforcement-designated personnel (ICE, CBP, Coast Guard) have been paid throughout the shutdown. The payroll cliff primarily affects non-enforcement civilian staff, but creates severe political pressure in the week immediately following May Day.

**ICE tracker apps — First Amendment injunction (April 18-23)**: U.S. District Judge Jorge L. Alonso (N.D. Illinois) granted a preliminary injunction finding the Trump administration likely violated the First Amendment by pressuring Apple and Facebook to remove ICE-monitoring apps (Eyes Up and ICE Sightings - Chicagoland). The apps use publicly available information to document ICE operations. The injunction restores access to these tools pending the full case. Directly relevant to May Day participant safety infrastructure.

**ProPublica / Frontline "Caught in the Crackdown" (April 14-21)**: Investigation found that in more than one third of 300+ protest arrests at anti-ICE demonstrations across 2025-2026, charges were dropped, refused by prosecutors, or lost at trial — including eight jury acquittals. Federal U.S. attorneys normally secure convictions in over 90% of cases they bring. The pattern is consistent across cities: officer statements contradicted by video evidence. Published April 14; PBS documentary aired April 14. This is the most significant documented assessment of legal risk to May Day protesters and should be read alongside the May Day action guide's legal section.

**Nashville Crenshaw ruling — still pending (April 26)**: Judge Waverly Crenshaw's ruling on vindictive prosecution has not been issued as of April 26. Acting AG Todd Blanche's public statements on the Abrego Garcia prosecution have, according to multiple sources, frustrated prosecutors' ability to proceed, and a ruling is described as "at any time." Dismissal of charges would be the wildcard that reshapes both the Nashville and Maryland cases entirely.

**Erez Reuveni whistleblower complaint being cited (April 2026)**: The former Acting Deputy Director for DOJ's Office of Immigration Litigation, who was removed for refusing to mislead the court, has a 27-page whistleblower complaint documenting DOJ's deliberate decisions to resist court orders during deportation proceedings. Abrego Garcia's attorneys are now citing this complaint in the Maryland civil case to argue the case should not be mooted. This is internal DOJ documentation of deliberate non-compliance — potentially usable as contempt evidence.
