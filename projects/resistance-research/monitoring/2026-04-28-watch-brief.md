## 1. Abrego Garcia / Xinis — April 28 Hearing

### Updated Pre-Hearing State (April 24 baseline)

#### The Sealed Stay — Critical New Development

On April 23, 2026, in a development not publicly explained:
- Both parties filed motions under seal.
- Judge Xinis issued a stay of all discovery — including the four-official deposition deadline — until **April 30, 2026, at 5:00 p.m.**
- The stay was issued "with the agreement of the parties."
- The reason for the stay is sealed. Neither side's attorneys have spoken publicly about current case status.
- Abrego Garcia's attorney Simon Sandoval-Moshenberg stated his team is "not at liberty to speak about the current status of the case at this time," citing the court seal.

This development is analytically significant on multiple levels:

1. **Deposition completion status is unknown.** The deadline passed as part of the sealed proceedings. Whether the four officials sat for depositions, refused to sit, or negotiated a modification to the deposition order is not in the public record.

2. **Sealed motions from both parties suggest negotiation.** When both parties file under seal simultaneously, the standard inference is that some form of negotiation or proposed resolution is being presented to the court. A unilateral defiance would typically not produce a joint filing.

3. **The April 28 hearing proceeds against this backdrop.** Xinis has not canceled or rescheduled the April 28 hearing. Whether the hearing will remain under full public access, partial seal, or produce public orders is unknown in advance.

4. **The April 30 deadline creates a hard second marker.** Whatever the April 23 sealed motions proposed, the stay expires at 5:00 p.m. on April 30. If the hearing produces no resolution on April 28, April 30 becomes the next hard deadline.

#### Pre-Sealed Substantive Posture

### April 28 Hearing — What to Watch

**1. Seal status at hearing open**
The first indicator is whether the hearing proceeds in open court or under seal. If Xinis opens in public session, the April 23 sealed matter has resolved enough to permit public proceedings. If the hearing is sealed or partially sealed, the negotiation is ongoing.

**2. Deposition disclosure**
If proceedings are public, the first substantive question is the deposition outcome. Did the four officials sit? Did they assert privilege broadly? Did the administration seek a modification? Xinis's tone and subsequent orders will track directly from this answer.

**3. April 30 deadline intersection**
With the stay expiring April 30, Xinis faces a structural choice: resolve the contempt/deposition question at the April 28 hearing, or treat the hearing as a status conference pending the April 30 deadline. The latter would compress the timeline to two days.

**4. Civil contempt mechanism — three possible outputs**
If Xinis is moving toward civil contempt, April 28 may produce:
- A **show cause order** with a short deadline (days, not weeks)
- A direct **civil contempt finding** with sanctions (daily fines, potential imprisonment of officials)
- An order directing Abrego Garcia's attorneys to **formally file a contempt motion**, initiating full briefing

Civil contempt sanctions in an injunction enforcement context — fines accumulating until compliance, potential imprisonment — are the coercive mechanism courts use when injunctions are defied. These are procedurally distinct from criminal contempt and are not foreclosed by the Boasberg ruling.

**5. Liberia position**
Any retreat from the Liberia demand is significant. Costa Rica has signed a removal agreement. The Liberia insistence, without a legal justification that survives Xinis's "fantasy" analysis, is now the clearest obstruction-of-return argument on the record. A Liberia retreat would allow Xinis to reframe the proceeding as compliance-monitoring rather than enforcement.

**6. Fourth Circuit emergency filing — post-hearing watch**
If Xinis issues any order at April 28, the administration's most aggressive response is an immediate Fourth Circuit emergency appeal. This would temporarily strip Xinis of jurisdiction and require the circuit to weigh in on whether Boasberg forecloses civil contempt in the immigration context. Watch for any Fourth Circuit docket entry in the 2-6 hours following the close of the April 28 hearing.

**7. Nashville / Crenshaw connection**
Any Crenshaw ruling before or on April 28 directly alters the hearing. A dismissal of the Nashville prosecution removes the "criminal presence required" argument from DOJ's Xinis posture. A denial strengthens it. Neither has been confirmed. Ten weeks of silence continues.

### Why April 28 Matters for Nashville

1. If Crenshaw dismisses the Nashville prosecution before the April 28 hearing, it removes DOJ's strongest justification for keeping Abrego Garcia in the U.S. while simultaneously resisting return. The dismissal would close the criminal-leverage circuit.

2. If Crenshaw dismisses after the April 28 hearing, DOJ will have already deployed the "Nashville requires presence" argument at Xinis. A post-hearing dismissal would undercut DOJ's record but not reverse the hearing's outcome.

3. If Crenshaw rules against dismissal, DOJ gains a Fourth Circuit-ready argument: the Nashville prosecution is legitimate and requires Abrego Garcia's presence, making return impractical regardless of Xinis's orders.

### Watch: Any Nashville PACER Entry

The only confirmed-public trigger is a PACER docket entry in United States v. Abrego Garcia, 3:25-cr-00115 (M.D. Tenn.). No prior warning is expected — Crenshaw can issue an order without scheduling a hearing.

## 4. Section 122 / CIT — Background Watch

### Status as of April 24

The Barnett/Kelly/Stanceu panel at the Court of International Trade continues to deliberate. As of April 28, they will be at **18 days post-oral argument** (April 10). No ruling, scheduling order, or supplemental briefing request has been issued.

**The stakes**: 24 states challenge the 10% global import surcharge as exceeding Section 122's statutory authority (requires a "balance of payments" problem as the legal trigger). A preliminary injunction would immediately halt the surcharge, which would be appealed to the Federal Circuit and potentially SCOTUS.

**The clock**: Section 122 tariffs expire by statute on **July 24, 2026** (150-day limit from imposition). If CIT does not rule before July 24, the case becomes moot without a ruling. Oregon AG Rayfield expressed hope for a ruling on the IEEPA timeline (approximately two weeks post-argument). That two-week window passed without a ruling.

**IEEPA context**: SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariffs in Learning Resources v. Trump. The administration pivoted to Section 122 as its primary tariff vehicle. If CIT also finds Section 122 unlawful, the administration's broad tariff authority is effectively exhausted on legal grounds.

**Watch for**: Any CIT order (docketed at Court of International Trade, New York). A preliminary injunction is the highest-priority vehicle. A ruling in either direction is immediately material to the resistance-research economic-disruption thread.

## 5. CAPE Phase 1 / IEEPA Tariff Refunds — Background

### What CAPE Phase 1 Is

On April 20, 2026, CBP launched Phase 1 of the Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries (CAPE) tool in the ACE Portal — the first federal mechanism for processing refunds of duties paid under IEEPA tariffs, which SCOTUS struck down. An estimated $166 billion in tariff refunds is at stake.

**Eligibility** (Phase 1 only): Unliquidated entries; entries within 80 days of liquidation; warehouse entries; ADD/CVD entries under suspension.

### Phase 1 Launch Problems

Multiple businesses reported technical difficulties immediately:
- "Duplicate tax ID" error messages linking importer accounts to other parties' accounts.
- Four-hour-plus hold times with CBP customer service.
- Zero-tolerance error policy: a single error in a CSV submission returns the entire submission for correction.
- Small businesses without customs brokers are at a systematic disadvantage relative to large importers with dedicated trade compliance staff.

Fortune reported (April 20) that $166 billion in refunds "just became available, but small businesses may already be at a disadvantage." CBS News confirmed CBP is "looking into reports of problems."

### Resistance-Research Significance

CAPE Phase 1's rollout problems reinforce a consistent pattern: post-SCOTUS remediation mechanisms are structurally better-positioned for large institutional actors than for the small businesses and workers who bore the tariff burden. This is relevant to the economic-disruption framing in May Day organizing — the cost of tariffs fell disproportionately on workers and small businesses; the refund mechanism is similarly regressive in access terms.

## 6. Trump v. Slaughter / Humphrey's Executor — Background

### Status

Argued at SCOTUS December 8, 2025. Decision expected by June 2026 (end of term). SCOTUS stayed the district court decision allowing Trump to remove FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter during pendency.

**Question presented**: Whether statutory removal protections for FTC commissioners violate the separation of powers, and whether Humphrey's Executor (1935) should be overruled.

**Post-argument consensus**: Post-argument reporting characterized the government as in a "strong position." Multiple justices questioned whether Humphrey's Executor remains workable given the modern FTC's powers. Scholars anticipate SCOTUS will narrow or overturn Humphrey's Executor, though the degree of carve-back is uncertain.

**Resistance-research significance**: A ruling overturning or narrowing Humphrey's Executor would eliminate independence protections for commissioners at the FTC, NLRB, CFPB, FEC, and other multi-member independent agencies. For the resistance project, NLRB independence is the most operationally relevant — NLRB's capacity to enforce labor protections for May Day participants depends on the independence of its board members from presidential removal. A Trump v. Slaughter ruling that allows at-will removal of NLRB members would structurally undermine the labor-law protections underlying May Day organizing.

## 11. Analytical Note: The Sealed Moment

The April 23 sealed filings change the character of the April 28 hearing from a fully anticipated contempt showdown to a partially unknown proceeding. Several interpretations deserve equal weight:

**Theory A — Negotiated compliance**. The sealed filings represent a government offer to comply with some portion of the discovery order, which both parties agreed to present to Xinis under seal. The April 30 deadline would be the moment when compliance is confirmed or denied. If this theory is correct, April 28 may be a status conference rather than a contempt hearing.

**Theory B — Sealed refusal with agreed pause**. The government formally refused the depositions through a sealed mechanism, and both parties agreed to pause proceedings while the administration prepared a Fourth Circuit emergency motion. The April 30 deadline would be the moment the government reveals its circuit-level challenge.

**Theory C — Partial compliance with privilege assertion**. Officials sat for depositions but asserted privilege extensively. The sealed filing documents the privilege log, which both parties agreed to litigate under seal. April 28 would then address the adequacy of the privilege assertions, not outright defiance.

Theory A is the most consistent with both parties agreeing to the pause — a unilateral defiance typically produces a contested, not joint, motion. But all three theories are live, and the sealed record prevents confident assessment.

The April 28 hearing is the first public window into what the sealed record contains.
