## White House Ballroom — Pre-SCOTUS Window, No New Filings

**Status since morning pass**: No new Leon ruling. No SCOTUS emergency application on the public docket as of this afternoon. The situation is unchanged from the morning: the D.C. Circuit's April 17 stay deadline is the controlling clock, and the administration's SCOTUS application is anticipated but not yet filed.

**New material in circulation**: An NBC News report published April 9 (first indexed widely this afternoon) details the administration's specific national security arguments, which go beyond the vague "security infrastructure" framing in earlier filings. The April 9 brief specifies: missile-resistant steel columns and beams, drone-proof roofing materials, bullet/ballistic/blast-proof glass, and — most significantly — "bomb shelters, hospital and medical area, protective partitioning, and Top Secret Military installations." This is the argument the administration will present to SCOTUS if and when an emergency application is filed. It is designed to create maximum ideological cover for conservative justices to grant an emergency stay: declining to do so would be framed as the Court exposing the president to physical danger.

**The counterargument remains the same**: National Trust's position — that the "absence of a massive ballroom on White House grounds has not stopped the President from residing at the White House" — does not engage directly with the underground security claim, which is where the constitutional battle is now being fought. The D.C. Circuit's April 11 remand was precisely about this: Leon needs to put on record whether his injunction reaches or affects the underground work. If Leon issues a narrow clarification explicitly excluding underground security work from his injunction's scope, the administration's SCOTUS hook weakens significantly — the Court could not credibly say halting above-ground ballroom construction creates a national security emergency if the underground work is expressly permitted.

**What to watch through April 17**: (1) Leon issues a clarification order — watch for narrowing or broadening language. (2) DOJ files SCOTUS emergency application before or at the deadline. If filed, it goes to Chief Justice Roberts (D.C. Circuit emergency applications) or Justice Kavanaugh as designated backup. (3) SCOTUS grants or denies. A denial is the most politically consequential outcome of this administration's judicial history: the government would face contempt proceedings if it continued construction in defiance of Leon's reinstated injunction.

The CNN April 11 reporting remains the controlling characterization: dismissal is "imminent" and the judge is "poised" to act. No new hearing was scheduled or reported. No new government filings were identified.

**What is unchanged**: The evidentiary record is closed. Abrego Garcia's attorneys told Crenshaw that the two government witnesses were "unable or unwilling" to fill in critical details that could sustain the prosecution, and that Blanche was not present to explain his own statements. The government has had its opportunity to rebut the vindictive prosecution presumption that Crenshaw established in his October ruling. The record is now before the judge for decision.

**Two paths still open**: (1) Crenshaw dismisses on the current record — most likely outcome given the procedural posture. (2) Crenshaw subpoenas Blanche to testify under oath — this would be an extraordinary step: a sitting Attorney General compelled to testify before a district judge about his own public statements. Either outcome is adverse to the administration; the subpoena path is more dramatic and would generate a significant constitutional confrontation over executive privilege.

**Significance of continued silence**: Every day without a ruling is a day the administration retains its strongest argument in the Maryland civil case for any future action against Abrego Garcia. Crenshaw's deliberateness may reflect the seriousness of the options before him.

**No new developments on April 17 Maryland deadline**: The administration's demand that Judge Xinis dissolve her Maryland injunction by April 17 is unchanged. Xinis has already rejected that deadline. This will generate a separate filing or confrontation on or around April 17 simultaneous with the ballroom deadline — two administration defiance scenarios potentially breaking on the same day.

## PRIORITY: May Day Coalition — Materially Expanded Since Morning Pass

The morning pass identified the No Kings / Indivisible coalition and the Chicago Teachers Union fight. Afternoon research confirms the coalition is substantially larger and more institutionally grounded than the morning data suggested.

**Confirmed union endorsements as of April 13 afternoon:**

- **Chicago Teachers Union** (30,000–31,000 members): House of Delegates has formally endorsed the May Day call. Mayor Brandon Johnson publicly supports CTU participation. CPS interim CEO's opposition now faces pressure from both the union and the mayor — the board could still override her.
- **UFCW Local 3000** (50,000 members, Washington state): Tabling at workplaces, predicting the largest May Day participation in Washington state history.
- **American Federation of Teachers (AFT)**: National endorsement confirmed.
- **National Education Association (NEA)**: Published a May Day 2026 toolkit at [nea.org/mayday-toolkit](https://www.nea.org/mayday-toolkit) — operational infrastructure for member participation.
- **AAUP** (college faculty): Endorsement confirmed.
- **United Electrical Workers (UE)**: Confirmed, with higher education locals mobilizing.
- **Starbucks Workers United**: Confirmed.
- **North Carolina AFL-CIO**: Confirmed.
- **Milwaukee Labor Council**: Confirmed.
- **Federal sector unions**: Participating in May Day events for the first time in significant numbers, per Payday Report.

**National AFL-CIO**: Has not issued a formal national endorsement. This remains the key institutional holdout. Many affiliated unions are participating individually. The AFL-CIO's legal exposure under the NLRA's prohibition on "sympathy strikes" is the likely reason for official caution at the federation level, even as locals and affiliated internationals sign on independently.

**Payday Report's organizing intelligence**: The May Day General Strike Movement Continues to Grow report (most recent) confirms 300 solidarity actions planned nationally. At least 23% of Minnesota voters said they or a loved one participated in the January Minnesota general strike, per a Blue Rose Research survey of 1,900 registered voters — a significant data point for organizers arguing mass strikes can achieve scale.

**The organizational infrastructure question**: The NEA's publication of a formal "toolkit" is a significant step beyond endorsement. It means the union's staff and legal infrastructure is being mobilized to help members navigate participation — including, presumably, guidance on protected vs. unprotected activity under the NLRA. This kind of institutional scaffolding is what separates the January Minnesota strike (which had it) from a protest with higher attendance but less worker participation.

**CPS board override scenario**: If the Chicago Board of Education votes to give CPS students and teachers May 1 as a civic day of action, it would be the first school system in the country to officially endorse the general strike as a civic matter. That would be replicated across other jurisdictions. Watch for board action in the next week.

**Strategic assessment**: The coalition has now crossed the threshold from activist-driven protest infrastructure into established institutional labor. 200,000 NNU nurses, 1.7 million AFT members, and 3 million NEA members represent a qualitatively different scale than No Kings rally attendance. Whether that institutional weight translates to actual economic disruption on May 1 — which requires workers to not show up, not just sign a statement — is the critical unknown.

## MSPB/NLRB — No New Reactions Located; Structural Analysis Updated

**Status since morning pass**: No new congressional statements or organized labor responses to Chief Justice Roberts' April 9 solo stay were located in afternoon searching. This absence is itself a data point — the institutional labor response has been muted at the federation level, consistent with the AFL-CIO's caution on the shadow docket litigation.

**What the silence means**: The MSPB/NLRB removal question is now legally parked pending Trump v. Slaughter (decision expected June 2026). Labor organizations face a difficult strategic situation: filing contempt motions or generating congressional hearings over Roberts' solo stay would antagonize a Court that will rule on the underlying removal-power question. The quieter strategy is to build the record (federal workers filing MSPB appeals that cannot be decided without a quorum) and wait for a June ruling that — if adverse — will require a different kind of response.

**The MSPB backlog is growing**: With the board unable to issue decisions, every federal worker who was fired in the mass probationary terminations and filed an MSPB appeal is in indefinite legal limbo. When (and if) a quorum is restored — either through SCOTUS ruling in Harris/Wilcox's favor, or through Trump nominating and the Senate confirming new members — the board will face a massive backlog. This creates both an accountability problem (delayed justice for fired workers) and a strategic problem for the administration (a future Democratic administration would inherit a massive queue of winnable cases).

**AFGE/BOP preliminary injunction hearing (April 30)**: This is the next significant federal worker litigation event on the calendar. This is the hearing on AFGE's motion challenging the Schedule F executive order's application to Bureau of Prisons workers. The court continued oral argument to April 30 after allowing additional briefing. A ruling for AFGE here would extend the collective bargaining protection framework for federal workers during the Schedule F rollout.

**VA collective bargaining ruling (March 13)**: Judge Melissa DuBose's preliminary injunction requiring the VA to restore its Master CBA with AFGE/NVAC (representing over 300,000 VA employees) is a significant win that was underreported in prior passes. The CBA restoration means the administration cannot unilaterally strip collective bargaining rights from VA workers while the litigation proceeds.

## Additional Labor Context: NYSNA Northwell and Kaiser AI Strike

Two additional labor actions not previously in the research record:

**NYSNA Northwell (Long Island, settled February 2026)**: Over 1,000 Northwell nurses at three Long Island hospitals struck in January 2026 alongside 15,000 NYC nurses in the largest nursing strike in New York City history. The 41-day strike ended February 21 with ratified 3-year contracts securing enforceable nurse-to-patient staffing ratios at Northwell/Huntington for the first time ever. This is strategically significant for May Day context: 15,000 nurses proved they could sustain a 41-day strike and win concrete contract gains. NNU's endorsement of May Day draws on this demonstrated capacity.

**Kaiser Permanente mental health workers (California)**: Kaiser mental health therapists struck over the administration's plan to implement AI in patient triage evaluation. NUHW president Sophia Mendoza: "Kaiser's overhauling of its mental health triage system shows that it is moving away from human-centered care." This strike is notable as an early example of healthcare AI deployment prompting direct labor action — a category of dispute likely to expand.
