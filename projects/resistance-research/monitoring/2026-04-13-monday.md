## 1. White House Ballroom — Leon Remand Window Open, No SCOTUS Filing

**Current posture**: No change from Sunday evening. Judge Leon has not issued his remand response. The administration has not filed an emergency application at the Supreme Court. Construction continues under the D.C. Circuit's stay, which expires April 17 at midnight.

**What happened over the weekend**: The April 11 D.C. Circuit ruling (Millett and Garcia, majority; Rao dissenting) did two things: extended the stay through April 17 and remanded to Leon to clarify whether his injunction reaches the underground infrastructure (bomb shelters, military hospital, classified installations). The National Trust responded on April 13 with a separability argument — above-ground ballroom work and below-ground security installations are "operationally and contractually separable," meaning Leon's injunction can halt the former without touching the latter.

**What Leon must do**: Answer the D.C. Circuit's question. His options:

- Narrow: Injunction covers above-ground only. National security framing deflates. SCOTUS application, if filed, loses its strongest hook. Roberts would face granting emergency relief to protect a ballroom without a security emergency.
- Expand: Injunction covers above and below ground. Administration has maximum justification for SCOTUS application. Roberts faces pressure to act solo and fast given classified underground construction language.
- No response before April 17: Stay expires, original injunction reinstates. Administration must halt or be in contempt.

**SCOTUS mechanics**: Application would go to Roberts as Circuit Justice for the D.C. Circuit. Roberts could grant an administrative stay (lowest commitment, buys another week of briefing), refer to the full Court, or deny. A solo Roberts grant without response is considered unlikely given his documented skepticism of shadow-docket process abuse. Most likely path if application filed: Roberts calls for response from National Trust, grants short administrative stay in the interim.

**What to watch for**: Any Leon docket entry. Any Supreme Court docket entry showing an emergency application in *National Trust for Historic Preservation v. GSA*. Construction crews on April 18 (day after stay expires) will be the most visible real-world indicator if none of the above occurs.

## 3. Section 122 Tariff Challenge — Panel Silent After April 10 Argument

**Current posture**: No ruling from the CIT three-judge panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) following April 10 oral argument. Panel is under no specific deadline to act.

**What the April 10 argument showed**: Panel posture was sharply skeptical of the administration's position. Judge Stanceu pressed hardest. At least two judges signaled the administration's interpretation "proves too much" — that a reading permitting Section 122 invocation based on trade deficits alone would allow a president to invoke it virtually anytime, eliminating any meaningful statutory constraint. The administration's position rested on three claims: (1) trade deficits alone satisfy the "balance of payments" trigger; (2) presidential determinations under Section 122 are unreviewable; (3) the major questions doctrine does not apply.

**Challengers' strongest argument**: Section 122 was written for a fixed-exchange-rate world that ended in 1973 (the collapse of Bretton Woods). The statutory conditions — a "large and serious" balance-of-payments deficit — require an actual finding, not a mere assertion, and the conditions are judicially reviewable. The administration's claimed unreviewability would effectively give the president an unlimited tariff power.

**Context**: This is the administration's backup authority after SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariffs 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (February 20, 2026). Chief Justice Roberts wrote that IEEPA's text "cannot bear such weight." If the CIT invalidates Section 122 tariffs on similar reasoning and the Federal Circuit and SCOTUS affirm, the administration has exhausted its main statutory tariff authorities. The economic stakes are enormous — this is a 10% global import surcharge currently in effect.

**Separate track — IEEPA refund process**: The April 14 conference before Senior Judge Eaton is about the administrative implementation of refunds for IEEPA duties already collected — CBP is required to file a status declaration by April 14 at noon EDT, with Phase 1 automated refunds targeted for April 20. This is a separate proceeding from the Section 122 legality question.

## 4. Abrego Garcia (Maryland — Xinis) — April 17 Pressure Defused; April 28 Is the New Line

**Current posture**: Judge Xinis held a hearing last week and rejected the government's demand that she rule by mid-April on dissolving her injunction. She called the motion "not ripe," rebuked DOJ for attempting to "dictate" the court's timeline, and set new briefing deadlines: filings due April 20, hearing April 28. She also ruled that immigration officials cannot re-detain Abrego Garcia without a prior hearing.

**Liberia deportation track**: The government is now pursuing deportation to Liberia rather than El Salvador, attempting to route around the existing withholding-of-removal order that blocks return to El Salvador. Xinis's injunction currently keeps him in the U.S. If she dissolves it at or after the April 28 hearing, the Liberia track becomes the active deportation vector. This matters beyond Abrego Garcia — it would establish a template for removing individuals with country-specific withholding orders by routing through third countries without such orders.

**Xinis's posture**: Openly hostile to the administration. She publicly stated "You can't have it both ways — he physically needs to be in this country to be prosecuted" when DOJ argued for both deportation and prosecution simultaneously. Her contempt threat (noted in search results) is real — she has threatened DOJ with contempt over non-responsiveness.

**What to watch**: April 20 briefing filings from both sides. April 28 hearing — whether Xinis dissolves the injunction, maintains it, or imposes conditions. Any emergency filings before April 28 if the government escalates to SCOTUS on this track.

## 5. Nashville (Crenshaw) — Blanche Comments Now the Active Subthread

**Current posture**: Judge Crenshaw has issued no ruling on dismissal. The April 11 CNN reporting materially changed the public picture of this case. The silence is now extended enough that Crenshaw appears to be considering more than simple dismissal.

**The Blanche problem**: Blanche made public comments in June 2025 directly linking the Maryland civil lawsuit to the criminal investigation of Abrego Garcia. Judge Crenshaw wrote in prior rulings that Blanche's statements "directly linked the Maryland lawsuit to the investigation of criminal behavior by Abrego" and that this "suggests the administration's frustration with Abrego" — language indicating the prosecution was not "run-of-the-mill." The threshold for vindictive prosecution dismissal is met if the government fails to rebut the presumption.

**What the government tried**: DOJ put two witnesses on the stand earlier this year to rebut the vindictive-prosecution presumption by arguing that charging decisions were made locally, not by Blanche or Washington. The witnesses' testimony left a gap: Blanche himself never appeared to explain his own comments. Abrego Garcia's lawyers argued in court papers that "the government has fought tooth and nail to put any explanations beyond the court's reach."

**The subpoena question**: Crenshaw has the option to summon Blanche to Nashville to testify under oath about the origins of the prosecution. This would be a significant escalation — an acting Attorney General compelled to testify in a criminal case about whether his own statements prove political motivation. The fact that Crenshaw has not simply dismissed but is also taking time suggests he may be weighing whether to take this step before ruling.

**What a dismissal means**: The charges are tossed. The government's most powerful leverage over Abrego Garcia — criminal conviction prospect — disappears. The Maryland civil case (Xinis) becomes the only active legal track. The administration loses its "he's a criminal" political narrative in its purest form.
