## 1. Leon Remand — STILL SILENT: Final 48 Hours

**Result**: Judge Leon has issued no clarification order as of April 15. The D.C. Circuit's April 11 remand order is now four full days old. Leon has not responded.

**Why this matters now**: The D.C. Circuit explicitly stayed Leon's preliminary injunction only through April 17 — framed as giving the administration time to "seek Supreme Court review of this order remanding for factual development and clarification." The stay is not contingent on Leon acting first. It expires at midnight April 17 regardless of whether Leon has issued the clarification the D.C. Circuit requested.

**The remand question**: The D.C. Circuit panel (Millett/Garcia majority, Rao dissenting) found it lacked a sufficient factual record to assess the national security implications of halting construction. The administration argued that the ballroom and the underground bunker beneath it are a unified national security project — incorporating missile-resistant steel columns, drone-proof roofing, and a hardened bunker complex — and that halting above-ground construction disrupts the security infrastructure below. The National Trust responded publicly that no "national security emergency" exists, characterizing the administration's framing as artificially constructed after Leon's March 31 ruling.

**Decision tree — final state**: Three branches remain, but the probability distribution has shifted:

1. **Leon issues clarification on April 15 or April 16** — administration files SCOTUS application the same day with a clean factual record. Roberts issues an administrative stay, preserving status quo pending full SCOTUS review. This is the best-case scenario for the administration, but Leon's four days of silence makes it increasingly unlikely he acts before Thursday morning.

2. **Administration files SCOTUS cold on April 16 without Leon's clarification** — technically permissible but procedurally awkward. SCOTUS receives an application in a case the circuit court said was not ready for review. Roberts faces a choice between an administrative stay to preserve status quo pending an imperfect application, or allowing the deadline to lapse. Roberts's prior pattern in democracy-adjacent emergency applications (he has administratively stayed in multiple lower-court cases) suggests he grants, but the compressed timeline — under 24 hours from filing to expiry — creates genuine risk.

3. **Neither Leon nor the administration acts before April 17 midnight** — the stay simply expires. Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction reinstates automatically. The administration must then decide: halt construction, or continue and accept contempt exposure. Construction cranes operating next to the White House against a reinstated injunction produce a contempt scenario that is physically visible and documentable, unlike the procedural Abrego Garcia contempt threads.

**Contempt exposure at branch 3**: The National Trust would file an emergency contempt motion with Leon immediately. The factual record would be photographic — construction activity is visible from Pennsylvania Avenue and monitored by multiple press cameras. This is the scenario that most directly threatens the administration's relationship with the federal judiciary, because unlike most contempt threats in this cycle, there is no ambiguity about the underlying order.

## 2. SCOTUS Application — STILL NO FILING

**Result**: No emergency application from the Trump administration appears on the SCOTUS public docket as of April 15. The window is now approaching final compression.

**The strategic bind**: The administration preferred to wait for Leon's clarification before filing at SCOTUS — this would give them a stronger factual record and avoid the procedural awkwardness of asking SCOTUS to review an order the circuit court itself said was incomplete. But Leon has not acted. Each day Leon stays silent is a day the administration loses from the SCOTUS response window.

**What cold filing means procedurally**: If the administration files at SCOTUS today (April 15) without Leon's clarification, Roberts has approximately two days before the stay expires. Based on SCOTUS emergency practice, Roberts would almost certainly issue an administrative stay upon receipt — this preserves the status quo (construction continues) while the full Court considers the application. An administrative stay is not a merits ruling; it is procedural preservation. But it requires the application to actually be filed.

**If no filing by EOD April 16**: The administration is 24 hours from the deadline. A filing at that point gives Roberts hours, not days. Administrative stays have been granted in compressed windows before, but the risk of a procedural miss or overnight delay rises sharply.

**The national security argument on the merits**: The administration's SCOTUS brief, if filed, will center on the D.C. Circuit's own acknowledgment that national security implications were inadequately considered. The Rao dissent — arguing the majority should have addressed national security rather than remanding — gives the administration a ready-made framework. Rao wrote that the original Leon injunction should have been vacated outright. If Roberts is persuaded that SCOTUS needs to clarify the scope of presidential authority over White House physical infrastructure, the administration has a plausible path to five votes.

**The National Trust's SCOTUS position**: The Trust responded publicly to the administration's national security framing, stating the existing order "plainly does not" present a national security emergency. The Trust's position is that the administration artificially linked the ballroom and the bunker after Leon's ruling — that the security infrastructure was always proceeding independently and that Leon's injunction does not meaningfully interfere with it.

## 3. Section 122 CIT — NO RULING, Silence Deepens

**Result**: The three-judge CIT panel (Chief Judge Barnett, Judges Kelly and Stanceu) has not issued a ruling following the April 10 marathon oral argument. Five business days of silence post-argument is consistent with normal deliberation timelines, not a signal of any particular outcome.

**What the April 10 argument established (confirmed record)**:

- Judge Stanceu challenged DOJ attorney Brett Shumate directly on whether the statutory trigger — "large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits" — had been established. Shumate conceded he could not state what the current balance-of-payments deficit is, could not even provide an estimate. This is on the record. Section 122 requires the president to find this specific condition exists before invoking the statute; the government's own lead counsel could not articulate it.

- Judge Barnett's concern — that the administration's reading "proves too much" — tracks the SCOTUS IEEPA majority's reasoning: the Court refused to read sweeping, permanent tariff authority into statutory text designed for narrower emergencies. Barnett appears to be applying the same interpretive instinct.

- The panel collectively expressed that it was "not quite sure how to translate 1974 into 2026," signaling that the statutory meaning of "balance-of-payments deficits" in the Bretton Woods era is not straightforwardly applicable to the current floating-exchange-rate context. This interpretive difficulty could produce a narrowing construction rather than a flat invalidation.

**Post-argument analysis (Volokh/Reason)**: The post-argument takeaway from experienced observers is that the panel is skeptical of the administration's position, but the exact form of any ruling is genuinely uncertain. The panel could: (a) issue a preliminary injunction halting the tariffs pending full briefing; (b) rule on summary judgment and invalidate the tariffs; or (c) find a procedural or standing ground that avoids the merits.

**The July 24, 2026 hard deadline**: Section 122 tariffs are 150-day emergency measures. They expire July 24, 2026 unless Congress votes to extend — Congress is politically unlikely to do so. The clock is running. A CIT ruling before late June produces an emergency Federal Circuit or SCOTUS posture with live economic stakes. A ruling after the tariffs expire may moot the emergency posture.

**Five takeaways from the NBC/Hill post-argument coverage**:
1. Panel skepticism of the balance-of-payments statutory standard was unmistakable.
2. The government had no coherent answer to the threshold evidentiary question (what is the deficit?).
3. The scope of Section 122 in the modern context is genuinely unsettled — this is first-impression statutory interpretation.
4. States' standing arguments appear solid — 24 AGs filed jointly.
5. The economic stakes are substantial: billions in tariff revenue collected on a potentially invalid statutory basis.
