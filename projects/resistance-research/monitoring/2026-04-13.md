## PRIORITY: White House Ballroom — April 17 Deadline Now the Whole Story

The April 17 stay expiry is the dominant legal clock in this monitoring cycle. Here is the full procedural state as of April 13 morning.

**Where things stand**: Judge Leon's March 31 preliminary injunction (blocking $400M construction without congressional approval) is stayed. The D.C. Circuit's April 11 2-1 order did not reinstate or modify the injunction — it remanded with a narrow task: Leon must clarify on the record how his injunction's "safety and security" exception handles the administration's claimed underground security infrastructure (bomb shelters, military installations, medical facility beneath the ballroom). The D.C. Circuit majority (Millett + Garcia, both Democratic appointees; Rao dissenting) said it could not determine — "on this hurried record" — whether halting above-ground ballroom construction would interfere with the below-ground security work the administration claims is "inseparable."

**National Trust's counter-argument**: In a filing to the D.C. Circuit, the National Trust for Historic Preservation argued there is no national security emergency. Their position: Leon's injunction does not touch underground security infrastructure — it only halts above-ground ballroom construction. The "absence of a massive ballroom on White House grounds has not stopped the President from residing at the White House or hosting events there." The DOJ's inseparability argument is, in the Trust's framing, an after-the-fact legal invention to justify a stay.

**The internal DOJ contradiction**: The April 12 evening monitoring pass flagged this and it bears restating. The administration's earlier filings represented the security work as *independent* from the ballroom. The emergency appeal before the D.C. Circuit then argued they are *inseparable*. The panel's majority noted this inconsistency explicitly as a reason it could not rule without more facts. This contradiction is central to the National Trust's argument and likely to be central to any SCOTUS application.

**What happens April 17**: Three paths.

1. Judge Leon clarifies his injunction in a way that satisfies the D.C. Circuit — ruling that underground security work may proceed unimpeded while above-ground ballroom construction remains halted. This resolves the D.C. Circuit's fact question, the stay may lapse, and Leon's injunction goes back into effect against ballroom construction.

2. Judge Leon does not provide a satisfactory clarification or explicitly holds the construction halted in a way the administration claims threatens security. Administration files emergency application to SCOTUS before or at the April 17 deadline — directed to Chief Justice Roberts or Justice Kavanaugh (who handles the D.C. Circuit on the emergency docket).

3. Administration files preemptively for SCOTUS review regardless of what Leon does, using the April 17 window as leverage. Given the administration's 80% win rate on emergency shadow docket applications, this is a credible threat.

**Why a SCOTUS application is likely**: The administration has already said publicly it will seek SCOTUS review. It has every incentive to do so. The 6-3 conservative supermajority has been willing to stay lower-court injunctions in executive-power cases during litigation. A stay here would be narrower than many others — construction of a building, not deportation or executive power over agencies — but the national security framing creates ideological cover for conservative justices.

**Strategic significance for resistance work**: If SCOTUS grants a stay pending cert, ballroom construction resumes, the case continues at the merits level, and the administration gets what it wants in the short term. If SCOTUS denies, Leon's injunction snaps back into effect and the government faces a real choice: respect it, or defy a federal court order on national television, in front of the White House. That second scenario would be the most politically visible defiance of a court order in this administration's history. Worth watching closely.

## Abrego Garcia — Nashville Dismissal Is Now the Imminent Event

**The key development since the last pass**: On April 11, CNN and multiple outlets reported that the Nashville federal criminal case against Kilmar Abrego Garcia — human smuggling charges brought after he returned from El Salvador — is "poised" to be dismissed because of statements made by acting Attorney General Todd Blanche. Judge Waverly Crenshaw has already signaled in prior rulings he is leaning toward granting the vindictive prosecution dismissal. No ruling has issued as of April 13 morning, but Crenshaw's comments from the bench indicate he is ready to act.

**Why Blanche's comments matter**: During a June 2025 press conference or media appearance, Blanche directly linked the Maryland civil litigation (Abrego Garcia's wrongful-deportation lawsuit) to the decision to open a criminal investigation. Judge Crenshaw, in earlier written orders, noted that this "suggests the administration's frustration with Abrego Garcia" and that the prosecution did not appear to be a "run-of-the-mill" case. The constitutional standard for vindictive prosecution requires a defendant to show that charges were brought in retaliation for exercising a constitutional or legal right. Abrego Garcia exercised his right to sue the government for wrongfully deporting him; Blanche's on-the-record connection of that lawsuit to the prosecution decision is the evidentiary foundation of the claim.

**What happened at the most recent hearing**: Abrego Garcia's attorneys argued that two government witnesses "were unable or unwilling" to provide details that might have kept the case alive, and that Blanche himself was not present. The judge has indicated he may subpoena Blanche for sworn testimony on the department's motivations. As of April 11-12, no dismissal ruling has issued — but the procedural trajectory points toward dismissal.

**Why this matters for the broader legal record**: The Nashville case was the administration's primary justification for re-detaining and potentially re-deporting Abrego Garcia. Per the broader Abrego Garcia civil litigation in Maryland (Judge Xinis): as of February 17, 2026, Xinis ruled ICE cannot re-detain Abrego Garcia because removal is not "reasonably foreseeable in the foreseeable future." If the Nashville charges are dismissed as vindictive prosecution, the government loses its strongest factual argument for any future enforcement action against him. It also creates a precedent that using criminal prosecution in retaliation for civil rights litigation is legally sanctionable.

**The Liberia deportation angle**: The administration separately filed in the Maryland case (March 20, 2026) arguing that allowing Abrego Garcia to remain in the U.S. rather than be deported to Liberia would "cast doubt on the diplomatic reliability of the United States" and asked Judge Xinis to dissolve her injunction by April 17. That April 17 deadline in the Maryland case is separate from the ballroom deadline but creates a remarkable coincidence — two administration legal asks on the same date.

## MSPB/NLRB Board Removal — Chief Justice Roberts Stays Reinstatement on April 9

This is the update the previous pass was waiting for. The April 9 oral argument at the Federal Circuit (Oguntade v. MSPB, 2025-1114) was one data point. The larger development is Chief Justice Roberts' April 9 emergency stay.

**What happened**: On April 7, the D.C. Circuit sitting en banc voted 7-4 to vacate an earlier three-judge panel ruling and temporarily reinstate Cathy Harris (MSPB chair, Biden appointee) and Gwynne Wilcox (NLRB member, Biden appointee). This was the D.C. Circuit's full bench, by a substantial majority, saying the firings were likely unlawful.

Two days later, on April 9, Chief Justice John Roberts issued a solo emergency stay suspending the D.C. Circuit's en banc order. This re-removed Harris and Wilcox, pending the Supreme Court's consideration of the full merits in Trump v. Slaughter (FTC commissioner case, argued December 8, 2025 — decision expected June 2026).

**Operational consequence**: The MSPB is again without a quorum. The MSPB requires a quorum of board members to issue decisions. Without a quorum, tens of thousands of federal workers who were fired and filed MSPB appeals — including the mass termination of probationary employees — have no venue to adjudicate their cases. The board can receive appeals but cannot decide them.

**Why Roberts' solo action matters**: An emergency stay issued by a single justice before full Court consideration signals that Roberts believes the administration is likely to prevail on the merits — or at minimum that the Court will not reinstate the board members pending its own decision. Given the signals from Trump v. Slaughter oral argument (where a majority of justices appeared skeptical of the independent-agency-protection framework from Humphrey's Executor), the Harris and Wilcox firings will likely be upheld when the Court rules in June 2026.

**The structural significance**: If SCOTUS upholds the firings in Trump v. Slaughter and consolidates the MSPB/NLRB question, it will fundamentally alter the independent-agency model. The FTC, NLRB, MSPB, EEOC, SEC, FCC, and CFPB all operate under frameworks that assume board members can only be fired for cause. If that protection falls, the president can staff all of these agencies with loyalists and remove anyone who resists. This is the biggest institutional-democracy case of the term.

**The Federal Circuit oral argument (April 9 — Oguntade v. MSPB)**: This is a separate, narrower track — an individual federal worker's appeal of an MSPB decision. Audio is posted on the Federal Circuit's website. This case is not the vehicle for the big removal-power question; it goes to the MSPB's administrative processes. The merits vehicle is Trump v. Slaughter at SCOTUS.

## CIT / IEEPA Tariff Refund — April 14 Deadline Tomorrow

**Status as of April 13**: The April 14 noon CBP status report to Senior Judge Richard Eaton (CIT, Atmus Filtration, Inc. v. United States) is the next formal checkpoint for the court-supervised $166 billion IEEPA tariff refund process.

**What the report will cover**: The four components of CBP's CAPE (Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries) system are between 60-85% complete as of March 31:
- Claim Portal: 85% complete
- Review and Liquidation/Reliquidation: 80% complete
- Refund component: 75% complete
- Mass Processing: 60% complete

Phase 1 covers approximately 63% of entries where IEEPA tariffs were paid: unliquidated entries, entries in the 90-day voluntary reliquidation window, and entries under suspension or review. Phase 2 (finalized liquidated entries and open protests) comes later.

**The April 20 rollout deadline**: CBP has committed to Phase 1 going live April 20. As of April 1, the CIT found CBP "on track." Tomorrow's status report confirms or modifies that finding. If CBP is off track, Eaton can take additional judicial action to enforce compliance. The 3 PM closed conference with Eaton follows the noon report.

**Background**: The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs in a 6-3 ruling on February 20, 2026, holding the statute does not grant the president unilateral tariff authority. The administration switched to Section 122 (Trade Act of 1974) within hours — which is explicitly temporary (150 days, expiring July 24, 2026 unless Congress acts). The refund process is the court's enforcement mechanism for the IEEPA ruling.

**What to watch April 14**: If CBP's report shows slippage from the 60-85% progress figures, the closed conference could produce an enforcement order with more specific milestones. If CBP hits the April 20 rollout, the next major checkpoint is whether the 45-day processing window for individual filers is honored in practice.

## Birthright Citizenship — Post-Argument Update

**Status**: Oral argument was held April 1, 2026 in Trump v. Barbara (No. 25-365). A majority of the Court — including Chief Justice Roberts, who called the administration's argument "quirky" — appeared skeptical of the administration's position. Roberts' characterization was notable given that he authored the 2010 Citizens United opinion and is not generally a liberal voice on constitutional interpretation.

**The administration's argument**: Solicitor General John Sauer argued the 14th Amendment ("subject to the jurisdiction thereof") was intended solely to guarantee citizenship to children of formerly enslaved people, not to create universal birthright citizenship. Under this theory, children of undocumented immigrants and temporary visa-holders are not "subject to the jurisdiction" of the United States.

**The consensus view after argument**: Multiple outlets (SCOTUSblog, NPR, ACLU, The Nation) reported that the justices — including conservatives — pushed back hard on the administration's textual and historical arguments. The 1898 precedent (United States v. Wong Kim Ark) guaranteeing birthright citizenship for children of Chinese immigrants was the central counterargument, and the government had no satisfying response to it.

**Decision expected**: Late June 2026. If the Court rules against the administration, birthright citizenship is affirmed and the executive order is permanently blocked. If the Court somehow narrows or eliminates birthright citizenship — still considered unlikely after argument — it would be the most consequential constitutional shift of the modern era.

**Healthcare impact flagged**: Georgetown Center for Children and Families (April 10, 2026) published analysis showing a ruling for the administration would immediately strip Medicaid eligibility from hundreds of thousands of newborns each year, as Medicaid eligibility is tied to citizenship status.

The question is whether the infrastructure that turns out millions for a Saturday rally can translate to a midweek work stoppage. The Minnesota General Strike (January 23, 2026, Minneapolis) is the model organizers point to. That action was local and geographically contained; a national general strike requires a qualitatively different level of coordination and risk tolerance among participants.

## Trump v. Slaughter — Decision Pending, Not Yet Issued

The Supreme Court heard oral argument in Trump v. Slaughter (No. 25-332) on December 8, 2025. The case tests whether the president can fire FTC commissioners at will, potentially overturning Humphrey's Executor (1935). A decision is expected in June 2026 — not yet issued. The MSPB/NLRB removal track (above) is being held in legal suspension until Slaughter resolves the underlying constitutional question about independent-agency protection.

**Signal from argument**: A majority of justices appeared ready to limit or overrule Humphrey's Executor. If they do, the removal protections for MSPB chair Cathy Harris, NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox, and by extension all members of all independent regulatory agencies, collapse. The EEOC, SEC, FCC, CFPB, CFTC, and others are all potentially affected.
