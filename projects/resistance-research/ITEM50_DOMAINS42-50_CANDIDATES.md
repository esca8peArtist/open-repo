# ITEM50: Domains 42–50 Candidate Scoping

*Completed May 5, 2026. Nine candidate topics evaluated for framework expansion into Domains 42–50. Each candidate fills a structural gap that remains after the first 41 domains are complete. Candidates are assessed on: urgency (2026–2028 policy window), coalition strength (cross-domain audience leverage), research complexity (estimated hours for full domain development), cross-domain dependencies (how the candidate integrates into the existing architecture), and policy window timing and institutional leverage. Top three candidates receive full research roadmaps. Format consistent with ITEM10, ITEM12, ITEM17, ITEM26, ITEM44.*

## Part I: Executive Summary

### What the 41-Domain Framework Now Covers

After the first 41 domains, the framework is comprehensive across: electoral architecture (voting rights, redistricting, RCV, elections infrastructure, foreign interference, federal executive interference in 2026 midterms); institutional integrity (civil service, anti-corruption, prosecutorial weaponization, congressional fiscal authority); judicial independence (SCOTUS structure, Loper Bright, OT2026 preview); economic architecture (digital government, fiscal reform, labor and employment, social safety net, antitrust, trade policy, financial sector independence); information governance (media ecosystem, higher education, AI governance); rights and civil liberties (press freedom, assembly, criminal justice, immigration, data privacy, reparations, reproductive rights); constitutional design (Article V reform, tribal sovereignty); domestic intelligence accountability; and disability rights and civic participation.

### Four Remaining Structural Gaps

Three years of framework development have compressed the gap space considerably. Four structural areas remain absent from or inadequately treated by the existing architecture:

**Gap A: Epistemic Infrastructure and Disinformation as a Democratic Design Problem.** Domain 8 (Media Ecosystem) addresses the business model crisis in local news and the structural conditions enabling disinformation. Domain 36 (AI Governance) addresses algorithmic accountability and AI-generated content governance. Neither domain frames the epistemic crisis — the systematic destruction of a shared factual foundation for democratic deliberation — as a coherent democratic-design problem requiring its own structural reform architecture. The 2026 midterms are the first electoral cycle where political deepfakes are deployed at industrial scale. An epistemic-infrastructure domain fills this gap.

**Gap B: Post-Insurrection Accountability and Transitional Democratic Justice.** The framework documents prosecutorial weaponization (Domain 29) and the 2026 elections (Domain 37), but has no domain analyzing transitional justice for insurrectionary conduct — how democracies restore institutional legitimacy after autocratic ruptures, what accountability mechanisms are available when the DOJ itself is captured, and what a future administration's accountability architecture would look like. The mass pardons of January 6 participants and the DOJ's motion to vacate Oath Keepers and Proud Boys convictions create an active transitional-justice problem without a framework domain to analyze it.

**Gap C: Drug Policy, Democratic Legitimacy, and the Carceral State.** Domain 16 (Criminal Justice and Policing) addresses mass incarceration and policing reform as broad structural problems. No domain addresses drug policy specifically as a democratic legitimacy problem: the 2026 Schedule III cannabis rescheduling created an unstable two-track federal framework that Congress could collapse within weeks; the racial disparities in drug enforcement are among the most extensively documented democratic-participation-suppressing mechanisms in American law; and the DEA June 2026 administrative hearing creates a specific institutional window. This is a distinct structural gap.

**Gap D: Trade Policy and Democratic Accountability for Executive Economic Power.** Domain 23 (Trade Policy and Tariff Unilateralism) exists but is scoped primarily as an economic architecture document. The democratic accountability dimension — how the constitutional transfer of tariff authority from Congress to the executive branch has enabled unilateral economic statecraft that bypasses democratic deliberation, and what the restoration of congressional trade authority would require — deserves deeper treatment than Domain 23's current framing provides. The 2026 midterm window, with trade-war fallout visible in consumer prices and agricultural sector distress, creates a specific advocacy audience.

### Candidate A: Reproductive Rights and Democratic Institutional Design

**Urgency (2026–2028):** CRITICAL

The 2026 midterms are the most consequential reproductive rights election since *Dobbs*. Three state ballot measures are confirmed for November 2026 — Missouri, Nevada, and Virginia — with Idaho and Nebraska in the signature collection phase. Missouri's ballot measure is a deliberate counter-measure designed to confuse voters and reverse the 2024 constitutional abortion rights amendment — a live democratic-manipulation scenario. Reproductive rights have been the highest-turnout driver in every post-*Dobbs* election cycle, making this a force multiplier for the midterm electoral context the framework already tracks in Domain 37.

The federal dimension has also sharpened: H.R.12 (Women's Health Protection Act of 2025) is active in the 119th Congress; DOJ has brought enforcement actions against states that have criminalized cross-state travel for abortion; and the mifepristone litigation chain (FDA approval challenge, SCOTUS OT2026 docket) creates a judicial window in the same period as the SCOTUS term Domain 35 tracks. Domain 39 covers reproductive rights primarily through the labor-rights coalition lens and the enforcement architecture for post-*Dobbs* criminal prosecution. A dedicated Domain 42 would address the democratic institutional design question: what happens when 60% of the US population supports abortion access but 22 states have near-total bans — what does this reveal about the structural failures of democratic responsiveness, and what is the reform architecture for restoring reproductive rights as a function of democratic governance rather than judicial lottery?

**Coalition Strength:** VERY HIGH

The reproductive rights coalition is the most electorally mobilized constituency in the post-*Dobbs* environment. Planned Parenthood Action Fund, NARAL Pro-Choice America (now Reproductive Freedom for All), the Center for Reproductive Rights, the National Women's Law Center, and ACLU Reproductive Freedom Project constitute a mature Washington advocacy infrastructure with 50-state organizing capacity. The coalition's demonstrated ballot-initiative success rate — seven of seven state measures protecting abortion rights passed between 2022 and 2024 — provides the strongest evidence of cross-partisan electoral leverage of any domain candidate. Suburban women who identify as Republican or independent have voted for abortion rights at 60%+ rates in every post-*Dobbs* election.

Cross-domain audience: Domain 39 (reproductive rights labor enforcement), Domain 16 (criminalization of abortion providers and patients), Domain 7 (First Amendment and speech restrictions on abortion counseling), Domain 6 (mifepristone litigation, SCOTUS), Domain 22 (racial disparities in maternal mortality and abortion access in Black communities). This candidate has the broadest coalition overlap of any domain in the 42–50 set.

**Research Complexity:** 12–15 hours. Primary sources are abundant and well-organized. The institutional design framing — rather than a pure policy or legal analysis — requires synthesis of comparative reproductive rights frameworks (Canada, Ireland, Germany) with the US structural failure analysis. KFF's abortion access dashboard, the Center for Reproductive Rights' *After Roe Fell* tracker, and the Brennan Center's 60-day post-Dobbs state legal analysis provide the empirical foundation. The comparative dimension (Ireland's 2018 referendum via citizens' assembly — the best international model for democratic resolution of contested reproductive rights questions) requires dedicated sourcing but the material is accessible.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 35 (SCOTUS — mifepristone OT2026), Domain 6 (judicial independence — reproductive rights at the intersection of the Loper Bright administrative deference collapse), Domain 39 (labor rights — pregnancy discrimination as a labor rights question), Domain 33 (state autocratization — anti-abortion legislation as part of the single-party trifecta autocratization playbook), Domain 16 (criminalization of patients and providers), Domain 22 (racial health equity).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** The November 2026 ballot measures are the immediate target. The federal legislative window opens if Democrats gain Senate seats and can schedule WHPA floor debate. The mifepristone litigation timeline (SCOTUS OT2026-2027 term preview) creates a judicial advocacy window. A domain document produced before the August 2026 primaries is actionable for voter mobilization in reproductive rights–focused campaigns.

### Candidate B: Epistemic Infrastructure and the Disinformation Crisis

**Urgency (2026–2028):** HIGH

The 2026 midterms are the first electoral cycle where political deepfakes are deployed at industrial scale, with AI deepfakes already documented in campaign advertising in Arizona, Georgia, and Pennsylvania primary races. The World Economic Forum (March 2026) identified AI-enabled cognitive manipulation as the primary information environment risk for the 2026 election cycle. The American Prospect (April 2026) documented industrial-scale AI deepfake deployment in 2026 midterm campaigns. The EU AI Act's deepfake labeling requirements (Article 50) became enforceable in August 2026 — creating a transatlantic regulatory divergence that is itself a policy window for comparative analysis.

No federal legislation exists prohibiting deepfakes in political campaigns, though 31 states have enacted state-level restrictions that have been challenged on First Amendment grounds (X Corp filed federal suit against Minnesota's political deepfakes law in 2025). The epistemic crisis extends beyond deepfakes to encompass the systematic collapse of institutional trust, the algorithmic amplification of disinformation, and the "liar's dividend" — the ability of political actors to dismiss authentic evidence by claiming it is AI-generated. This is a democratic foundation problem: democracy requires a shared epistemic basis for deliberation, and the systematic destruction of that foundation is a structural democratic design failure.

Domain 36 (AI Governance) and Domain 8 (Media Ecosystem) both touch this problem but neither frames it as an epistemic infrastructure design problem with its own reform architecture. The gap is the synthesis: what institutional mechanisms — regulatory, technical, media-design, civics education — would rebuild the epistemic infrastructure that democratic deliberation requires?

**Coalition Strength:** HIGH

The epistemic infrastructure coalition is emerging but not yet institutionally mature. The Brennan Center's deepfake election research team, the Stanford Internet Observatory, the Harvard Kennedy School's Shorenstein Center, the Knight Foundation's journalism and democracy program, and the News Literacy Project constitute the primary research infrastructure. The EFF and ACLU's First Amendment concerns about content moderation create genuine coalition tension that the domain document must navigate — but that tension itself represents an unusual cross-ideological engagement opportunity.

Cross-domain audience: Domain 8 (media ecosystem — same institutional audience), Domain 36 (AI governance — same technical audience), Domain 37 (election security — deepfakes as an election interference mechanism), Domain 27 (higher education — epistemic institutions under attack).

**Research Complexity:** 10–14 hours. Primary sources are well-documented: Brennan Center deepfakes research, WEF cognitive manipulation report (March 2026), EU AI Act Article 50 provisions, EU DisinfoLab reporting, First Amendment litigation records, state deepfake law tracker (31 states). The institutional design component — what does epistemic infrastructure reform look like — requires engagement with the comparative regulatory approaches (EU DSA, EU AI Act) and the research literature on information disorders (UNESCO, First Draft, Information Futures Lab at Brown University).

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 8 (media ecosystem as the delivery mechanism for disinformation), Domain 36 (AI governance — the technical regulation of generative AI and deepfakes), Domain 37 (election security — deepfakes as a foreign and domestic interference tool), Domain 27 (higher education — universities as epistemic institutions whose credibility is under attack), Domain 7 (First Amendment — the constitutional constraint on content-based regulation).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** August 2026 primaries are the first major deepfakes-in-elections stress test; November 2026 general is the peak risk period. EU AI Act deepfake labeling enforcement begins August 2026 — creating a transatlantic comparison window. Congressional hearings on AI and elections are likely in the post-midterm period. State deepfake law litigation timeline (X Corp v. Minnesota) is expected to produce federal circuit court decisions in 2026–2027 that will shape the federal legislative landscape.

### Candidate C: Post-Insurrection Accountability and Transitional Democratic Justice

**Urgency (2026–2028):** HIGH

The Trump administration's vacating of January 6 convictions — including motions to overturn the seditious conspiracy convictions of Oath Keepers founder Stewart Rhodes and Proud Boys leaders — represents the most acute transitional-justice crisis in modern US democratic history. The Stanford Law Review (January 2026) explicitly characterized the DOJ's attacks on January 6 prosecutors as "a notable case of democratic backsliding" and "capturing the referees." The House Judiciary Committee Democrats released two reports on the fifth anniversary (January 6, 2026) documenting the coup plotters' careers, the public safety consequences of mass pardons, and the evidentiary record.

The transitional justice problem is structural: what mechanisms remain available for democratic accountability when the prosecutorial system itself has been captured by the perpetrators of the democratic attack? The domain would analyze four available mechanisms beyond DOJ prosecution: civil litigation (ongoing Section 3 litigation in Domain 37a), state-level prosecutions (Fulton County's jurisdiction, which exists independently of federal pardons), congressional accountability (select committee authority, contempt enforcement, expulsion of members who participated in January 6 planning), and international mechanisms (ICC complementarity doctrine, universal jurisdiction). The 14th Amendment Section 3 disqualification litigation is the most important near-term legal avenue, with active federal cases that intersect with Domain 37a.

**Coalition Strength:** MEDIUM-HIGH

The January 6 accountability coalition is institutionally well-organized but polarized along partisan lines in a way that limits its cross-partisan outreach. The Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, the States United Democracy Center, the Democracy Docket (Section 3 litigation track), and the American Oversight project constitute the primary advocacy infrastructure. The coalition gains cross-partisan strength through the "rule of law" framing — some libertarian-conservative voices (the Federalist Society's rule-of-law wing, former Republican officials who testified before the January 6 committee) provide genuine ideological diversity. The Frank Church Institute and the broader civil liberties community provide a principled-accountability framing that transcends partisan politics.

Cross-domain audience: Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization — the DOJ capture that enabled the conviction reversals), Domain 37a (Section 3 litigation, already in the domain portfolio), Domain 6 (judicial independence — the courts that will hear Section 3 and civil accountability claims), Domain 2 (civil service — the fired January 6 prosecutors as civil service accountability subjects).

**Research Complexity:** 12–16 hours. The research requires engagement with the specialized transitional justice literature (the International Center for Transitional Justice, Carnegie Endowment's comparative transitional justice work, the Brennan Center's post-backsliding recovery analysis) alongside the specific evidentiary record of January 6. Primary sources are extensive: the January 6 Committee Final Report (845 pages), DOJ prosecution records, the Stanford Law Review's democratic backsliding analysis, the House Judiciary Democrats' January 2026 anniversary reports, and the active Section 3 litigation docket. The comparative dimension — how other democracies have handled post-insurrection accountability (Germany post-1933, Portugal post-Carnation Revolution, South Africa's TRC, Spain's transition justice debates) — requires dedicated hours but provides the domain's most distinctive contribution.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 29 (DOJ capture as the mechanism blocking federal accountability), Domain 37a (Section 3 litigation as an active accountability pathway), Domain 6 (courts as the institutional venue for remaining accountability), Domain 2 (civil service — the prosecutors fired for pursuing accountability), Domain 19 (international mechanisms — ICC complementarity, universal jurisdiction).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** The Section 3 litigation timeline produces federal circuit court decisions in 2026; SCOTUS cert petition probable in OT2026–2027. Congressional investigations become more viable if Democrats gain Senate committee chairmanships in November 2026. State-level prosecution in Georgia has its own timeline independent of federal pardon authority. Civil litigation (ongoing federal and state civil suits by January 6 victims against the Proud Boys, Oath Keepers, and planning participants) has 2026–2027 trial dates. A domain document that provides the transitional justice framework would be immediately actionable for legal teams pursuing civil accountability and for congressional staff preparing investigation architectures.

### Candidate D: Labor Power, Collective Bargaining, and Democratic Participation

**Urgency (2026–2028):** MEDIUM-HIGH

Private sector union density has collapsed from one-third of the workforce in the 1950s to 5.9% in 2024, the lowest in modern US history. The connection to democratic erosion is empirically documented: as union density collapsed, wealth inequality reached pre-Depression levels, and the communities with the lowest union density voted most strongly for authoritarian-populist politics. Domain 39 addresses labor rights through the lens of the labor-reproductive rights coalition and the enforcement architecture for worker organizing. But the structural democratic-participation question — how the union density collapse has removed the primary working-class institution that connected workers to political participation — requires its own domain-level analysis.

The 2026 NLRB is effectively non-functional under the current administration, with multiple Board members removed, the General Counsel's enforcement posture reversed, and the Emergency Workplace Organizing Committee documenting the alternative organizing strategies workers are pursuing outside the formal NLRB election process. The "building worker power in a precarious federal landscape" framing (OnLabor, 2026) is the exact domain brief: what alternative organizing pathways exist, what legislative architecture would restore collective bargaining rights, and what does the union density–democracy connection mean for the framework's overall democratic renewal thesis?

**Coalition Strength:** HIGH

The labor movement coalition is institutionally deep — AFL-CIO, SEIU, UAW, CWA, and the independent union wave (Starbucks Workers United, Amazon Labor Union) represent both established and emergent organizing infrastructure. The cross-domain coalition is unusual: labor connects to reproductive rights (Domain 39), immigrant rights (Domain 14/Candidate I), criminal justice (Domain 16 — formerly incarcerated workers excluded from labor organizing), and disability rights (Domain 41/ITEM44 — SSDI benefit rules that penalize working disabled people). The union density–democratic participation academic literature (Harvard Kennedy School, EPI, Jacobin's labor coverage) provides a rich source base.

**Research Complexity:** 10–14 hours. Primary sources are well-organized: EPI's labor rights state solutions report, OnLabor's sectoral bargaining analysis, Jacobin's union density crisis coverage (February 2026), Labor Notes' organizing pathways analysis (February 2026), and the EWOC documentation of alternative organizing. The sectoral bargaining comparative dimension (Germany's Tarifpartnerschaft, Scandinavian centralized bargaining, Canada's card-check certification) requires dedicated sourcing. The constitutional law dimension (the NLRA's commerce clause foundation, Taft-Hartley's Section 14(b) right-to-work preemption, card-check authorization as a constitutional question) requires engagement with labor law scholarship.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 39 (labor-reproductive rights convergence), Domain 4 (digital government — gig economy workers and platform labor), Domain 17 (social safety net — unemployment insurance and labor displacement), Domain 20 (antitrust — employer monopsony power over labor markets), Domain 22 (racial justice — race-union density correlations), Domain 14 (immigration — undocumented workers and organizing rights).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** NLRB board restoration is a 2027 legislative priority if Democrats gain Senate seats. The PRO Act (Protecting the Right to Organize Act) is the primary federal legislative vehicle — stalled under current majority but positioned for a new Congress. State-level labor law is the near-term window: Virginia's proposed 2026 legislation expanding public-sector collective bargaining to home care workers is the live test case. Card-check authorization bills have passed in multiple state legislatures and provide empirical evidence for federal advocates.

### Candidate E: Drug Policy, Democratic Legitimacy, and the Carceral State

**Urgency (2026–2028):** MEDIUM-HIGH

On April 23, 2026, Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche issued a final order placing FDA-approved marijuana products and state-licensed medical cannabis in Schedule III — the first federal cannabis rescheduling in US history. This two-track framework (medical cannabis Schedule III, recreational cannabis Schedule I) is explicitly unstable: DEA will hold a new administrative hearing beginning June 29, 2026, and Congress has active legislation that could freeze DEA implementation. The drug policy domain has an unusually specific and time-bounded 2026 policy window — the DEA hearing date — that no other candidate can match.

The democratic legitimacy dimension is structural: marijuana remains federally illegal despite majority public support for full legalization (68% in 2024 Gallup polling), despite 24 states having legalized recreational use, and despite the documented racial disparity in enforcement (Black Americans are 3.7 times more likely to be arrested for marijuana possession than white Americans despite similar usage rates). The drug enforcement regime is a democratic-legitimacy failure in the specific sense that policy has been systematically insulated from democratic accountability through prosecutorial discretion, mandatory minimums, and criminal justice mechanisms that suppress the political participation of the communities most affected by enforcement. In 2024, more than 6 million formerly incarcerated Americans could not vote in many states due to felony disenfranchisement laws that disproportionately stem from drug convictions.

**Coalition Strength:** MEDIUM

The drug policy reform coalition has unusual cross-partisan strength in principle — the FAIR Act model, the Koch-funded criminal justice reform through Right on Crime, and the ACLU's criminal law reform program all support reform — but the coalition is politically constrained by the complexity of the rescheduling two-track framework. The marijuana industry (NCIA, the National Cannabis Industry Association) is an institutional stakeholder with its own lobbying infrastructure that sometimes conflicts with the civil rights framing the domain requires. The criminal justice reform community (ACLU, Brennan Center, The Sentencing Project, Prison Policy Initiative) provides the democratic-legitimacy framing that is the domain's distinctive contribution.

**Research Complexity:** 12–15 hours. Primary sources are well-organized: the April 2026 DOJ rescheduling order, DEA June 2026 hearing records, MPP (Marijuana Policy Project) legislative tracker, Moritz College Drug Enforcement and Policy Center research, The Sentencing Project's felony disenfranchisement data, and Prison Policy Initiative's 2026 winnable reforms report. The constitutional law dimension (Controlled Substances Act and the commerce clause, Gonzales v. Raich, federalism implications of state-federal cannabis law conflicts) requires engagement with constitutional scholarship. The comparative dimension (Canada's Cannabis Act full federal legalization framework, Germany's April 2024 partial legalization, Portugal's decriminalization model) is well-documented.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 16 (criminal justice and policing — drug enforcement as the primary driver of mass incarceration), Domain 22 (racial justice — racial disparities in drug enforcement as a civil rights violation), Domain 9 (federalism — the state-federal conflict in cannabis law), Domain 23 (trade — cannabis rescheduling's impact on US pharmaceutical and agricultural trade), Domain 7 (civil liberties — felony disenfranchisement and the democratic participation consequences of drug enforcement).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** DEA administrative hearing begins June 29, 2026 — the most specific near-term institutional window of any candidate. Congressional action to block or expand rescheduling is likely in 2026 appropriations. State-level ballot measures for full legalization are in preparation for 2026 in multiple states. Felony disenfranchisement reform legislation is active in 8–10 state legislatures with specific 2026–2027 session windows.

### Candidate F: Civil Asset Forfeiture, Property Rights, and Democratic Due Process

**Urgency (2026–2028):** MEDIUM

In February 2026, Congressmen Tim Walberg (R-MI) and Jamie Raskin (D-MD) introduced the Fifth Amendment Integrity Restoration (FAIR) Act — the most significant bipartisan civil asset forfeiture reform legislation in years. The House Judiciary Committee approved a version of the bill in 2024. The Senate version has cosponsors from across the political spectrum: Rand Paul (R-KY), Mike Lee (R-UT), Angus King (I-ME), Ron Wyden (D-OR). This is the strongest bipartisan reform coalition of any criminal justice domain candidate, making 2026 the most favorable legislative window in a decade.

Civil asset forfeiture is a democratic accountability problem in a specific technical sense: law enforcement agencies can seize and keep property without criminal conviction, using proceeds to fund their own operations — creating a direct conflict of interest between institutional financial incentives and democratic accountability for law enforcement conduct. The "equitable sharing" program allows federal law enforcement to share seized assets with local agencies, explicitly designed to circumvent state-level restrictions on forfeiture. This is the privatization of law enforcement incentives at the expense of constitutional due process.

**Coalition Strength:** HIGH (cross-partisan)

The cross-partisan reform coalition for civil asset forfeiture is the strongest of any domain candidate in the 42–50 set: left (ACLU, Brennan Center, NAACP), libertarian-right (Cato Institute, Reason Foundation, Institute for Justice), and institutionalist-Republican (the FAIR Act's Republican House and Senate cosponsors). The Institute for Justice has built the most developed public litigation and advocacy infrastructure around this issue; their *Policing for Profit* reports provide the primary empirical evidence base.

**Research Complexity:** 8–12 hours. The lowest research complexity of any candidate in this set. Primary sources are well-organized and publicly accessible: FAIR Act text and committee records, Institute for Justice's state forfeiture data, Cato Institute analysis, Brennan Center reform framework, ACLU documentation of racial disparities in forfeiture targeting. The constitutional law dimension (Fifth Amendment due process, Eighth Amendment excessive fines after Timbs v. Indiana 2019, Fourth Amendment and property seizure) is well-documented in recent SCOTUS precedent.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 29 (prosecutorial weaponization — DOJ's forfeiture equitable sharing program as a mechanism of prosecutorial leverage), Domain 16 (criminal justice and policing — forfeiture as a policing incentive problem), Domain 22 (racial justice — documented racial disparities in forfeiture targeting), Domain 7 (civil liberties — due process and property rights).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** FAIR Act 2026 introduction creates a specific 119th Congress window. House Judiciary Committee markup is the immediate lever. State-level action is ongoing: Maine, New Mexico, and North Carolina have passed strong forfeiture reform laws that provide implementation models. The Timbs v. Indiana SCOTUS precedent (2019 — unanimous: Eighth Amendment excessive fines clause applies to state forfeiture) has created a litigation window that remains open. Note: the domain's urgency is somewhat diluted by the fact that the cross-partisan reform coalition is already well-mobilized; the framework domain would provide the democratic-design synthesis that the current advocacy literature lacks.

### Candidate G: Tribal Sovereignty, Self-Determination, and Democratic Authority

**Urgency (2026–2028):** MEDIUM

*Note: Tribal sovereignty was identified as Candidate C in ITEM10 and as a co-primary candidate in ITEM26/Domain 40. However, Domain 40's scope centers on constitutional architecture and the Article V amendment process, with tribal sovereignty addressed as a co-equal topic but not at full domain depth. A standalone Domain 48 would provide the complete structural analysis that tribal sovereignty as a democratic design question requires — treating Indigenous nations not as a protected class within US legal structures but as co-governing entities whose democratic authority predates and is not derived from the US Constitution.*

The Trump administration's FY2026 budget proposed nearly $1 billion in cuts to tribal programs: a 41% reduction in Contract Support Costs (from $337M to $200M), a 37% reduction in Tribal Government programs (from $401M to $253M), and an 80% reduction in Education Construction ($234M to $48M). Congress rejected these cuts in a bipartisan FY2026 funding package — but the administration's next budget cycle will propose similar reductions, and the Interior Department's reorganization (targeted reduction of Bureau of Indian Affairs federal presence) continues administratively independent of appropriations. The treaty rights dimension — public lands extraction leases on territories with active treaty protections — is generating new federal litigation that the framework has no domain to analyze.

The democratic design argument is substantive: the Haudenosaunee Confederacy (Iroquois Confederacy) provided documented precedent for the US Constitution's federal design; the participatory governance mechanisms in Domain 3 (citizens' assemblies) have direct antecedents in Indigenous deliberative traditions; and the nation-to-nation relationship is a distinct democratic architecture question that is not resolved by civil rights framing. Domain 40's partial coverage addresses this but does not provide the structural depth the question requires.

**Coalition Strength:** MEDIUM

The tribal sovereignty coalition is institutionally well-organized but geographically concentrated and outside the primary distribution networks for the existing framework. The National Congress of American Indians (NCAI), the Native American Rights Fund (NARF), the National Indian Health Board, the Association on American Indian Affairs, and Tribal Business News constitute the primary advocacy infrastructure. Cross-partisan support exists in Congress — the bipartisan rejection of tribal budget cuts demonstrates that tribal sovereignty is not a purely partisan issue — but the coalition's geographic concentration in specific states (South Dakota, Montana, Oklahoma, New Mexico, Alaska) limits its national distribution leverage.

**Research Complexity:** 14–18 hours. This is the most research-intensive candidate in the 42–50 set. The legal framework (treaty law, federal Indian law, the trust responsibility, the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act, ISDEAA Section 638 compacting) is a specialized field requiring engagement with dedicated legal literature. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy's democratic design influence on the US Constitution is well-documented historically but requires careful sourcing to avoid overclaiming. The comparative dimension (New Zealand's Waitangi Tribunal process as the best international model for treaty rights adjudication; Canada's Truth and Reconciliation Commission; Australia's ATSIC governance model and its abolition) requires dedicated international sourcing.

**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Domain 22 (reparations and racial justice — tribal sovereignty as distinct from but related to racial justice claims), Domain 9 (federalism — nation-to-nation relations as a distinct federal design question), Domain 15 (environment — treaty rights and public lands as an environmental governance question), Domain 3 (participatory democracy — Indigenous deliberative traditions as precedent), Domain 40 (constitutional architecture — the partial tribal sovereignty coverage that this domain would deepen).

**2026–2028 Policy Window:** FY2027 appropriations battle over tribal program cuts is the most specific near-term window. The Interior Department's BIA reorganization is proceeding administratively — litigation by tribal nations challenging reorganization is active. The UN Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples produced a thematic report in 2025 relevant to US treaty obligations. UNDRIP (UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples) — which the US endorsed in 2010 but has not implemented — provides a reform architecture anchor.

### Candidate H: Housing as Democratic Infrastructure

**Urgency (2026–2028):** MEDIUM

Housing instability is among the most acute forms of democratic exclusion: renters who move frequently are less likely to be registered to vote, less likely to participate in local political processes, and are systematically excluded from the homeowner-voter coalitions that dominate municipal and suburban politics. The eviction crisis — which is simultaneously an economic crisis and a civic participation crisis — has intensified since the COVID-era eviction moratoriums ended. The National Low Income Housing Coalition documents a 7.3 million unit shortage of affordable housing for the lowest-income renters. Corporate landlords now own a significant share of single-family housing stock, with documented patterns of rent increases that exceed inflation by 2–4x in previously affordable markets.

Domain 13 (Housing) covers affordable housing policy as a social provision question. The democratic infrastructure dimension — how housing instability functions as a democratic participation suppressor, how the corporate consolidation of housing markets extracts political power from tenant communities, and what tenant organizing and ownership democratization would look like as a democratic design project — requires a distinct framing.
