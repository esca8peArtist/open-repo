# Legal Liability Assessment: Democratic Renewal Framework Distribution

**Overall Risk Assessment: LOW-TO-MEDIUM (with protective measures)**

Without protective measures: MEDIUM. With the recommended structures below: LOW.

The core finding is direct: the 35-domain framework as described — research documentation, policy analysis, civic organizing guidance, and electoral integrity research — is core First Amendment-protected political speech with robust constitutional protection going back to the founding era. The present legal environment has materially elevated the risk of *targeted harassment and pretextual investigation*, but it has not eliminated the constitutional protections that courts would apply. The risk is primarily from prosecutorial overreach and chilling effects, not from facially valid legal claims. The mitigations below are designed for the elevated-threat environment of 2025-2026, not a baseline analysis.

---

## 1. CFAA Exposure: Low Risk, Clearly Inapplicable

The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. § 1030) targets unauthorized access to computer systems. Its relevance to distributing a research framework is close to zero under current law.

**Why the CFAA does not apply to this work**: The CFAA requires either accessing a computer "without authorization" or "exceeding authorized access." The framework is original research and analysis composed from publicly available sources. There is no computer system being accessed without authorization, no database being scraped in violation of a technical barrier, and no system being exploited. The authors are creating and distributing content, not accessing protected systems.

**Van Buren v. United States (2021) settled the key question**: The Supreme Court held 6-3 that the "exceeds authorized access" provision does not criminalize using an authorized system for an unauthorized purpose (e.g., a police officer using a license-plate database for personal gain). The majority opinion explicitly protected journalism and academic research that involves collecting publicly available data or creating research accounts, even where this technically violates a platform's terms of service. This decision removed the most aggressive theory under which CFAA had previously been used against researchers and activists.

**The Aaron Swartz problem no longer applies as broadly**: The infamous over-prosecution of Aaron Swartz (2013) arose from downloading a large volume of academic journal articles from an institutional network in a way that violated terms of service and triggered rate-limiting systems — conduct that looks much more like exploiting a computer system than what the framework here involves. Even that case is now weaker precedent after Van Buren.

**Residual CFAA risk**: Near-zero. A pretextual CFAA investigation could not plausibly survive a motion to dismiss. The only conceivable scenario would be if research collection methods involved accessing restricted systems or bypassing paywalls at scale — nothing in the framework description suggests this. Use only publicly available sources; do not use institutional login credentials to access restricted databases for research you then redistribute.

**Recommendation**: No structural mitigation required for CFAA. Standard practice of using public-domain and openly licensed sources is sufficient.

## 2. Federal Election Law: Low Direct Risk, Elevated Interpretive Ambiguity

Federal election law creates two distinct concerns: election interference statutes that could theoretically apply to distribution of election documentation, and campaign finance law that applies to how the work is funded and framed.

### 2a. Election Interference Statutes

**52 U.S.C. § 10307 (Voting Rights Act, Section 11)**: Prohibits intimidation, threats, or coercion of voters. It also prohibits giving false information to affect the eligibility of voters. **Distributing research documentation about election security threats is the opposite of this**: it is informing voters and election protection practitioners about threats to the electoral process. This statute has no plausible application to a research and organizing framework.

**52 U.S.C. § 20511 (NVRA criminal penalties)**: Prohibits depriving residents of fair elections through fraudulent voter registrations or ballots. Again, inapplicable to a research framework.

**18 U.S.C. § 594 (Intimidation of voters)**: Prohibits intimidating or threatening voters. A framework analyzing election security threats does not constitute voter intimidation.

The Brennan Center's analysis confirms that election interference statutes are aimed at direct interference with voting or election administration — not at research, documentation, or civic organizing frameworks that help people understand and protect democratic processes.

**The critical distinction**: The framework *documents threats to elections and provides protective guidance*. This is the work of election protection lawyers, democracy researchers, and civil society organizations like Democracy Docket, CREW, and the Brennan Center itself. There is no credible theory under which this falls within election interference law.

### 2b. Campaign Finance Law

**The real risk zone**: If distribution is funded by an organization with 501(c)(3) status and the framework advocates for specific candidates or specific electoral outcomes in a manner that crosses the IRS bright line on "political campaign intervention," the sponsoring organization faces tax-exempt status jeopardy.

**What triggers the IRS prohibition**: Under the absolute prohibition on 501(c)(3) political campaign intervention, an organization that directly or indirectly participates in, or intervenes in, any political campaign on behalf of or in opposition to any candidate for elective public office risks revocation of exempt status and excise taxes under IRC § 4955.

**What does not trigger the prohibition**: Voter education, nonpartisan civic engagement, policy research, legislative advocacy (within the "substantial part" ceiling), get-out-the-vote drives conducted in a nonpartisan manner, and issue advocacy that does not expressly advocate for or against candidates.

**Assessment of the framework**: Based on the domain descriptions, the 35-domain framework is issue and institutional analysis — separation of powers, election security, judicial independence, voting rights. This is squarely within the safe harbor for nonpartisan civic education and policy research available to 501(c)(3)s. The framework does not endorse candidates or advocate for specific electoral outcomes beyond urging democratic participation and institutional protection.

**The watchlist**: Domain 37 (federal executive interference with the 2026 midterms) is the most politically charged domain. If its language advocates for specific electoral outcomes in a way that could be characterized as partisan electioneering, this domain creates the highest campaign finance risk in a 501(c)(3) distribution context. The A+37 Hybrid path's practice of routing Domain 37 specifically to election protection specialists rather than general audiences somewhat reduces this risk.

**Recommendation**: If distributing under a 501(c)(3) umbrella, review Domain 37 language to ensure it remains issue-advocacy framing (protecting democratic processes) rather than candidate advocacy. A 501(c)(4) has more latitude here.

## 3. Tradecraft Security: Protective Structures for Authors and Distributors

### 3a. Organizational Structure Options

**501(c)(3) — Public Charity or Private Foundation**
- Strongest reputation with institutional recipients (universities, community foundations, libraries)
- Absolute prohibition on candidate advocacy; substantial part test for lobbying
- Donor contributions are tax-deductible (advantage for fundraising)
- Form 990 is public; Schedule B (donors) is not required to be disclosed publicly to non-government entities
- *Best for*: Distribution to academic institutions, libraries, nonpartisan civic education channels, professional associations
- *Risk*: IRS enforcement attention under the current administration's pattern of targeting progressive organizations; 501(c)(3) status can be revoked, though this takes time and is subject to Tax Court challenge

**501(c)(4) — Social Welfare Organization**
- May engage in political activity as long as it is not the primary activity (under-50% test, though practice varies)
- More latitude for issue advocacy, including advocacy closely tied to electoral outcomes
- Contributions are not tax-deductible
- Schedule B donor disclosure is not required to be made public (confirmed in Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta, 2021)
- *Best for*: Distribution with an explicit civic engagement or election protection angle; more aggressive advocacy work
- *Risk*: Political activities may trigger § 527(f) tax on political expenditures; faces same targeting risk as (c)(3)s under DOJ investigation pattern

**LLC (single-member or multi-member, Delaware or Wyoming)**
- No political activity restrictions
- Anonymous formation possible: Delaware and Wyoming allow LLCs without naming beneficial owners in public filings
- Complete flexibility in activities and distribution
- No tax exemption; no donor tax deduction
- *Best for*: Distribution under a pseudonymous or anonymous publisher identity; maximum operational flexibility
- *Risk*: Subpoena in litigation can pierce organizational structure; no nonprofit reputational benefit

**Unincorporated Association or Individual Publication**
- Maximum simplicity, minimum overhead
- Anonymous distribution as an individual author is fully protected under McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission (1995)
- *Risk*: Personal liability exposure if the organization is treated as an unincorporated entity; no corporate liability shield

**Recommended structure**: A 501(c)(4) operating organization for distribution and institutional outreach, with a Delaware anonymous LLC as the publishing vehicle that licenses content to the 501(c)(4) for distribution. This separates the protected anonymity of authorship from the institutional credibility of the distributing organization. The combination is used in practice by serious political research operations.

### 3b. Anonymous and Pseudonymous Publishing

**Constitutional basis**: The Supreme Court has protected anonymous political speech in an unbroken line of decisions:
- *Talley v. California* (1960): Struck down a Los Angeles ordinance requiring handbills to identify their distributor. The Court noted that anonymous pamphlets have "played an important role in the progress of mankind" and that "persecuted groups and sects... throughout history have been able to criticize oppressive practices and laws either anonymously or not at all."
- *McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission* (1995): Struck down Ohio's requirement that campaign literature identify the author. Applied "exacting scrutiny" to anonymous speech restrictions. Specifically invoked the Federalist Papers as the exemplar of constitutionally protected anonymous political speech.
- *NAACP v. Alabama* (1958): Held that the state could not compel disclosure of NAACP membership lists because the right of free association includes the right to private group membership. Established associational privacy as a First Amendment right. Applied to protect organizers from government exposure during a period of state-sponsored repression.

These precedents directly protect Anya and collaborators' right to publish and distribute the framework anonymously or pseudonymously. A government attempt to compel disclosure of authorship would face strict scrutiny.

**Practical pseudonymous publishing measures**:
1. Use a pen name or organizational byline ("The Democratic Resilience Project") rather than personal names
2. Register any distribution website through a WHOIS privacy service (all major registrars; cost: included or ~$5/year) — this replaces registrant contact information with the registrar's proxy
3. Use a Delaware or Wyoming anonymous LLC as the formal publisher of record — the LLC's formation documents do not include beneficial owner names in public records (though beneficial ownership may be required in IRS filings; consult a tax attorney)
4. Host distribution infrastructure outside your primary professional accounts — separate email addresses, GitHub accounts, and distribution infrastructure
5. If distributing via Substack or similar platforms, use an account with a non-identifying name

### 3c. WHOIS Privacy and Domain Registration

WHOIS privacy services replace the registrant's personal information (name, address, phone) with a proxy provided by the registrar. Since ICANN's 2018 GDPR-era policy changes, this practice has become the default for privacy-conscious registrants. The proxy does not affect the legal ownership or operation of the domain.

**Limitations**: WHOIS privacy is not impermeable. A valid subpoena to the registrar can unmask the beneficial owner. It provides protection against unsophisticated adversaries and harassment — it does not provide protection against a federal grand jury subpoena. For that level of protection, the organizational structure (LLC, anonymous entity) is the relevant layer.

### 3d. Secure Distribution Channels

For direct communication with recipients:
- Signal for individual and small-group distribution — end-to-end encrypted, metadata-limited. Signal does not retain content or metadata in a form it can disclose in response to legal process.
- ProtonMail or Tutanota for email distribution to institutional recipients — encrypted at rest, servers outside US jurisdiction
- SecureDrop for anonymous submission if receiving documents from third parties
- Avoid: standard Gmail/Outlook for sensitive distribution; social media DMs which retain metadata

**Important**: Signal preserves message content securely, but the *existence* of a Signal conversation can be compelled from a recipient's device. Distribution via Signal is secure; it is not legally invisible.

## 4. Historical Precedent: Four Case Studies

### Case Study 1: NAACP v. Alabama (1958) — The Foundational Protection Model

In 1956, Alabama used state corporation law to demand the NAACP's membership lists — a transparent attempt to expose civil rights organizers to violence and economic retaliation. The NAACP refused and was held in contempt and fined $100,000. The Supreme Court unanimously reversed, holding that the First Amendment's freedom of speech and assembly protects private group membership from state compelled disclosure. The state must demonstrate a compelling justification before it can pierce associational privacy.

**Relevance**: The NAACP case is the precise constitutional analog for what Anya's network faces: a government hostile to the organizing work seeking to expose the identities of participants. The precedent directly protects:
- The right to distribute the framework without revealing author or recipient identities
- The right to maintain organizational membership lists privately
- The right to resist government subpoenas seeking to identify participants in civic associations

**Application**: NAACP v. Alabama, combined with *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta* (2021, where the Court struck down California's requirement that 501(c)(3)s disclose major donors to the state attorney general), establishes that donor and member identity disclosure can only be compelled upon a showing of a compelling government interest narrowly tailored to achieve it. A RICO or terrorism subpoena seeking to identify recipients of the framework would face this standard.

### Case Study 2: Open Society Foundations DOJ Investigation (2025) — The Current Threat Model

In September 2025, the DOJ directed multiple US Attorney offices to prepare investigations into the Open Society Foundations, listing potential charges including material support for terrorism, arson, wire fraud, and racketeering. The memo cited OSF's funding of organizations including the Sunrise Movement, Center for Third World Organizing, and Ruckus Society.

**What this tells us about the threat environment**:
1. The current DOJ is willing to use investigative tools against organizations engaged in entirely lawful advocacy work
2. The charges listed — material support for terrorism, RICO — are designed to generate chilling effects through the investigation itself, not to produce convictions
3. The "political violence" framing is deliberately vague to capture organizations whose only connection to violence is funding groups that were in the same vicinity as civil unrest
4. OSF immediately characterized the investigation as "politically motivated attacks on civil society, meant to silence speech the administration disagrees with"
5. Major law firms immediately identified the memo's "deliberately vague language" as designed to capture protected activity

**What happened**: As of the research date, no charges were filed against OSF. The investigation appears to have been more threat than substance. However, the investigation itself caused real costs: donor hesitation, partner organization anxiety, and diversion of legal resources.

**Application for distribution decisions**: The OSF investigation is the most relevant recent precedent for the risk profile of a high-visibility 35-domain research framework distribution. The mechanism of risk is not criminal conviction — it is pretextual investigation designed to produce chilling effects. The protective measures (anonymous publishing, organizational separation, secure distribution) are calibrated to reduce the investigative surface area, not to defend against charges that would not survive challenge.

### Case Study 3: SPLC Indictment (April 2026) — Pretextual Prosecution in Practice

On April 21, 2026, the DOJ indicted the Southern Poverty Law Center on charges including wire fraud, false statements, and conspiracy to commit money laundering, alleging that the SPLC secretly funneled over $3 million to individuals affiliated with extremist groups while publicly representing it was working to dismantle those groups. The charges relate to the SPLC's use of confidential informants.

**Why this matters for distribution risk**: Legal experts told CNN that the case "likely faces a number of major hurdles" given that "the theory under which it's premised runs counter to the reality of the SPLC's stated objectives." The SPLC's defense will include First Amendment arguments that the activities "are basically speech and association." Fidelity halted donor-advised fund grants to the SPLC following the indictment — the economic disruption preceded any conviction.

**The SPLC indictment is the clearest illustration of the current threat model**: charges that (a) are predicated on entirely legitimate civil society activities (maintaining confidential informants to monitor hate groups), (b) are framed as financial crimes to sidestep First Amendment scrutiny, (c) produce immediate operational damage through chilling effects on donors and partners, and (d) may not survive legal challenge but accomplish the disruption goal regardless.

**Application**: A framework distribution operation that publicly identifies its organizers, banks with major financial institutions, uses a high-visibility 501(c)(3), and maintains donor records in a single location is more exposed to the SPLC pattern than one that distributes pseudonymously, separates publishing from fundraising structures, and uses distribution infrastructure that minimizes centralized data retention.

### Case Study 4: The Federalist Papers / Talley-McIntyre Line — The Deep Historical Protection

When Hamilton, Madison, and Jay wrote the Federalist Papers under the pseudonym "Publius" in 1787-1788, they were distributing a political research and advocacy framework arguing for a specific form of democratic governance against powerful opposition. They were publishing without names because of the risk of political retaliation and because they wanted the arguments judged on their merits.

The Supreme Court in *McIntyre* cited this directly: anonymous pamphlets "have played an important role in the progress of mankind." In *Talley*, the Court noted that Manuel Talley's distribution of unsigned handbills urging the boycott of businesses that refused to hire minorities was constitutionally protected even though it violated a Los Angeles ordinance.

**The deep precedent**: What Anya's framework represents — research, analysis, and civic organizing guidance distributed to advance democratic values — is precisely what the anonymous speech doctrine has protected for 250 years. Courts have consistently held that this protection applies with greatest force when the speaker faces "fear of economic or official retaliation." The current political environment satisfies that condition.

## 5. Risk Differentiation by Distribution Path

### Path A: Immediate Broad Distribution (All 35 Domains, Single Wave)

**Risk profile: MEDIUM at launch without protective structures; LOW-MEDIUM with structures**

**Elevated risk factors**:
- Single high-visibility launch event creates maximum investigative surface area at one moment
- Domain 37 (federal executive interference with midterms) in a general distribution creates the highest single-domain political sensitivity
- Broad institutional outreach under a named organizational identity maximizes the profile that attracts DOJ attention
- A simultaneous send to election-protection organizations, academic institutions, and general public creates the widest recipient network — and potentially a wider subpoena target pool

**Mitigating factors**:
- Breadth of distribution to legitimate institutional recipients (universities, nonprofits, law firms) establishes the work's civic credibility and makes a "criminal enterprise" framing implausible
- Large recipient pool means any attempt to suppress distribution would face immediate First Amendment challenge from multiple institutional actors
- Core protected political speech; no viable criminal theory survives challenge

**Recommended protective measures for Path A**:
- Publish under organizational pseudonym from day one; do not attach personal names to distribution
- Use anonymous/WHOIS-protected domain for any associated website
- Distribute via email through a non-personally-identified account
- If a named organization is used, use a 501(c)(4) or LLC rather than an unregistered association or personal author identity

### Path A+37 Hybrid: General 34-Domain Distribution + Targeted Domain 37 to Election Organizations

**Risk profile: LOW-MEDIUM overall; the recommended path for minimum legal risk**

**Why this path has a better risk profile**:
- Staggered launch reduces single-event investigative surface area
- Domain 37 reaching election protection specialists with tailored framing means its recipients are institutions (Democracy Docket, ACLU, Brennan Center) with in-house legal teams capable of First Amendment challenge
- The separation between the general civic framework and the specifically election-focused material reflects the legally meaningful distinction between 501(c)(3) educational work and 501(c)(4) electoral advocacy
- Targeted institutional distribution creates a record that Domain 37 was sent to *election protection lawyers* (a clearly legitimate purpose) rather than a general political audience

**Additional advantage**: If Domain 37 creates any legal exposure, it is isolated to a separate distribution event with a documented legitimate purpose. This compartmentalization is legally meaningful.

**Recommended protective measures for Path A+37**:
- Same pseudonymous publishing approach as Path A
- For the Domain 37 send: document the institutional nature of recipients (legal organizations, civil society groups) in the distribution record — this supports a "lawful professional advice" characterization if challenged
- Consider having Domain 37 reviewed by an election law attorney before distribution (ACLU, Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights, or a private election law firm with progressive clients)

### Path B: Continued Updates, 14-28 Day Delay

**Risk profile: LOWEST for the period of delay; the risk profile at launch is equivalent to Path A**

**Why Path B has the lowest near-term risk**:
- No distribution event means no investigative surface area during the delay period
- Additional domain development time allows for legal review before any launch
- The delay gives time to establish the organizational structure (LLC, pseudonym infrastructure) properly before distributing

**Why the delay does not eliminate risk**:
- The legal environment in 14-28 days is not demonstrably better than today; May 2026 is not a lower-scrutiny environment
- The framework's core content — political analysis, election security documentation — remains in the same legal risk category regardless of when it is distributed
- Delay means the spring legislative windows and May-June advocacy opportunities (including the May 30 DOJ consent decree deadline noted in PROJECTS.md) are partially or fully missed

**Recommended protective measures for Path B**:
- Use the delay period to establish proper organizational structure before launch
- Commission legal review of Domain 37 specifically during the delay period
- If Path B is chosen, set it up as a deliberate legal-preparation period rather than an indefinite delay

## 6. Recommended Protective Structures: Implementation Priorities

In priority order, from highest-impact to lowest:

**Priority 1 — Pseudonymous publishing identity (Cost: ~0; Time: 2 hours)**
Create a stable organizational pen name or project name that is used consistently across all distribution. The author identity needs to be separable from personal professional identities. This is the most effective single protective measure and is constitutionally robust.

**Priority 2 — Anonymous LLC or WHOIS privacy for any web infrastructure (Cost: $50-200; Time: 4 hours)**
If creating a website, email list, or distribution channel, register it through an anonymous LLC or WHOIS privacy service. Delaware or Wyoming anonymous LLCs can be formed for ~$200. This prevents the low-effort adversary tactic of using domain registration records to identify authors.

**Priority 3 — 501(c)(4) organizational home for distribution (Cost: ~$2,000 in formation; Time: 4-8 weeks for IRS processing)**
A 501(c)(4) provides liability protection, political activity flexibility, and the reputational credibility of an organized entity. If distribution is time-sensitive (Path A or A+37), this step can be done in parallel with initial distribution under the pseudonymous identity. The 501(c)(4) is the recommended long-term organizational home.

**Priority 4 — Encrypted distribution channels for sensitive recipients (Cost: 0; Time: 1 hour setup)**
Use Signal or ProtonMail for distribution to individual recipients who face elevated risk. Use standard email for institutional recipients who have their own security infrastructure. Do not use Google Workspace accounts that retain metadata for bulk institutional distribution if personal privacy is a concern.

**Priority 5 — Legal counsel on retainer for a named organization (Cost: $1,500-5,000 for initial consultation and retainer)**
An election law and First Amendment attorney on retainer is the last-resort protection. Organizations like the National Lawyers Guild, ACLU, and First Amendment Coalition provide legal support. For a named organization, proactively establishing a relationship before a legal challenge is more useful than finding counsel after a subpoena arrives.

## 7. Conclusion

The 35-domain democratic renewal framework, as described, is core political speech: research, analysis, and civic organizing guidance aimed at protecting democratic institutions. It is not in any plausible legal category that creates criminal liability for its authors under currently operative law. The CFAA does not apply. Election interference statutes do not apply. The long constitutional tradition of anonymous political speech — running from the Federalist Papers through Talley, McIntyre, and NAACP v. Alabama — directly protects its distribution.

The elevated risk in the 2025-2026 environment is not legal invalidity of these protections. It is the documented pattern of the current DOJ using investigation, indictment, and the threat of prosecution as tools of disruption against civil society organizations engaged in entirely protected work. The OSF investigation and SPLC indictment are not evidence that the law has changed — they are evidence that the law will be tested.

The protective measures recommended here are designed for that environment: they reduce the investigative surface area, invoke constitutional protections that courts have consistently upheld, and separate authorial identity from organizational identity in ways that make pretextual investigation more procedurally difficult. They do not eliminate risk — nothing does in this environment — but they move the framework into a legal posture that the most aggressive adversary would have significant difficulty attacking without triggering immediate First Amendment challenge from the institutional recipients themselves.

**Final recommendation on path selection**: From a purely legal risk standpoint, Path A+37 Hybrid with pseudonymous publishing and a 501(c)(4) organizational structure is the lowest-risk profile for immediate distribution. Path B is lowest-risk during the delay period but does not reduce the distribution-event risk profile when launch eventually occurs.
