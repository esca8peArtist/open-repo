## PART 1: DOMAIN 41 — CONSUMER FINANCIAL ARCHITECTURE AND DEMOCRATIC EQUITY

### Research Scope Summary

Domain 41 establishes that the U.S. consumer financial system functions as infrastructure for political exclusion through three documented pathways: cognitive bandwidth depletion from financial precarity, structural suppression of wealth-based civic mobilization through the racial wealth gap, and the removal of democratic accountability over $10-trillion institutional power through CFPB dismantling. The democratic-design angle is not that predatory lending is economically unjust — it is that the financial architecture is structured to extract from the same communities it structurally disqualifies from political participation, and the accountability mechanism that could correct this has been deliberately removed.

Estimated word count target: 6,500–7,500 words (production document currently ~5,800; depth-extension work needed in Sections 3–5)
Citation target: 50–60 (current document: 36; 15–25 additional sources to integrate)
Estimated research hours for complete production: 40–50 hours total (outline + deep research + writing + QA)

## PART 2: DOMAIN 43 — SPATIAL DEMOCRACY / HOUSING ARCHITECTURE AND POLITICAL POWER

### Research Scope Summary

Domain 43 establishes that American residential zoning architecture is a spatial democratic suppression mechanism — not through legislative intent in its current form, but through a design inheritance that has been empirically confirmed as racially motivated (Sahn, Journal of Politics, 2025) and has never been dismantled. The democratic-design angle is distinct from housing instability (Domain 47): it operates on stably-housed populations through the spatial segregation of political constituencies, the destruction of neighborhood political infrastructure through gentrification, the structural time deficit imposed by housing cost burden, and the systematic override of local democratic outcomes through ALEC-model state preemption laws. Housing architecture is a precondition for civic participation in the same structural sense that voting rights are.
