## 2. Phase 2 Expansion Prioritization — Top Three Domain Candidates

The 36-domain framework's current coverage is strong across electoral integrity, war powers, prosecutorial weaponization, higher education, immigration enforcement, healthcare access, AI governance, epistemic infrastructure, and drug policy. Based on a review of existing domain files and current May 2026 developments, three domains stand out as high-value gaps:

### Domain Candidate 1: Labor Rights, Worker Organizing, and the NLRB Crisis (HIGHEST PRIORITY)

**The gap**: Domain 17 (Labor) in the original framework addresses worker dislocation and wage effects of trade policy as a downstream consequence of tariff policy. It does not address the institutional infrastructure of labor rights — the NLRB as a democratic accountability mechanism, the constitutional right to organize as a democratic participation right, or the attack on collective bargaining as an attack on the only democratic power mechanism available to workers without political access. This is a significant omission in a framework focused on democratic exclusion architecture.

**What happened that makes this urgent**:
- In January 2025, Trump fired NLRB member Gwynne Wilcox in an unprecedented action, eliminating the board's quorum. The board lacked quorum for 345 days. During that period, unfair labor practice cases — covering illegal firings of union organizers — could not receive final decisions. Workers who had been illegally fired for organizing were left waiting for nearly a year.
- The NLRB's quorum was restored only in December 2025, when the Senate confirmed Trump's nominees. The restored board will only maintain quorum through August 2026 when member Prouty's term expires — creating a second quorum crisis window.
- In *Trump v. Slaughter*, the Supreme Court's conservative majority signaled it may rule that presidents can fire independent agency officials at will, which would functionally eliminate NLRB independence and extend the quorum-destruction tactic into a permanent structural feature.
- NLRB-overseen union elections fell in 2025 amid the attacks. California AB 288 (2026) responded by expanding state labor board jurisdiction to cover NLRB gaps — but this is a stopgap, not a solution.
- 44 states have laws barring cities from enacting local minimum wage or labor standards — state-level preemption as a democratic accountability suppression mechanism.

**Why this fits the democratic design framework**: The right to organize is a democratic right. Workers without collective bargaining have no effective political voice in the economic decisions that most affect their daily lives. The NLRB's destruction-by-quorum is an attack on the institutional infrastructure of labor democracy, analytically parallel to the attack on CISA (election security) and HHS (drug scheduling). This domain would be the labor rights complement to Domain 39 (immigration enforcement) and Domain 41b (disability rights) — institutional infrastructure attacks on democratic participation for specific communities.

**Research scope**: 4,500-5,500 words. Four sections: (a) NLRB institutional design and its democratic accountability function; (b) the quorum crisis — timeline, case backlog, worker impacts; (c) *Trump v. Slaughter* and the *Humphrey's Executor* threat to independent agency independence; (d) state preemption of local labor standards as democratic exclusion. Reform architecture: independent NLRB appointment process, statutory quorum requirement with mandatory interim appointment mechanism, sectoral bargaining framework.

**Primary sources**: Center for American Progress NLRB report, Economic Policy Institute preemption map, OnLaborBlog, HR Dive quorum reporting, Congress.gov bill tracking for the NLRA restoration bill.

Sources: [CAP: NLRB-Overseen Union Elections Fell in 2025](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/nlrb-overseen-union-elections-fell-in-2025-amid-trump-administration-attacks/) | [EPI: Workers' Rights Preemption Map](https://www.epi.org/preemption-map/)

### Domain Candidate 2: Climate Policy Rollback as Democratic Design Failure (HIGH PRIORITY)

**The gap**: The existing framework's Domain 15 (Environment/Climate) covers the structural design questions of environmental governance — NEPA, EPA authority, the Clean Air Act architecture. What it does not cover, and what has become dramatically more acute since the framework's original design, is the specific democratic design failure of the climate rollbacks: that the scientific consensus and the democratic majority supporting climate action have been systematically overridden through administrative capture of the regulatory process. This is analytically parallel to DEA regulatory capture of drug scheduling (Domain 42).

**What happened that makes this urgent**:
- On February 12, 2026, the EPA finalized the rescission of the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding — the first time since the Clean Air Act that the EPA has formally retracted a scientific determination that was legally valid and scientifically reaffirmed. The National Academy of Sciences disputed the EPA's justification. A federal court found that the Trump EPA violated federal law in forming the scientific group that prepared the report used to justify the rescission.
- NEPA regulations have been replaced with non-binding guidance. Federal agencies have begun shortening or eliminating public comment periods for environmental reviews — removing the primary democratic participation mechanism for environmental decision-making.
- The Appalachian Voices analysis (May 8, 2026) documents that "permitting reform" language in 2026 legislation is being used to eliminate environmental review requirements that are the structural basis for public participation in infrastructure decisions.
- The endangerment finding rescission would have avoided $87 billion in climate damages annually had it been maintained; the rollback is projected to cause at least 12,000 more premature deaths and 8.5 million more asthma attacks.

**Why this fits the democratic design framework**: The Endangerment Finding is not merely a regulatory document — it is the legal translation of scientific consensus into policy authority. Rescinding it is not a policy disagreement; it is the administrative overriding of the epistemic infrastructure of democratic climate governance. The public comment periods being eliminated were the mechanisms by which communities could participate in decisions about infrastructure that directly affects their health. This is a democratic design failure in the same analytical sense as DEA regulatory capture: the institutional structures that translated democratic will and scientific evidence into policy have been systematically disabled.

**Research scope**: 4,000-5,000 words. Four sections: (a) Endangerment Finding rescission — legal basis, scientific dispute, court challenge; (b) NEPA dismantlement as elimination of democratic participation in environmental decisions; (c) EPA budget and enforcement capacity cuts as capacity destruction; (d) the international comparative record — how other democracies have maintained climate policy accountability structures under political pressure (EU Green Deal governance, UK Climate Change Act, Canada's climate accountability framework).

**Primary sources**: EPA Federal Register notices, Harvard EELP Climate Rollback Tracker, Earthjustice litigation docket, WRI endangerment finding analysis, Appalachian Voices permitting reporting, The Regulatory Review endangerment analysis.

Sources: [EPA: Final Rule Rescission of Endangerment Finding](https://www.epa.gov/regulations-emissions-vehicles-and-engines/final-rule-rescission-greenhouse-gas-endangerment) | [WRI: EPA's Endangerment Finding Repeal, Explained](https://www.wri.org/insights/endangerment-finding-repeal-explained) | [The Regulatory Review: EPA's Repeal Will Cost Society](https://www.theregreview.org/2026/04/21/adler-welty-epas-repeal-of-the-endangerment-finding-will-cost-society/) | [Appalachian Voices: Permitting Reform and Environmental Rollbacks](https://appvoices.org/2026/05/08/permitting-reform-and-environmental-rollbacks/)

### Domain Candidate 3: Science and Research Policy — Federal Funding Weaponization as Epistemic Attack (MEDIUM-HIGH PRIORITY)

**The gap**: Domain 27 (Higher Education) covers the four-track attack on universities — federal funding leverage, DEI prohibition, student visa revocations, and accreditation weaponization. What it does not cover is the federal research funding architecture as a distinct democratic institution: the NIH, NSF, ARPA-H, and DOE national labs as the infrastructure through which democratic societies produce the evidence base for policy. When that infrastructure is politically captured or destroyed, the downstream effect is that democratic deliberation about policy operates without an evidence base — which is a democratic design failure independent of the higher education attack.

**What makes this urgent in May 2026**:
- NIH grant terminations have been documented in the hundreds, with peer-reviewed grants eliminated for keyword triggers (DEI, climate, transgender, vaccine hesitancy) rather than scientific merit. This is not budget cutting — it is content-based suppression of research.
- NSF has been directed to prioritize research that advances "American competitiveness" in ways that science policy analysts have documented as excluding basic research and social science from funding eligibility.
- The brain drain documented in Domain 27 (ERC applications from US researchers tripled) is partly a research funding story, not only a visa story. Researchers are leaving because the federal funding infrastructure for independent research is degrading.
- The DOGE-directed NIH restructuring has eliminated entire research offices and combined institutes in ways that have no scientific justification but eliminate research programs that produced politically inconvenient findings.

**Why this fits the democratic design framework**: Domain 43 (Epistemic Infrastructure) covers the media and disinformation dimensions of the epistemic crisis. A complementary domain on federal research policy would cover the upstream epistemic infrastructure: the institutions that produce the evidence on which democratic deliberation depends. When the federal government eliminates funding for vaccine safety research, climate impact research, and health equity research, the democratic process operates without the evidence base those decisions require. This is epistemic infrastructure destruction at the production level, distinct from Domain 43's focus on the distribution and disinformation level.

**Research scope**: 3,500-4,500 words. Three sections: (a) NIH grant terminations — documented pattern, keyword triggers, constitutional challenges; (b) NSF and ARPA-H restructuring as politically directed capacity destruction; (c) the international comparative record and brain drain data (complement and extend Domain 27's data). Reform architecture: statutory independence for research funding decisions, restoration of peer review as the sole eligibility criterion, international cooperation frameworks that insulate US research from political disruption.

**Primary sources**: Nature reporting on NIH terminations, Science magazine NSF analysis, Domain 27 brain drain data (extend), AAAS policy coverage, ACLU constitutional challenges to content-based grant terminations.
