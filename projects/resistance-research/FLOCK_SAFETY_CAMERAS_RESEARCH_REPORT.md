## Executive Summary

Flock Safety has built the largest private license plate surveillance network in American history — over 100,000 cameras across 7,000+ law enforcement networks, scanning billions of license plates per month. The company markets its cameras as stolen-vehicle and crime-solving tools to municipalities, school districts, HOAs, and private businesses. What those buyers are not told is that every camera added to the network is accessible to 4,800+ law enforcement agencies nationwide, including federal immigration enforcement, through a system that allows cross-jurisdiction plate searches with minimal oversight.

The documented harms are severe and specific. Flock cameras have been used to track a woman who sought abortion care in Illinois. They have been used to surveil No Kings protests, pro-Palestinian activism, and animal rights organizing. They have been used to run immigration enforcement searches on school district cameras installed for campus safety — 733,000 searches in a single month at Alvin ISD near Houston, including 620 immigration enforcement searches. U.S. Customs and Border Protection was given access to Illinois municipal cameras through a "pilot program" that Flock itself claimed its leadership was unaware of. In cities where ICE lacked a direct Flock contract, local police ran plate searches on ICE's behalf.

The resistance is organized and working. As of late June 2026, 53 municipalities across 20 states have ended or rejected Flock contracts — 38 of those decisions in the six months following the Trump administration's immigration enforcement intensification. Amazon's Ring cancelled its planned Flock integration in February 2026 after a Super Bowl ad triggered a public backlash. Colorado enacted a warrant requirement for federal ALPR data sharing. Washington state banned using ALPR data for immigration enforcement and at places of worship. A California class action seeks $2,500 per violation across 1.6 million unauthorized searches.

The critical resistance finding: this is winnable at the local level. The city council is the operative terrain in most jurisdictions. Flock contracts require council approval for renewal, and a majority of council members willing to vote no — or willing to direct restrictions and audits — is sufficient. More than 80 localities have made that choice. Houston has not yet acted, but the infrastructure for the campaign exists and the advocacy window is now.

Houston has approximately 3,000 Flock cameras across the greater metro, a $6.39 million city contract covering 318 HPD cameras, and documented school district cameras searched for immigration enforcement purposes. HPD officers logged search justifications including "ASDF," "donut," and blank fields. The Harris County Commissioners Court renewed its separate 480-camera contract on May 28, 2026 (4-0 vote; Judge Lina Hidalgo abstained, characterizing the process as rushed). The HPD city contract — structured as one year plus four annual options — is in its final renewal window. The moment for action is now.

## Section 1: Flock Safety — Company Overview and Deployment Scope

### 1.1 Corporate Structure and Funding

Flock Safety was founded in 2017 in Atlanta, Georgia by Garrett Langley (CEO), Matt Feury, and Paige Todd. The company's founding pitch was narrow: affordable automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for HOAs and small municipalities that could not afford traditional surveillance systems. Seven years later it has become a dominant infrastructure player in American law enforcement technology.

**Funding**: Approximately $957 million across nine rounds as of mid-2026. The largest single round was a $275 million Series F (March 2025) led by Andreessen Horowitz — which also backed Amazon's Ring. Other major investors include Tiger Global, Founders Fund, Kleiner Perkins, Matrix Partners, and Y Combinator. **Current valuation: approximately $8.4 billion.** Andreessen Horowitz and Founders Fund have both publicly supported the Trump administration's deregulatory agenda — a conflict of interest not disclosed in Flock's municipal marketing.

**What Flock cameras capture**: License plate numbers, vehicle make, model, color, and "vehicle fingerprint" data (bumper stickers, damage, accessories). The fingerprint data enables vehicle identification even when plates are obscured or changed.

### 1.2 Network Architecture: Why Scale Matters

The critical design feature is the reciprocity model. Agencies that share locally-collected plate data with the national network gain access to all plates captured by every other participating agency. Flock training materials describe this as "You show me yours, I'll show you mine." The practical consequence: a small city's 20 cameras automatically become searchable by thousands of agencies once that city enables national sharing — a fact frequently not disclosed during the municipal sales process.

**Scale as of June 2026:**
- 100,000+ cameras (crossed this threshold June 2026)
- 7,000+ law enforcement networks with access
- 4,800+ law enforcement agencies able to query the national system
- 10,000+ cameras in Texas alone
- 75% of Flock law enforcement customers use the National Lookup Tool
- Billions of plate reads per month

**Expansion beyond ALPRs**: Flock has integrated with Amazon Ring (now cancelled) and launched "Drones as Automated Security" for commercial clients. The drone system enables vehicle pursuit beyond property boundaries — a qualitative escalation from passive plate capture to active mobile tracking.

**The February 2026 contract terms changes** are the most important contractual development of 2026. Flock rewrote its standard municipal contract in four ways that significantly increase risk for municipalities:
1. Removed the sentence "Flock does not own and shall not sell Customer Data" — no guarantee against data sale
2. Granted Flock "exclusive right to determine and control the method, timing, format, and medium" of customer data access — municipalities receive degraded copies, not raw data
3. Created perpetual, non-expiring licensing rights to surveillance data that survive contract termination — the company keeps the data forever
4. Moved disputes to mandatory private arbitration under Georgia law, eliminating municipal access to state courts

Contracts signed before February 2026 under prior terms may contain stronger protections. The removal of the data sale prohibition may constitute a material change sufficient to support termination for cause at renewal.

**The Illinois CBP Pilot**: An Illinois state audit found that Flock had allowed U.S. Customs and Border Protection to access state cameras through a "pilot program" that Flock leadership claimed to be "unaware of." CBP had warrantless access to Illinois plates without any Illinois official's knowledge.

**Mountain View**: 250+ unapproved agencies conducted approximately 600,000 queries of the city's cameras over 12 months ending December 2025.

**Dayton**: Audit revealed 7,100 immigration enforcement searches of city cameras — explicitly prohibited under the city's own written policy. The searches continued for months before discovery.

**Evanston**: When the city canceled its Flock contract and ordered cameras removed, Flock subsequently reinstalled some cameras without the city's permission.

**Syracuse**: LPR data searched 4.4 million times by out-of-state police and shared with ICE despite prior public assurances to residents.

## Section 2: Legal Landscape

### 2.1 Federal Constitutional Framework — Weak Baseline

The federal constitutional floor is bare until the Fourth Circuit rules.

**Schmidt v. Norfolk (E.D. Va., January 27, 2026)**: U.S. District Court Judge Mark Davis granted summary judgment to the City of Norfolk, holding that 176 Flock cameras scanning plaintiffs' vehicles 475 and 325 times over four months did not constitute a Fourth Amendment search. The court held the cameras did not capture "enough information to catalogue citizens' movements in their entirety." The court explicitly left open a density-dependent threshold: ALPR surveillance "could become too intrusive" as camera density increases.

**Fourth Circuit appeal (Case No. 26-1227)**: Institute for Justice (plaintiff's counsel) is appealing. Amicus briefs filed April 20, 2026: ACLU, ACLU of Virginia, and Electronic Frontier Foundation (arguing *Carpenter v. United States*'s mosaic theory applies to ALPR aggregation); EPIC (separate brief); Cato Institute (on behalf of plaintiffs); New Civil Liberties Alliance. A Fourth Circuit ruling would bind Virginia, Maryland, North Carolina, South Carolina, and West Virginia — and would be the most persuasive precedent in every other circuit.

**Carpenter v. United States (2018, SCOTUS)**: Held that government access to seven days of cell-site location data constitutes a Fourth Amendment search requiring a warrant. No court has yet squarely applied *Carpenter* to hold Flock-scale ALPR networks unconstitutional, but the analytical framework is available and multiple amicus briefs are pressing it.

Until a circuit-level ruling, the operative terrain is state law and local policy — not federal constitutional litigation.

### 2.2 California Class Action Litigation

A parallel litigation front has opened in California that may prove more immediately consequential than constitutional challenges.

**Gibbs Mura class action (Feb. 26, 2026)**: Oakland law firm Gibbs Mura, co-filed with Milberg PLLC, filed in San Francisco Superior Court alleging Flock Safety violated California privacy laws by sharing California driver data with out-of-state and federal agencies without authorization. Key facts alleged: 1.6 million searches of SFPD's Flock database by outside agencies over seven months; 1 million+ additional unauthorized searches tied to Los Altos. An amended complaint was filed April 3, 2026. The lawsuit seeks $2,500 per violation — representing potential aggregate liability of billions of dollars if class-certified.

**Home Depot class action (Schmierer v. Home Depot USA, N.D. Cal., Case No. 3:26-cv-03967)**: A separate class action alleges that Home Depot's parking lot Flock cameras constitute a "covert surveillance operation" sharing customer vehicle data with law enforcement nationwide without disclosure. This case extends liability to **private businesses** using Flock — not just government clients — and could fundamentally change the risk calculus for commercial Flock deployments.

## Section 3: Removal and Resistance Options

### 3.1 What Has Worked: The 53-City Model

As of late June 2026, 53 municipalities across 20 states have ended or rejected Flock contracts — 38 decisions in the six months of 2026. The pattern of successful campaigns is consistent.

**Records first, always.** Every successful campaign obtained audit logs through public records requests before any public organizing. Audit logs reveal which outside agencies searched local cameras, how many searches lacked legitimate purpose, and whether federal agencies accessed data in violation of city policy. The logs are the political tool — they allow council members to act in response to documented evidence, not in opposition to "public safety."

**The audit trigger.** Dayton: 7,100 immigration searches violating city policy. Mountain View: 600,000 unauthorized searches by 250 agencies. Syracuse: 4.4 million searches including ICE sharing. In Houston: "ASDF," "donut," and blank search justifications. The audit is both the advocacy tool and the political protection for sympathetic council members.

**Council majority math.** Flock contracts require council approval at renewal. Cancellation examples: Ithaca (unanimous), Cleveland (Safety Committee 3-1), El Cerrito (3-2), Lockhart TX (6-1), Denver (unanimous), Oshkosh (7-0), Bandera TX (unanimous). The target is a majority willing to vote against renewal or direct audits and restrictions.

**Coalition breadth.** No successful campaign was won by a single organization. The Austin cancellation involved 30+ groups: civil liberties, immigrant rights, reproductive rights, faith communities, neighborhood associations, tech workers. Political diversity matters: Flock opposition has crossed partisan lines in libertarian-leaning communities uncomfortable with surveillance infrastructure.

**Media partnership.** Local investigative journalism has been the accelerant: Texas Standard (HPD "donut" searches), The 74 (Alvin ISD), Mountain View Voice (unauthorized access), NPR and LAist (national unauthorized access). If you have documented misuse evidence, a reporter covering this beat is a strategic partner.

### 3.2 Legal Pathways

**Contract termination review.** The February 2026 terms changes — removal of data sale prohibition, perpetual licensing, mandatory Georgia arbitration — may constitute material contract changes at renewal. Evanston's experience (Flock reinstalled cameras after cancellation) provides potential breach-of-contract grounds. Engage a municipal attorney to review exit clause triggers and termination-for-cause provisions.

**State public records law (Texas).** Under Chapter 552, Texas Government Code (Texas Public Information Act), the following are public records: Flock contracts and amendments; data-sharing agreements; ALPR search audit logs; internal communications about federal access; HPD-ICE communications about Flock; camera location lists; usage policies. File simultaneously with the police department Records Division and the City Secretary's Office. Response deadline: 10 business days for routine business records; up to 45 days when third-party notification is required. Ready-to-file template in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md`.

**Fourth Amendment litigation (watch *Schmidt v. Norfolk*, Case No. 26-1227).** Monitor for the Fourth Circuit ruling. If the court holds that dense ALPR networks constitute Fourth Amendment searches, it creates grounds for injunctive challenges in Fourth Circuit states and provides the strongest persuasive precedent elsewhere. Track at: [ACLU case page](https://www.aclu.org/cases/schmidt-v-norfolk).

**California model — CIPA litigation.** The Gibbs Mura class action theory — $2,500 per violation under California privacy law — is specific to California, but similar consumer privacy laws exist in other states. The Home Depot precedent, if it advances, could create commercial liability for private businesses anywhere that has equivalent state privacy statutes.

**State legislative pathway (2027 for Texas).** No Texas ALPR bill gained traction in 2026. The 2027 legislative session is the next opportunity. The Washington and Colorado models are the strongest templates. Contact the ACLU of Texas legislative advocacy team.

### 3.3 Policy Solutions (No Contract Change Required)

Any of the following can be directed by city council resolution without terminating the existing Flock contract:

1. **Disable the National Lookup Tool** — prevents Houston cameras from being queried by ICE and out-of-state agencies immediately
2. **Mandatory documented justification** for every Flock search, subject to supervisory review
3. **Immigration enforcement prohibition** — direct HPD not to share Flock data with ICE, CBP, or any federal immigration agency except pursuant to a valid judicial warrant
4. **Quarterly public audit reports** on Flock search activity
5. **Data retention reduction** — negotiate amendment reducing retention from 30 days to 7 days
6. **Transparency ordinance** — require public disclosure of all Flock camera locations

### 3.4 Technical Countermeasures

Technical approaches reduce individual exposure but do not address the systemic problem. They must be evaluated against local law. Use only alongside — never instead of — policy advocacy.

**Legal vehicle-fingerprint disruption (Texas):**
- Rotate bumper stickers, car magnets, and window decals periodically
- Remove dealership frames and stickers
- Rotate removable accessories (roof racks, bike racks, hitch covers)
- Request a replacement license plate at the county clerk (new plate changes searchable fingerprint data)
- Switch to specialty or personalized plates

**Illegal in Texas**: Texas Transportation Code prohibits tinted plate covers, reflective or IR-blocking sprays, and any physical obstruction of plate readability. Illegal countermeasures undermine legitimate advocacy and expose drivers to traffic stops.

**DeFlock route mapping**: [maps.deflock.org](https://maps.deflock.org) is a crowdsourced, open-source ALPR camera map with 336,000+ cameras globally. The companion route planner generates privacy-optimized routes avoiding known cameras. Available without user tracking, analytics, or ads. Android app via Google Play. Report Houston cameras at [deflock.org/report](https://deflock.org/report).

**Home surveillance alternatives**: Since Ring cancelled its Flock integration, the integration risk is reduced — but Ring's law enforcement portal still exists. Privacy-preserving alternatives: TP-Link Tapo, Reolink NVR, Lorex NVR (all support local storage without law enforcement API integrations).

### 3.5 Organizing Strategies

**The three-phase sequence:**

Phase 1 — Information gathering (before any public campaign): File FOIA/PIRA requests for audit logs, contracts, and data-sharing agreements. Map which council members have any existing position. Identify one sympathetic council member willing to introduce a resolution. Connect with ACLU of Texas and the No ALPRs coalition for technical assistance.

Phase 2 — Coalition building and public record construction: Brief coalition partners on the audit log findings. Build the factual narrative around Houston-specific findings. Identify media contacts with existing Flock beat coverage (Texas Standard, Houston Public Media, Houston Chronicle). Do not go public before the records are in hand.

Phase 3 — Council engagement and public pressure: Deliver demand letters before the renewal vote. Organize Public Safety Committee testimony. Have a friendly council member introduce the model resolution. Coordinate with DeFlock to publish the Houston camera map as a visual demonstration of surveillance scope.

### 4.2 HPD Misuse: The Documented Record

A Texas Standard investigation documented that HPD officers use the Flock system "like a search engine, like Google, than an official law enforcement tool." Specific findings:
- HPD logged hundreds of thousands of search calls in the past year
- Officers entered "ASDF," "donut," "random checks," or left the purpose field blank
- HPD's governing ALPR policy dates to **2015** — before Flock's existence — and contains no documentation requirements

This documentation is the foundation for the audit demand: these records are public under the Texas Public Information Act and their content supports the political case for restrictions.

## Section 5: Integration with Broader Resistance Infrastructure

### 5.1 Connection to ICE Victim Support Networks

The existing `ice-victim-support-networks-guide.md` and `houston-ice-victim-support.md` cover immediate response networks, Know Your Rights materials, and rapid-response resources for immigration enforcement encounters. Flock is the upstream data collection infrastructure that feeds the enforcement actions those guides respond to.

**Additions to immigrant community safety guidance:**
- **Assume vehicle movements are recorded.** Any travel near Houston schools, businesses, and residential neighborhoods passes Flock cameras. Data is retained 30 days and accessible to 4,800+ agencies. Even after contract cancellation, Flock's perpetual licensing clause means the company retains the data.
- **The ICE-by-proxy mechanism.** ICE does not require its own Flock contract. HPD searches on ICE's behalf — documented in Kyle TX, Dayton OH, and structurally incentivized by Houston's April 2026 ICE cooperation amendment.
- **The 30-day window is past.** Data from the last month is already in the network. Contract cancellation does not retrieve it.
- **DeFlock for route planning.** [maps.deflock.org](https://maps.deflock.org) identifies routes avoiding known camera clusters. Practical harm-reduction while systemic advocacy proceeds.

### 5.2 Connection to Illegal Surveillance Camera Removal Guide

The `illegal-surveillance-camera-removal-guide.md` covers the reasonable expectation of privacy framework, audio consent law, and demand-letter processes for illegal private surveillance cameras. Flock cameras on public streets operate under a different legal framework: the challenge is not to a specific camera's placement (public-space video capture is generally lawful) but to the mass aggregation program.

**Three connection points:**
- **Audio recording law**: Texas is a one-party consent state (Tex. Penal Code § 16.02). Audio-capable Flock cameras do not violate Texas wiretap law — but federal ECPA analysis still applies to any audio capture of in-vehicle conversations.
- **Private spaces adjacent to public spaces**: A Flock camera angled to capture the interior of a private driveway, gated courtyard, or medical clinic entrance may implicate the reasonable expectation of privacy analysis from the removal guide.
- **Demand letter framework**: The removal guide's demand-letter structure — identify the harm, state the legal basis, demand specific action, set a response deadline — is directly adaptable for city council communications. The toolkit in `flock-removal-advocacy-toolkit.md` applies this structure.

### 5.3 Connection to Voter Surveillance and Civic Chilling Effect

The domain-40 surveillance capitalism file and broader democracy crisis analysis address the chilling effect on civic participation. Flock cameras at or near polling locations, voter registration events, and political meetings capture participant plates without disclosure. This data is retained and accessible to thousands of agencies.

The structural parallel to historical civil rights suppression is precise: the combination of lawful public-space observation with political weaponization of the records is identical to the use of public records to identify civil rights demonstrators.

**Documented protest surveillance through Flock:**
- No Kings protests (June 2025): 19 agencies ran searches including Spokane County Sheriff, Beaumont PD (TX), San Bernardino County Sheriff, Tempe PD
- February 50501 protests: Carson City Sheriff searched across 178 networks
- Hands Off protests (April 2025)
- Pro-Palestinian protests: Tulsa PD conducted 38+ protest-related searches
- May Day rally (Seminole County, FL)
- Border Patrol: 7 searches for a motorist who had extended his middle finger at CBP vehicles

For voter registration organizers and canvassers in Houston: assume your vehicles are logged at organizing events. This is not a reason to stop — it is a reason to fight the infrastructure.

Source: [EFF — Cops Using Flock to Surveil Protesters](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/11/how-cops-are-using-flock-safetys-alpr-network-surveil-protesters-and-activists)

### 5.4 Connection to Corporate Accountability Framework

The `corporate-accountability-ice-contractors.md` file documents the corporate accountability framework for companies that profit from immigration enforcement. Flock fits this analysis: $8.4 billion valuation; investors including Andreessen Horowitz (which publicly supports the Trump administration); a documented pattern of providing ICE access while denying it to municipal clients.

The California class action litigation — seeking $2,500 per violation — is the clearest current corporate accountability mechanism. The Home Depot lawsuit extends liability to private businesses using Flock. Shareholder engagement, consumer pressure on major Flock investors, and legislative disclosure requirements are additional leverage points in the corporate accountability toolkit.
