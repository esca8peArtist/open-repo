## Executive Summary

The federal democratic erosion documented in this proposal's core domains (Domains 1–29) has a parallel state-level track that operates with greater speed, less federal check, and in some states has proceeded further than the federal level. Three mechanisms define this track and together constitute what comparative politics researchers now describe as state-level autocratization:

**First: Preemption as municipal democracy annihilation.** State legislatures — dominated in 23 Republican trifecta states by unified single-party control — are systematically stripping municipal governments of the authority to govern in the areas where residents most acutely need local policy: worker protections, public health, environmental standards, LGBTQ+ non-discrimination, housing stability, election administration integrity, and immigration enforcement discretion. The Local Solutions Support Center tracked 800+ abusive preemption bills in 2025 legislative sessions and is on pace to equal or exceed that total in 2026. Texas's "Death Star" law (HB 2127, 2023) field-preempts eight broad categories of state statutory law, eliminating local ordinances on labor, natural resources, finance, agriculture, and property without any remaining municipal authority to respond. This is not a debate about regulatory uniformity — it is the wholesale elimination of the democratic capacity of cities.

**Second: Structural self-entrenchment through institutional redesign.** State legislatures in North Carolina, Georgia, Wisconsin, and Michigan have enacted lame-duck power transfers stripping incoming governors and attorneys general of appointment authority over election boards, utilities commissions, and judicial nomination panels — specifically timed to prevent opposing-party executives from exercising authority the framers assigned to the executive. North Carolina's December 2024 lame-duck legislation transferred election board appointment authority from the Democratic governor-elect to the state auditor (a Republican), reorganized the State Highway Patrol out of the governor's chain of command, and restricted the attorney general's authority to take positions contrary to the legislature — enacted after Republicans learned they had lost their supermajority, precisely to preserve control they knew they would not hold through normal electoral accountability.

**Third: Racial targeting of municipal democracy.** The most consequential pattern that distinguishes contemporary state preemption from ordinary state-local policy disagreement is the racial targeting of the preemption mechanism. When Alabama preempted Birmingham's minimum wage ordinance, it preempted a majority-Black city. When Mississippi created a state-appointed court system within Jackson, it created a parallel unelected judiciary for a majority-Black capital city. When Tennessee enacted felony penalties for blocking streets — in direct response to Nashville and Memphis municipal ordinances targeting hate demonstrations — it used state preemption of local civil rights policy as an instrument of racial governance. The academic literature on this pattern uses the term "racial authoritarian preemption": preemption specifically targeting cities whose residents are majority people of color and whose locally-elected officials reflect those communities' political preferences.

**The April 29, 2026 SCOTUS ruling in Callais v. Landry** compounds all three mechanisms. By requiring proof of intentional racial discrimination to sustain a Section 2 Voting Rights Act challenge — rather than discriminatory effect — the 6-3 majority eliminated the most effective federal check on racially disparate redistricting and, by analogical extension, on racial preemption targeting. The ruling arrives at the moment state-level autocratization is most accelerated and federal oversight capacity is most reduced.

**Five core findings** (corresponding to the five research questions):

1. State preemption has scaled from a targeted instrument to a comprehensive system of municipal incapacitation: 800+ abusive preemption bills in 2025, 850+ on pace in 2026, covering worker rights, elections, public health, LGBTQ+ protections, immigration enforcement, and environmental regulation simultaneously.
2. State-level gerrymandering post-Rucho operates through judicial capture, and the North Carolina sequence (gerrymander → state court strike-down → dark money judicial election → reversal) is now the operating model in Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, and Georgia.
3. Structural democratic self-entrenchment — mid-decade redistricting, lame-duck stripping of gubernatorial authority over election boards, state takeover of municipal judicial systems — is documented in at least nine states.
4. International IDEA's Global State of Democracy framework identifies the U.S. as having suffered the most severe single-year democratic decline among established democracies in its 2025 reporting period, generating 20 alerts between January and April 2025 — twice the total for any of the prior two full years.
5. Reform pathways exist at the federal level (John Lewis VRA, Redistricting Reform Act, federal preemption floor for worker rights) and at the state level (state voting rights acts, merit selection for judicial appointments, ballot initiative process protection), but all require either federal legislative majority or state-level democratic breakthrough — and the mechanism of state autocratization actively reduces the probability of achieving either.

## Section 1: State Preemption — From Policy Uniformity to Municipal Incapacitation

### 1.1 The Scale and Scope of the Preemption Campaign

State preemption — a state legislature overriding local ordinances — is constitutionally ordinary: states have plenary authority over municipalities, which are creatures of state law. What has changed is not the legal authority but the intent, the scale, and the target. In 2025, the Local Solutions Support Center (LSSC) tracked more than 800 abusive preemption bills across 48 states. As of mid-session 2026, LSSC is tracking nearly 850 abusive preemption bills — matching the entire 2025 total with months remaining in the legislative year, representing a 33% increase over the same period in 2025.

The policy areas targeted span the full range of municipal governance:

- **Worker protections**: At least 25 states have enacted preemption laws removing local authority to set minimum wages above the state floor; more than half were enacted after 2013. In 2025, five states banned local guaranteed income programs (Arkansas, Idaho, Iowa, Kansas, South Dakota), and three more have active legislation (Illinois, North Carolina, Wisconsin). The Economic Policy Institute's preemption tracker documents that the 2025 state-level coordination on this issue exhibits ALEC signature: identical bill language, simultaneous introduction in multiple sessions, and consistent framing.

- **Elections and democracy**: 137 election-related preemption bills were tracked as of March 2026 — 67% more than the 81 bills tracked in all of 2025. Nineteen states have now banned ranked-choice voting (Ohio and Indiana enacted bans in 2026). Multiple states are targeting philanthropic election funding and drop box availability.

- **Public health**: 66 public health preemption bills tracked by late March 2026, up from 43 in all of 2025. Six states are restricting local vaccine or masking authority; seven states are considering fluoride bans.

- **LGBTQ+ protections**: More than 100 anti-LGBTQ+ preemption bills in the current session. Iowa's preemption bill affects 13 municipalities and one county that had enacted local non-discrimination ordinances. Idaho's bill would affect 12 municipalities.

- **Immigration enforcement**: 86 immigration-related preemption measures currently tracked, with Tennessee's SB 6002 (signed February 12, 2025) establishing criminal penalties and removal from office for local officials who adopt sanctuary immigration policies — converting municipal governance decisions into personal criminal liability for locally-elected officials.

The LSSC's framing captures the structural shift: "Abusive preemption is where federal authoritarianism and corporate greed meet." The two drivers — ideological state uniformity projects (anti-LGBTQ+, anti-immigration) and corporate deregulation projects (eliminating local labor standards, environmental protections) — converge in the preemption mechanism because both benefit from preventing local democratic policy innovation.

### 1.2 Texas: "Death Star" and "Death Star 2.0"

Texas's HB 2127 (signed June 13, 2023; effective September 1, 2023) is the most comprehensive single preemption instrument enacted in U.S. history and the model for ALEC's next-generation preemption strategy nationally. The law — dubbed "Death Star" by opponents because of its field-preemption scope — eliminates local ordinance authority in eight broad statutory categories: Agriculture Code, Business and Commerce Code, Finance Code, Insurance Code, Labor Code, Natural Resources Code, Occupations Code, and Property Code.

The practical effect is total: when local ordinances conflict with any aspect of these codes, the ordinance is void. Dallas and Austin had enacted mandatory water break ordinances for outdoor construction workers — those ordinances are now void. Laredo's plastic bag ban is void. Houston's local concrete batch plant regulations are void. Any local ordinance on employment leave, hiring practices, breaks, employment benefits, scheduling, or any other term of employment is void if it "exceeds or conflicts with" any provision of the Labor Code — which is to say, void by default, because state law does not generally mandate worker protections that exceed federal standards.

A Travis County judge ruled the law unconstitutional in August 2023. The Third Court of Appeals reversed that ruling in July 2025, allowing the statute to remain in effect while litigation continues. The Texas Municipal League notes the law creates a mechanism by which any person can bring suit to enforce the preemption — meaning corporations can directly challenge any municipal ordinance in any of the eight code areas without alleging any actual harm to themselves.

The 2025 Texas legislative session extended the model. Bills HB 5042, HB 5203, HB 5612, SB 2858, and SB 3016 collectively constitute "Death Star 2.0": expanding preemption to health and safety codes, election administration, and penal code provisions, while additionally restricting local governments from using their own funds to challenge preemption laws in court. The litigation-cost restriction is the innovation: it converts legal challenge from a viable counter-strategy into a political decision to spend public funds on a fight the legislature has preemptively made more expensive.

Missouri's SB 459 adds an additional enforcement mechanism: a "Death Star" analog allowing the state legislature to initiate investigations of noncompliant municipalities, with non-compliance triggers leading to state withholding of tax revenue. The financial penalty mechanism — Oklahoma similarly allows state seizure of local tax revenue from sanctuary municipalities — converts preemption from a negative prohibition (you may not enact this ordinance) into an affirmative extraction (you will lose your municipal revenue if you do not comply with state directives). This crosses from preemption into fiscal coercion: the municipal democracy question is no longer whether cities have ordinance authority, but whether they have financial independence sufficient to exercise any remaining authority.

### 1.3 Florida: The Comprehensive Anti-City Architecture

Florida under Governor DeSantis (2019-2025) constructed the most comprehensive preemption architecture of any state, operating across ten years and accelerating through 2024. Unlike Texas, which concentrated power in a single omnibus statute, Florida deployed serial, targeted preemption across dozens of legislative sessions:

- **Tenant protections (2023)**: HB 1417 preempted an estimated 46 tenant protection ordinances in 35 cities, including tenants' bill of rights ordinances in Miami-Dade, Broward, Orange, Hillsborough, and Pinellas counties — the state's most diverse, most urban counties. Source-of-income protections and rent stabilization measures were simultaneously eliminated.

- **Worker health (2024)**: HB 433 preempted local heat protection ordinances for outdoor workers — including the Miami-Dade County heat protection rule that had been enacted after multiple construction worker deaths. The state legislature eliminated a worker safety standard that local governments had enacted in direct response to documented fatalities.

- **Energy regulation**: SB 1084/HB 1701 preempted local regulation of EV charging stations; other sessions preempted local energy ordinances.

- **Ballot initiative suppression (2023)**: Florida's preemption of its own ballot initiative process — imposing a $1 million bond requirement, felony disqualification for signature gatherers, and shortened timelines (documented in Domain 33) — functions as preemption of the meta-democratic instrument by which municipalities and voters could override captured state authority.

The Florida Association of Counties' 2025 preemption tracker lists 47 active preemption bills in the current session, continuing the decade-long pattern.

The Local Solutions Support Center's 2024 analysis identified Florida's preemption pattern as the clearest example of what they term "abusive preemption": preemption enacted not to achieve uniform statewide standards but to prevent specific types of local democratic innovation, targeted at the policy areas where diverse urban populations diverge most from the preferences of a predominantly rural legislative majority.

### 1.4 Racial Authoritarian Preemption

The most analytically important — and most politically understated — feature of contemporary state preemption is its racial targeting. The academic literature now has a term for this pattern: "racial authoritarian preemption," defined as state governments overriding the governing power of racially diverse municipalities specifically targeting the policy innovations those municipalities have enacted to address their residents' expressed needs.

The pattern is documented by peer-reviewed scholarship. Kuswa and Mayer's analysis in *Social Sciences* (2025), "Racial Authoritarian Preemption and the Politics of Tennessee," documents a 2010-2024 pattern in which Tennessee's conservative legislature used preemption to revoke locally-based civil rights protections, racial equity initiatives, and redistributive measures championed by Nashville and Memphis — the state's majority-minority cities — while leaving rural, white-majority municipalities' local authority intact.

The documented instances include:

**Alabama/Birmingham (2016)**: Birmingham enacted a minimum wage ordinance in 2016, explicitly responding to local economic conditions. The Alabama legislature responded within days with a preemption statute preventing any city from setting minimum wages above the state level. The city with the highest proportion of Black workers in the state was preempted within weeks of exercising the democratic authority its residents had elected officials to exercise.

**Mississippi/Jackson (2023)**: Mississippi's Republican-controlled legislature enacted HB 1020 creating the Capitol Complex Improvement District Court — a state-appointed judiciary operating within the boundaries of Jackson, a majority-Black city that is the state capital. The court's judge is appointed by the state Supreme Court chief justice (a conservative white elected official); its prosecutors by the state Attorney General (also conservative white). The jurisdiction covers the Capitol Complex Improvement District, which includes Jackson State University. The NAACP challenged the law as creating a separate, racially discriminatory judicial system for Black Jackson residents who would be subject to prosecution by state-appointed officials accountable to no one in Jackson. A federal judge allowed the court to proceed; the legal challenge was ultimately withdrawn by the NAACP but the structural concern remained: Mississippi created an unelected, state-controlled judicial apparatus operating within a majority-Black city, bypassing the city's elected officials entirely.

**Tennessee/Nashville-Memphis (2025)**: Tennessee SB 6002 (signed February 12, 2025) establishes criminal penalties and removal from office for local officials who adopt sanctuary immigration policies. The law was introduced specifically after Nashville's Metro Council and Memphis' city government had considered or enacted local policies limiting cooperation with federal immigration enforcement. The criminalization mechanism — converting a local governing decision into a basis for criminal prosecution and removal of locally-elected officials — is preemption weaponized beyond regulatory uniformity into direct suppression of local democratic governance by elected officials in majority-minority cities.

The Economic Policy Institute's regional preemption analysis confirms the pattern nationally: "Southern states are more likely than states in other regions to use preemption to stop local governments from setting strong labor standards. Preemption laws in the South passed by majority-white legislatures tend to create barriers to economic security in cities whose residents are majority people of color."

The April 29, 2026 Supreme Court ruling in *Callais v. Landry* — requiring proof of intentional racial discrimination rather than discriminatory effect under Section 2 of the VRA — makes this racial targeting substantially harder to challenge. The preemption decisions described above do not generally state a racial intent; they operate through facially neutral authority (state supremacy over municipal law). Proving intentional racial discrimination in the Alabama minimum wage preemption, or the Tennessee sanctuary criminalization, requires evidence of discriminatory purpose that legislators are skilled at not creating. The effect standard was the viable litigation pathway. The Court eliminated it.

## Section 2: Structural Self-Entrenchment — When Legislatures Use Democratic Procedure to Defeat Democratic Accountability

### 2.1 The Lame-Duck Pattern: North Carolina as the Case Study

The term "structural self-entrenchment" describes legislative actions that use procedurally legitimate instruments to insulate the legislature from future electoral accountability. The mechanism does not require any single illegal act — each step is formally authorized by the rules. The cumulative effect is to convert electoral defeat into continued structural control.

North Carolina has executed this mechanism twice in eight years, documenting the pattern most completely:

**First cycle (2016-2017)**: After Democrat Roy Cooper defeated Republican Governor Pat McCrory in the 2016 general election, the Republican-controlled legislature called a lame-duck session before Cooper took office and enacted legislation:
- Imposing a legislative approval requirement on gubernatorial cabinet appointments
- Reducing the number of at-will appointments the governor could fill from 1,500 to roughly 400
- Restructuring the State Board of Elections to equal partisan representation (eliminating the governor's majority), changing from a 3-2 majority for the governor's party to equal representation — effectively removing the new Democratic governor's control over the state's election administration infrastructure

These laws were challenged in court. The North Carolina Supreme Court (then with a Democratic majority) struck down portions of the election board restructuring. The Republican legislature then attempted to end judicial elections and replace them with an appointment process — timed to prevent the Democratic governor from making appointments. That effort was also blocked in court.

**Second cycle (2024)**: After Democrats won the governorship (Josh Stein), the attorney general's office (Jeff Jackson), and the state superintendent (Mo Green) in the November 2024 election, the Republican legislature — having learned it had lost its supermajority — enacted legislation in December 2024, described publicly as a disaster relief bill but actually including:

- Transfer of election board appointment authority from the governor to the state auditor (a Republican) effective spring 2025
- Transfer of the State Highway Patrol from the governor's Department of Public Safety to an independent agency under legislative oversight
- Reduction of governor's appointments to the Public Utilities Commission from three to two
- Prohibition on the attorney general taking any legal position contrary to the legislature's expressed preferences

Governor Cooper vetoed the legislation; the Republican legislature overrode the veto 72-46 on December 11, 2024. A North Carolina appeals court ruled on April 30, 2025, that the election board appointment transfer could take effect even though a trial court had struck it down as unconstitutional. The constitutional question remains in active litigation.

The Harvard Civil Rights-Civil Liberties Law Review's analysis of these lame-duck power grabs identifies a structural problem that applies across all three documented cycles (North Carolina, Michigan, Wisconsin): "The most practical reading of separation of powers principles is that no major legislation which alters the powers between branches should be allowed to be enacted in lame-duck sessions." The reason is constitutional logic, not partisan preference: a legislature that knows it is losing power has no electoral accountability for the changes it makes to the institutional structure in its final days. The separation-of-powers rationale for legislative authority — democratic accountability through regular elections — is specifically absent in a lame-duck session.

### 2.2 Georgia: Converting Election Board Control into Pre-Certification Authority

Georgia's structural entrenchment sequence operates through the election board rather than the gubernatorial appointment mechanism:

**SB 202 (2021)**: The Republican-controlled legislature stripped Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger of his position as chair of the State Election Board — in direct response to Raffensperger's refusal to alter the certified 2020 Georgia election results. The new structure gave the legislature the power to appoint the chair and, effectively, control three of the five election board seats. Raffensperger accurately characterized this as the legislature's attempt to ensure the next secretary of state who refused to act for partisan advantage could be overridden by a legislature-controlled board. The chair is now appointed by majority vote of the General Assembly when in session, or by the governor when out of session — a dramatic shift from the prior structure in which the secretary of state's role provided a check on partisan manipulation.

**2024 developments**: With the legislature-appointed Election Board majority in place, the board in summer 2024 passed rules that:
- Mandated hand counts of all ballots on Election Day (subsequently struck down by two state judges as exceeding the board's authority)
- Allowed counties to delay certification pending "administrative review" of ballot counts — creating a mechanism for county officials aligned with one party to delay certification pending political intervention

Georgia Recorder documented that Lt. Governor Burt Jones appointed Salleigh Grubbs, a top Georgia Republican Party official and elections activist, to the State Election Board in 2024 — continuing the pattern of direct partisan appointment to the body overseeing election administration and certification.

The structural risk Georgia created is distinct from voter suppression: it is not primarily about which voters can cast ballots, but about who controls the certification process. A state election board with legislative-appointed majorities, equipped with rules permitting certification delays, creates a mechanism by which electoral outcomes can be contested procedurally after votes are cast and counted — without any allegation of vote fraud, simply through the exercise of "administrative review" authority by partisan-aligned officials.

### 2.3 Wisconsin: Constitutional Amendments as Entrenchment Infrastructure

Wisconsin provides a different structural entrenchment mechanism: using constitutional amendment procedures to permanently reduce executive authority in ways the sitting governor cannot veto.

In December 2018, following Tony Evers' defeat of incumbent Governor Scott Walker, the Republican legislature called a lame-duck session and passed legislation limiting Evers' authority to withdraw from legal settlements or lawsuits without legislative approval, and restricting early implementation of rules. These laws were partially struck down by the Wisconsin Supreme Court (after the court flipped to a liberal majority in 2023).

The Republican legislature's 2026 response was structural: they advanced constitutional amendments to the voters that the governor cannot veto. A proposed 2026 amendment would prohibit the governor from using the partial veto to create or increase any tax or fee — specifically targeting Governor Evers' 2023 partial veto that extended school revenue limits for 400 years. Because constitutional amendments require passage in two consecutive legislative sessions before going to voters, and the Republican legislature controls both sessions, the amendment will appear on the November 2026 ballot regardless of the governor's preferences.

A further proposed amendment would limit the governor's partial veto to entire sections of appropriations bills (rather than the current ability to veto individual words and letters). If enacted, this would fundamentally constrain the executive's budget authority and shift appropriations power toward the legislature in ways designed to survive future Democratic legislative majorities (because amending a constitutional provision requires a future legislative supermajority and referendum).

The Wisconsin pattern illustrates the long game: where lame-duck statutory power grabs face judicial challenge (as North Carolina's did), constitutional amendment mechanisms provide a harder-to-challenge structural entrenchment pathway. The entrenchment is slower — constitutional amendments take years — but more durable.

### 2.4 Mid-Decade Redistricting as Structural Lock-In

The mid-decade redistricting cycle of 2025-2026 is fully documented in Domain 33 (Section 1.1) and should be read in conjunction with this section. The structural self-entrenchment dimension — beyond the maps themselves — is the explicit presidential coordination of a structural power grab:

President Trump publicly called for Republican-controlled states to redraw congressional maps in summer 2025. Six states responded: Texas (August 2025), Missouri, North Carolina, Ohio, California (defensive counter), and Utah. The Supreme Court's December 4, 2025 6-3 stay of the federal three-judge panel ruling in the Texas case — allowing maps found to constitute an unconstitutional racial gerrymander to be used in 2026 elections — means that structurally locking in electoral advantage through redistricting carries no immediate electoral cost to the actors who commission it. The structural entrenchment succeeds regardless of subsequent judicial findings on the merits, because the 2026 elections will have already occurred under the challenged maps.

### 2.5 The Independent State Legislature Theory — Rejected But Unresolved

Moore v. Harper (600 U.S. 1, 2023) rejected the most extreme version of the Independent State Legislature (ISL) theory — the claim that state legislatures have plenary authority over federal elections unchecked by state constitutions or state courts. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for a 6-3 majority, held that state courts retain authority to review legislative election laws against state constitutional standards.

But the ruling left critical questions open: exactly how much state court "review" is permissible before it becomes the functional displacement of legislative authority that the ISL theory claimed to prohibit? Several justices in the majority noted that federal courts retain authority to review whether a state court has exceeded legitimate judicial review of election law. This residual federal court review of state court election law decisions creates what legal scholars have called "ISL light" — a doctrine that limits (without eliminating) state court review of legislative election decisions, with federal courts as the ultimate arbiter of whether state courts have gone too far.

The practical effect of ISL light is asymmetric: it creates litigation risk for state supreme courts that strike down legislative election decisions (as the North Carolina Supreme Court originally did in Harper v. Hall), but it creates no equivalent risk for state courts that uphold legislative election decisions. This asymmetry structurally favors captured state courts — which uphold rather than strike legislative election decisions — over independent state courts that seek to enforce state constitutional protections against partisan gerrymandering.

## Section 3: International IDEA Framework — Measuring State-Level Autocratization

### 3.1 The GSoD Indicators and the U.S. Score

International IDEA's Global State of Democracy (GSoD) Indices provide the most rigorous cross-national measurement of democratic performance, aggregating 154 indicators from 22 data sources across four core categories: Representation, Rights, Rule of Law, and Participation. The 2025 GSoD report (covering data through 2024, released September 2025) documents:

- 94 countries — 54% of all assessed — suffered decline in at least one democratic performance factor compared with their own performance five years earlier
- Only 55 countries (32%) advanced in at least one factor over the same period
- International IDEA issued 20 alerts for the United States between January and April 2025 — twice as many as in any of the previous two full years

The specific November 2025 U.S. tracker report documents indicator declines in Representation, Rule of Law, Rights (specifically Civil Liberties and Freedom of Expression), and notes that "some observers have described these developments as a 'presidential coup' in the USA, warning that the country lies at the 'cusp of autocracy.'"

The GSoD's four-category framework maps directly onto the mechanisms documented in this analysis:

| GSoD Category | State-Level Mechanism | Current Status |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Representation | Partisan gerrymandering; ballot initiative capture; preemption of local representative bodies | Acute decline — Domain 33 + this document |
| Rights | Anti-protest laws; LGBTQ+ preemption; racial authoritarian preemption | Acute decline |
| Rule of Law | State court capture; election board partisan appointment; lame-duck judicial recomposition | Acute decline |
| Participation | Voter suppression; ballot initiative restriction; municipal democracy elimination | Acute decline |

### 3.2 Sub-National Autocratization Indicators

International IDEA's GSoD Indices do not currently publish sub-national state-level scores for the United States. The V-Dem project (Varieties of Democracy) maintains sub-national analyses for some countries but does not currently publish a state-level U.S. index. This is a gap in the international comparative democracy literature that is increasingly being identified as significant: the United States' federal structure means that meaningful democratic erosion can occur at the state level without registering as a national-level decline (or registering only partially, as the aggregate U.S. score masks interstate variation).

However, the framework for what sub-national scores would measure is derivable from the GSoD indicators. Applying the GSoD framework to documented state-level conditions:

**North Carolina**: Would score in the "hybrid" regime category on Representation (gerrymandered maps, ballot initiative restriction), Rights (anti-protest felony law, preemption of LGBTQ+ municipal protections), and Rule of Law (captured state supreme court, lame-duck election board appointment transfer). The state's democratic performance on these dimensions is more consistent with the GSoD's "hybrid" classification range than with its "democracy" range.

**Florida**: Would score below the democracy threshold on Representation (ballot initiative $1 million bond requirement, gerrymander) and Rights (anti-protest laws, preemption of tenant protections, heat protection elimination). The Brennan Center's 2025 state voting laws roundup documents that Florida enacted the highest number of voting restrictions of any state in 2025.

**Texas**: Would score at the democracy-hybrid boundary on Representation (Death Star preemption eliminating municipal worker democracy, mid-decade redistricting targeting Black and Latino voters) and Rights (criminal penalties for local officials, preemption of worker health protections). The racial targeting dimension — federal courts found the 2025 Texas congressional map to constitute an unconstitutional racial gerrymander, and the Supreme Court allowed it to proceed regardless — would score as an acute Rule of Law failure.

**Georgia**: Would score in the hybrid range on Rule of Law (election board converted to legislative-partisan appointment, certification delay rules) and Representation (mid-decade map allowed by Supreme Court despite racial gerrymander finding, secretary of state stripped of election board chair authority).

The critical finding from applying the GSoD framework sub-nationally: these states score below the democracy threshold on specific dimensions that the aggregate U.S. national score obscures. Democratic erosion in the United States is not uniform — it is concentrated in specific states, on specific dimensions, and its full severity requires sub-national measurement to document accurately.

### 3.3 The Comparative Autocratization Pattern — State Level

The academic literature on comparative autocratization identifies three patterns that distinguish democratic erosion from ordinary political competition. All three are present in the state-level U.S. evidence:

**Simultaneous multi-front attack**: Autocratization in Hungary, Turkey, and Poland was distinguished from ordinary democratic politics by the simultaneity of attacks on multiple institutional pillars at once — courts, media, civil service, election administration, and opposition capacity all targeted in the same legislative session. The state-level U.S. evidence shows the same pattern. Ohio's 2024 lame-duck session enacted a strict photo ID voting requirement, stripped the state board of education of most powers, and advanced the 60% ballot initiative supermajority proposal in the same session — three distinct mechanisms operating simultaneously. North Carolina's December 2024 session transferred election board appointment authority, reorganized the Highway Patrol, restricted the attorney general, and reduced utilities commission appointments in one bill.

**Using democratic procedure to defeat democratic accountability**: Orbán's Fidesz converted ordinary policy areas into "cardinal laws" requiring supermajorities to change — using the Hungarian parliament's legitimate procedure to entrench policies against future democratic reversal. The U.S. state-level parallel: Wisconsin's use of constitutional amendment procedures to limit gubernatorial veto authority in ways the sitting Democratic governor cannot prevent. Missouri's use of the ballot initiative process to place a measure before voters that would make future ballot initiatives structurally impossible for any urban-majority coalition to pass.

**Institutional long-game appointment**: Orbán placed loyalists in positions with 9- and 12-year mandated terms, ensuring continuity across multiple governments. The U.S. state-level parallel: North Carolina and Georgia transferring election board appointment authority from the governor to the legislature or to partisan statewide officials, with those officials holding multi-year terms that extend through multiple subsequent administrations. The judges and election officials installed during a period of legislative control do not leave when the legislature loses its majority.

## Section 4: Anti-Protest Laws as State-Level First Amendment Carve-Back

### 4.1 Scope and Escalation

Anti-protest legislation at the state level is a domain where the preemption mechanism and the direct rights-restriction mechanism converge. As of July 2024, the International Center for Not-for-Profit Law (ICNL)'s US Protest Law Tracker listed 305 bills introduced since 2017, 49 of which had been enacted. The enacted laws include:

- Florida's HB 1 (2021): Creates new criminal penalties for protest-adjacent conduct, including "aggravated rioting" as a third-degree felony; allows civil liability suits against protest organizers; provides legal immunity for drivers who injure protesters blocking traffic. The law was described by the Florida ACLU as creating "a chilling effect on all protest activity."

- Oklahoma's HB 1674 (2021): Makes it a felony (punishable by up to 10 years in prison) to obstruct traffic during a protest; imposes up to $10,000 fines on organizations whose members commit the offense.

- Tennessee's enhanced anti-protest law (2025): Expands existing penalties for blocking streets to a Class D felony punishable by 2-12 years in prison and a $5,000 fine. The offense applies to protesters who make passage "unreasonably inconvenient" even if no vehicles are present. The law's legislative origins were explicitly in response to Nashville Metropolitan Council's ordinances targeting hate demonstrations — making this a direct example of the preemption-protest convergence: a state using preemption-adjacent authority to override a municipal civil rights ordinance with a felony criminal statute.

PEN America's *Closing Ranks* report documents that between June 2020 and March 2021 alone, state lawmakers proposed 100 anti-protest bills — approximately 10 per month, compared to three per month earlier in 2020. The surge correlates with the 2020 Black Lives Matter protests and represents a coordinated state-level First Amendment carve-back: creating categories of protest activity that are criminal rather than protected, specifically in the contexts where protest has historically been most effective (highway disruption, sustained occupation, economic disruption).

### 4.2 The Tennessee Preemption-Protest Nexus

Tennessee provides the most complete documented example of state anti-protest law used as preemption of local civil rights policy. The sequence:

1. Nashville Metropolitan Council, responding to constituent pressure and white supremacist demonstrations in 2023-2024, passed ordinances including buffer zones around public buildings and provisions targeting hate speech-adjacent demonstrations.

2. The Tennessee legislature introduced legislation expanding anti-protest criminal penalties explicitly referencing Nashville's ordinances as the problem.

3. The resulting law (SB 263, 2025) creates felony penalties for street-blocking that are broader than Nashville's civil rights ordinances — replacing the city's calibrated response to specific demonstrations with a state-level felony that would apply to any protest activity meeting the "unreasonably inconvenient" standard.

The legislative record is unusually explicit: the bill's origins lie with Tennessee state legislators' objections to Nashville's local civil rights response. This makes the Tennessee law the clearest documented case of racial authoritarian preemption in the anti-protest context: state preemption of a majority-minority city's civil rights ordinance through the mechanism of creating a felony that effectively makes the type of protest those ordinances were designed to accommodate — or protect from violent counter-protest — into criminal conduct.

## Section 5: Reform Pathways — Structural Responses to State-Level Autocratization

### 5.1 Federal Preemption Floor Legislation

The most structural federal response to state preemption abuse is legislation establishing a federal floor for specific municipal authority categories — preventing states from preempting below the floor. Three legislative models exist:

**Worker rights floor**: The Raise the Wage Act, if enacted, would create a federal minimum wage floor that eliminates the municipal minimum wage preemption problem at the highest-priority labor standard. More targeted legislation could establish a federal anti-preemption rule specifically for local worker safety ordinances (like the Dallas and Austin heat protection rules): the federal Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) lacks jurisdiction over state and local government workers in some states, creating a gap that targeted federal legislation could fill.

**Election administration independence floor**: Federal legislation establishing minimum structural independence requirements for state election boards — requiring that appointment authority for election administration officials not be vested entirely in partisan legislative bodies, and establishing removal-for-cause standards — would address the Georgia and North Carolina certification authority restructuring. The precedent is the Help America Vote Act (2002), which established federal minimum standards for state election administration infrastructure.

**Anti-lame-duck entrenchment**: Federal legislation addressing lame-duck state power grabs is constitutionally complex (states have plenary authority over their internal organization except as constrained by the Constitution), but a federal floor requiring advance notice and public comment for structural changes to state executive appointment authority enacted in lame-duck legislative sessions is feasible under the Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4) for changes affecting federal election administration.

### 5.2 VRA Preclearance Extended to State Structural Changes

The John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act (H.R. 4/S. 4 — repeatedly introduced, not enacted) would restore preclearance requirements under a new formula. The current legislative drafts focus preclearance on voting rule changes (voter ID, polling hours, registration rules, redistricting). An extension of the preclearance framework to cover structural changes affecting election administration authority — appointment of election board members, jurisdiction over certification, changes to ballot initiative processes — would address the Georgia and North Carolina patterns directly.

Under extended preclearance, a covered jurisdiction that sought to transfer election board appointment authority from a bipartisan or executive-controlled structure to a legislature-controlled structure would be required to obtain federal (DOJ or D.C. Circuit) approval before the change takes effect. The precedent from the original VRA's operation is that this kind of advance review effectively deters the most discriminatory structural changes: the Brennan Center documents that Section 5 blocked 86 discriminatory changes between 1998 and 2013, including many structural changes that courts might not have reached in post-hoc litigation.

The constitutional basis for extending preclearance to structural election administration changes is stronger than it might appear: *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) struck down the Section 4(b) coverage formula as outdated but did not strike down the preclearance mechanism itself. A new formula based on recent documented violations — including the Georgia and North Carolina election board restructuring, the 2025 mid-decade redistricting, and the documented voter roll purge violations — would be constitutionally distinguishable from the formula the Court struck down.

### 5.3 State-Level Voting Rights Acts and Home Rule Protections

Nine states have enacted state voting rights acts (Washington, Oregon, California, Colorado, Illinois, Minnesota, New York, Connecticut, and Virginia) that provide independent state preclearance, private rights of action for voting discrimination, and sub-national coverage formulas. These state VRAs are the most immediately available structural response to state-level voting rights erosion in states where they exist.

Where they do not exist — specifically in the Southern states most affected by racial authoritarian preemption — the legislative pathway requires changing state legislative composition. This is the structural circularity problem: the mechanism of state autocratization reduces the probability of electing the legislative majorities that would be required to enact the structural responses.

**Home rule constitutional protection**: Several states provide constitutional home rule protections that limit state preemption authority. California's constitution provides home rule protection for municipal affairs (though courts have narrowed "municipal affairs" over time). Colorado's constitution provides more robust home rule protections for Statutory Cities and Home Rule Cities. Creating or strengthening home rule protections requires state constitutional amendment processes — which in most states require either a legislative supermajority or a ballot initiative, both of which are subject to the same capture mechanisms documented above.

The most immediately actionable state-level reform is litigation-based: challenging preemption laws that violate existing home rule provisions (which Texas cities are pursuing regarding HB 2127), challenging lame-duck power transfers as violations of state constitutional separation of powers (which the North Carolina litigation is pursuing), and challenging racial authoritarian preemption as violating state civil rights laws (where state civil rights laws provide the only remaining coverage after Callais eviscerated the Section 2 effect standard).

### 5.4 Municipal Democracy Protection Act (Federal Floor Model)

The most comprehensive federal response to state preemption abuse that has been proposed — though not yet enacted or introduced as a single bill — would establish a federal municipal democracy protection floor with five elements:

1. **Worker rights**: Prohibit state preemption of local worker protection ordinances that exceed federal minimums (minimum wage, heat protection, scheduling stability, paid leave)

2. **Election administration independence**: Require state election boards to have appointment structures that include representation not solely controlled by one party and the legislature, with removal-for-cause protections for board members

3. **Lame-duck structural change restrictions**: Prohibit changes to state election administration appointment authority during lame-duck legislative sessions, defined as sessions occurring after a general election in which incumbents' successors have been elected but have not yet taken office

4. **Anti-preemption civil rights floor**: Prohibit state preemption of local non-discrimination ordinances that exceed state-floor anti-discrimination protections, where the preemption demonstrably reduces protections in majority-minority jurisdictions

5. **Ballot initiative process floor**: Prohibit state legislation restricting ballot initiative petition procedures from taking effect within 18 months of a qualifying election, preventing mid-cycle changes that disadvantage ongoing campaigns

The constitutional basis for items 1, 4, and 5 is clearest under the Commerce Clause (worker protections affecting interstate commerce), the Fourteenth Amendment (civil rights floor), and the Elections Clause (ballot initiative restriction affecting federal elections). Items 2 and 3 have a plausible basis in the Elections Clause as applied to federal election administration.

None of these proposals are currently enacted or introduced as pending legislation in the 119th Congress. Their feasibility depends on a change in the Senate majority and House composition — the conditions documented in Section 6 of Domain 33.

## Section 6: Implementation Priorities and Advocacy Windows

### 6.1 Immediate (May 2026 — November 2026)

**Texas HB 2127 litigation — Third Court of Appeals ruling (July 2025)**: The Texas municipal appeals of the Third Court of Appeals ruling are pending. Dallas and Austin are pursuing the litigation. The Texas Municipal League's technical legal analysis of the constitutional vulnerabilities is the current best resource for amici brief development.

**North Carolina election board litigation**: The April 30, 2025 appeals court ruling allowing the election board appointment transfer to take effect is subject to further appeal. The constitutional question — whether a lame-duck legislature can strip an incoming governor of election administration authority — is a federal question under the Elections Clause and the Fourteenth Amendment's due process clause, available for federal court review if the state courts ultimately sustain the transfer.

**April 29, 2026 Callais ruling response**: The Supreme Court's elimination of the Section 2 effect standard requires immediate strategic adjustment by all redistricting and racial preemption litigants. The Campaign Legal Center, NAACP LDF, and Brennan Center are all developing post-Callais litigation strategies. The VRA Section 3(c) "bail-in" provision — allowing federal courts to impose preclearance on jurisdictions found to have intentionally discriminated — becomes more important as Section 2 is narrowed. Documenting intentional discrimination in state preemption decisions is the new litigation priority.

**2026 state legislative elections**: The states with the most acute preemption and entrenchment problems are also the states with the most immediate electoral opportunity for structural interruption. Wisconsin and Pennsylvania state supreme court elections (November 2026) directly affect redistricting and ballot initiative litigation outcomes for the remainder of the decade.

### 6.2 Medium-Term (2027–2028)

**John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act**: Requires the 120th Congress (post-November 2026 elections) with a sufficient Senate majority to overcome the filibuster or adopt a carve-out. If the Senate flips to Democratic control in November 2026, the VRA restoration becomes viable in the 120th Congress's first session (January-December 2027). The extended preclearance framework for election administration structural changes is a viable amendment to the current JLVRAA draft.

**State Voting Rights Act campaigns**: Campaigns to enact state voting rights acts in non-covered states — specifically Georgia, Arizona, Michigan, and Pennsylvania — are in development by state-level civil rights organizations. These campaigns require state legislative majorities or ballot initiative success in states where the initiative process is not yet fully restricted.

**Federal redistricting reform**: The Redistricting Reform Act of 2025 (S. 2885 / H.R. 5449), requiring IRCs and prohibiting mid-decade redistricting in all states for congressional elections, has a viable window in the 120th Congress if House Democrats win the majority in November 2026 (partially enabled by mid-decade redistricting backlash in Texas, North Carolina, and Missouri districts where the gerrymanders are visible to affected voters).

### 6.3 Long-Term (2029–2031)

**Pre-redistricting structural reform**: All structural reforms relevant to the 2031 redistricting cycle must be enacted by no later than the 2030 census data release. Specifically: federal IRC mandate, federal mid-decade redistricting prohibition, and state-level IRC ballot initiative campaigns in target states (Ohio, Wisconsin, Pennsylvania). The window narrows with each passing session.

**Constitutional dimensions**: The Harvard CRCL Law Review analysis identifies a constitutional principle — that major legislation altering inter-branch powers should be prohibited during lame-duck sessions — that has not yet been tested before the U.S. Supreme Court. Federal court development of this principle under the Elections Clause would provide the most durable structural protection against lame-duck entrenchment, but it requires the right case, the right factual record, and a court willing to apply the principle. The North Carolina election board transfer litigation is the current best vehicle.
