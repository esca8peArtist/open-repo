# Phase 6 Research Zones Mapping
## Domains B–F: Five-Domain Framework

*Created: June 23, 2026 — Phase 6 Pre-Production*
*Launch target: November 15, 2026*
*Baseline: Phase 3 complete (Domain K: Federal Judiciary Restructuring; Domain H: Constitutional Resilience Architecture)*

---

## PURPOSE

This document maps per-domain research zones for Phase 6, providing: (1) 5–7 research zones per domain, (2) 3–5 research questions per zone, (3) sourcing strategies per zone (by source type), (4) expert contact categories, (5) time estimates, and (6) integration notes connecting Phase 6 domains to completed Phase 3 material. Phase 6 does not duplicate Phase 3's judicial accountability and constitutional resilience work — it extends into the economic, political-structural, multilateral, and implementation dimensions that Phase 3 deferred.

## DOMAIN B: ECONOMIC FOUNDATIONS

**Scope**: Fiscal policy architecture, monetary policy governance, structural inequality, tax policy reform, labor market design, international economic coordination.

**Phase 3 gap**: Phase 3 research (Domain H Zone 1: emergency powers; Domain K) did not systematically address economic structural conditions that either enable or constrain democratic resilience. Domain 59 (Economic Precarity and Civic Participation) covers economic hardship as a voter-suppression and participation mechanism; Domain B goes deeper into systemic architecture: what economic structures are prerequisites for functioning democracy?

**Lead finding for research framing**: The World Inequality Report 2026 documents the richest 10% commanding 75% of global wealth and the top 1% controlling 37% — and PNAS research (Rau and Stokes, 2025) confirms that the more unequal income distribution is in a democracy, the more at risk that democracy is of electing a power-aggrandizing, norm-shredding leader. Economic inequality is not merely a social problem — it is a causal mechanism in democratic erosion.

---

### Zone B-1: Fiscal Policy and Democratic Stability

**Research questions:**
1. What fiscal structures (progressive taxation, robust social spending, deficit management regimes) are empirically associated with democratic stability across OECD countries?
2. How does the US fiscal cliff (TCJA expiration 2025, "One Big Beautiful Act" spending trajectory) affect democratic infrastructure — specifically, which programs that support civic participation (legal aid, election administration, public defender funding) are most at risk?
3. What is the relationship between sovereign debt levels and democratic erosion? At what debt-to-GDP ratio do fiscal constraints begin forcing governments to cut civic infrastructure?
4. How have other democracies (Germany's Schuldenbremse, Nordic balanced-budget norms) structured fiscal constraints in ways that protect democratic infrastructure rather than hollowing it out?
5. What is the fiscal cost of democratic backsliding? Can we quantify the economic losses (institutional fragility, investor flight, rule-of-law discounts on growth) in countries that have backslid?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: CBO projections; OMB historical tables; IMF Fiscal Monitor 2025-2026; CRS reports on TCJA
- Academic: PNAS (Rau-Stokes 2025); OECD Economic Outlook; Brookings Hutchins Center fiscal analysis
- Practitioner: Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP); Economic Policy Institute (EPI); Committee for Responsible Federal Budget
- Historical precedent: Weimar Republic fiscal collapse; UK 2010 austerity and civic infrastructure erosion; Greek fiscal crisis and democratic stress

**Expert contact categories:** Fiscal economists (Brookings, EPI, Peterson Institute); CBO analysts; comparative political economists studying democracy-inequality links; CBPP budget analysts

**Timeline estimate:** 10–14 hours

### Zone B-2: Monetary Policy and Democratic Accountability

**Research questions:**
1. What is the appropriate level of central bank independence in a functioning democracy — and where is the line between technocratic independence and democratic accountability deficit?
2. How does the current threat to Federal Reserve independence (Trump administration statements, Powell pressure campaign, anticipated 2026 chair appointment) compare historically to other instances of political interference with central banks?
3. What governance reforms (transparency requirements, GAO audit authority, congressional oversight mechanisms) can strengthen accountability without compromising anti-inflationary credibility?
4. What do international models show? How do the European Central Bank, Bank of England, and Bundesbank balance independence with democratic legitimacy?
5. How does monetary policy (interest rates, QE, inflation outcomes) interact with political polarization — do high-inflation episodes systematically produce authoritarian turns?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Federal Reserve Act; CRS R48390 (Federal Reserve Policy Issues, 119th Congress); GAO audit proposals (S.2327); Fed FOMC minutes
- Academic: CEPR analyses on central bank independence; Brookings (Yellen remarks on Fed independence and fiscal dominance); Bernanke-era historical analysis
- Practitioner: Peterson Institute for International Economics; Brookings Hamilton Project; CEPR VoxEU columns
- Historical precedent: Bundesbank pre-ECB; Volcker appointment political context; Argentina/Venezuela central bank capture case studies

**Expert contact categories:** Monetary economists; Federal Reserve alumni; central bank governance scholars; comparative political economists

**Timeline estimate:** 8–12 hours

### Zone B-3: Inequality Structures and Political Capture

**Research questions:**
1. What is the mechanism linking extreme wealth concentration to political capture? Trace the pathway from wealth inequality → campaign finance → regulatory capture → policy feedback → more inequality.
2. At what Gini coefficient do democracies become functionally oligarchic? Is there an empirically observable threshold?
3. How does wealth inequality differ from income inequality in its political effects? The World Inequality Report 2026 documents the top 0.001% holding 3× the bottom 50%'s assets — what political power does that asset concentration translate to?
4. Which US policy domains show the clearest signature of wealth-capture: tax policy, antitrust, labor law, financial regulation, or trade?
5. What have been the most effective counter-capture mechanisms — public financing of elections, revolving-door restrictions, lobbying disclosure, or anti-monopoly structural remedies?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: SEC lobbying disclosure data; Federal Election Commission records; GAO reports on regulatory capture
- Academic: World Inequality Database / WID.world; Gilens and Page (2014, "Testing Theories of American Politics"); Mayer ("Dark Money"); Zucman and Saez wealth concentration analyses
- Practitioner: Issue One; Campaign Legal Center; Open Secrets; Public Citizen
- Historical precedent: Gilded Age antitrust era; New Deal financial regulation; post-WWII deconcentration period

**Expert contact categories:** Political economists (Piketty, Zucman, Saez frameworks); political scientists studying oligarchic capture; campaign finance researchers; antitrust scholars

**Timeline estimate:** 10–12 hours

### Zone B-4: Tax Policy Architecture

**Research questions:**
1. What tax structures (marginal income tax rates, estate taxes, wealth taxes, capital gains treatment, corporate minimum taxes) are most consistent with maintaining democratic equality — meaning roughly equal political weight across economic strata?
2. Is a US wealth tax constitutionally feasible post-Moore v. United States (2024)? What is the current constitutional landscape for taxing unrealized gains?
3. How have Nordic states, Germany, and Canada structured their tax systems to fund robust civic infrastructure (public media, legal aid, election administration, civil society) while maintaining competitive economies?
4. What is the fiscal gap created by the One Big Beautiful Act (tax provisions, Medicaid cuts, SNAP cuts) and what civic infrastructure programs are defunded in that gap?
5. What are the strongest proposals currently on the table — the Wyden progressive wealth tax, Biden unrealized gains minimum tax, global minimum corporate tax (Pillar 2) — in terms of revenue potential and political viability?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: TCJA (P.L. 115-97); One Big Beautiful Act text; Pillar 2 OECD framework; CRS tax policy reports
- Academic: Saez-Zucman "Progressive Wealth Taxation" (Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, 2019); Piketty "Capital and Ideology" (2019); Tax Policy Center distributional analyses
- Practitioner: ITEP (Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy); Tax Foundation; Brookings tax policy program; Americans for Tax Fairness
- Historical precedent: Eisenhower-era 91% marginal rate + civic investment; Reagan tax cuts and inequality acceleration; Clinton-era tax increase without recession

**Expert contact categories:** Tax policy economists; constitutional law scholars (taxing power, apportionment doctrine); ITEP analysts; revenue economists

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

### Zone B-5: Labor Market Architecture and Democratic Participation

**Research questions:**
1. What labor market structures (union density, collective bargaining coverage, wage floors, misclassification rules) are associated with higher civic participation rates — and is the causal relationship established?
2. How has the gig economy's reclassification of employees as independent contractors affected civic participation (fewer benefits = more economic precarity = less time/capacity for civic engagement)?
3. What is the current status of union organizing rights under the Trump NLRB and what are the legislative options to restore collective bargaining infrastructure?
4. What do cross-national comparisons show? Do countries with strong co-determination (Germany) or centralized bargaining (Nordic countries) show systematically higher civic engagement?
5. How does the minimum wage floor — currently $7.25 federal since 2009 — interact with democratic participation? Is there measurable evidence that workers below a living wage vote less, volunteer less, and have lower knowledge of civic processes?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: NLRA; NLRB annual reports; PRO Act legislative text; ILO Platform Economy Convention (expected 2026); Raise the Wage Act
- Academic: EPI "State of Working America"; Human Rights Watch gig economy report (March 2025); Mayer "Dark Money" on union-busting coordinated campaigns
- Practitioner: Economic Policy Institute; National Employment Law Project (NELP); AFL-CIO research department; SEIU policy analysis
- Historical precedent: Wagner Act and mid-century civic health; Taft-Hartley rollback effects; UK Trade Union Act 2016 impact

**Expert contact categories:** Labor economists; NLRB alumni; industrial relations scholars; organizing strategists; ILO specialists

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

### Zone B-6: International Economics and Democratic Sovereignty

**Research questions:**
1. How do international economic dependencies (import concentration, debt-to-foreign-creditor ratios, trade agreements with investor-state dispute settlement) constrain democratic policy choice?
2. What is the democratic accountability deficit in WTO governance — and what reforms (MC14 2026, dispute settlement restoration) would create more legitimacy?
3. How do IMF structural adjustment conditions interact with democratic stability? Do countries under IMF programs show measurable democratic regression?
4. What are the feasibility and tradeoffs of "economic sovereignty" strategies — reshoring, industrial policy, strategic autonomy — for maintaining democratic policy space?
5. How should a democracy respond to economic coercion by authoritarian states (China market dependencies, Russian energy leverage) while maintaining trade openness?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: WTO reform documents; UNCTAD Global Trade Update March 2026; IMF Article IV consultations; USMCA text; OECD investment guidelines
- Academic: UNCTAD trade and development reports; Dani Rodrik "The Globalization Paradox"; Ostry-Berg-Ghosh IMF research staff papers on capital account liberalization
- Practitioner: Peterson Institute for International Economics; Brookings trade program; CSIS trade and economics program; Atlantic Council
- Historical precedent: NAFTA Chapter 11 ISDS cases; Greek debt crisis EU conditionality; Argentina 2001 crisis and democratic collapse

**Expert contact categories:** Trade economists; international economic law scholars; ISDS reform advocates; WTO reform practitioners

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

## DOMAIN C: POLITICAL GOVERNANCE

**Scope**: Legislative institutional design, executive authority limits, federalism and inter-governmental relations, consent of governed mechanisms, political party architecture, electoral systems.

**Phase 3 gap**: Domain H addressed constitutional fragility points and anti-entrenchment mechanisms, but did not systematically cover operational political institutions — how legislatures are designed, how executive authority is bounded in practice, how federalism functions as both resilience and risk, and how electoral systems shape representation quality.

**Lead finding for research framing**: The 2026 midterm primary problem: 7% of voters in partisan primaries effectively elected 87% of Congress in 2024. Electoral system architecture — not merely individual voter behavior — is producing the polarization that makes governance dysfunction structurally inevitable.

---

### Zone C-1: Legislative Design and Congressional Dysfunction

**Research questions:**
1. What legislative institutional design features (committee structure, floor procedure, filibuster rules, budget process rules) are most associated with productive legislation versus gridlock?
2. How does the Senate filibuster function as a minority veto that systematically undermines majority consent — and what are the constitutional and precedential paths to filibuster reform?
3. What reforms would most directly improve legislative productivity: rule changes, independent redistricting, public financing, or structural changes like four-year House terms to reduce the permanent campaign?
4. How do other bicameral democracies (Germany's Bundestag/Bundesrat, UK Parliament, Australian Senate) manage inter-chamber coordination more effectively than the US?
5. What is the Brennan Center's "Eight Solutions to Unstick Congress" and what is the evidence base and political feasibility of each?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Brennan Center "Eight Solutions to Unstick Congress"; Filibuster history CRS reports; Rules of the House and Senate; Congressional Review Act
- Academic: Sarah Binder (Brookings) on congressional gridlock; Brookings Governance Studies program; Cambridge Core "Bringing the Legislature Back In"
- Practitioner: Issue One; Common Cause; States United Democracy Center; Fix Our Senate coalition
- Historical precedent: 1964 cloture reform; Byrd Rule history; Budget Act of 1974 reconciliation origins

**Expert contact categories:** Congressional scholars; former Rules Committee staff; Senate procedure experts; comparative parliamentarians

**Timeline estimate:** 10–12 hours

---

### Zone C-2: Executive Authority and Unilateral Power

**Research questions:**
1. What are the constitutional and statutory limits on executive unilateralism — and how have those limits eroded since the post-Watergate reform period?
2. How does the unitary executive theory interact with administrative state capacity? If the president has plenary removal authority (post-Trump v. Slaughter), what institutional constraints remain on executive agency capture?
3. What specific executive authority reforms are most feasible: IEEPA reform, NEA modernization, restoration of Congressional Review Act for midnight rules, or presidential immunity limitations?
4. How do parliamentary systems and semi-presidential systems (Germany, France, Finland) structure executive authority to prevent unilateral overreach while maintaining effective governance?
5. What is the current empirical record on presidential emergency declarations — how many are active, what is their average duration, and how many have survived legislative termination attempts?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: IEEPA statute; NEA (50 USC § 1601 et seq.); CRS reports on presidential emergency powers; Trump v. United States (2024) full opinion; Trump v. Slaughter ruling (when available)
- Academic: Elizabeth Goitein "The Alarming Scope of the President's Emergency Powers" (Atlantic, 2019); Kim Lane Scheppele "Autocratic Legalism"; Corwin "The President: Office and Powers"
- Practitioner: Brennan Center Liberty and National Security program; Protect Democracy; Lawfare Institute; Just Security
- Historical precedent: Youngstown Sheet & Tube v. Sawyer (1952); Steel Seizure case; Nixon impoundment; Reagan Iran-Contra

**Expert contact categories:** Administrative law scholars; former OLC attorneys; separation-of-powers litigators (Protect Democracy legal team); comparative executive power scholars

**Timeline estimate:** 10–14 hours

### Zone C-3: Federalism Architecture — Resilience and Risk

**Research questions:**
1. How does US federalism function as both a democratic resilience mechanism (states as laboratories, state AGs as resistance infrastructure) and a democratic risk factor (voter suppression laboratories, federalism weaponized for preemption of local progressive measures)?
2. What is the current state of federal preemption versus state innovation — specifically, which state-level democratic reforms are being preempted by federal law or federal administrative action?
3. How does cooperative federalism (EPA, Medicaid, education) function as leverage for federal democratic norms enforcement — and what are the limits after NFIB v. Sebelius (2012)?
4. What does the 2024–2025 literature show about "state of American federalism" — are states actually functioning as counterweights to federal democratic erosion, or are partisan alignment patterns making state-level resistance episodic rather than structural?
5. What are the most promising federalism reform proposals: Guarantee Clause enforcement, interstate compacts, revived cooperative federalism conditions, or state constitutional amendment coordination?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Publius: The Journal of Federalism (2024–2025 issues); State Attorneys General coalition documents; NCSL state policy tracking; Brennan Center state court report
- Academic: Publius journal annual state of federalism reports; Oxford Academic "State of American Federalism 2024–2025"; Harvard Law Review (federalism and national security, 2025)
- Practitioner: Council of State Governments; National Governors Association; State Innovation Exchange (SiX); State Democracy Defenders Fund
- Historical precedent: New Deal cooperative federalism; Civil rights era federalism conflicts; Reagan "new federalism" and devolution; ACA Medicaid expansion NFIB ruling

**Expert contact categories:** Federalism scholars (Publius editorial board); state AG network contacts; CSG policy analysts; Guarantee Clause constitutional scholars

**Timeline estimate:** 8–12 hours

### Zone C-4: Consent of Governed — Legitimacy and Participation Mechanisms

**Research questions:**
1. What democratic participation mechanisms beyond voting (participatory budgeting, citizens' assemblies, deliberative polling, direct democracy) have demonstrated the strongest effects on perceived legitimacy and civic health?
2. How does the current legitimacy crisis (64% of young Americans say democracy is "in trouble or failed") connect to structural participation deficits — and what institutional designs address it?
3. What is the theoretical framework for "authentic consent" in modern mass democracies — Rawls' public reason, Habermas' deliberative democracy, or participatory democratic theory — and what practical institutional structures follow from each?
4. How do Ireland's Citizens' Assemblies, Iceland's constitutional crowdsourcing (2011), and Australia's Voice referendum (2023) inform the design of consent-generation mechanisms in the US context?
5. What is the relationship between voter ID/suppression laws and democratic legitimacy? If a government is elected by a restricted franchise, is its claim to consent diminished in a measurable way?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: APA (American Political Science Association) democracy task force reports; state citizens' assembly models (Oregon Citizens' Assembly pilot, 2020); Ireland Citizens' Assembly documentation
- Academic: Habermas "Between Facts and Norms"; Fishkin "Democracy When the People Are Thinking"; Rawls "Political Liberalism"; Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Political Legitimacy)
- Practitioner: Deliberative Democracy Consortium; OECD Observatory of Public Sector Innovation (participatory democracy section); Niskanen Center democracy program
- Historical precedent: New England town meeting tradition; Progressive Era direct democracy (initiative, referendum, recall); Swiss direct democracy model

**Expert contact categories:** Deliberative democracy practitioners; political theorists; direct democracy scholars; citizens' assembly facilitators; OECD public governance analysts

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

### Zone C-5: Political Party Architecture and Reform

**Research questions:**
1. How does the current US party system (two-party duopoly reinforced by plurality voting, campaign finance law, ballot access barriers) compare to multi-party democracies in terms of representation quality and government responsiveness?
2. What is the evidence base for party organizational reform — specifically, does channeling campaign money through party organizations (rather than super PACs) reduce polarization as the Brookings/Rajah-Schaffner research suggests?
3. How do party primary systems affect representation? The Fuller Evidence Project's 2026 documentation shows 7% of partisan primary voters effectively choosing 87% of Congress — what structural reforms address this?
4. What are the most constitutionally and practically feasible party system reforms: top-two primaries, ranked-choice primaries, open primaries, mandatory same-day registration, or public financing tied to party participation requirements?
5. How should party platform and accountability mechanisms be strengthened — should party platforms be legally binding on elected officials, and what enforcement mechanisms exist internationally?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: FEC party committee funding rules; ballot access laws by state (Ballotpedia comprehensive tracker); state primary reform ballot measures
- Academic: Brookings "Party Polarization and Campaign Finance"; La Raja-Schaffner "Campaign Finance and Political Polarization: When Purists Prevail" (2014); British Journal of Political Science (political finance and party systems)
- Practitioner: FairVote (RCV research); Issue One; SIEPR "Moderates and Legislator Salaries" brief; Fulcrum coverage of 2026 primary problem
- Historical precedent: Progressive Era direct primary reform; Australian preference voting history; German 5% threshold and constructive vote of no confidence

**Expert contact categories:** Electoral reform advocates (FairVote); party finance scholars; comparative party systems researchers; state election administrators

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

### Zone C-6: Electoral Systems Design

**Research questions:**
1. What electoral system designs (FPTP, proportional representation, mixed-member, ranked-choice, multi-member districts) produce the most representative and stable governments — and what is the current comparative evidence base?
2. How does electoral system choice interact with two-party duopoly — can RCV or proportional representation be implemented within the existing constitutional framework, or does it require constitutional amendment?
3. What is the current legal landscape for electoral reform — is proportional representation in federal elections constrained by the Uniform Congressional District Act (2 USC § 2c)?
4. What do recent RCV elections (over 100 US elections analyzed by 2026) show about voter satisfaction, representation quality, and candidate civility effects?
5. How do the most successful electoral reform states (Alaska RCV, Maine RCV) inform a federal reform strategy — what can be replicated and what requires different legal treatment?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: 2 USC § 2c (single-member district requirement); Alaska and Maine RCV ballot measure texts; H.R. 4000 (Fair Representation Act); American Bar Association 2025 RCV analysis
- Academic: FairVote research database; V-Dem Electoral Democracy indicators; Electoral Systems Simulator (2025 open framework); International IDEA electoral system database
- Practitioner: FairVote; Represent.us; RepresentWomen; Voter Choice America
- Historical precedent: UK 2011 AV referendum failure; New Zealand 1996 switch to MMP; Australian preferential voting history

**Expert contact categories:** Electoral system scholars; FairVote researchers; comparative electoral systems experts; election law litigators

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

## DOMAIN D: JUDICIAL INDEPENDENCE

**Scope**: Courts as independent constitutional actors, constitutional interpretation theory, judicial selection processes, appellate structure and jurisdiction, comparative international court models.

**Phase 3 boundary**: Domain K covers federal judiciary restructuring (ethics enforcement, term limits, shadow docket, appointment reform, court expansion, judgeships expansion) as a reform agenda. Domain D goes deeper into the theoretical and structural foundations: what makes courts genuinely independent, what interpretive frameworks are most consistent with democratic legitimacy, and how do international court models address the problems the US is currently experiencing.

---

### Zone D-1: Courts as Independent Constitutional Actors

**Research questions:**
1. What structural features of court design — appointment process, tenure length, compensation protections, jurisdiction, enforcement mechanisms — are most empirically associated with genuine judicial independence versus nominal independence?
2. How does the insurance theory of independent courts (courts created by leaders who anticipate losing power) explain current US judicial dynamics — and what does it predict about the durability of current judicial alignment with executive power?
3. What is the current empirical measurement of US judicial independence? V-Dem judicial independence indicators show the US declining — what specific indicators have fallen and why?
4. What role do lower federal courts play in maintaining independence when the Supreme Court is captured — and what is the evidence from the current period (2025–2026)?
5. What institutional features protect individual judge independence from external pressure (threats, social media campaigns, ethics investigations as weapons)?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: UN Special Rapporteur on judicial independence (May 2025 statement); ABA journal articles on threats to judges; Judicial Conference security reports
- Academic: Cambridge Core "The Case for Independent Courts: The Insurance Theory of Judicialization in Autocracies" (2025); ELI-Mount Scopus European Standards of Judicial Independence (2025); V-Dem judicial independence indicators dataset
- Practitioner: American Judges Association; Federal Judges Association; National Center for State Courts; Brennan Center courts program
- Historical precedent: FDR court-packing failure; Nixon enemies list judicial targeting; Nixon v. United States (1993); Marbury v. Madison structural logic

**Expert contact categories:** Empirical judicial scholars; V-Dem researchers; comparative judicial independence specialists; judicial security experts

**Timeline estimate:** 10–12 hours

### Zone D-2: Constitutional Interpretation Theory

**Research questions:**
1. What is the evidence that originalism as currently practiced by the Roberts Court is selective — applied when results are ideologically convenient, abandoned otherwise? What are the most rigorous academic analyses of this selectivity?
2. How do alternative interpretive frameworks — living constitutionalism, common law constitutionalism, democratic process theory, structural interpretation — handle the legitimacy problem differently than originalism?
3. What are the strongest academic critiques of rule originalism (Columbia Law Review 2025) and selective originalism (Texas Law Review 2025) — and what do they imply for reform?
4. How does constitutional interpretation relate to democratic consent? If original meaning is 1787 white male landowning consent, is there a democratic legitimacy problem with textualist approaches applied to rights claims by excluded groups?
5. What is the most persuasive academic argument for a democratic-process approach to constitutional interpretation — the idea that courts should be especially skeptical of laws that restrict democratic participation (Ely's "Democracy and Distrust")?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Supreme Court opinions (Loper Bright, Corner Post, Jarkesy, Trump v. United States, Callais); Scalia's "A Matter of Interpretation" (1997)
- Academic: Michigan Law Review "After Courts: Democratizing Statutory Law" (2025); Columbia Law Review "Rule Originalism" (2025); Texas Law Review "Selective Originalism and Judicial Role Morality" (2025); John Hart Ely "Democracy and Distrust" (1980)
- Practitioner: Constitutional Accountability Center; American Constitution Society; Federalist Society debates (for opposing framing)
- Historical precedent: Lochner era judicial review; Warren Court democratic process theory; Rehnquist Court anti-commandeering; Roberts Court Chevron overruling

**Expert contact categories:** Constitutional theorists; law review editors; comparative constitutional scholars; ACS academic network

**Timeline estimate:** 8–12 hours

### Zone D-3: Judicial Selection Processes

**Research questions:**
1. What are the comparative models for judicial selection that best achieve both independence and democratic accountability — executive appointment, legislative confirmation, merit commissions, judicial elections, international applications processes?
2. What is the current state of the US federal judicial selection process — vacancy crisis in district courts, appointment politicization, confirmation obstruction history — and what reforms are most legally feasible?
3. How do Mexico's 2024 judicial reform (direct popular election of all judges) and Israel's 2025 judicial selection law reform compare as cautionary models — what went wrong and what does it show about the fragility of judicial independence when selection is politicized?
4. What is the Missouri Plan (merit selection) model and how have the 14 states that use it performed on judicial independence indicators versus partisan election states?
5. What is the IAALS (Institute for the Advancement of the American Legal System) research on judicial nominating commissions and what does it recommend for federal court adaptation?

**Sourcing strategy:**

- Academic: Comparative Constitutions Project (judicial selection provisions); arxiv "Convergences and Divergences in the 2024 Judicial Reform in Mexico" (neural network analysis); Tandfonline "Institutions of Independence? Judicial Councils and the Strategic Politics of Compliance" (2026)
- Practitioner: IAALS (iaals.du.edu); Brennan Center state court report; National Center for State Courts; American Judicature Society
- Historical precedent: Federalist No. 76-78 (Hamilton on appointment); Progressive Era campaign for judicial recall; Missouri Plan origins (1940)

**Expert contact categories:** IAALS researchers; judicial selection reform advocates; comparative judicial appointment scholars; state court administrators

**Timeline estimate:** 8–10 hours

### Zone D-4: Appellate Structure and Jurisdiction

**Research questions:**
1. How does the current circuit court structure (11 numbered circuits + DC + Federal) function as either a check on or transmission belt for Supreme Court doctrinal errors — and what structural reforms of the circuit system are available?
2. What is the current state of forum shopping in federal courts — specifically, how has the practice of filing in single-judge divisions in the Fifth Circuit enabled plaintiffs to select judges who will grant nationwide injunctions?
3. What jurisdiction-stripping proposals are currently on the table — can Congress strip SCOTUS appellate jurisdiction over specific constitutional questions (abortion, elections, executive removal) consistent with Ex Parte McCardle?
4. What is the optimal size and composition of the federal appellate system for the current caseload — the district court backlog (346% increase in cases pending) requires more judges, but what structural changes accompany the judgeships?
5. What do specialist courts (Federal Circuit, FISA Court, Court of International Trade) show about the benefits and risks of specialized federal adjudication?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Federal caseload statistics (uscourts.gov annual statistical tables 2025–2026); District of Montana order restricting nationwide injunctions; 28 USC § 1254, § 1257 (SCOTUS appellate jurisdiction)
- Academic: Federal courts scholarship on nationwide injunctions; Lawfare pieces on forum shopping; jurisdiction-stripping doctrine analysis
- Practitioner: Federal Bar Association; American College of Trial Lawyers; Brennan Center federal courts program
- Historical precedent: Reconstruction-era jurisdiction stripping (Ex Parte McCardle 1869); New Deal court-Congress conflict; Circuit Reorganization Act of 1948

**Expert contact categories:** Federal courts scholars; appellate practitioners; former circuit judges; judicial administration experts

**Timeline estimate:** 6–10 hours

### Zone D-5: International Court Models

**Research questions:**
1. What features of the European Court of Human Rights, Inter-American Court of Human Rights, African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights, and International Court of Justice are most applicable to US judicial independence reform debates?
2. How does the European Court of Justice's enforcement of rule-of-law standards against member states (Poland, Hungary) provide a model for federal enforcement of judicial independence standards against states?
3. What is the German Federal Constitutional Court's institutional design — mandatory retirement, term limits, supermajority appointment, eternity clauses protecting BVerfG structure — and how did the December 2024 Basic Law amendment strengthen those protections?
4. What is Canada's Supreme Court appointment process (independent Advisory Board established 2016) and how does the Bill C-9 judicial conduct reforms compare to the SCERT Act?
5. How do South Africa's Constitutional Court (transformative constitutionalism), India's Supreme Court (collegium system), and Australia's High Court (executive appointment without parliamentary confirmation) compare on independence and accountability?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: German Basic Law (Articles 92–104; 2024 amendment text); ELI-Mount Scopus European Standards of Judicial Independence (2025); ECHR Rules of Court; Canadian Judicial Council Bill C-9 implementation reports
- Academic: Cambridge Core "Judicial Independence in European Constitutional Law" (2025); Verfassungsblog (German constitutional court analysis); SMU comparative judicial independence (2025)
- Practitioner: Max Planck Institute for Comparative Public Law; Venice Commission (Council of Europe); CEJIL (Americas court system)
- Historical precedent: Nuremberg Tribunal design; International Military Tribunal institutional architecture; Rome Statute ICC negotiation history

**Expert contact categories:** Comparative constitutional court scholars; Venice Commission experts; BVerfG alumni; ECtHR practitioners

**Timeline estimate:** 10–12 hours

## DOMAIN E: MULTILATERAL COORDINATION

**Scope**: International treaty architecture, human rights enforcement frameworks, multilateral dispute resolution mechanisms, supranational governance models (EU, UN, regional bodies), exit/voice/loyalty dynamics in international institutions.

**Phase 3 gap**: Domain 57 (Multilateral Withdrawal / Executive Authority) covers the immediate crisis of US treaty withdrawal and executive overreach in international commitments. Domain E is the structural complement: what does functioning multilateral architecture look like, and how should the US reform its relationship with it?

---

### Zone E-1: International Treaty Architecture

**Research questions:**
1. What is the current US treaty engagement posture — how many treaties are active, how many have been unilaterally exited, and what is the constitutional and statutory basis for the executive withdrawal authority the current administration claims?
2. What treaties and international commitments most directly underpin democratic norms — human rights conventions, electoral integrity standards, anti-corruption conventions — and what is their current US status?
3. How does Congressional-executive agreement status differ from Article II treaty status, and which international commitments can be more durably protected by being elevated to Article II treaty status?
4. What is the BBNJ Agreement (entered into force January 2026) and what does its architecture show about current multilateral treaty-making capacity?
5. What treaty reform proposals would make international commitments more durable against unilateral executive withdrawal — sunset clauses, legislative ratification requirements, automatic re-entry provisions?

## DOMAIN F: IMPLEMENTATION FEASIBILITY

**Scope**: Transition pathways from current democratic erosion to reformed democratic architecture, sequencing of reforms, cost-benefit analysis of reform packages, scalability of successful models, contingency planning for implementation failures.

**Phase 3 gap**: Phase 3 research (Domains K and H) produced the reform agenda and identified the mechanisms and coalition architecture. Domain F asks the harder operational question: how do you actually implement these reforms at scale, what does it cost, what is the sequence, and what happens when it goes wrong?

---

### Zone F-1: Transition Pathways

**Research questions:**
1. What are the general lessons from successful democratic transitions — Spain's post-Franco transition, South Africa's post-apartheid consolidation, Poland's 1989 Solidarity transition, Eastern European EU accession transitions — about sequencing institutional reform?
2. What is the difference between democratic restoration (returning to prior institutional functioning) and democratic reform (building better structures than existed before) — and which is the appropriate frame for the US context?
3. What does Bangladesh's 2024–2025 transition (15 independent commissions, Muhammad Yunus technocratic government) show about phased reform implementation when governing institutions are contested?
4. How should reform sequencing be structured — economics first (stabilize civic participation conditions), constitutional/legal architecture second, enforcement third — or is simultaneity required for mutually reinforcing reforms?
5. What is Venezuela's Democratic Transition Framework (updated 2026) and what sequenced benchmarks does it offer that might inform a US-context transition planning document?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Bangladesh reform commission reports (January 2025); Atlantic Council "Updating the Democratic Transition Framework for Venezuela" (2026); International IDEA "Global State of Democracy 2025: Democracy on the Move"
- Academic: ScienceDirect "Economic Reform, Democracy and Growth during Post-Communist Transition" (comparative); ScienceDirect "Sequencing of Reform in Transition Economies"; GIGA Focus "Bangladesh's Democratic Transition" (2025)
- Practitioner: National Democratic Institute (NDI) transition support materials; International Republican Institute (IRI) democracy assistance frameworks; CFR "Pathways to Freedom" excerpts
- Historical precedent: Spain Moncloa Pacts (1977); South Africa CODESA process; Chile post-Pinochet pacted transition; Taiwan 1980s–1990s democratization

**Expert contact categories:** Transitional democracies scholars; NDI/IRI practitioners; comparative political scientists; Constitutional design scholars (IDEA network)

**Timeline estimate:** 10–12 hours

### Zone F-2: Reform Sequencing

**Research questions:**
1. What are the optimal sequencing principles for US democratic reform — which reforms create conditions for other reforms (enabling reforms) and which are most vulnerable to reversal if implemented too early?
2. How does the Phase 3 research (Domain K's six reform pathways, Domain H's three-track approach) create a sequencing framework — and what dependencies exist between judicial reform, constitutional architecture, and political governance reform?
3. What is the evidence from comparative research that "economic reform first" strategies succeed or fail at creating the political conditions for democratic consolidation — the debate between Washington Consensus sequencing and heterodox approaches?
4. How should reform windows (electoral cycles, court composition changes, supermajority windows) be integrated into sequencing — what reforms are only feasible in specific political moments?
5. What is the minimum viable reform package — the smallest set of reforms that creates self-reinforcing democratic stability, rather than reforms that can be individually reversed?

**Sourcing strategy:**
- Policy documents: Phase 3 Domain K research (six pathways with Tier 1/Tier 2 sequencing); Domain H (three-track approach: immediate statutory, medium-term reclamation, long-term amendment); IDEA Global State of Democracy reports
- Academic: "Economic Reform, Democracy and Growth" (ScienceDirect); Carothers "Critical Mission: Essays on Democracy Promotion" (2004); North-Wallis-Weingast "Violence and Social Orders"
- Practitioner: NDI research on reform sequencing; Brookings Governance Studies; Carnegie Endowment Democracy, Conflict, and Governance program
- Historical precedent: Korean democratization sequence (economic growth → middle class → democratic transition); Taiwan single-party to democracy; Portugal 1974–1976 Carnation Revolution sequencing failures and recoveries

**Expert contact categories:** Democratic consolidation scholars; Carnegie Endowment researchers; comparative political scientists; NDI program directors
