## Part 1: Domain Candidate Selection Framework

### What the First 47 Domains Cover — and Where the Gaps Are

The current framework has a clear structural bias: it was built from the institutional policy-maker perspective downward. The 35 Phase 1 domains are analytically strongest at the federal constitutional level — war powers, judicial independence, surveillance architecture, academic freedom, prosecutorial capture. Phase 2 domains 44–47 extended the frame into frontline policy sectors where Phase 1 had no standalone treatment: reproductive freedom, labor/NLRB, federal research suppression, and housing displacement. Domains 52 and 53 added civil society suppression and mutual aid legal infrastructure.

The remaining gap is not primarily topical. It is constituency-structural. The framework has not yet reached the organizing sectors that move fastest, amplify most broadly, and connect across the most other domains: the criminal justice abolitionist ecosystem, the environmental frontline community network, the LGBTQ+ legal and advocacy infrastructure, the campaign finance reform coalition, and the youth civic power network. These are not the same as the think-tank-and-AG-network audiences Phase 1 targets. They have different adoption models — faster, more decentralized, more relational — and they reach communities whose democratic exclusion is most acute in 2026.

### Selection Criteria for the Next Seven Domains

Four criteria govern prioritization. Each candidate domain must clear at least three of the four to qualify as a near-term priority; all four to qualify as Tier 1 urgent.

**1. Democratic system gap — not yet covered by any of the first 47 domains.** The framework has now addressed voting rights, judicial independence, healthcare, housing, reproductive freedom, labor rights, and epistemic infrastructure. The remaining gaps are: criminal justice as a structural civic exclusion mechanism (distinct from Domain 29's prosecutorial capture angle); environmental participation rights (distinct from Domain 15's environmental rollback tracker); LGBTQ+ rights as a legal infrastructure under simultaneous cross-domain attack; campaign finance as the connective tissue explaining how sector-specific captures operate; migrant civic participation beyond the detention enforcement frame of Domain 39; digital equity and algorithmic discrimination as democracy infrastructure; and youth civic power as its own structural domain.

**2. Timely advocacy window — hard external deadline within the research-to-distribution pipeline.** The most compelling domains have external events that make a research product actionable in a specific month: a SCOTUS ruling, a legislative committee vote, a ballot initiative signature deadline, a regulatory comment period. Domains without time-bound advocacy windows are lower priority regardless of their analytical importance.

**3. Influencer network overlap — broker organizations already in the distribution apparatus.** The fastest distribution velocity comes from domains where the primary recipient organizations have already received Phase 1 material or are one degree of separation from organizations that have. Phase 2 domains that can be introduced to existing contacts as an expansion — "this is what we've now added to the framework you already have" — will adopt faster than cold-outreach domains.

**4. Electoral and legislative trigger points — connection to November 2026 outcomes.** In 2026, every domain that can be framed as a pre-midterm research deliverable carries additional urgency. The midterm window (research ready by September 1, 2026 for maximum advocacy impact) effectively halves the available research time for domains in the October-November window. Domains that also have ballot measures, state legislative triggers, or judicial rulings timed to 2026 score highest.

### Identified Gaps in the Current 47-Domain Framework

Seven confirmed gaps remain:

- **Criminal justice as civic exclusion architecture**: Domain 29 covers prosecutorial weaponization and DOJ capture. No domain covers the structural democratic exclusion mechanism — felony disenfranchisement (4.4 million people), jury exclusion, public employment and housing restrictions, debt burden, and civic status degradation — as a systemic democracy problem distinct from individual prosecutorial abuse. The Movement for Black Lives policy ecosystem is the highest-leverage broker network in American progressive organizing and is currently unreached.

- **Environmental justice and community participation rights**: The environmental rollbacks tracker documents EPA and NEPA rollbacks. No domain analyzes environmental decision-making participation as a democratic right — the right of frontline communities to have voice in decisions that directly threaten their health, property, and survival. The Climate Justice Alliance's 100+ frontline organization network is the fastest-moving distribution channel into communities whose civic exclusion is most acute.

- **LGBTQ+ rights as systematic legal attack across multiple state and federal vectors**: 740 anti-LGBTQ bills in 42 states in 2026, 113 national bills. The Advocates for Trans Equality (merger of NCTE and TLDEF), Lambda Legal, and the ACLU LGBTQ+ program are an established litigation and advocacy infrastructure that is unreached by Phase 1's distribution apparatus.

- **Campaign finance as structural democracy infrastructure**: Dark money at $1.9 billion in 2024, four active 2026 state ballot measures targeting disclosure reform, and the DISCLOSE Act of 2026 with active legislative momentum. This domain is the meta-analysis — it explains how all the sector-specific captures documented throughout the framework (pharma, financial sector, energy) operate through a single Citizens United-derived legal architecture. Campaign Legal Center, Common Cause, and Issue One are all existing Phase 1 contacts or one degree removed.

- **Migrant civic participation**: Domain 39 covers detention enforcement. No domain covers the civic participation chilling effect — the documented suppression of school enrollment, healthcare access, and civic engagement by legally present people, the TPS/DACA status-termination crisis, and the asylum adjudication backlog that places 3.7 million people in permanent civic limbo.

- **Digital justice and algorithmic discrimination**: Domain 36 covers AI governance in government decision-making. No domain covers private-sector algorithmic discrimination in hiring, credit, housing, and insurance; broadband access equity as democratic infrastructure; or platform accountability and deplatforming as civil rights issues. The BEAD broadband program ($42.5B) was cut to $14B in the OBBBA, leaving 21 million Americans — disproportionately rural, low-income, and minority — without broadband infrastructure that is now prerequisite to civic participation.

- **Youth civic power**: Student visa revocations (6,000+), campus polling place closures, student ID restrictions, and OBBBA restrictions on Department of Education voter registration engagement are documented. No domain synthesizes these into a youth-specific democratic exclusion analysis with the organizing capacity of NextGen America, Campus Vote Project, and Students Learn Students Vote as the distribution infrastructure.

### Domain 48: Criminal Justice, Abolitionism, and the Civic Exclusion Architecture

**Why this domain.** Domain 29 documents prosecutorial weaponization — how DOJ is used as a political instrument by the executive. Domain 48 covers the structural downstream consequence: how criminal justice contact, conviction, and collateral consequences systematically remove people from the civic body. Felony disenfranchisement affects 4.4 million people; 19 states permanently bar people with felony records from jury service; public employment restrictions cover 40,000+ job categories at the federal level alone. The Brennan Center's "Democracy and Justice" framing and the Movement for Black Lives Policy Table are the bridge from abolitionist organizing to institutional advocacy. This domain has the highest cross-domain leverage multiplier of any remaining gap — M4BL's policy ecosystem touches criminal justice, reproductive justice, environmental justice, economic democracy, healthcare, and immigration simultaneously.

**Advocacy windows.** The Supreme Court's 2025-2026 term (OT2025) includes two cases with direct disenfranchisement implications. Several state legislative sessions are considering automatic restoration bills in 2026. The November 2026 midterms create a pre-election window for state-level disenfranchisement reform advocacy in the 20 states where restoration is pending.

**Key sources.** Brennan Center "Democracy and Justice" series; Sentencing Project annual disenfranchisement survey; Worth Rises debt burden data; Critical Resistance abolition framework; Michelle Alexander's "The New Jim Crow" updated with 2025-2026 enforcement data; Prison Policy Initiative civic exclusion research; individual state restoration progress reports.

**Influencer networks.** Movement for Black Lives Policy Table (50+ Black-led organizations), Critical Resistance, Brennan Center Justice Program, Worth Rises, Color of Change, NAACP Legal Defense Fund (existing Phase 1 contact through Domain 42 drug policy distribution).

**Estimated scope.** 6,500–7,500 words; 45–55 citations; 15–18 research hours. Cross-references: Domains 1, 22, 29, 39, 42, 44.

### Domain 49: Environmental Justice and Frontline Community Participation Rights

**Why this domain.** The environmental rollbacks tracker documents EPA and NEPA rollbacks at the federal level. Domain 49 covers the community participation rights dimension — the right of frontline communities to meaningful voice in environmental decisions, the destruction of EPA's environmental justice regional offices (all ten closed), the cancellation of $400 million in EJ grants, and the collapse of Title VI civil rights enforcement in environmental permitting. The analytical frame is democratic participation rights, not climate policy: communities that bear the greatest environmental burden are systematically denied the legal and procedural tools to challenge it. Earthjustice's active litigation (NEPA removal, Endangerment Finding revocation) and the Climate Justice Alliance's federated network provide immediate distribution pathways.

**Advocacy windows.** The EPA Endangerment Finding revocation is in the public comment phase through summer 2026. Multiple Earthjustice cases are in briefing in the D.C. Circuit. The June 2026 United Nations Environment Assembly and the IPCC regional chapter releases create international framing opportunities for the domestic EJ analysis. State-level EJ laws in California, New York, and New Jersey create implementation comparison points.

**Key sources.** EPA's formal environmental justice program termination orders; Earthjustice complaint documentation; Climate Justice Alliance organizational reports; WE ACT for Environmental Justice data; Deep South Center for Environmental Justice case documentation; Robert Bullard's environmental justice research archive; Title VI enforcement history and current status; international comparisons (EU and UK environmental participation rights frameworks).

**Influencer networks.** Climate Justice Alliance (100+ frontline orgs — the single fastest distribution channel for this domain), WE ACT for Environmental Justice, Deep South Center for Environmental Justice, Earthjustice (existing contact), Center for Biological Diversity, Indigenous Environmental Network. Bridge to Phase 1: NRDC and Sierra Club legal teams already have Domain 15 environmental rollback material.

**Estimated scope.** 6,000–7,000 words; 40–50 citations; 12–15 research hours. Cross-references: Domains 9, 15, 22, 27, G (tribal sovereignty), 39, 45.

A document that analyzes LGBTQ+ rights in isolation misses this structural connection; one that positions it as a component of the broader democratic erosion framework creates analytical value unavailable in existing LGBTQ+ advocacy literature. 740 anti-LGBTQ bills in 42 states, 113 national bills, four states moving anti-trans ballot measures to 2026 midterms — the advocacy window is acute. The Advocates for Trans Equality (AT4E, merger of NCTE and TLDEF), Lambda Legal, and GLSEN provide established distribution infrastructure directly into this sector.

**Advocacy windows.** Four anti-trans ballot measures are on November 2026 state ballots — research ready by August 2026 to enable state-level ballot advocacy integration. ### Domain 51: Campaign Finance, Dark Money, and Corporate Capture of Democratic Institutions

**Why this domain.** This is the meta-analysis domain: it explains the structural mechanism by which all of the sector-specific corporate captures documented throughout the framework operate. How did the pharmaceutical industry maintain its regulatory capture through five administrations? How does the financial sector continue to resist structural reform? How do energy companies shape environmental enforcement? The answer runs through Citizens United, shell corporation disclosure gaps, dark money 501(c)(4) vehicles, and the $1.9 billion in undisclosed 2024 spending. Four active 2026 ballot measures (Arizona, Massachusetts, Montana, North Dakota) are targeting dark money disclosure reform — creating a specific pre-November advocacy window. The DISCLOSE Act of 2026 has active Senate momentum with Whitehouse as lead sponsor (existing Phase 1 contact).

**Advocacy windows.** DISCLOSE Act of 2026 Senate markup window (late summer 2026). Four state ballot measures on November 2026 ballots — research ready by August 2026 to enable campaign integration. FEC commissioner vacancies create an administrative law angle (who is governing the agency that governs dark money). Four states are also considering state-level DISCLOSE equivalents in 2026 legislative sessions.

**Key sources.** OpenSecrets 2024 dark money database; Brennan Center campaign finance reform reports; Issue One nonpartisan disclosure research; FEC enforcement record and commissioner vacancy documentation; Citizens United decision and subsequent circuit court development; DISCLOSE Act of 2026 legislative text and CBO score; state ballot measure campaigns and their legal challenges; international comparisons (UK Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Act; Canada Elections Act disclosure requirements).

**Influencer networks.** Campaign Legal Center (existing Phase 1 contact adjacent — Issue One), Common Cause, Brennan Center Democracy Program, Issue One (former members of Congress network — 200+ members across both parties), End Citizens United / Let America Vote (electoral organizing branch). Bridge to existing contacts: Whitehouse Senate staff already receive Phase 1 judicial independence material — Domain 51 is a direct extension.

**Estimated scope.** 5,000–6,000 words; 40–50 citations; 10–12 research hours. Cross-references: Domains 3, 33, 37, 38, 42, 44.

## Part 3: Research Pipeline — June 2026 – May 2027

The pipeline is governed by three principles: hard external deadlines override internal sequencing; domains with shared influencer networks are released in clusters to enable combined outreach campaigns; and domains that are Tier 1 amplifiers of Phase 1 content (because they share target audiences) are prioritized over domains that open entirely new distribution channels.

**June 2026 (Research Month 1): Domain 48 + Domain 51**

Domain 48 (Criminal Justice / Civic Exclusion) and Domain 51 (Campaign Finance) are sequenced together because they share the Brennan Center as a common institutional contact point. Domain 48 research begins immediately given the SCOTUS OT2025 disenfranchisement cases and the midterm pre-window (research complete by end of June allows three months of advocacy integration before November). Domain 51 research begins concurrently: the DISCLOSE Act markup window and the four state ballot measure campaigns both have August deadlines for maximum advocacy impact.

*Dependency*: Domain 51 research benefits from completion of Domain 38 (Financial Sector Independence) cross-reference review — already complete. No blocking dependency.

**July 2026 (Research Month 2): Domain 49 + Domain 50**

Domain 49 (Environmental Justice) and Domain 50 (LGBTQ+ Rights) are the two domains with the highest urgency from their respective ballot measure campaigns. Domain 49 needs research complete before the EPA Endangerment Finding comment period closes (expected late July 2026). Domain 50 needs research complete by August 1 to be integrated into the four anti-trans ballot measure campaigns before early-vote periods open (September-October in most states).

*Dependency*: Domain 49 benefits from review of environmental rollbacks tracker (current through May 9) — no blocking dependency. Domain 50 benefits from Domain 44 cross-reference on reproductive justice / LGBTQ+ intersectionality — already complete.

**August 2026 (Distribution Month 1): Phase 2 Wave 1 outreach**

The four new Phase 2 domains (48-51) are distributed in a coordinated cluster campaign: (1) M4BL Policy Table receives Domain 48; (2) Campaign Legal Center and Common Cause receive Domain 51; (3) Climate Justice Alliance and Earthjustice receive Domain 49; (4) Advocates for Trans Equality and Lambda Legal receive Domain 50. Simultaneous outreach signals that the framework has made a coherent Phase 2 expansion into frontline organizing sectors — not just added isolated documents.

**September–October 2026 (Pre-midterm focus): Domain 52 and 53 distribution**

Domains 52 (Civil Society Suppression) and 53 (Mutual Aid) are already researched; their distribution is timed to the pre-midterm window when nonprofit compliance concerns and mutual aid infrastructure questions will be most acute. Florida HB 1471's July 1 effective date will have generated real-world enforcement data by September — the advocacy window for pre-enforcement litigation framing closes to post-enforcement narrative.

**November–December 2026 (Post-midterm analysis): Domain 54 initiation**

Domain 54 (Youth Civic Power) begins research immediately after the November midterms, integrating real 2026 data on campus turnout, polling place access outcomes, and student ID enforcement. A December 2026 research completion positions the document for January 2027 state legislative sessions considering campus access bills.

**January–March 2027 (Phase 3 Preview): Domains 55–57 scoping**

The next three candidate domains — Food Systems and Agricultural Justice (Domain 55), Church-State Separation and Christian Nationalism (Domain 56), and Economic Democracy / Worker Ownership (Domain 57) — are scoped in Q1 2027 based on Phase 2 adoption data. ## Part 4: Influencer Network Expansion

Phase 1 distribution reached a high-quality but structurally narrow network: law professors, AG offices, policy analysts, think-tank researchers, and legislative staff. Phase 2 domains 48–54 require cultivating three new network categories that Phase 1 did not systematically reach.

**State AG coalition expansion.** The Phase 1 AG playbook (24 Democratic AGs) focuses on litigation coordination and constitutional framing. Phase 2 domains — particularly Domain 48 (criminal justice civic exclusion), Domain 49 (environmental justice), and Domain 51 (campaign finance) — require reaching AG civil rights divisions and AG consumer protection divisions, not just the appellate and constitutional litigation teams. The state-level disenfranchisement restoration campaigns (ongoing in Florida, Alabama, Tennessee) are coordinated through AG offices in ways that require the civil rights division as the entry point, not the appellate team.

**State constitutional law and reform experts.** Phase 2 domains 48 and 49 both require engaging the state constitutional law scholar network — particularly those working on state ERAs (Equal Rights Amendments), state environmental rights provisions (Hawaii, Montana, New York), and state-level voting rights acts (Hawaii, Washington, California, Minnesota, Virginia's pending VROA). This network was outside Phase 1's scope because Phase 1 focused on federal constitutional questions. State constitutional reform is emerging as the primary near-term accountability layer as federal institutional capacity degrades.

**Ballot access and third-party legal infrastructure contacts.** Domain 51 (campaign finance) and the broader democratic renewal framework require engaging ballot access litigators and third-party advocates — the ACLU's Voting Rights Project state operations, the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights' state election teams, and the Institute for Free Speech (cross-partisan campaign finance reform) — who are not natural recipients of the current federal-institutional framing but are essential to the state-level ballot measure campaigns for dark money disclosure reform.

**Frontline community organizations and federated networks.** The Climate Justice Alliance, M4BL Policy Table, SisterSong, and National LGBTQ Task Force are broker organizations with infrastructure to distribute Phase 2 domains to 200–1,000 member organizations simultaneously. These organizations have different adoption models than think tanks: they do not adopt documents into formal research programs; they share them as movement resources in newsletters, training programs, and campaign toolkits. The distribution standard for these contacts is not a formal adoption signal — it is newsletter inclusion or training curriculum reference.

## Part 7: Success Metrics

Phase 2 success is not measured by research output volume. It is measured by whether the expansion into new constituencies generates adoption signals from those constituencies — and whether Phase 1 recipients who receive Phase 2 material deepen their engagement rather than treating it as additional unsolicited research.

**Organization adoption rate (primary metric).** For each Phase 2 domain, the target is two or more documented adoption events within 90 days of distribution. An adoption event is defined as: (a) a responding organization that cites a specific section in a public document, (b) an organization that requests integration into a training curriculum or toolkit, (c) an organization that shares the document with its own membership network, or (d) an organization that makes a follow-up outreach contact for consultation. Phase 1 set the baseline; Phase 2 domains should achieve similar or higher per-domain adoption rates given their more targeted sector distribution.

**Policy influencer engagement (secondary metric).** Three specific influencer classes to track: (1) State AGs who receive Phase 2 domains 48–51 and subsequently file litigation or legislative testimony that references the framework; (2) Brennan Center or EPI researchers who cite a Phase 2 domain in a published report; (3) Congressional staff who request domain documents directly, signaling legislative research integration. These are leading indicators of institutional adoption that precede formal citation by 4–8 weeks.

**Media pickup (tertiary metric).** Phase 2 domains that reach frontline organizing networks will generate different media pathways than Phase 1 institutional outreach: movement media (Truthout, In These Times, Democracy Now) rather than elite policy media (Politico, The Atlantic). Track separately. Both matter, but movement media pickup indicates the framework is being used as an organizing resource, which is the Phase 2 constituency goal.

**Institutional endorsement (12-month target).** By May 2027, the target is one formal institutional endorsement — an organization that formally incorporates the Phase 2 framework into its published advocacy infrastructure, names it as a primary resource in a grant application or organizational strategy document, or co-produces a derivative document with attribution. This threshold distinguishes sustained institutional adoption from transient research consumption.

**Phase 1 ongoing adoption tracking (concurrent).** Phase 2 expansion does not replace Phase 1 monitoring. Domain 37 baseline re-measurement is scheduled for December 2026 (seven quantified metrics established in Session 717). Litigation tracker continues monthly maintenance passes. Adoption tracking dashboard (specified in adoption-tracking-dashboard-spec.md) tracks Phase 1 domain citations continuously regardless of Phase 2 expansion activity.
