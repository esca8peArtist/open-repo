### Branch 3: Controversy or Adversary Response

**Definition**: Institutional resistance, negative press attention, or targeted pushback from actors with incentives to discredit the framework.

This is the least likely branch but requires a pre-positioned response because it is the one that cannot be improvised.

**Most likely form of adversary response:**

The research is published under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0, which prevents copyright claims but does not prevent misrepresentation of the content. The most plausible adversary action is selective quotation to misrepresent domain conclusions as partisan advocacy rather than structural analysis. The second most plausible is an attempt to discredit the methodology (independent researcher, no institutional affiliation).

**Pre-positioned response framework:**

- The methodological credibility question has already been pre-empted by the research approach: 160+ case studies, V-Dem data, primary court records, legislative text. The framework does not assert partisan conclusions — it documents structural mechanisms with evidentiary support. The response to a methodology challenge is to reference the primary sources, not to defend the conclusions.
- If a specific domain is targeted as partisan: the framework's structure insulates it. Each domain's reform proposals include bipartisan or non-partisan structural mechanisms (independent agencies, statutory requirements, judicial standing) not tied to partisan electoral outcomes. Point to the specific reform proposals, not the crisis documentation.
- Do not respond publicly to adversary characterizations. Engage only if a named academic or institutional contact asks for clarification. Respond in writing, briefly, with references to the primary sources.
- If an adversary response generates press attention: contact Goodman (Just Security) and Bassin (Protect Democracy) if they have already responded positively. Both organizations have institutional credibility to contextualize the framework for press inquiries without you needing to speak directly.

**Institutional pushback from the contacts themselves:**

If a Batch 1 contact pushes back on a specific domain conclusion (most likely to come from Weiser on election administration characterizations or from Goodman on war powers legal theory), this is the most valuable feedback you can receive. Do not defend the conclusion. Ask what the correct framing would be. A methodological correction from Wendy Weiser is worth more than 100 social media shares.
