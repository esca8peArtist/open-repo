## Part I: Domain 19 Geopolitical Deepening

### The Gap

The existing framework documents war powers constitutionalism (Domain 19f) and Venezuela unilateralism (Domain 28) in detail. What is missing is the geopolitical cascade analysis: how US institutional commitment degradation — the combination of unilateral war-making, NATO abandonment threats, Taiwan credibility erosion, and adversary opportunism — creates conditions in which allied democracies become more vulnerable and adversary ambitions are emboldened. This is not redundant with the war powers analysis. It is the systemic downstream consequence of the documented institutional failures.

### What the Research Found (April 2026)

**NATO commitment degradation — documented:**

The April 2026 reporting establishes a clear factual record. Trump threatened withdrawal from NATO after allied members refused to provide bases, airspace, or naval support for the Iran war. He called allied leaders "cowards." He publicly made Article 5 commitments conditional on allies' willingness to support US military ventures outside NATO's geographic mandate — a direct inversion of the alliance's text, which limits Article 5 to armed attacks in Europe or North America. The Carnegie Endowment's April 2026 assessment found that trust in US Article 5 commitment has "crossed a line" and "there will be no return to business as usual in NATO, during neither this U.S. administration nor the next one."

The 2023 National Defense Authorization Act includes a provision (Kaine-Rubio) requiring Senate two-thirds concurrence or an Act of Congress to withdraw from NATO. Trump cannot unilaterally exit, but he can: reduce personnel assigned to NATO, eliminate participation in exercises, declare publicly that the US will not honor Article 5, close bases in non-cooperative countries, and withdraw from NATO command structures. The legal constraints prevent formal withdrawal; they do not prevent functional nullification.

The democratic vulnerability angle is specific: six European NATO members — Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Finland, Sweden — are frontline democracies that have structured their entire defense posture around the credibility of Article 5. When Trump makes Article 5 conditional, these countries face a choice between appeasement of Russia (to reduce the military threat without US protection) or dramatically accelerated defense spending that strains their democratic welfare states. Germany's Chancellor Merz framed this accurately: "a trans-Atlantic stress test" whose outcome will determine whether European democratic alliances survive US institutional erosion.

**Taiwan security degradation — documented:**

The Iran war has created a measurable Taiwan vulnerability window. USS Abraham Lincoln has been redeployed to the Persian Gulf, leaving one carrier in the Asia-Pacific. US reconnaissance sorties in the South China Sea have fallen 30% since February 2026. A Marine Expeditionary Unit was redeployed from Japan. 48 THAAD interceptors were shifted off the Korean Peninsula.

China's PLA has conducted an elevated pace of exercises, including naval drills in the South Pacific and Gulf of Tonkin in January 2026 ("Justice Mission-2025"), and carrier operations in the South China Sea. Beijing is "working subtly to shape the regional environment ahead of the meeting — and take advantage of Washington's focus on the Iran war" (The Diplomat, April 2026).

Taiwan's own procurement pipeline is degraded: Trump refused to approve $400 million in military aid for Taiwan in September 2025, and defense procurement delays leave Taiwan militarily dependent on systems that have not been delivered. Only 34% of Taiwanese believe the US is a credible partner. The US intelligence community assesses that the PLA has orders to be ready for a Taiwan contingency by 2027.

The democratic vulnerability angle is direct: Taiwan is a functioning democracy — the 2024 IDEA Democracy Index rates it 8.92, placing it in the "full democracy" category alongside most European allies. A US credibility collapse in the Taiwan Strait is not merely a geopolitical event; it is the potential disappearance of a democracy of 23 million people. The institutional erosion documented throughout this framework (WPR nullification, unilateral war-making, alliance degradation) has a concrete human and democratic cost at the other end.

**Iran geopolitical cascade — documented:**

The Iran war's cascade effects on other democratic institutions are documented by the Foreign Policy "Strategic Aftershocks" analysis (April 20, 2026):

- Oil prices spiked 50%, providing Moscow a massive revenue windfall at the moment when Western sanctions were beginning to bite. In March 2026, the US suspended sanctions on purchases of Russian oil to relieve energy pressure — a direct policy concession to Moscow's energy leverage that had been a cornerstone of Western democratic solidarity with Ukraine.
- US missile defense assets have been diverted from Ukraine to the Persian Gulf, leaving Ukrainian cities more vulnerable to Russian attacks at the moment of the war's most intense phase.
- China is receiving an "intelligence dividend" from the conflict: observing US operational tempo, weapons performance, and force deployment patterns in a live conflict environment — information that directly informs PLA planning for a Taiwan contingency.
- The Global South is increasingly interpreting US actions as evidence that international law is selectively applied by great powers, reducing diplomatic costs to other authoritarian actors who wish to act similarly.
- Carnegie's International Crisis Group (Crisis Monitor #2, April 21, 2026) documents the Iranian domestic situation: Supreme Leader Khamenei's death in the February 28 strikes has created a succession crisis, with hardliners mobilizing against the prospect of ceasefire negotiations. The instability creates a binary outcome: either a negotiated resolution that leaves Iran as a humiliated but intact state, or a prolonged conflict that exhausts US military resources and credibility simultaneously.

**The democratic vulnerability synthesis:**

The key analytical insight that no existing domain makes explicit: democratic institutions have external security requirements. A democracy under military siege — whether from Russia (Baltic states), China (Taiwan), or domestic militarism (all US allies watching the WPR nullification) — cannot maintain its democratic character indefinitely. The Project on Government Oversight and dozens of foreign policy analysts have noted that the US Iran war posture has simultaneously: (1) demonstrated that US presidents will start wars without congressional authorization; (2) demonstrated that US presidents will ignore the 60-day clock when it expires; (3) made US security commitments to allies conditional on their compliance with US unilateral military decisions; and (4) created a military and economic environment in which Russia and China have both gained strategic advantage.

The resistance research implication: democratic recovery in the US requires not just domestic institutional reform but international credibility restoration. The two are connected. Allies who distrust US Article 5 commitments will not cooperate on democratic standards enforcement. Taiwan facing Chinese military pressure while the US military is stretched across the Persian Gulf cannot maintain democratic governance without explicit, legally-binding US security commitments backed by the democratic accountability mechanisms that were bypassed in the Iran war.

## Part II: Domains 21/25 — FISA Section 702 Outcome Tracking

### The Gap

Domain 21 (Data Privacy and Digital Surveillance) and the surveillance-tracking.md document have thorough pre-deadline documentation of the 702 reauthorization fight. The outcome — lapse, clean extension, or reform package — will be the definitive data point for assessing whether the civil liberties coalition can hold and whether the commercial data broker loophole survives as a surveillance mechanism.

### What the Research Found (as of April 27, 2026)

The situation is unresolved as of this writing. Four attempts to reauthorize have failed. The Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act (three-year, no warrant requirement, enhanced internal oversight) is the live vehicle as of April 24. Speaker Johnson has attempted to attach it to unrelated legislation. The SAVE Act attachment demand from Trump further complicates the vote count. April 30 is tomorrow.

**Key civil liberties stakes:**

1. **Warrant requirement**: The core reform that civil liberties advocates want — and that has blocked every reauthorization attempt. The Foreign Intelligence Accountability Act explicitly excludes it. Sen. Ron Wyden called the included reforms "fake." Rep. Warren Davidson called them "robust." The bipartisan coalition (Freedom Caucus + progressive Democrats) that killed the five-year and 18-month proposals has not formally committed to blocking the three-year proposal.

2. **Commercial data broker loophole**: The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act, which would close the loophole through which ICE and DHS purchase commercial location data without a warrant, is not attached to any live FISA vehicle. None of the reauthorization proposals would close this loophole. This means: regardless of the FISA outcome, the primary surveillance mechanism documented in this research (Venntel/Babel Street/Accurint location tracking of protest environments and immigrant communities) remains legally unaddressed.

3. **What a lapse means structurally**: If Section 702 lapses, it does not eliminate NSA surveillance — it creates legal vulnerability for the specific 702 program while surveillance continues under EO 12333 (less legally constrained, entirely outside FISA protections). Civil liberties organizations have litigation ready to convert a lapse into a court challenge. Historical pattern: Congress has never allowed a major surveillance authority to lapse for more than hours; the intelligence community's pressure for reauthorization is typically irresistible. Even if 702 lapses on April 30, it will almost certainly be reauthorized within days.

**Post-deadline tracking protocol:**

Once the outcome is known, the surveillance-tracking.md document should be updated with:
- The vote result and final bill text
- Whether the warrant requirement was included or excluded
- Whether the commercial data broker loophole was addressed
- What civil liberties organizations say about the outcome
- The new expiration date (if reauthorized for 3 years: April 2029; if for 2 years: April 2028)

### Effort Estimate

- **Type**: Light refresh to existing surveillance-tracking.md
- **Scope**: 300-500 words
- **Sources needed**: Brennan Center 702 resource page post-outcome, EFF statement, vote record
- **Time required**: 30 minutes once outcome is known
- **Output location**: New section in surveillance-tracking.md, Part 1, Section 702 "Outcome"
- **Readiness**: Research-ready the moment the outcome is confirmed (April 30 or shortly after)

## Part III: Domain 33 — Ballot Initiative Suppression New State Examples

### The Gap

Section 1.3 of Domain 33 documents ballot initiative suppression through three mechanisms (pre-election barriers, supermajority requirements, post-approval nullification). The original research centered on Missouri, Florida, Oklahoma, North Dakota, South Dakota, and the 2025-2026 aggregate statistics. The task called for 3-5 new state-specific examples and a map of simultaneous action.

### What the Research Found

Six additional state examples are documented with sufficient specificity for integration:

**Utah — simultaneous three-mechanism attack:**
The Utah legislature enacted a $1.4 million newspaper publication fee (pre-election barrier), a 60% supermajority threshold specifically for tax-related initiatives, and a mandatory funding disclosure requirement (pre-election). These three mechanisms operate simultaneously, targeting the ballot initiative on cost, threshold, and procedural grounds at once. The funding disclosure requirement was framed as transparency — but the $1.4 million publication fee, which must be paid by ballot sponsors, is an economic barrier that functions as a wealth filter on direct democracy. Senate President Stuart Adams's public framing — that unelected "special interest groups" were using initiatives to "override our republic" — is notable for characterizing the people's direct vote as an override of representative government, inverting the constitutional design of direct democracy.

**Ohio — the supermajority precedent fight:**
Ohio Secretary of State Frank LaRose and State Rep. Brian Stewart proposed a 60% supermajority threshold for ballot initiatives — rejected after advocacy pressure, but the attempt revealed the coalition. The proposal was timed during a lame-duck session alongside a strict photo ID voting requirement and a bill stripping the state board of education. The simultaneity is documented: these are not independent policy decisions, they are a coordinated restructuring of the democratic infrastructure. Ohio courts ultimately blocked the supermajority proposal, but the attempt established the template.

**Nebraska — legislature overrides and the 4/5 counter-response:**
Nebraska's Republican-controlled legislature has systematically overridden voter-approved initiatives: it weakened the 2024 voter-approved paid sick leave law (adding restrictions and exceptions through a two-thirds majority vote) and slow-rolled the voter-approved medical cannabis law through administrative rulemaking. In response, civil society advocates launched the "Respect Nebraska Voters" initiative — a constitutional amendment requiring a four-fifths legislative vote (40 of 49 senators) to change or repeal voter-approved laws, up from the current two-thirds requirement. The counter-initiative must reach voters by July 2026. Nebraska is thus operating as a real-time laboratory for both the attack mechanism (override) and the democratic counter (supermajority protection amendment).

**Arizona — judicial mechanism:**
The Arizona Supreme Court is actively weighing a constitutional challenge to the state's voter-approved Proposition 211 (dark money disclosure law). The mechanism here is judicial, not legislative: the legislature enacted the initiative suppression via proxy, by relying on a sympathetic court to strike down a voter-approved transparency measure that would have constrained the dark money funding ballot initiative attacks. Campaign Legal Center's analysis identifies this as a direct circuit: dark money funds campaigns against disclosure requirements; the dark money-funded legislators appoint or elect judges who strike down disclosure requirements; the undisclosed dark money then funds subsequent attacks on future initiatives. Arizona's dark money fight is the most complete documented example of the judicial mechanism operating as the third leg of the suppression apparatus alongside legislative and financial mechanisms.

**Montana — anti-dark-money counter-initiative under judicial attack:**
Montana Initiative 194 ("The Montana Plan") would prohibit corporations, nonprofits, trusts, and all artificial persons from contributing to state and local candidate and ballot measure elections — effectively eliminating dark money in Montana politics. The initiative represents the most comprehensive attempt to close the dark money pipeline funding ballot initiative attacks. Industry groups and corporations filed suit to block it before it could reach voters. The Montana Attorney General attempted to block it from the ballot on procedural grounds (the AG's office said it "doesn't meet legal requirements" in October 2025 — a challenge that was dismissed by the Montana Supreme Court in April 2026). The pattern: a voter-driven transparency measure is attacked by the same dark money interests it would constrain, using the legal process to prevent it from reaching the ballot. The Montana case inverts the usual attack vector — instead of the legislature restricting initiatives, the dark money interests are using courts to block a pro-transparency initiative. Both vectors — attacking initiatives that restrict dark money, and restricting initiatives generally — have the same beneficiary.

**Arkansas — criminalizing volunteer organizing:**
Arkansas enacted SB 207 and SB 210, requiring canvassers to warn signers of petition fraud penalties (which had a chilling effect on volunteer participation) and requiring signers to physically read complete ballot language or the canvasser faces criminal penalties. Both bills were blocked by courts after challenge by BISC. The criminal penalty provisions for volunteer canvassers are the most direct documented attempt to convert civic participation in the signature-gathering process into a criminal liability — an innovation in the suppression toolkit that, if upheld, would deter paid and volunteer canvassing nationwide.

| Florida | $1M bond, felony disqualification, SSN requirement | — | — | — | Yes | Litigation by BISC |

**The coordination evidence**: The BISC's Fairness Project September 2025 report and ProPublica's April 2026 investigation both document "strikingly similar arguments" across multiple states — "out-of-state interests," "fraud prevention," "unelected special interests overriding representative government." This language is consistent with ALEC model legislation deployment and shared messaging from organizations like Americans for Prosperity and the US Chamber of Commerce. The BISC's "attacks are increasingly systematic, institutionalized, and forward-looking" language reflects the same observation: this is not coincidence; it is a coordinated field operation.

**Three-mechanism simultaneity in specific states**: Missouri, Utah, and Ohio demonstrate all three mechanisms operating concurrently: raising procedural barriers before signature collection, raising the threshold at the ballot, and overriding approved measures through legislative action. When all three mechanisms operate simultaneously in a state, the expected-value of any citizen initiative campaign approaches zero: the barriers make it expensive to qualify, the threshold makes it hard to pass, and the override mechanism voids it if it succeeds. The residual function of ballot initiatives — as a release valve on captured legislative bodies — is effectively eliminated.

- **Readiness**: Write-ready now. All source material confirmed.

## Part VI: Impact of Optional Domains on Distribution

### Path A (Distribute now, 35 domains) — No additional research required

The 35-domain framework is complete, production-ready, and distributable without any optional Phase 2 work. Optional domains add marginal value at the margin; they do not fill critical gaps.

### Path A + Hybrid (Distribute now, complete 2-3 quick items first)

The three autonomous items listed above (Domain 19 deepening, FISA update, Domain 33 addendum) require approximately 2 additional sessions and would allow the framework to be described as:
- Geopolitically current (NATO/Taiwan/Iran cascade analysis included)
- Surveillance-tracking current (702 outcome documented)
- Ballot initiative empirically deepened (8-state simultaneous action map)

None of these change the domain count or distribution templates. They are depth additions to existing content.

### Path B (Delay distribution, complete Phase 2 expansion first)

If Path B is chosen, the full optional queue adds approximately 8-12 sessions of work before distribution. The additions that most significantly change the distributable product:
- Institutional playbooks (Candidate 2): Converts the proposal from research to action guide — fundamentally different
- Adversary response modeling (Candidate 3): Adds strategic sophistication valued by think tanks and institutional audiences
- International precedent deepening (Candidate 4): Strengthens academic credibility

The risk of Path B is time: each month of delay reduces the proposal's currency (court cases resolve, legislative sessions end, political dynamics shift), and each month without distribution is a month the coalition-building work has not started.

**Assessment**: Path B is only worth choosing if the institutional playbooks (Candidate 2) are considered essential for the distribution target. If the target is researchers, journalists, and civil society organizations who can operationalize from the existing proposal structure, Path A with the three autonomous additions is sufficient. If the target is sector-specific practitioners who need explicit action guidance (AG coalitions, law school clinics, civil service unions), the playbooks add genuine value.
