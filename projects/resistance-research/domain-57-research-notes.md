## Key Findings Summary

### Finding 1: The Constitutional Gap is Structural, Not Accidental

The asymmetry between treaty *entry* (requires Senate two-thirds supermajority) and treaty *exit* (no constitutional requirement) was not designed — the Framers simply did not address withdrawal. Three centuries of political practice have resolved the uncertainty in the executive's favor through the path of least resistance: no court has ever invalidated a presidential treaty withdrawal, and *Goldwater v. Carter* established a non-justiciability precedent that has insulated this executive power from judicial review for 45 years.

The Nebraska Law Review analysis establishes a viable workaround: where Congress *explicitly* prohibits withdrawal by statute (as with Section 1250A), the Youngstown framework demands the president demonstrate exclusive constitutional authority. No such authority is established in precedent. This is the litigation opening that currently lacks enforcement standing.

### Finding 2: The IEEPA Tariff Ruling is the Strongest New Doctrinal Resource

The February 2026 *Learning Resources* decision is the most important new legal development since the research outline was written (May 17, 2026). The Supreme Court's reasoning — that Congress's core Article I powers (taxing, tariff) cannot be seized by executive emergency declaration — is directly analogous to the treaty argument. Just as Article I vests tariff authority in Congress, Article II vests treaty-entry authority in the President and Senate jointly. The parallel argument: just as IEEPA cannot delegate Article I tariff power by implication, an OLC opinion cannot vest exclusive Article II exit authority in the president by assertion.

This doctrinal parallel should be the centerpiece of the constitutional argument in distribution materials for legal audiences.

### Finding 3: The Section 1250A Standing Gap is the Most Tractable Reform

The most consequential near-term reform gap is not substantive law — Section 1250A already exists — but enforcement standing. The Lawfare analysis identifies three pathways, with the Congressional Standing Authorization Act as the most robust. This reform is politically achievable because it extends rather than creates new protective law, and it has bipartisan precedent (Kaine-Rubio co-sponsorship of Section 1250A).

The recommended advocacy framing: "Congress passed the law but forgot to include the enforcement mechanism. Fix the enforcement gap." This is less politically contentious than passing new treaty protection legislation and more immediately actionable.

### Finding 4: Hungary's 2026 Reversal is the Empirical Anchor for Democratic Resilience

The April 12, 2026 Hungarian election result — Tisza supermajority ending Orbán's 16-year rule — provides the most important comparative data point for the production document. It demonstrates:
- Even well-entrenched institutional erosion (16 years, constitutional capture, media capture) is reversible through democratic elections
- International institutional pressure (EU financial conditionality, €36 billion frozen) contributed to economic conditions that drove voter defection
- The institutions that survived are the ones that enabled the reversal — the EU membership that Orbán exploited was also the mechanism that maintained economic pressure

The counter-argument — that US withdrawal from international institutions makes democratic reversal harder rather than preventing it — is directly supported by the Hungarian evidence.

### Finding 5: The UPR Withdrawal Created a Measurable Domestic Civil Society Accountability Deficit

The August 2025 UPR withdrawal is underappreciated in its domestic implications. The UPR process was not just international accountability — it was a subsidized civil society documentation mechanism. ACLU, Human Rights Watch, and NAACP used UPR cycles to generate international documentation of racial injustice, immigration detention conditions, and Indigenous rights violations that then fed back into domestic advocacy and litigation. The withdrawal eliminates this documentation pipeline.

This is a cross-domain connection to Domain 58 (Tribal Sovereignty): tribal nations' international law arguments about consultation obligations and treaty violations were part of the UPR documentation landscape. The US withdrawal from UPR reduces the international documentation layer that tribal advocates used in international forums.

## Evidence Gaps Remaining

**Gap 1: Full treaty-status mapping of the 66 organizations**
The White House list names organizations; the specific treaty obligations and their ratification history need independent mapping. Some withdrawals may be from executive agreements (easier to exit) vs. Senate-ratified treaties (more constitutionally protected). This mapping would strengthen the constitutional argument by identifying which withdrawals are in Youngstown Zone 3 vs. Zone 2. Estimated research: 4-6 hours. Available through State Department Treaty Database and ASIL publications.

**Gap 2: ICC sanctions real-world impact on court functioning**
PBS reporting documents Microsoft email cutoff and bank account freezes. Deeper documentation of which specific investigations have been slowed or blocked would strengthen the empirical argument. Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch maintain ICC tracking resources.

**Gap 3: Quantified domestic civil society impact of UPR withdrawal**
The EJIL: Talk! analysis documents the normative argument; empirical documentation of which specific domestic advocacy campaigns used UPR documentation for US litigation or legislative advocacy would strengthen the domestic accountability argument. ACLU and Human Rights Watch archives are the primary sources.

**Gap 4: Post-*Learning Resources* fallback tariff litigation**
The administration imposed fallback tariffs under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 after the IEEPA ruling. Are these being challenged? WilmerHale and Lawfare have the current docket. Understanding the scope of the remaining tariff authority matters for the broader executive unilateralism argument.
