# Institutional Resilience Implementation Guide
## For Foundations, Think Tanks, and Civil Society Organizations

**Who this is for**: Foundation program officers and executive directors; think tank research directors; civil society organization board chairs and executive directors; organizational counsel; and any governance body responsible for an institution's long-term independence.

**The core task**: Use the 35-domain framework — specifically the analysis of how democratic backsliding enables institutional capture — to design your organization's resilience against the four known capture mechanisms, build operational redundancy, and protect the institutional independence necessary to do democracy work through a period of sustained pressure.

---

## The Monday Morning Question

Your organization has received a subpoena, a funding threat, an IRS inquiry, or a demand to disclose donor lists. Or you have not received any of those yet — but you have read Domain 29's analysis of the SPLC indictment and know that prosecutorial weaponization is now operating against civil society monitoring organizations. What institutional structures should you have in place before the demand arrives?

Answer: run the capture vulnerability audit, close the gaps, and build the independence safeguards the framework identifies as decisive in comparative cases.

## Five-Step Decision Tree

**Step 1: Run the four-track capture vulnerability audit.**

Domain 27 (Higher Education) documents a compound four-track assault: funding leverage, ideological compliance demands, targeting of individuals, and accreditation weaponization. The same four-track model applies to civil society organizations:

- **Track 1: Funding leverage.** Federal grants, contracts, or tax-exempt status used as leverage for compliance. Does your organization have federal funding exposure? What percentage of your budget flows through federally controlled channels (including pass-through grants from state agencies)?
- **Track 2: Ideological compliance demands.** Requirements to comply with federal ideological priorities as a condition of operating. Has your organization received any demands — formal or informal — to modify programs, publications, or positions as a condition of continued operation?
- **Track 3: Individual targeting.** Subpoenas, visa actions, criminal referrals, or IRS inquiries targeting key staff. Does your organization have legal defense infrastructure for staff? Does your board chair know what to do if executive leadership is targeted?
- **Track 4: Structural interference.** Changes to the legal or regulatory environment that constrain your organization's activities. Is your organization's tax-exempt classification, lobbying capacity, or donor privacy vulnerable to regulatory change?

Score each track: Green (no current vulnerability), Yellow (potential vulnerability, mitigation underway), Red (active vulnerability, immediate action required).

**Step 2: Eliminate federal funding dependency on a two-year timeline.**

The most important structural change a civil society organization doing democracy work can make in 2026 is reducing federal funding dependency. Organizations with more than 25% of their budget in federal grants are structurally vulnerable to funding leverage (Track 1) in ways that no amount of institutional courage can fully mitigate — when the checks stop, operations stop.

The replacement strategy: private foundation diversification, individual donor base expansion (small-dollar giving is structurally independent of institutional funding leverage), and earned revenue where mission-compatible. The two-year timeline is realistic for organizations with existing donor bases; it requires earlier action for organizations that have never built individual donor programs. The framework's Domain 7 (Rights Protection) analysis of whistleblower protections and its Domain 29 analysis of the SPLC indictment's suppression logic are both relevant here: the goal of the suppression is not conviction — it is resource depletion and deterrence. An organization that can survive a 12-18 month legal siege without federal funding has substantially higher deterrence value than one that cannot.

**Step 3: Design organizational redundancy for leadership continuity.**

Domain 29 documents that prosecutorial suppression operates through process, not conviction. A key executive being indicted, subpoenaed, or arrested — even on charges that will be dropped or acquitted — can effectively incapacitate an organization if there is no succession plan. This is not hypothetical: the SPLC indictment (April 21, 2026) was announced by the FBI Director personally, with maximum public visibility, on charges with documented legal defects. The goal was deterrence and disruption, not conviction.

Resilience design for leadership continuity:
- Document every key operational process that currently lives only in a single person's head
- Establish deputy authority for every decision type, in writing, with board ratification
- Create an emergency board activation protocol — under what conditions does the board convene within 24 hours, and what decisions can it make without executive leadership present?
- Retain outside counsel in advance on a standing retainer, briefed on your organization's work, who can respond within hours to an enforcement action
- Establish secure communication channels that do not depend on organizational email infrastructure (which can be subject to seizure or access demands)

**Step 4: Build institutional independence safeguards against board capture.**

The comparative democratic recovery research (Section 3.5) documents that autocratic enclaves persist after electoral change because of institutional capture at the governance level — judges, board members, and executives installed during the backsliding period who remain in place. The civil society analog: foundations and think tanks can experience board capture if their governance structures allow ideologically motivated actors to gain board seats and then shift organizational mission.

Independence safeguards at the governance level:
- Require supermajority (two-thirds or three-quarters) for any mission statement change, organizational merger, or dissolution
- Establish staggered board terms with overlapping classes, so no single election cycle can produce a majority of new board members
- Codify staff input on board appointments — board members who will not operate collaboratively with staff are captured governance liabilities
- Review your organization's conflict-of-interest policy for financial relationships between board members and government entities that may create leverage

**Step 5: Integrate the framework's three-wave timeline into your organization's strategic plan.**

The implementation roadmap defines Wave 1 (0-18 months), Wave 2 (months 6-36), and Wave 3 (years 3+). Foundation program officers and think tank research directors should align their grant-making and research agendas to this timeline. Wave 1 is where the most urgent under-investment exists: litigation support, emergency organizational defense, civic infrastructure maintenance. Wave 2 is where forward-looking investment generates the highest return: building parallel institutions, interstate coordination mechanisms, democratic alternatives that do not depend on the existing system functioning correctly.

The specific grant-making implication: organizations that apply for your funding and cite the framework's domain priorities are signaling that they have done the analytical work to align their activities with the recovery sequence. Treat framework alignment as a positive signal in grant evaluation — not as a credential, but as evidence of strategic thinking about timing and sequencing.

## Three Concrete Domain Applications

**Application 1: Using Domain 29 (Prosecutorial Weaponization) to conduct an organizational threat assessment.**

Domain 29 documents the 22-case retaliatory prosecution pattern across four categories: political opposition, Democratic officials, protest suppression, and civil society monitoring. The SPLC indictment demonstrates that the fourth category — targeting organizations that document government misconduct — is now operationally active. Every civil society organization that produces documentation of government misconduct, tracks corporate or law enforcement behavior, or files public records requests should treat itself as a potential Domain 29 target.

The threat assessment: review your last 24 months of published work. Which pieces could be construed — however absurdly — as making false statements about government officials or entities? Do you have documented evidence backing every material claim? Is your donor list protected by Doe v. Reed standing? Do you have standing relationships with media organizations that could generate First Amendment protection through coverage?

**Application 2: Using Domain 3 (Democratic Participation) and the parallel institutions analysis (Section 3.4) to align foundation grant-making with Wave 2 infrastructure.**

The proposal's Wave 2 analysis identifies $446B in CDFIs, 900-1000 worker cooperatives, 1,000+ community health centers, and Community Land Trust housing as existing parallel institutional infrastructure that needs scaling, not building from scratch. A foundation whose program areas include economic justice and civic infrastructure can use this inventory as a grant-making landscape map: which of these institutions in your geography are at capacity constraint? Which are facing federal defunding? Which have demonstrated their model but lack the capital to scale? The framework's domain sections on housing (13), healthcare (11), and economic justice (3) provide the evidence base for each institution type.

**Application 3: Using the Democracy Playbook 2025 (Brookings/Eisen) framework compatibility check.**

The Brookings Democracy Playbook 2025 (seven foundational pillars) and the 35-domain framework overlap substantially: protecting elections (Domains 1, 7), rule of law (Domains 6, 14, 29), combating corruption (Domains 2, 5), and countering disinformation (Domain 8). Organizations already using the Brookings framework can use this overlap map to integrate the 35-domain framework's additional analytical depth without starting over. The 35-domain framework's primary additions to the Brookings model are: (a) the three-wave sequencing architecture, (b) the international fiscal benchmarks in Section 5.5, and (c) the cross-domain feedback loop analysis that makes the integrated argument for why piecemeal reform fails. These three additions are the gap-fillers for organizations already working from the Brookings pillars.

## Common Mistakes in Foundation and Think Tank Settings

**Treating resilience as a one-time compliance exercise.** Updating bylaws, retaining counsel, and reviewing conflict-of-interest policies are necessary but not sufficient. Resilience is an ongoing operational posture, not a checklist. The organizations that survived the first year of escalating pressure had made resilience investments years before they needed them.

**Assuming tax-exempt status provides protection.** The IRS has been used as a political weapon before (see the 2013 TIGTA report on Tea Party applications); the current environment creates new risk for organizations whose work is visible and politically inconvenient. Tax-exempt status is a legal protection, not a political one. Ensure your legal counsel has reviewed your 501(c)(3) activities for any exposure under the current enforcement posture, and ensure your lobbying activity is accurately characterized under your 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(4) classification.

**Underfunding legal defense relative to program work.** Organizations doing democracy work should budget legal defense at a meaningful percentage of operating expenses — not as a crisis contingency, but as a line item. The Democracy Protection Network, launched in 2025, provides crisis-response and resilience-building services; membership in such networks is a cost-effective alternative to maintaining full in-house legal capacity.

## Implementation Tracking Checklist

- [ ] Four-track capture vulnerability audit completed; Red/Yellow/Green classification assigned
- [ ] Federal funding percentage calculated; two-year reduction plan drafted
- [ ] Succession plan documented for all key leadership roles
- [ ] Emergency board activation protocol established in writing and ratified
- [ ] Outside counsel retained on standing retainer; briefed on organizational work
- [ ] Secure communication channels established for emergency use
- [ ] Board governance independence safeguards reviewed; supermajority requirements in place for mission changes
- [ ] Donor list protection reviewed under Doe v. Reed precedent
- [ ] Grant-making or research agenda aligned with Wave 1 and Wave 2 timeline
- [ ] Quarterly review scheduled against implementation tracking template
