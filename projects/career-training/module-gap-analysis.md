# Career Training Curriculum — Module Gap Analysis

> **Purpose.** This is a structured audit of the 33-module career training curriculum. It inventories every module, maps coverage against competency frameworks for three construction career paths, identifies remaining gaps after the gap-fill series (Modules 29–33), and prioritizes new modules to write. The case-study workbook (150 scenarios, ~121,000 words) is treated as a companion to the module corpus, not a standalone curriculum element.

---

## Section 2: Coverage Analysis

### Competency Area Mapping

The curriculum organizes into six competency areas. Below, each area is rated: **Comprehensive** (multiple dedicated modules, worked examples), **Good** (one full module or major sections), **Light** (partial or introductory treatment), or **Gap** (essentially absent).

| Competency Area | Modules | Rating | Notes |
|----------------|---------|--------|-------|
| **Legal and Contracts** | 01, 08, 13, 29, 30, 32 | Comprehensive | Prime contracts, subcontracts, lien rights, dispute resolution, change orders all covered well |
| **Technical Field Execution** | 02, 05, 10, 11, 19, 26, 27 | Comprehensive | MEP residential and industrial, structural, civil, specialty scopes all addressed |
| **Financial and Business** | 08, 12, 25, 28 | Comprehensive | Estimating, cash flow, business setup, lending, marketing |
| **Organizational and PM** | 03, 07, 20, 23, 30, 33 | Comprehensive | Trade coordination, luxury PM, subcontractor management, QC |
| **Regulatory Compliance** | 04, 09, 14, 15, 31 | Good | Codes, Cal/OSHA, ADU law, prevailing wage covered; insurance regulatory compliance is thin |
| **Strategic / Career Development** | 04, 17, 18, 20, 28 | Good | Technology, sustainability, design-build, business development; scaling strategy is thin |

### Well-Covered Competencies

**Contracts and payment security** (Modules 01, 13, 29, 30, 32) form the curriculum's strongest cluster. A student working through these five modules will understand GMP versus lump sum versus cost-plus, subcontract flow-down, lien perfection, dispute escalation, and change order pricing — the full arc of construction payment risk.

**Residential technical depth** (Modules 07, 10, 11) is unusually thorough for a career-training curriculum. Module 07 alone (~19,800 words) covers trade sequencing, inspection discipline, and field management with checklist density that competes with most commercial field manuals.

**California regulatory specificity** (Modules 09, 12, 14, 15, 22, 27, 31) is a genuine differentiator. Seven modules anchor specifically to California law, code, or regulatory framework — Title 24, CSLB licensing, Cal/OSHA, ADU law, WUI codes, NEM tariffs, and DIR prevailing wage.

### Weak or Underserved Competencies

**Residential scheduling practice** is the most functionally important gap. Module 03 teaches CPM for industrial projects. Module 07 describes trade sequencing narratively. Neither teaches the week-to-week live scheduling practice a residential superintendent actually uses: 3-week lookahead schedules, constraint-identification, Last Planner System pull planning, or recovery logic when a framing crew runs two weeks behind. Every residential GC needs this; the curriculum currently teaches the theory but not the practice.

**Safety program construction** (not just compliance) is underdeveloped. Module 14 describes what Cal/OSHA requires. What it does not do is walk a new GC through building a written IIPP from scratch, populating required sections, structuring weekly toolbox talks, auditing site compliance, and handling a recordable incident from report through OSHA Form 300 and workers-comp notification. The distinction matters: knowing the rules is different from operating the system.

**Construction insurance program design** has a structural gap. Module 12 lists insurance types — GL, workers comp, umbrella, bonding — in a business formation context. It does not address: how to read a certificate of insurance and spot gaps, what an OCIP or CCIP means for subcontractor pricing (sub must exclude covered items from their bid), when builder's risk applies versus a standard CGL, how professional liability (E&O) interacts with design-build contracts, or how to structure a wrap-up program on a large commercial project. GCs who don't understand this systematically underbid OCIP projects or carry coverage gaps.

## Section 3: Learning Outcome Mapping

### Competency Framework by Career Level

#### Residential GC (Single-Family and Small Commercial)

**Must know**: Trade sequencing and field coordination; California permitting and inspection workflow; lien rights and payment protection; basic estimating and markup; subcontract structure; Cal/OSHA IIPP; project closeout and warranty. **Module support**: Strong — Modules 07, 08, 09, 11, 13, 14, 21, 30, 32, 33 collectively cover all core competencies.

**Should know**: Civil and structural reading; MEP coordination; business setup and insurance; marketing and client management; residential scheduling practice (live job). **Module support**: Good for all except residential scheduling practice, which lacks a dedicated module.

**Can develop over time**: Specialty scopes (ADU, renovation, hillside, solar); high-end and luxury management; design-build delivery; sustainability certification. **Module support**: Strong specialty depth — Modules 15–27 and Module 20 provide extensive path-specific material.

**Module coverage for this path**: 9 of 11 core competencies fully covered. The two weak spots are residential scheduling practice and insurance program design.

#### Industrial GC (Manufacturing, Complex Systems)

**Must know**: Prime contract structures (GMP, lump sum, EPC); ASME piping codes and NDE requirements; industrial PM and CPM scheduling; SIMOPS and outage management; commissioning support; prevailing wage for federal and state contracts. **Module support**: Modules 01–04 cover all of this except commissioning — the Cx workflow from pre-functional testing through owner training and final acceptance is absent.

**Should know**: Civil engineering for industrial sites; industrial codes (ASME, NFPA, API); insurance and bonding for large prime contracts (OCIP/CCIP on industrial projects are common); claims and delay analysis. **Module support**: Good for codes and civil; insurance program design and claims are in the curriculum but less developed for industrial-scale contract values.

**Module coverage for this path**: 7 of 10 core competencies fully covered. Commissioning workflow and insurance program design are the material gaps.

#### Specialty Subcontractor (Electrical, Plumbing, HVAC)

**Must know**: Their own trade codes (NEC, UPC/IPC, mechanical codes); sub contract structure and lien rights from the sub's position; lien waivers and conditional/unconditional forms; how to read and respond to a schedule; change order documentation from the field level. **Module support**: Good — Modules 13, 24, 30, 32 collectively cover sub-facing legal and administrative skills.

**Should know**: How to read the full drawing set (not just trade sheets); CPM schedule logic; what the GC needs from the sub for coordination; OSHA requirements for their trade; quality documentation. **Module support**: Modules 06, 03, 14, 33 cover these well.

**Can develop toward PM**: Estimating, business setup, project management, client management, dispute resolution. **Module support**: Full PM development path exists — Modules 08, 12, 07, 29 form a clear progression.

**Module coverage for this path**: Best served by current modules. The specialty sub path has the cleanest progression and the fewest gaps.

## Section 4: Prioritized Gap List

### Tier 1 — High-Leverage: Write Immediately

These three gaps affect more than 50% of target students and represent regulatory exposure, systematic revenue leakage, or daily operational need.

---

**Gap 1: Residential Lookahead Scheduling and Pull Planning (Proposed Module 34)**

*Why it's needed*: Every residential GC superintendent runs a job on a schedule — but the curriculum teaches CPM theory, not field scheduling practice. The gap is the difference between knowing what a critical path is and actually running a 3-week lookahead with a framing crew that's five days behind. This is the highest-frequency operational gap in the curriculum. It affects every residential GC student and most commercial-path students.

*Learning outcomes*:
1. Build a 3-week lookahead schedule from the master CPM schedule using the correct level of detail for field execution.
2. Apply Last Planner System pull planning to identify and remove constraints before they become delays.
3. Identify float consumption and distinguish critical-path delay from non-critical activity slippage.
4. Execute a schedule recovery plan when a trade falls behind, including replanning, acceleration discussion, and documentation.
5. Maintain a daily field report that feeds schedule update data without duplicating effort.

*Content outline*: (1) Why master CPM schedules fail in residential field use; (2) The 3-week lookahead format — structure, cadence, who fills it; (3) Last Planner System concepts and constraint log; (4) Communicating schedule status to owners without creating panic; (5) Recovery planning — acceleration, overtime, adding resources; (6) Documentation when a trade delay becomes a potential claim; (7) Template set: lookahead form, constraint log, daily log.

*Case-study scenarios*: Framing sub two weeks behind due to lumber delivery delay (owner-furnished) — who pays? Roofing sub dry-in delayed by unseasonable rain — how do you document and recover? HVAC rough-in conflict with electrician sequence — how do you resequence without adding cost?

*Estimated word count*: 4,000–5,000 words. *Estimated write time*: 10–12 hours. *Prerequisites*: Module 07, Module 03 (conceptual).

---

**Gap 2: Construction Insurance Program Design — Builder's Risk, OCIP/CCIP, and Professional Liability (Proposed Module 35)**

*Why it's needed*: Module 12 lists insurance types in a business-formation context. What is entirely absent is the field-level insurance literacy GCs need to (a) price OCIP/CCIP projects correctly (subs must exclude covered items from bids — failure to do so is systematic underpricing), (b) read a certificate of insurance and identify gaps, (c) understand when builder's risk versus a project CGL is the appropriate coverage, and (d) know when professional liability (E&O) is triggered on a design-build contract. This gap directly costs GCs money on commercial bids.

*Learning outcomes*:
1. Identify the five core insurance types for a construction project and the party responsible for each (GL, builder's risk, workers comp, umbrella, professional liability).
2. Read a certificate of insurance and identify coverage gaps, expired policies, and incorrect additional insured endorsements.
3. Price a subcontract bid correctly under an OCIP (exclude covered items) and verify which items the wrap-up policy covers versus what the sub must carry.
4. Explain builder's risk coverage triggers, exclusions, and how theft, vandalism, collapse, and natural-disaster claims are handled.
5. Determine when E&O / professional liability is required on a design-build, design-assist, or BIM-coordination contract and what minimum limits are appropriate.

*Content outline*: (1) The insurance stack on a construction project — who carries what; (2) Reading and verifying certificates of insurance; (3) OCIP and CCIP — how wrap-up programs work, bidding correctly under them; (4) Builder's risk — structure, exclusions, claims procedures; (5) Professional liability and when it attaches; (6) Umbrella and excess layers; (7) Common coverage gaps and how to close them before a loss occurs.

*Case-study scenarios*: Sub bids OCIP project including insurance in their price — GC catches it at bid review, discovers 8% pricing error. Sub's workers-comp lapses mid-project — injury occurs; who pays? Design-build GC provides structural layout; slab error found at framing — E&O triggered?

*Estimated word count*: 4,500–5,500 words. *Estimated write time*: 12–15 hours. *Prerequisites*: Module 12, Module 01 (OCIP context in industrial contracts).

---

**Gap 3: Safety Program Design — Building and Operating a Written IIPP (Proposed Module 36)**

*Why it's needed*: Module 14 describes Cal/OSHA regulatory requirements. It does not walk a GC through actually building and operating the Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP) that California law mandates. Most small GCs have a downloaded template with their company name typed into the header — not a functional program. This exposes them to Cal/OSHA citations, inflated workers-comp EMR (experience modification rate), and personal liability on serious injuries. A GC reaching 10+ field employees needs a real program, not a template.

*Learning outcomes*:
1. Draft a complete written IIPP meeting all T8 §3203 requirements for a small-to-mid residential GC firm.
2. Structure a weekly toolbox talk program with documented attendance, topic log, and corrective action tracking.
3. Conduct a job-site safety audit using a standardized checklist, produce a written finding, and route corrective actions.
4. Manage a recordable injury from first report through OSHA Form 300 completion, workers-comp notification, and return-to-work protocol.
5. Calculate and interpret the company's EMR (experience modification rate) and identify the interventions that reduce it over a 3-year cycle.

*Content outline*: (1) What a real IIPP contains versus a boilerplate template; (2) Hazard identification and periodic inspection procedures; (3) Training documentation — who, when, what, how to record; (4) Toolbox talk structure, frequency, and topic rotation; (5) Incident investigation — root cause analysis, not blame assignment; (6) OSHA Form 300/300A/301 — what to file, when, and what triggers electronic submission; (7) EMR mechanics and the business case for safety investment.

*Case-study scenarios*: Cal/OSHA inspection finds IIPP but no training records — what is the citation and what can the GC do? Worker injured on site — supervisor's first 30 minutes; GC's first 24 hours. Workers-comp EMR spikes after two recordable incidents — what's the 3-year impact on bid eligibility and insurance premiums?

*Estimated word count*: 4,000–5,000 words. *Estimated write time*: 10–12 hours. *Prerequisites*: Module 12, Module 14.

### Tier 2 — Niche but High-Value: Write When Resources Available

**Gap 4: Industrial Commissioning — Mechanical Handoff, Functional Testing, and Owner Training (Proposed Module 37)**

*Why it's needed*: Module 02 mentions commissioning support in a sentence. The actual commissioning workflow — pre-functional checklist execution, functional performance testing, deficiency tracking, system startup sequences, training-package preparation, and final acceptance documentation — is unknown territory for most GCs entering industrial work. The cost of missing it is deferred punch lists, owner disputes at final payment, and warranty callbacks on systems that were never properly started up. Estimated audience: 100% of industrial GC students, ~30% of commercial GC students.

*Learning outcomes*:
1. Build and execute a pre-functional test (PFT) checklist for a mechanical or electrical system before functional performance testing begins.
2. Conduct a functional performance test (FPT) for a representative system (HVAC, process piping, electrical distribution) and document pass/fail conditions.
3. Manage a commissioning deficiency log from discovery through resolution and retesting.
4. Prepare a training package for owner operations staff covering system design intent, operating parameters, emergency shutdown, and routine maintenance.
5. Execute the commissioning acceptance sequence and obtain owner sign-off in a form that protects final payment timing.

*Content outline*: (1) Commissioning defined — design intent, system installation, functional verification, owner acceptance; (2) Pre-functional testing — what it is, what documents it, who executes it; (3) Functional performance testing — procedures by system type; (4) Deficiency logging and resolution workflow; (5) Training package components and delivery; (6) Final acceptance documentation and its relationship to final payment; (7) ASHRAE Guideline 0 and ASHRAE Guideline 1.x context for industrial commissioning.

*Estimated word count*: 4,500–5,500 words. *Estimated write time*: 12–15 hours. *Prerequisites*: Module 02, Module 03.

**Gap 5: Multi-Family and Light Commercial Construction Fundamentals (Proposed Module 38)**

*Why it's needed*: The curriculum has strong poles — single-family residential (Modules 07–28) and heavy industrial (Modules 01–04). Multi-family (Type III and V wood-frame, Type I concrete) and light commercial (tenant improvement, retail, small office) are the most common middle-market project types in California, and they are largely absent. The occupancy classifications, fire separations, accessible-path-of-travel requirements, accessibility (ADA/CBC Chapter 11B), and commercial MEP coordination in these project types differ materially from single-family residential. A GC moving from residential to a 20-unit apartment or a tenant improvement job faces a code and coordination environment the curriculum does not prepare them for.

*Learning outcomes*:
1. Identify the IBC and CBC occupancy classifications for multi-family and light commercial projects and the fire-resistive construction implications.
2. Read and execute the accessible path-of-travel requirements for tenant improvement projects, including the 20% disproportionate cost rule.
3. Sequence a multi-family framing project accounting for fire-separation walls, shear walls, and MEP shaft coordination.
4. Administer the submittal and RFI process on a design-bid-build commercial project using AIA A201 general conditions.
5. Manage a commercial MEP coordination process (clash detection, mechanical room layout, BIM coordination) at a level appropriate for a GC without a dedicated MEP coordinator.

*Estimated word count*: 5,000–6,000 words. *Estimated write time*: 14–18 hours. *Prerequisites*: Module 07, Module 09, Module 11.

## Deployment Readiness

The curriculum is **ready to deploy now.** The 33 existing modules and 150-scenario workbook constitute a complete, production-quality resource for residential GC, industrial GC, and specialty sub career paths. The five gaps identified above are enhancements — they deepen coverage in specific areas — but no existing module contains critical errors or missing foundations that would block a student from developing functional competency.

**Module naming cleanup recommended before deployment**: Modules 29–33 use the generic `module-29.md` through `module-33.md` naming while Modules 01–28 use descriptive slugs. Renaming to `29-dispute-resolution-claims.md`, `30-subcontractor-management.md`, `31-prevailing-wage-labor-compliance.md`, `32-change-order-value-engineering.md`, `33-quality-assurance-qc.md` would make the directory self-documenting.
