# Subcontractor Management and Trade Coordination

> **Purpose.** The GC's margin lives or dies in the subcontractor relationship. A capable sub who shows up on time, does quality work, and invoices correctly is worth protecting. A problem sub can destroy a project, a relationship, and a reputation. This module gives you systematic procedures for finding the right subs, contracting with them correctly, managing their performance, and handling the paper trail that protects you when things go wrong.

> **This module complements** Module 07 (Residential GC: Scope, Trades, and People Management), which covers day-to-day field coordination. Here the focus is on the administrative and legal infrastructure: pre-qualification, contracts, pay applications, lien waivers, bonds, and dispute resolution.

## 0. Mental Model — The Sub Is Your Firm's Product Quality

When a homeowner's tile work fails, they don't call the tile setter — they call you. When a mechanical system leaks three years after closeout, the owner doesn't think "bad plumber." They think "bad general contractor." **The sub's work is your product.** Every decision you make in subcontractor management — who you hire, how you write the contract, how you manage their performance — either protects or exposes your reputation and your balance sheet.

The contractors who run tight sub programs do three things well:
1. They **pre-qualify** relentlessly and maintain a bench of proven performers.
2. They **write subcontracts** that clearly allocate risk and flow contract obligations downward.
3. They **pay on time** and demand the same compliance in return.

The GCs who struggle with subs are usually doing the opposite: taking the cheapest bid without vetting, using thin subcontracts or none at all, and either paying too slowly (which drives subs away) or paying without controlling lien waivers (which creates exposure).

## 1. Subcontractor Pre-Qualification and Vetting

### 1.1 Why Pre-Qualification Is Not Optional

The average residential project involves 12–20 subcontractors. On a $1M custom home, your sub spend might represent 55–65% of the contract value — $550,000–$650,000 flowing through your firm. That concentration of money and performance dependency demands a systematic approach to who you allow onto your jobs.

A pre-qualification failure typically shows up in one of three ways:
- **Technical failure:** Work doesn't meet specification; costly rework.
- **Financial failure:** Sub becomes insolvent mid-project; you finish their work at your cost.
- **Insurance failure:** Sub causes a site injury with lapsed workers comp; owner's claim lands on your policy.

**The cost of a bad sub hire on a residential project:** Typically 2–5× the sub's contract value in rework, delay, and management time. On a $60,000 framing subcontract, a failure can cost $120,000–$300,000 all-in.

### 1.2 Pre-Qualification Requirements by Tier

Not every sub requires the same vetting depth. Calibrate to the dollar value and criticality:

| Tier | Sub Value | Vetting Level | Documents Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Core Trades** | >$50,000 (framing, MEP, concrete) | Full pre-qual | All items in §1.3 |
| **Tier 2 — Mid-Scale** | $10,000–$50,000 (insulation, drywall, roofing) | Standard pre-qual | License, insurance, 3 references, 1 financial reference |
| **Tier 3 — Small Specialty** | <$10,000 (flooring, painting, landscaping) | Minimum | License verification, insurance, one reference |

Never skip license and insurance verification regardless of tier. A single unverified sub can create unlimited liability exposure.

### 1.3 Full Pre-Qualification Checklist (Tier 1 Subs)

**Legal and Licensing:**
- [ ] California Contractors State License Board (CSLB) license — verify at cslb.ca.gov: license number, classification, status, expiration date, any disciplinary history
- [ ] Business license in the county/city of operation
- [ ] Entity verification — confirm legal business name matches license (check Secretary of State records at bizfile.sos.ca.gov)

**Insurance:**
- [ ] Commercial General Liability — minimum limits: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate (increase for Tier 1; verify against your prime contract requirements)
- [ ] Workers Compensation — verify coverage is current; request Certificate of Insurance with your firm as certificate holder; call the carrier to verify policy is active
- [ ] Auto Liability — if sub operates vehicles on site
- [ ] Umbrella/Excess — required for subs over $250K on commercial work
- [ ] Verify that your firm is named as **Additional Insured** on the GL policy (endorsement, not just certificate)

**Financial:**
- [ ] D&B or credit report (for Tier 1 over $100K) — look for judgments, tax liens, slow-pay history
- [ ] Bank reference — ask directly: "Has this firm maintained its account in good standing? Have there been NSF incidents in the last 12 months?"
- [ ] Bonding capacity letter (if bonds are required) — from their surety
- [ ] Two most recent tax returns (if you need full financial transparency on large public projects)

**Performance:**
- [ ] Three project references — call them. Questions: Was the work completed on schedule? On budget? Did the sub show up consistently? Would you hire them again?
- [ ] One customer reference from the last 18 months minimum
- [ ] Project history — similar project type and size to your scope
- [ ] California DIR registration number — required for all public works subs (Department of Industrial Relations registration; verify at dir.ca.gov)

**Operational:**
- [ ] Designated superintendent or foreman for your project (name and direct contact)
- [ ] Crew size capacity — can they staff your project without pulling from active jobs?
- [ ] Equipment — do they own or rent? Is it available?
- [ ] Safety record — OSHA 300 logs for last 3 years; EMR (Experience Modification Rate) below 1.0 preferred; above 1.25 is a warning sign

### 1.4 The Approved Vendor List

Build and maintain an **Approved Vendor List** organized by trade. Every project should draw from this list. A sub that fails a pre-qualification or fails on a project gets removed or suspended.

The list should track:
- Last pre-qualification date
- Last job performance rating (scale of 1–5)
- Current insurance expiration dates
- CSLB license expiration date
- Preferred projects (project type, size, geography)

Review the list at least annually and require re-submission of insurance certificates every 12 months (carrier policies renew; certificates expire).

## 2. Subcontract Terms and Flow-Down Requirements

### 2.1 The Subcontract Is Your Most Important Risk Document

Most residential GCs use a one-page subcontract or a short letter. This is one of the most expensive habits in the industry. A three-page subcontract on a $90,000 mechanical contract leaves ambiguous the very issues that become disputes: what's included in scope, who provides what material, who pays for code upgrades, when payment is due, and what happens if the sub walks.

**Use a written subcontract for every sub over $5,000 with no exceptions.** Below that threshold, a written scope letter with basic terms is adequate.

**Recommended baseline subcontract template sources:**
- **AGC of California Short Form Subcontract** — industry-standard; regularly updated.
- **AIA A401 Contractor-Subcontractor Agreement** — mirrors AIA A201 prime contract terms.
- **Custom template** drafted by a construction attorney ($1,500–$3,000 one time; then adapt per project).

### 2.2 Critical Subcontract Terms

**Scope of Work:**
- Describe the work with specificity. Do not simply say "electrical work per plans." Say "All electrical work shown on sheets E1.0 through E4.2, dated [date], including rough-in, fixtures, panel installation, and required inspections. Excludes: temporary power, exterior site lighting, and low-voltage systems."
- Attach the relevant drawing sheets as an exhibit.
- List explicit **exclusions** — saves the argument about what was "assumed."

**Contract Price and Schedule of Values:**
- Fixed-price lump sum or unit price; specify clearly.
- For larger subcontracts, require a **schedule of values** — a breakdown of the lump sum by work element — used for progress payment calculation.

**Schedule:**
- Start date, milestone dates, and completion date tied to the master project schedule.
- Specify that the sub must maintain pace with the GC's master schedule and cooperate with re-sequencing if needed.
- Include: "Subcontractor shall submit a detailed three-week lookahead schedule each Friday."

**Pay-When-Paid vs. Pay-If-Paid:**
California law is specific here. A **pay-when-paid** clause (GC pays sub within a reasonable time after GC is paid by owner) is valid and enforceable in California. A **pay-if-paid** clause (GC is only obligated to pay sub if GC is paid by owner — transfers owner insolvency risk to sub) is **generally unenforceable in California** for residential projects and is disfavored in commercial work. Use pay-when-paid language; specify a payment turnaround (e.g., "within 10 business days of receipt of owner payment").

**Retainage:**
- Standard: 10% held through substantial completion; released on final acceptance.
- California Business and Professions Code and standard industry practice allow 5% retainage on projects over $250,000 (check your prime contract — you cannot hold more retainage from subs than the owner holds from you).

**Insurance and Indemnification:**
- Require sub to maintain the insurance types and limits specified in §1.3.
- Sub must name GC (and owner, if prime contract requires) as **Additional Insured**.
- Require sub to indemnify GC for claims arising from sub's work, sub's employees, and sub's sub-subcontractors.
- California anti-indemnity statute (Civil Code §2782) limits the scope of indemnification — do not use a "Type I" unlimited indemnity clause; use the standard qualified indemnity.

**Change Order Procedures:**
- No extra work without a written change order signed by GC's authorized representative.
- Sub must submit pricing for any change within 5 business days of direction.
- Verbal authorizations are not valid — specify this explicitly.

**Warranty:**
- Minimum 1 year from substantial completion on all workmanship.
- Manufacturer warranties flow through to owner as specified.
- Sub must promptly remedy any defect that arises during the warranty period at no cost.

**Dispute Resolution:**
- Disputes submitted to GC first; if unresolved, mediation; if still unresolved, binding arbitration (AAA Construction Rules).
- Sub's dispute with GC shall not be basis for stopping work or removing equipment.

### 2.3 Flow-Down Requirements

**Flow-down** means that the obligations in the prime contract between the GC and owner are passed down to subs through the subcontract. When the prime contract requires specific things — safety plans, quality control documentation, prevailing wage compliance, schedule requirements — the subcontract must impose those same obligations on the relevant subs.

**Common flow-down requirements:**

| Prime Contract Requirement | Flow-Down to Sub? | How to Include |
|---|---|---|
| Davis-Bacon / Prevailing Wage compliance | Yes, for every sub on covered projects | Attach certified payroll requirement; specify wage determinations |
| OCIP / CCIP insurance programs | Depends on program structure | Reference the insurance program in the subcontract; sub may be enrolled |
| Safety program requirements | Yes | Attach safety requirements as exhibit; require sub's tailgate safety meetings |
| Quality control documentation | Yes | Specify what QC records sub must maintain and submit |
| No-lien provisions on bonded public works | Yes | Sub's rights are against the payment bond, not the property |
| Liquidated damages | Partial — sub responsible for delays they cause | Specify sub's LD exposure in proportion to their contribution to any project delay |
| Minority/Women Business Enterprise requirements | Yes, on projects with MBE/WBE goals | Sub must report spend; may need to be a certified MBE/WBE itself |
| Buy America / domestic content | Yes, on federal projects | Sub certifies materials country of origin |

## 3. Pay Application Review and Lien Waiver Administration

### 3.1 The Pay Application Review Process

Every sub pay application requires a structured review before payment. A lax review process leads to overpayment (paying for work not done), missed retainage, and lien exposure from subs who aren't paying their lower-tier subs.

**Standard pay application review checklist:**

| Review Item | What to Check | Common Issues |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule of values | Amounts add to subcontract total; line items match agreed SOV | Subs try to front-load early line items; catch at the first application |
| Percent complete claimed | Match against actual field progress — inspect or ask superintendent | 90% complete claimed on a 70% complete scope; common |
| Prior payments reconciled | Prior period balance adds up correctly | Math errors; check every time |
| Change orders included | Only approved, signed COs should be in the application | Unapproved CO amounts sometimes inserted |
| Retainage calculated correctly | Typically 10% of gross billing this period; check math | Subs routinely submit incorrect retainage calculations |
| Lien waiver submitted | Conditional waiver for current draw; unconditional for prior | Missing waivers are a lien exposure; do not pay without them |
| Workers comp certificate current | Check expiration dates | Expired certificates — call the carrier before paying |
| License current | Spot-check at CSLB; required for each payment on public works | Lapses can happen mid-project |

**Payment timing:** California Business and Professions Code §7108.5 (private works) requires GC to pay subs within 7 days of receiving owner payment. For public works, California Public Contract Code §7107 requires 30-day payment terms. Many GCs run a 10-business-day payment cycle after receiving owner funds — this is reasonable and should be stated in the subcontract.

### 3.2 Lien Waiver Administration — The Four California Forms

California mandates four statutory lien waiver forms (Civil Code §§8132–8138). Using any other form is ineffective under §8122.

| Form | When to Collect | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Conditional Waiver on Progress Payment** (§8132) | With each sub invoice, before paying | Waives lien rights for that invoice amount upon payment |
| **Unconditional Waiver on Progress Payment** (§8134) | After prior payment has cleared | Confirms lien rights released for amounts paid |
| **Conditional Waiver on Final Payment** (§8136) | With final invoice | Waives all remaining lien rights upon receipt of final payment |
| **Unconditional Waiver on Final Payment** (§8138) | After final payment has cleared | Releases all lien rights completely |

**The payment-waiver cycle:**

```
Week 1: Sub submits Draw #2 invoice for $25,000
         Sub attaches: Conditional Waiver on Progress Payment (Draw #2, $25,000)
         Sub attaches: Unconditional Waiver on Progress Payment (Draw #1, previously paid)

GC reviews → Owner pays GC → GC pays sub within 7 days

Week 2: GC files sub's conditional waiver in project lien file
         GC notes date check cleared; retains copy of sub's unconditional for Draw #1
```

**Lien waiver tracking log — maintain per project, per sub:**

| Sub | Draw # | Invoice Date | Invoice Amount | Conditional Received | Date Paid | Cleared Date | Unconditional Received |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smith Plumbing | 1 | Mar 5 | $18,000 | Mar 5 | Mar 12 | Mar 14 | Mar 19 |
| Smith Plumbing | 2 | Apr 2 | $22,000 | Apr 2 | Apr 10 | Apr 12 | Apr 17 |
| Smith Plumbing | 3 | Apr 28 | $15,000 | Apr 28 | (pending) | — | — |

**Hard rule:** Do not pay a sub who has not delivered the prior period's unconditional waiver. "No waiver, no check" must be a firm policy communicated at subcontract signing.

### 3.3 Joint Checks

When a sub's material supplier calls you because they haven't been paid and threatens to lien, consider a **joint check agreement**: a check made jointly payable to the sub and the supplier. Both must endorse it, ensuring the supplier actually receives payment.

Joint checks protect against the sub's insolvency and against lien exposure from unpaid suppliers. However, they complicate the relationship and signal financial distress. Use them when you have reason to believe the sub isn't paying their bills, but be aware it may also signal to the sub (and the supplier) that you don't trust the sub — which is usually true by the time you need a joint check.

## 4. Progress Tracking and Performance Management

### 4.1 The Three-Week Lookahead Schedule

The most effective field management tool for trade coordination is the **three-week lookahead schedule** — a rolling, short-interval plan updated weekly showing exactly which crew is doing exactly which task in which location each day.

**Why three weeks:** One week is too short to flag upcoming conflicts; six weeks is too speculative for daily planning. Three weeks is the practical window for making commitments, booking inspection appointments, and sequencing trades.

**Format:**

```
THREE-WEEK LOOKAHEAD — [Project] — Week of [Date]
Updated by: [Superintendent] — Issued: [Date]

WEEK 1 (Apr 14–18):
  Mon-Wed:  Framing (Smith Framing) — Master bedroom roof trusses, Rm 201-205
  Mon:      Plumbing rough (Jones Plumbing) — Kitchen stack, main floor slab
  Tue-Wed:  Electrical rough (ABC Electric) — Panel rough-in, main service
  Fri:      Framing inspection — CALL Apr 10 to schedule

WEEK 2 (Apr 21–25):
  Mon-Thu:  Insulation (Warm-Right Insulation) — Walls, ceiling main floor
  Mon-Tue:  HVAC rough (Climate Control) — Duct layout 2nd floor
  Fri:      Insulation inspection

WEEK 3 (Apr 28 - May 2):
  All week: Drywall hang (Rapid Drywall) — 2nd floor walls and ceiling
  Wed:      Owner selection meeting — tile and flooring finishes
  PENDING:  Windows delivery — confirm with supplier by Apr 21
```

Distribute the lookahead at every weekly coordination meeting. Update it the same day each week. Subs who don't read it still have no excuse for scheduling conflicts — you delivered it.

### 4.2 Performance Metrics — What to Track and When to Act

Track these indicators for every active sub. Address any red flag promptly:

| Metric | Acceptable | Warning | Action Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Schedule adherence** | On time or <3 days behind | 3–7 days behind | >7 days behind / no recovery plan |
| **Crew size** | Per committed crew size | 10–20% below committed | >20% below committed consistently |
| **Quality — first-time inspection pass rate** | >90% | 75–90% | <75% (investigate root cause) |
| **Communication response time** | <4 hours for urgent; <24 hours for routine | 24–48 hours | >48 hours or unresponsive |
| **Change order timeliness** | Pricing submitted within 5 days | 5–10 days | >10 days, or disputes without basis |
| **Invoice accuracy** | Correct amounts, waivers attached | Minor errors corrected same day | Repeated errors, missing waivers, overbilling |
| **Safety compliance** | No incidents; PPE used; tailgates documented | Minor compliance gaps; addressed promptly | Repeated violations; OSHA recordable incidents |

**When to issue a formal written performance notice:** Any time a sub is in "Action Required" territory on two or more metrics. The written notice creates the paper trail required before a default notice.

**Written performance notice template:**

```
Date: [Date]
To: [Sub Company, Superintendent Name]
Re: Performance Notice — [Project Name]

We are writing to document the following performance concerns:
1. [Specific issue with dates and facts]
2. [Specific issue with dates and facts]

We require the following corrective actions by [date]:
1. [Specific action required]
2. [Specific action required]

Failure to meet these requirements may result in [default notice / withholding 
of payment / substitution of alternative contractor] per the terms of your 
Subcontract Agreement dated [date].

Please confirm receipt and your plan to address these items within 48 hours.

[GC Superintendent / PM name]
```

### 4.3 The Site Visit and Sub Inspection Protocol

A superintendent who only calls the trade — never walks the work — will miss quality problems until they're expensive to fix. Build a formal site inspection habit:

**Pre-cover inspection (mandatory — no exceptions):**
Before ANY concealment of work (drywall over rough-in, backfill over underground, concrete over base rock), the superintendent must personally inspect or have a third party inspect the concealed work. Photograph extensively. Log the inspection in the daily report.

The cost of a pre-cover inspection: 30 minutes. The cost of opening a wall to inspect plumbing rough after drywall is installed: $3,000–$12,000. Do the inspection.

## 5. Coordination Meetings and Communication Protocols

### 5.1 Meeting Types and Frequency

| Meeting Type | Frequency | Attendees | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Owner-Architect-Contractor (OAC)** | Bi-weekly during active construction | GC PM, owner or owner's rep, architect | Decisions, RFI responses, CO approvals; formal minutes |
| **Weekly Subcontractor Coordination** | Weekly | GC superintendent, all active trade foremen | Updated three-week lookahead; conflict resolution; coordination items |
| **Pre-task planning meetings** | Before any complex or high-risk operation | GC superintendent, trade foreman, safety rep | Task-specific safety and quality plan; documented |
| **Inspection scheduling calls** | As needed | Superintendent, inspection service | Inspector scheduled 48–72 hours in advance |
| **Monthly project review** | Monthly | GC PM and principal, owner | Budget status, schedule status, open claims, upcoming decisions |

### 5.2 The Weekly Coordination Meeting — Running It Well

A poorly run weekly meeting wastes an hour from every trade foreman present. A well-run one resolves conflicts before they become delays.

**Standard agenda (30–45 minutes):**

1. **Safety moment** (5 min) — One safety topic; rotate leadership.
2. **Schedule review** (10 min) — Review last week's lookahead: what got done, what didn't, why.
3. **Next three weeks** (10 min) — Walk through the updated lookahead; identify conflicts.
4. **Trade coordination** (10 min) — Open issues between trades; who needs access to whose work area.
5. **Upcoming inspections** (5 min) — What's getting inspected and when; who needs to be ready.
6. **Action items** (5 min) — Assigned with names and dates; read back from last week's list.

**Meeting minutes:** Assign someone to document action items and decisions. Email minutes to all attendees within 24 hours. The minutes become your evidence if a trade foreman later says "nobody told us about the inspection."

### 5.3 Communication Rules That Prevent Disputes

- **Text messages are not contract documents.** For any direction that has schedule or cost implications, follow up with email or a written field directive. A text message approving a change can be forgotten, denied, or misunderstood.
- **Subs communicate with GC, not directly with owner.** This is a critical boundary. A sub who goes around you to the owner creates scope confusion, triggers change orders you didn't price, and undermines your authority. State this explicitly in the subcontract and reinforce it in the kickoff meeting.
- **Document every oral directive on the same day.** If a sub foreman tells you their crew can't work Thursday because of another job, log it that day with the reason. This becomes your evidence if their delay causes a claim.

## 6. Performance Bonds and Default Procedures

### 6.1 When to Require Performance Bonds from Subs

A **performance bond** is a three-party contract between the sub (principal), a surety company, and the GC (obligee). If the sub defaults, the surety must either complete the work or pay damages up to the bond amount.

Performance bonds on subcontracts are standard practice on public works (often required by the prime contract) and on large commercial projects. They are less common in residential work but should be considered when:

- Sub contract value exceeds $250,000.
- The trade is high-risk (specialty mechanical, structural concrete, below-grade waterproofing).
- The sub's financial profile raised concerns during pre-qualification.
- The prime contract requires the GC to bond all sub contracts above a threshold.

**Bond cost:** Typically 1–3% of the bond amount. On a $200,000 subcontract, that's $2,000–$6,000 — worth it when the trade failure risk is high.

**Payment bonds:** Also require payment bonds from critical subs. The payment bond ensures that the sub's suppliers and sub-subs are paid — reducing your lien exposure. On public works, payment bonds from subs are often required by California Public Contract Code.

### 6.2 Sub Default — The Procedure

Sub default is serious. Handle it systematically; a procedural error can expose the GC to a wrongful termination claim that exceeds the cost of the original default.

**Step 1 — Document the default.** Before taking any action, assemble the evidence: the specific contract obligations that were breached, the dates of breach, and the documentation (daily logs, written notices sent, photos, schedule comparisons).

**Step 2 — Issue a formal Notice of Default** (written, certified mail, email):

```
NOTICE OF DEFAULT

Date: [Date]
To: [Sub Company Name, Registered Agent Address]
Re: [Project Name], Subcontract Agreement dated [Date]

This notice constitutes a formal Notice of Default pursuant to Section [X] 
of the above-referenced Subcontract Agreement.

You are in default for the following reasons:
1. [Specific breach, dates, and evidence]
2. [Specific breach, dates, and evidence]

You must cure each item listed above within [5–10] calendar days of this 
notice. If you fail to cure within this period, [GC Name] will exercise its 
rights under the Subcontract to complete the work at your expense and/or 
call upon your surety to perform.

[GC Representative Signature]
```

**Step 3 — Allow the cure period.** The subcontract should specify a cure period (7–14 days is standard). Do not skip this even when the default is egregious — the cure period is often contractually required, and skipping it creates a breach-by-GC argument.

**Step 4 — Notify the surety (if bonded).** If the sub has a performance bond, notify the surety in writing simultaneously with or immediately after the default notice. The surety has its own rights and obligations — they must be given the opportunity to investigate and respond. Failure to timely notify the surety can impair your bond claim.

**Step 5 — Termination (if cure fails).** After the cure period expires without cure, issue a written **Notice of Termination**. Document the condition of the work at time of termination (photos, inventory of sub's materials on site, measurement of work in place).

**Step 6 — Completion.** Either engage a replacement sub, complete the work with your own forces, or call upon the surety to perform. Track all completion costs separately — these become your damage claim against the defaulting sub and/or its surety.

**Step 7 — Backcharge accounting.** Compile all costs to complete: replacement sub costs, additional GC management time, extended overhead, cost of remediating defective work. This total, less the unpaid balance of the original subcontract, is your net claim.

### 6.3 Retainage as Default Protection

The 10% retainage you hold gives you a buffer for the completion scenario. If the sub's retainage covers your completion costs, the math is cleaner. This is one of several reasons not to release retainage early just because the sub asks.

**On residential projects:** California Civil Code §3260.2 provides specific retainage rules. For contracts between $25,000 and $150,000, the owner/GC may not withhold retainage from a sub after 45 days following completion of the sub's work if the sub's work is accepted and the sub has provided proper documentation. Know these timelines.

## 7. Dispute Resolution with Subs

### 7.1 The Most Common Sub Disputes

| Dispute Type | Prevention | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope disputes** — sub claims work outside their contract | Clear written scope with explicit exclusions; require sub to bid based on drawings | Written scope comparison; reference contract language |
| **Extra work claims** — sub claims directed changes without CO | Written CO required; document all verbal directives same day | Review daily logs, emails, meeting minutes; negotiate documented extras |
| **Delay claims against GC** — sub claims GC caused delay | Three-week lookahead; written notices of delays the GC causes; accurate scheduling | Schedule analysis; compare sub's baseline to actual |
| **Payment disputes** — disputed retainage or withheld progress payments | Written subcontract with clear payment terms; pay-when-paid language; clear retainage release conditions | Review contract; compare invoice to SOV and work in place |
| **Quality disputes** — GC backcharges for defective work** | Written notice and opportunity to cure before bringing in replacement sub | Document defect with photos and specifications comparison; compare to contract quality standard |
| **Lien claims from sub's sub-subs** | Require lien waivers from subs AND written confirmation that their sub-subs are paid | Joint check agreements; verify through lien service |

### 7.2 Backcharges — The Paper Trail

A **backcharge** is a charge the GC makes against a sub's contract balance for costs the sub caused — cleanup, damage repair, work the sub failed to perform, or completion costs after default.

**Backcharge documentation requirements:**

1. **Written notice to the sub** before you incur the cost — give the sub the opportunity to perform or remedy first.
2. **Written notice when the cost is incurred** — describing what was done, by whom, at what cost.
3. **Invoices or time records** supporting the actual cost.
4. **Formal backcharge invoice** submitted to the sub in the same format as a sub invoice.

Without this paper trail, backcharges become contested and often uncollectible. Many GCs backcharge informally (just withholding from the sub's payment) without documentation — this leads to lien claims from the sub and is often unenforceable.

### 7.3 When Subs Threaten to Lien

A sub threatening a mechanic's lien is exercising a statutory right. Do not ignore it or respond with threats. Respond with facts and process:

1. **Verify the sub's preliminary notice status** — did they serve preliminary notice? If not, their lien rights may be limited (see Module 13, §2).
2. **Review what is owed** — is the sub's number accurate? Is retainage properly held? Are there legitimate backcharges to offset?
3. **If the sub is owed money, pay it** — a lien that clouds title on a house you're trying to sell or refinance costs you far more than paying the disputed amount.
4. **If the amount is disputed** — respond in writing, explain the dispute, and propose mediation.
5. **Document your position** — if a sub files a lien for an amount that is clearly inflated or disputed, your documentation of what is actually owed is your defense.

On public works projects, subs have no lien rights against the property (the government can't be liened). Instead, they file claims against the **payment bond**. The procedure is different but the underlying obligation is the same — pay what you owe.

## 8. Templates and Quick Reference

### 8.1 Subcontractor Kickoff Checklist (Use Before Sub Mobilizes)

- [ ] Executed subcontract on file — both parties signed
- [ ] Current Certificate of Insurance received — verified with carrier
- [ ] Additional Insured endorsement confirmed
- [ ] Workers comp certificate verified — call carrier to confirm active
- [ ] CSLB license verified at cslb.ca.gov
- [ ] DIR registration number confirmed (public works)
- [ ] Superintendent and crew introduced; emergency contacts on file
- [ ] Scope walkthrough completed with sub foreman
- [ ] Sub reviewed and initialed project-specific safety requirements
- [ ] Communication protocol explained (no direct contact with owner without GC presence)
- [ ] Pay application schedule explained — due dates, required documents
- [ ] Lien waiver procedure explained — "no waiver, no check" policy
