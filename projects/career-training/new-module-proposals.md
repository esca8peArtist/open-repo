# New Module Proposals — Priority-Ordered Outlines

> **Reading note.** These are detailed content outlines for the five new modules most needed to close identified curriculum gaps. They are ready to hand to a writer. Each proposal includes: why the gap exists, who it affects, what the student will be able to do after the module, the full content structure, two or three case-study scenarios for integration, and production estimates. Modules are ordered by priority — write Module 34 first.

## Module 34: Residential Lookahead Scheduling and Pull Planning

**Priority**: Tier 1 — Write immediately. Affects 100% of Residential GC path students and ~60% of Specialty Sub path students.

### Why It's Needed

The curriculum teaches CPM theory (Module 03, industrial context) and describes residential trade sequencing narratively (Module 07). Neither teaches the week-to-week scheduling practice a residential superintendent or PM actually uses on a live job. The gap is operational: a student who finishes the current curriculum knows *what* a schedule is but not how to *run one* when the framing crew is five days behind, the window delivery is delayed, and the HVAC rough-in window is closing.

This is the highest-frequency operational gap. Every residential GC runs a schedule; most run it on instinct and a phone call. Providing a structured, repeatable scheduling practice is the single fastest way to compress project duration, reduce carrying costs, and improve client experience — three outcomes that directly affect profitability.

**Estimated audience reach**: 100% of Residential GC path students; 60% of Specialty Sub path students targeting PM roles.

### Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, the student will be able to:

1. Build a 3-week lookahead schedule from the master CPM or project schedule using the correct level of field-execution detail — not too high-level to be useful, not so granular it can't be maintained.
2. Conduct a weekly Last Planner System constraint-removal meeting with trade foremen, identifying and clearing constraints before they become delays.
3. Read a CPM schedule update and distinguish critical-path delay (impacts project completion) from non-critical activity slippage (recoverable without impacting completion).
4. Design and execute a schedule recovery plan — including replanning options, acceleration discussions with subs, and documentation of owner-caused vs. contractor-caused delays.
5. Maintain a daily field report and weekly schedule update without duplicating data entry, using a simple field-documentation system.

### Content Outline

**Section 1 — Why Master CPM Schedules Fail in Residential Field Use** (~500 words)
- The master schedule is for the owner and the lender; the field schedule is for the superintendent
- Why a 12-month, 200-activity project schedule is useless at the morning huddle
- The two scheduling problems that destroy residential margins: (a) trade gaps (dead time between trades), (b) trade pile-ups (two crews arriving for work that doesn't have space or prerequisites)
- The concept of "make-ready" work — clearing constraints before a trade shows up

**Section 2 — The 3-Week Lookahead Format** (~700 words)
- What a lookahead contains: activities starting or continuing in the next 3 weeks, responsible party, planned vs. actual, constraints to clear
- The right granularity: "Rough plumbing — master bath" not "install 3/4" PEX from manifold to master bath" and not just "MEP rough-in"
- Who fills it in and when: superintendent fills weekly, updated Mondays for the week ahead
- How it connects to the master schedule: lookahead activities should pull directly from the master; deviations are the first sign of schedule drift
- Template: sample 3-week lookahead form with column structure

**Section 3 — Last Planner System: Constraint Identification and Removal** (~700 words)
- The Last Planner concept in residential terms: foremen are the "last planners" because they commit to specific work; the GC's job is to remove constraints so commitments can be kept
- The constraint log: what types of constraints delay residential trades (material not on site, preceding work not complete, permit not approved, design not resolved, manpower not available)
- The weekly constraint removal meeting: 20-minute standing meeting, each foreman identifies constraints for the coming 2 weeks, GC commits to clearing each by a specific date
- Tracking Percent Plan Complete (PPC): the ratio of completed commitments to total commitments — a management metric, not a client metric
- What a PPC below 80% tells you and what to do about it

**Section 4 — Reading a Schedule Update and Finding the Real Problem** (~600 words)
- The difference between activity delay and critical path delay: how to check if a delay affects the completion date
- Float consumption: how to tell when a non-critical activity is using up its float and becoming critical
- Early warning signals: which trades are historically the leading indicators of residential schedule problems (MEP rough-ins, finish carpentry, flooring)
- How to communicate a schedule delay to an owner without creating panic or setting up a dispute

**Section 5 — Recovery Planning: When a Trade Falls Behind** (~700 words)
- The recovery conversation with a sub: what to say, what to ask, what to document
- Recovery options in priority order: (1) sub adds resources at their cost, (2) sub works overtime at shared cost, (3) schedule is resequenced to allow recovery without crashing the critical path, (4) critical path compression (crashing) with documented cost impact
- Owner-caused delays versus contractor delays: how to identify the difference, how to document it, and when it matters for the contract
- The acceleration directive: what it is, when the owner can issue one, what it costs, and why you need it in writing before starting accelerated work
- Sample recovery plan format: current status, recovery actions, responsible party, new milestone dates, cost impact

**Section 6 — Documentation That Feeds the Schedule Without Creating Double Work** (~500 words)
- The daily field report as a schedule-update data source: what to record so Monday's schedule update takes 20 minutes, not two hours
- Photo documentation: time-stamped, GPS-tagged photos as constraint-removal evidence and delay documentation
- The single-page weekly status report: format that satisfies the owner's project visibility need without creating a second document system
- How to use construction management software (Buildertrend, Procore) for schedule tracking without requiring field crew to learn software

**Section 7 — Template Set** (~300 words + templates)
- 3-week lookahead template (table format, ready to print or use in a spreadsheet)
- Constraint log template (constraint, responsible party, clear-by date, status)
- Weekly status report template (1 page, one per week)
- Delay documentation memo template (for owner-caused delays)

### Case-Study Scenarios (for integration into workbook)

**Scenario 34.1 — The Late Framing Crew**
You're the GC on a 3,200 SF new build. The framing crew finishes the first floor but is 8 days behind schedule when they start the second floor. The HVAC sub is booked for rough-in starting in 3 weeks and has another job starting the week after. The window/door unit delivery is 4 weeks out. *Decision point*: Do you (A) accept the delay and push HVAC rough-in, (B) negotiate with the framing crew to add a second crew at a cost you'll absorb, (C) document the framing delay, determine the cause, and present the owner with a cost-impact analysis for their decision, or (D) tell the HVAC sub to start on schedule regardless of framing completion?

**Scenario 34.2 — The Owner-Delayed Windows**
The owner selected custom windows with a 12-week lead time. You told them in writing that you needed the order placed by Week 6. They placed it at Week 10. The windows now arrive at Week 22, pushing dry-in from Week 18 to Week 22 — a 4-week push to the completion date. The owner claims "you should have ordered standard windows as a backup." *Decision point*: How do you document this delay, what does your contract say about owner-caused delays, and what do you include in the formal delay notice?

**Scenario 34.3 — The Recovery That Costs More Than the Delay**
You're 6 weeks from project completion on a $650,000 remodel. A tile sub defect (wrong grout color, entire master bath floor) requires demo and redo — 2-week setback. The owner demands you "crash" the schedule to make the original move-in date. Crashing requires weekend work for your finish crew and all subs — estimated premium cost $18,000. Your contract has a liquidated damages clause at $500/day. *Decision point*: Do you absorb the $18,000 crashing cost to avoid LD risk, or do you issue a notice of delay, quantify the LD exposure at $7,000 (14 days × $500), and accept the delay rather than crash?

### Production Estimates

- **Estimated word count**: 4,500–5,200 words (excluding templates)
- **Estimated write time**: 10–12 hours (writer with residential GC field experience)
- **Templates required**: 4 (lookahead schedule, constraint log, weekly status report, delay notification memo)
- **Prerequisites**: Module 07, Module 03 (conceptual familiarity)
- **Reading time**: ~3 hours

## Module 35: Construction Insurance Program Design — Builder's Risk, OCIP/CCIP, and Professional Liability

**Priority**: Tier 1 — Write immediately. Affects 100% of commercial GC path students and ~70% of Residential GC path students; directly prevents systematic bid pricing errors.

### Why It's Needed

Module 12 lists construction insurance types in a business-formation context. It does not address the field-level insurance literacy GCs need to price projects correctly and avoid coverage gaps. The costliest version of this gap: a GC bids an OCIP (owner-controlled insurance program) project without knowing what the wrap-up covers — so they include their own GL and workers-comp in their price. That's 3–8% systematic overpricing on every OCIP project bid. They lose these jobs to competitors who know to exclude covered costs. The second costliest version: a design-build GC triggers professional liability exposure and discovers they don't have E&O coverage when the claim arrives.

**Estimated audience reach**: 100% of Commercial/Industrial GC path; 70% of Residential GC path students (especially those moving into commercial renovation or design-build work).

### Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, the student will be able to:

1. Name the five required insurance coverages for a construction project, identify the responsible party for each, and recognize which are owner-controlled versus contractor-controlled on a given project type.
2. Read a certificate of insurance and identify coverage gaps, incorrect additional insured endorsements, and expired or about-to-expire policies before signing a subcontract.
3. Price a bid correctly under an OCIP or CCIP — identify which costs the wrap-up policy covers, exclude those items from the bid, and verify the exclusion with the owner's OCIP administrator.
4. Describe the structure, coverage triggers, exclusions, and claims procedures for a builder's risk policy and identify the most common coverage gaps that lead to uncovered losses.
5. Determine whether professional liability (E&O) coverage is required on a specific contract, what minimum limits are appropriate, and how to obtain project-specific E&O for a design-build engagement.

### Content Outline

**Section 1 — The Insurance Stack on a Construction Project** (~600 words)
- The five layers: General Liability (GL), Workers' Compensation, Builder's Risk, Umbrella/Excess, and Professional Liability
- Who carries each: standard allocation on design-bid-build vs. design-build vs. OCIP/CCIP
- The difference between project-specific coverage and annual (occurrence) policies
- Why the GC's insurance and the sub's insurance must both be correct — and why a sub's certificate of insurance is not the same as verified coverage

**Section 2 — Reading and Verifying Certificates of Insurance** (~600 words)
- The ACORD certificate: what each field means and what to look for
- Additional insured endorsements: what they must say, the difference between a blanket AI endorsement and a specific project endorsement, and why "additional insured as required by contract" is insufficient
- Primary and non-contributory language: why it matters and how to verify it's actually in the policy (not just stated on the certificate)
- Expired policies: how to calendar certificate renewals and what happens if a sub's policy lapses mid-project
- The single most common mistake: accepting a certificate without verifying the endorsements are actually attached

**Section 3 — OCIP and CCIP — Wrap-Up Programs** (~800 words)
- What OCIP and CCIP are and why owners use them: consolidated purchasing power, broader coverage, elimination of "insurance gaps" between GC and subs
- What a wrap-up policy typically covers: GL on the project site, workers comp for enrolled parties, completed operations coverage
- What a wrap-up typically does not cover: offsite fabrication, equipment owned by contractors, auto liability, professional liability, sub's tools and equipment
- How to bid correctly under an OCIP: the enrollment process, the insurance credit calculation (subtract the cost of excluded coverages from your normal insurance load), and how to document the credit on your bid
- The most common OCIP bidding error: not reducing the bid by the insurance credit — resulting in a bid 3–8% above market with no margin improvement
- Questions to ask the OCIP administrator before finalizing a bid: coverage start date, enrolled sub list, exclusions schedule, completed-operations period

**Section 4 — Builder's Risk: Structure, Coverage, and Claims** (~700 words)
- What builder's risk covers: buildings under construction, materials on site and in transit, temporary structures, formwork, scaffolding
- Standard exclusions: earthquake and flood (available by endorsement), design defects, faulty workmanship, contractor's equipment, mysterious disappearance
- Who insures what: is builder's risk the owner's policy or the GC's policy? Depends on the contract — read the contract before buying a policy
- Theft and vandalism: how claims work, what documentation is required, why serial theft from a job site is usually not covered under a single claim
- The "under construction" trigger: when does builder's risk start and stop? Most policies attach at commencement of work and expire at substantial completion or occupancy — whichever comes first
- Common builder's risk gaps that GCs discover too late: inadequate limits (policy based on contract value, not replacement cost), failure to notify insurer of major scope additions, materials stored offsite not scheduled on the policy

**Section 5 — Professional Liability (E&O) — When It Attaches and What It Costs** (~600 words)
- What professional liability covers: claims arising from design errors, omissions, and professional judgment — not construction defects
- When a GC triggers E&O exposure: design-build contracts, BIM coordination agreements that include design responsibility, design-assist contracts where the GC commits to performance outcomes (not just construction)
- Project-specific E&O vs. annual professional liability: when to buy each, typical minimums ($1M per occurrence, $2M aggregate on most commercial design-build), and what "tail coverage" means for completed projects
- The design-build GC who doesn't have E&O: what happens when a structural calculation error becomes a claim and the GC's CGL excludes professional services
- Cost ranges: annual E&O for a GC with design services, 2026 market — $5,000–$25,000/year depending on design revenue exposure

**Section 6 — Umbrella and Excess Layers** (~400 words)
- What an umbrella policy does: extends limits on underlying GL, auto, and employers liability; fills certain gaps in underlying policies
- Excess policies vs. umbrella: an excess policy only increases limits; an umbrella may also fill gaps — understand which you're buying
- Required limits for different project types: residential typically $1M–$2M occurrence; commercial TI $2M–$5M; large commercial or institutional often $5M–$25M
- How to verify your umbrella actually sits over all the right underlying policies

**Section 7 — Common Coverage Gaps and How to Close Them** (~400 words)
- The sub with lapsed workers comp: project shut down; whose problem is it?
- The GC whose GL excludes "your work" — discovering this at a defect claim
- The spec builder who discovers their builder's risk expired at substantial completion but the buyer hasn't taken possession
- The OCIP project where the owner's completed-operations coverage ends 3 years after substantial completion but the statute of limitations on latent defects is 10 years
- Practical checklist: 10 things to verify before every project start

### Case-Study Scenarios

**Scenario 35.1 — The OCIP Underpriced Bid**
Your estimator priced a $2.1M tenant improvement bid for a school district OCIP project. Your normal insurance load is 3.8% of construction cost. Under the OCIP, GL and workers comp are covered; you need to exclude $65,000 from your bid (your normal insurance credit for those two coverages). Your estimator didn't know to ask and included full insurance costs. You submitted your bid at $2.1M. The apparent low bid from a competitor is $2.03M. *Decision point*: After bid opening, you review the OCIP enrollment package and discover your error. What are your options and what do you do?

**Scenario 35.2 — The Sub With the Lapsed Certificate**
Your electrical sub's workers-comp policy expires October 31. You're in the middle of electrical rough-in on a $680K custom home. You didn't calendar the certificate renewal. November 3, an electrician falls off a ladder — serious injury, three-week hospitalization. The sub's workers-comp lapsed November 1. *Decision point*: What are your immediate obligations (to the injured worker, to your insurer, to the owner)? What is your exposure? Could this have been prevented?

**Scenario 35.3 — Design-Build Without E&O**
You're the GC on a design-build contract for a $1.4M commercial kitchen build-out for a restaurant group. Your architect provided the kitchen layout design. Post-construction, the health department fails the kitchen because the ventilation design doesn't meet hood-capture velocity requirements — a design error. The restaurant owner's damages: $140,000 in rework, $80,000 in lost revenue during construction redo, $22,000 in health permit re-review. They're coming after you. *Decision point*: Does your GL policy cover this? Does your architect's E&O cover this? What coverage gap does this expose, and what should you have done before signing a design-build contract?

### Production Estimates

- **Estimated word count**: 4,800–5,800 words
- **Estimated write time**: 12–15 hours (writer with commercial GC experience and insurance familiarity)
- **Prerequisites**: Module 12, Module 01 (OCIP context on industrial prime contracts)
- **Reading time**: ~3 hours
- **Expert review recommended**: A construction insurance broker review of Sections 3 and 5 before publication

## Module 36: Safety Program Design — Building and Operating a Written IIPP

**Priority**: Tier 1 — Write immediately. Legal compliance requirement; affects all GC-track students. Module 14 describes regulations; this module builds the program.

### Why It's Needed

California T8 §3203 requires every employer — including one-person LLCs with a single hire — to maintain a written Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP). Most small GCs have a boilerplate template with their name on the cover. That template will not survive a Cal/OSHA inspection, will not lower their workers-comp EMR, and will not protect them personally when a serious injury occurs.

Module 14 describes what the regulations require. It does not walk anyone through building and operating a real program. This module is the difference between having a written IIPP and having a functioning safety management system.

**Estimated audience reach**: 100% of all GC-track students; 70% of Specialty Sub path students who manage crews.

### Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, the student will be able to:

1. Draft a complete written IIPP that meets all T8 §3203 requirements — not a template, but a working document with company-specific content in every required section.
2. Design and operate a weekly toolbox talk program with documented attendance, tracked topics, and corrective action follow-through.
3. Conduct a field safety audit using a standardized checklist, write a finding report, and route corrective actions to the responsible party with a deadline.
4. Manage a recordable injury from first report through Cal/OSHA Form 300 completion, workers-comp carrier notification, and return-to-work protocol — within regulatory timelines.
5. Calculate the company's Experience Modification Rate (EMR) from workers-comp loss history, explain what drives it, and identify the practical interventions that reduce it over a 3-year cycle.

### Content Outline

**Section 1 — What a Real IIPP Contains vs. a Boilerplate Template** (~600 words)
- The nine required elements of a California IIPP under T8 §3203: (1) responsible person, (2) system for ensuring employee compliance, (3) communication system, (4) hazard assessment, (5) accident/exposure investigation, (6) hazard correction, (7) training, (8) recordkeeping
- What "responsible person" actually means — who is named, what their authority and accountability is, and why naming your office manager is a compliance failure
- The difference between a general hazard assessment ("site hazards include falls, electrical, struck-by") and a site-specific hazard assessment that a Cal/OSHA inspector will accept
- Why most downloaded IIPP templates fail: the hazard section is generic, the training section has no records, the corrective action section has no closure loop
- Exercise: what to add to make a boilerplate IIPP company-specific (30 minutes of customization that reduces citation risk by ~80%)

**Section 2 — The Written Hazard Assessment: Identifying What Can Kill You** (~500 words)
- The field walkthrough methodology: systematic hazard identification by work area and activity type
- Job Hazard Analysis (JHA): how to write one for each critical activity (concrete forming, roof work, electrical, excavation, crane lifts)
- Hierarchy of controls applied to construction hazards: elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE — in that order
- How to keep the hazard assessment current when scope changes mid-project
- Template: one-page JHA format for a residential GC

**Section 3 — Toolbox Talks: Structure, Frequency, and Documentation** (~600 words)
- What makes a toolbox talk effective: 10–15 minutes, one topic, site-specific example, employee participation, no PowerPoint required
- Topic rotation: annual calendar with 52 pre-planned topics (seasonal: heat illness in summer, wet-weather slips in winter; perpetual: fall protection, struck-by, silica, housekeeping)
- Documentation requirements: written sign-in sheet with date, topic, and employee signatures; how to store and retrieve on Cal/OSHA demand
- The corrective action loop: if a toolbox talk reveals a non-compliance issue (foreman mentions subs aren't using PPE), the corrective action must be documented and followed up — not mentioned once and forgotten
- Who runs the toolbox talk: the superintendent, not the GC. PM can prepare the topic; super delivers it in the field.

**Section 4 — Safety Audits: From Paperwork to Field Verification** (~600 words)
- The difference between a safety audit and a walkthrough: an audit uses a standardized checklist, produces a written finding, routes corrective actions, and verifies closure
- What a residential GC safety audit covers: fall protection compliance, electrical safety, housekeeping, PPE use, equipment condition, emergency procedures posted, first aid kit stocked
- Frequency: minimum monthly formal audit; weekly informal walkthroughs by superintendent documented in daily report
- Non-conformance procedure: written finding → responsible party assignment → deadline → verification → closure
- How audit documentation protects the GC: evidence that hazards were identified and corrected is the strongest defense in a Cal/OSHA citation or personal injury lawsuit
- Template: one-page residential safety audit checklist

**Section 5 — Incident Management: The First 30 Minutes and the First 30 Days** (~700 words)
- The first 30 minutes: medical attention first, scene preservation second, notification chain third (your superintendent, your insurer, the owner if required by contract)
- The distinction between first aid (no reporting required), recordable incident (OSHA Form 300 required), and reportable incident (notify Cal/OSHA within 8 hours if hospitalization, fatality, or amputation)
- OSHA Form 300, 300A, and 301: what each is, who fills it out, what the annual posting requirement is (Form 300A posted February 1 – April 30 each year)
- Workers-comp notification: first report of injury to your carrier — most policies require notice within 24 hours; late notice can void coverage
- The incident investigation: who does it, what format, what questions to answer (what happened, what conditions enabled it, what systemic failure allowed those conditions, what corrective action prevents recurrence)
- Return-to-work programs: why modified duty benefits the employer (keeps injured worker active, reduces claim cost, maintains employment relationship) and how to structure a simple RTW policy

**Section 6 — EMR: The Business Case for Safety Investment** (~600 words)
- What the Experience Modification Rate is: a multiplier (0.50–2.50+) applied to your workers-comp base premium based on your 3-year loss history relative to your industry average
- How it's calculated: the WCIRB in California produces a mod based on payroll, industry code, and actual vs. expected losses
- The compounding effect: an EMR of 1.25 means you pay 25% more for workers-comp every year; an EMR of 0.85 means a 15% discount
- The three-year lag: losses from Year 1 affect EMR in Year 4; the investment in safety today reduces premium 3 years from now
- The bid-eligibility threshold: many public agencies and larger GCs require subs to have an EMR below 1.00 or 0.95 — a high EMR locks you out of work
- The practical interventions that move the EMR: frequency reduction (fewer incidents) matters more than severity reduction; a return-to-work program that keeps claims small matters more than one big catastrophic prevention
- ROI example: $15,000 invested in safety equipment and training eliminates one $80,000 recordable claim; EMR impact over 3 years saves approximately $30,000–$60,000 in premium

**Section 7 — IIPP Maintenance and Annual Review** (~300 words)
- What changes annually: hazard assessment updated for new work types; toolbox talk calendar refreshed; responsible person confirmed; training records audited for completeness
- How to handle employee turnover: new hires must receive safety orientation and training documented before starting field work — not at the end of Week 1
- Recordkeeping retention: OSHA Form 300 retained 5 years; training records retained for duration of employment plus 5 years; incident investigation reports indefinitely

### Case-Study Scenarios

**Scenario 36.1 — The Cal/OSHA Inspection**
Cal/OSHA conducts a routine inspection of your residential new-build site. The inspector asks for your IIPP, training records, and the most recent safety audit. You produce: your downloaded IIPP (generic, company name on cover, no site-specific content); no training records (you've been doing toolbox talks verbally, not documenting); no safety audit records. Three citations are pending. *Decision point*: What are the likely citation types and penalties? Which citation is most immediately dangerous to your license? What can you do in the next 30 days to reduce penalty amounts through abatement?

**Scenario 36.2 — The Injured Sub's Employee**
A roofing sub's employee falls from your project. The fall is from 14 feet — serious injury, 6-week hospitalization. Cal/OSHA investigates. They cite *you* (the GC) under §1509 (the Multi-Employer Citation policy) because you had "control over the work area" and failed to require the sub to implement fall protection. Your IIPP exists; your toolbox talk records exist; but no record exists that you specifically directed the roofing sub on fall protection at this site. *Decision point*: What is the multi-employer citation standard? What documentation would have protected you? How do you respond to the citation?

**Scenario 36.3 — The EMR Spike**
Your company had three recordable incidents in the past 18 months — a back strain (lost time: 8 days), a laceration (medical treatment only), and a heat illness case (emergency room visit). Your EMR was 0.88 two years ago; it is now 1.14. You lost a public works bid last month because the GC required an EMR below 1.00. *Decision point*: What is the dollar impact of the EMR increase on your workers-comp premium (assume $800,000 annual payroll, base rate 8.5%)? What specific interventions would have prevented each of the three incidents? What is the earliest your EMR can return to below 1.00?

### Production Estimates

- **Estimated word count**: 4,200–5,000 words (excluding templates)
- **Estimated write time**: 10–12 hours (writer with Cal/OSHA and construction safety familiarity)
- **Templates required**: 3 (JHA form, safety audit checklist, incident report form)
- **Prerequisites**: Module 12, Module 14
- **Reading time**: ~3 hours

## Module 37: Industrial Commissioning — Functional Testing, Mechanical Handoff, and Owner Training

**Priority**: Tier 2 — Write when Tier 1 modules are complete. Critical for industrial GC students; secondary value for commercial mechanical/electrical subs moving to prime GC role.

### Why It's Needed

Module 02 mentions "commissioning support" in passing as part of field execution. The actual commissioning workflow — pre-functional checklist execution, functional performance testing, deficiency tracking and resolution, system startup sequences, owner training documentation, and final acceptance — is not taught anywhere in the curriculum. This gap is concentrated in the industrial GC path (Modules 01–04) and matters whenever a mechanical or electrical system must be handed to an owner who operates it. The cost of skipping real commissioning is deferred punch lists, owner disputes at final payment, warranty callbacks on systems that were started incorrectly, and relationship damage with the operations team the GC will need as a repeat client.

**Estimated audience reach**: 100% of Industrial GC path; 30% of Commercial GC path; 20% of Specialty Sub path (MEP subs).

### Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, the student will be able to:

1. Build and execute a pre-functional test (PFT) checklist for a mechanical or electrical system, verifying that all prerequisites for functional performance testing are met.
2. Conduct a functional performance test (FPT) for a representative system and document pass, conditional pass, or fail for each tested function.
3. Create and manage a commissioning deficiency log from discovery through resolution, retest, and owner acceptance.
4. Prepare a system-specific owner training package including design intent, normal operating parameters, alarm response, emergency shutdown, and routine maintenance intervals.
5. Execute the commissioning acceptance sequence — owner sign-off, final documentation package, and the relationship between commissioning acceptance and final payment milestones.

### Content Outline

**Section 1 — What Commissioning Is and Why It Matters for the GC** (~500 words)
- The commissioning process defined: verify that systems are installed and operate per design intent; train owner operations staff; hand over documentation package
- ASHRAE Guideline 0 (The Commissioning Process) and Guideline 1.x series: the industry framework
- The GC's role in commissioning vs. the commissioning agent's role vs. the design engineer's role
- Why commissioning matters for final payment: most industrial and commercial contracts tie the final retainage release to commissioning acceptance — not to substantial completion

**Section 2 — Pre-Functional Testing: Before You Test, Verify Installation** (~700 words)
- What a pre-functional test (PFT) verifies: installation is complete, systems are clean, all components are in place, startup prerequisites are met
- PFT checklist structure by system type: HVAC (air handling units, VAV boxes, exhaust fans), plumbing (pressure tests, insulation, trap priming), electrical (breaker labeling, ground continuity, IR thermography), process piping (hydrostatic test, flushing, NDE completion)
- Who executes the PFT: typically the installing contractor, with the commissioning agent or GC witnessing key steps
- What happens when PFT fails: deficiency is logged, corrected, and retested before FPT begins — FPT on a system that hasn't passed PFT is a waste of time and money
- Documentation: the PFT checklist becomes part of the O&M package

**Section 3 — Functional Performance Testing: Does the System Actually Work?** (~700 words)
- The difference between PFT (is it installed?) and FPT (does it work as designed?)
- FPT procedures by system type: HVAC (air balancing, setpoint verification, control sequence verification, alarm testing); process piping (pressure hold, flow testing, relief valve verification); electrical (load bank testing, UPS failover, bus transfer, generator transfer); fire protection (flow test, alarm verification, suppression system)
- Accepting FPT results: pass, conditional pass (minor deficiency noted but system functional), fail (significant deficiency, cannot accept)
- The owner's role in FPT: owner operations staff should witness FPT — this is their first live exposure to the system; their questions during FPT become training priorities
- Seasonality issues: HVAC systems cannot be fully tested in heating mode during summer — how to handle deferred testing and acceptance conditions in the contract

**Section 4 — The Deficiency Log: From Discovery to Closure** (~500 words)
- What the deficiency log contains: deficiency number, system, description, severity (critical/major/minor), responsible party, target resolution date, resolution description, retest date, accepted by
- Severity classification: critical deficiency = system cannot be operated safely (must be resolved before owner acceptance); major = system functional but outside design parameters (must be resolved within agreed timeframe); minor = aesthetic or non-operational issue (resolved before final payment)
- The GC's role in managing deficiencies from subs: tracking sub resolution is the GC's accountability, not the commissioning agent's
- The deficiency closeout meeting: who attends, how final acceptance is confirmed, what documentation is issued

**Section 5 — Owner Training: Preparing the Operators** (~600 words)
- What owner training must cover: design intent (why the system works the way it does), normal operating parameters (setpoints, pressures, flow rates), startup and shutdown sequences, alarm identification and response, emergency shutdown, routine maintenance intervals and who performs them
- Training format options: in-person hands-on with systems running (best), video recording (good for ongoing reference), written O&M manuals (required, not sufficient alone)
- The training package: draft agenda by system → confirm owner availability → execute with key operations staff → document attendance and topics covered
- The training sign-off: operator's acknowledgment that they have received training and understand their responsibilities — this limits GC warranty claims based on "operator error"
- How to handle operators who weren't in the training: document the gap and notify the owner in writing; offer a follow-up session

**Section 6 — Final Acceptance Documentation and Payment** (~500 words)
- What the final commissioning package contains: PFT checklists (completed), FPT reports (completed), deficiency log (closed), training attendance records, as-built drawings, O&M manuals, warranty documentation, attic stock schedule
- The relationship between commissioning acceptance and final payment: most contracts condition final retainage release on delivery of the commissioning package — know your contract
- Partial acceptance: when the owner takes occupancy before commissioning is complete, how to structure a partial acceptance document that protects the GC from "deemed acceptance" and allows retainage release on completed systems
- The first-year performance period: many industrial and institutional contracts include a 12-month performance period post-commissioning; understanding what triggers callbacks under warranty vs. what triggers a new change order

**Section 7 — ASHRAE and LEED Commissioning Context** (~300 words)
- When ASHRAE Guideline 0 applies formally vs. informally
- LEED EA Prerequisite 1 (Fundamental Commissioning) and EA Credit 3 (Enhanced Commissioning): what they require from the GC
- California Title 24 Part 6 commissioning requirements for commercial HVAC

### Case-Study Scenarios

**Scenario 37.1 — The Premature System Startup**
An owner's operations manager, impatient with schedule delays, starts up a process cooling system on their own without waiting for the GC's FPT. The system runs for 3 days before a bearing failure in the primary chiller — $85,000 repair. The owner claims this is a warranty item. The GC claims the unauthorized startup voided the startup warranty. *Decision point*: What documentation would the GC need to have in place to support their position? What contract clause governs unauthorized startups? What do you do now?

**Scenario 37.2 — The Incomplete Training Package**
Final payment is withheld because the owner's facilities manager says the control system training was inadequate — the operators don't know how to respond to the most common alarm condition. The GC conducted 4 hours of training and has sign-in sheets. The facilities manager was not in the training. *Decision point*: What does your training sign-off form say? Was the facilities manager's absence your responsibility to resolve? What is the commercially reasonable response, and how do you protect the retainage?

## Module 38: Multi-Family and Light Commercial Construction Fundamentals

**Priority**: Tier 2 — Write after Tier 1 modules. Fills the structural gap between the curriculum's residential (single-family) and industrial poles; serves the largest middle market in California construction.

### Why It's Needed

The curriculum's two strongest tracks are single-family residential (Modules 07–28) and heavy industrial (Modules 01–04). The middle market — multi-family housing (Type III and V wood-frame; Type I concrete), tenant improvement, retail build-out, small office, and light commercial — is where most California GCs actually work most of the time. This market has different occupancy classifications, fire-separation requirements, accessibility obligations (ADA and CBC Chapter 11B), MEP shaft coordination challenges, and contract administration demands (AIA A201 general conditions, commercial spec sections) that the current curriculum does not address.

A residential GC who wins their first 20-unit apartment contract, or their first grocery-anchored retail TI, faces an unfamiliar code environment, a more formal contract structure, and coordination complexity the curriculum has not prepared them for.

**Estimated audience reach**: 80% of Residential GC path students (most will encounter commercial or multi-family work); 100% of students using Path 2 (Commercial).

### Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, the student will be able to:

1. Identify the IBC and CBC occupancy classifications and construction types for multi-family and light commercial projects, and apply the resulting fire-resistive construction requirements to a project scope.
2. Navigate the accessible path-of-travel requirements for tenant improvement projects, including the 20% disproportionate cost rule, and identify the GC's obligations when accessible path-of-travel upgrades are triggered.
3. Sequence a multi-family wood-frame project accounting for fire-separation assemblies, shear walls, MEP shaft coordination, and inspection hold points that differ from single-family construction.
4. Administer the formal submittal and RFI process under AIA A201 general conditions on a design-bid-build commercial project, including response time requirements and the design professional's authority.
5. Manage a commercial MEP coordination process at a GC level appropriate for a project without a dedicated MEP coordinator — enough to detect clashes before they become field conflicts.

### Content Outline

**Section 1 — IBC and CBC Occupancy Classifications for Multi-Family and Commercial** (~600 words)
- Occupancy groups relevant to this module: R-2 (multi-family residential), A (assembly), B (business/office), M (mercantile/retail), S-1/S-2 (storage)
- Construction types and their fire-resistance implications: Type V-A (wood-frame with fire-resistive ratings — the standard multi-family type), Type III-A, Type I-A/I-B (concrete and steel)
- Mixed-occupancy buildings: how podium construction works (Type I concrete base with Type V wood above) and why it's California's dominant multi-family form
- Sprinkler requirements: when NFPA 13, 13R, and 13D apply; why multi-family above 3 stories almost always requires 13 or 13R even in unsprinklered jurisdictions
- High-rise triggers: what changes at 55 feet above grade (CBC definition)

**Section 2 — Fire Separation Requirements That GCs Miss** (~600 words)
- Party walls and corridor assemblies: what a 1-hour versus 2-hour assembly requires in terms of materials, framing, and inspection hold points
- Fire blocking and draft stopping: locations required by code in Type V-A multi-family; how to inspect and document before drywall
- The shaft wall: MEP shaft penetration assemblies — UL-listed assemblies, fire-caulking, and why the mechanical sub and the drywall sub will fight over the shaft
- Corridor doors: self-closing, latching, fire-rated assemblies; the most common inspection failure in multi-family
- Common GC error: treating a multi-family project like single-family construction and discovering fire-separation deficiencies at final — the most expensive point to fix them

**Section 3 — Accessibility (ADA and CBC Chapter 11B) in the GC's World** (~700 words)
- The two frameworks: federal ADA (design standards enforced by DOJ/DOT/HUD) and California CBC Chapter 11B (enforced by local building departments — often stricter than ADA)
- What "accessible path of travel" means for tenant improvement projects: when a TI triggers a path-of-travel upgrade and what the 20% disproportionate cost rule allows
- Multi-family specific: accessible and adaptable units, Type A and Type B unit requirements under CBC Chapter 11B, the difference between "accessible" and "usable"
- Common GC obligations at closeout: accessible parking space markings, ramp slopes verified, signage at accessible entrances, door hardware confirmed
- Why the GC (not the designer) is often cited for accessibility violations: the GC built it; if the installed work doesn't comply, the GC is responsible regardless of what the drawings showed
- When to call the DSA (Division of the State Architect): public schools, state-funded projects — accessibility enforcement is different under DSA

**Section 4 — Multi-Family Project Sequencing: What Changes from Single-Family** (~600 words)
- The critical difference: unit repetition creates a zone-by-zone construction sequence, not a single-building sequence
- Zone sequencing: framing by zone → MEP rough-in by zone → inspections by zone → drywall by zone → finish by zone; this creates a wave across the building
- Inspection hold points that stop work: shear wall nailing inspection, fire blocking inspection, MEP rough-in inspection — each must be signed before the zone advances
- Managing multiple trade crews in the same building: how to organize work areas so framing crew on floors 3–4 doesn't create safety hazards for finish crew on floors 1–2
- MEP shaft coordination: the shaft is the critical coordination point; establish shaft layout in the early coordination meeting before framing closes it

**Section 5 — AIA A201 Contract Administration on Commercial Design-Bid-Build** (~600 words)
- How AIA A201 differs from a residential owner-contractor agreement: the design professional (architect) has specific authority to interpret the contract, review submittals, issue certificates for payment, and make binding interpretations of the drawings and specs
- Submittals under A201: the required approval chain (sub → GC review → architect review → return), turnaround time requirements, what "approved as noted" means, and what to do when the architect's response time is causing schedule delay
- RFIs under A201: how to write an RFI that doesn't waive your right to a time extension or cost claim, the required response time, and what to do with a non-responsive design team
- Certificates for payment: the architect's role in certifying pay apps, what justifies a certification reduction, and how to respond to a disputed certification
- Disputes under A201: the Initial Decision Maker (IDM) process before arbitration or litigation — a required step that most GCs don't know about

**Section 6 — Commercial MEP Coordination Without a Dedicated MEP Coordinator** (~500 words)
- What a GC needs to manage MEP coordination: a single floor-by-floor coordination drawing showing all trades' rough-in locations and elevations, with clashes identified before installation
- How to run a coordination meeting without BIM: (1) print overlays of mechanical, electrical, and plumbing sheets on translucent paper and physically overlay them; (2) walk the field with each sub before they install; (3) establish clear elevation rules (plumbing controls elevation; electrical yields to plumbing and mechanical; low-voltage yields to all)
- The shaft review meeting: every MEP sub present, shaft drawing marked up, penetration locations agreed before framing, sleeve sizes confirmed before drywall
- What triggers a BIM coordination requirement: project size (typically >$5M), specification language, or owner requirement — and what it costs to hire a BIM coordinator if required
- The GC's liability when coordination fails: if two subs install conflicting work because the GC didn't coordinate, the GC pays for the redo — not the subs

### Case-Study Scenarios

**Scenario 38.1 — The Tenant Improvement Accessible Path-of-Travel Trap**
You're the GC on a $320,000 retail TI. The tenant is building out a new fast-casual restaurant space in an existing strip center. During plan check, the building department requires accessible path-of-travel upgrades from the parking lot to the accessible entrance — ramp reconstruction, new accessible parking space, and door hardware replacement — total estimated cost $68,000. The tenant says "that's not part of our TI, the landlord should pay for it." The landlord says the TI triggered it, so it's the tenant's cost. *Decision point*: Who is contractually responsible? What does the 20% disproportionate cost rule actually mean here? Can any of the path-of-travel work be deferred?

**Scenario 38.2 — The Missing Fire Blocking**
You're the GC on a 24-unit Type V-A wood-frame multi-family project. At the pre-drywall inspection, the framing inspector identifies missing fire blocking in 14 of 24 units — the framing sub installed blocking at some locations but missed the code-required locations in the attic/floor interface. Correcting the fire blocking requires reopening finished framing in some areas. The framing sub says it's an interpretation issue, not a mistake. *Decision point*: Who is responsible for the cost of correction — the GC, the framing sub, or shared? What does your subcontract say about code compliance? What would have prevented this?
