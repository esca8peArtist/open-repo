# Sustainable and High-Performance Building for the Residential General Contractor

> A working reference for California residential GCs who want to deliver real high-performance homes — Passive House, Net Zero, LEED, or simply "exceptionally well built" — and not just market the language. The focus is what changes on the jobsite, in the details, and in the numbers, not the brochure copy.

---

## 0. Orientation: Going Beyond CalGreen

CalGreen (Title 24 Part 11) is a mandatory minimum. It governs:
- 50% construction waste diversion
- Mandatory low-VOC finishes (adhesives, sealants, paint, composite wood)
- Water-efficient plumbing fixtures (1.28 GPF toilets, 1.8 GPM showerheads, etc.)
- Basic envelope sealing and minimum HERS-verified duct leakage

A code-minimum CalGreen + Title 24 home in California is typically:
- HERS Index 55–70
- ACH50 of 3–5 (envelope leakage)
- Mixed-fuel (gas furnace, gas water heater) or partial-electric
- Ducted central air with R-8 ducts in unconditioned space

A genuine high-performance home is a different animal:
- HERS Index 0–25 (Net Zero or Passive House territory)
- ACH50 of 0.6–1.5
- All-electric, fully heat-pumped
- Continuous insulation, balanced ventilation with heat/energy recovery
- Designed and built as a *system*, not a stack of code requirements

The gap is not money. It is **detailing, sequencing, and discipline**. A skilled GC who understands the building science can deliver a Passive House for 5–10% more than a conventional custom home. A GC who doesn't understand it will spend 20–30% more and still build a leaky, mold-prone house.

## 1. High-Performance Building Fundamentals

### 1.1 The Three Heat Flow Mechanisms

Every wall, roof, slab, and window loses or gains heat via three mechanisms. Skill is knowing which one is dominant in a given assembly.

**Conduction** — heat moving through solid materials. Governed by R-value (thermal resistance) and the cross-section of the assembly. A 2x6 wood stud has an R-value of about R-6.9; the cavity insulation between studs is R-19 to R-23. Studs are thermal short-circuits, which leads to thermal bridging (see §1.5).

**Convection** — heat carried by moving air. Two flavors matter:
- *Air leakage convection*: air bypassing the assembly entirely (cracks at top plate, electrical penetrations, rim joist). This bypasses R-value completely.
- *Cavity convection*: air looping inside an under-insulated or poorly sealed cavity. Fiberglass batts that are not in full contact with all six sides of the cavity lose 30–50% of nominal R-value to convection.

**Radiation** — heat as infrared electromagnetic waves. Dominant in:
- Window heat gain/loss (solar radiation in, longwave radiation out at night)
- Attic spaces (radiant barrier reduces summer ceiling heat gain)
- Low-emissivity (low-E) coatings on glass

### 1.2 Moisture Movement (Four Mechanisms)

Moisture destroys high-performance assemblies more than any other failure mode. Moisture moves four ways, in order of magnitude:

1. **Bulk water** — rain, irrigation, plumbing leaks. Controlled by WRB, flashings, drainage plane. **100,000x more water than vapor diffusion.**
2. **Capillary action** — water wicking through porous materials (concrete, brick, wood end grain). Controlled by capillary breaks (foam sill seal, peel-and-stick at slab edge, kerfed siding).
3. **Air-transported moisture** — water vapor riding on air leaks through the envelope. **100x more moisture than vapor diffusion.** This is why air-sealing is moisture-safety, not just energy.
4. **Vapor diffusion** — water vapor moving through materials by partial pressure gradient. Smallest of the four, but it's the one everyone obsesses over with "vapor barriers."

**Builder takeaway**: spend money on flashing, drainage, and air-sealing before you spend a dime on vapor retarders.

### 1.3 Pressure Differentials and Stack Effect

Air does not move randomly. It moves from high pressure to low pressure. Three drivers:

- **Wind** — pressurizes windward side, depressurizes leeward side and roof
- **Stack effect** — warm air rises; in winter, top of house is positive pressure (exfiltration), bottom is negative (infiltration). The "neutral pressure plane" sits roughly mid-height
- **Mechanical** — bath fans, range hoods, dryers, HRV/ERV imbalance, leaky returns in unconditioned space

A 24-inch range hood at 400 CFM in a tight house can depressurize the house enough to backdraft an atmospheric water heater or pull radon up through the slab. This is why **tight houses require make-up air and balanced ventilation**.

### 1.4 House as a System

Every change to one subsystem cascades to others. Examples:

- Tighter envelope → must add mechanical ventilation (HRV/ERV)
- More insulation → smaller heating system → mini-split or right-sized heat pump, no more 100 kBTU furnace
- Better windows → less perimeter heat loss → no more baseboard convectors under windows; in-floor radiant may be overkill
- Heat pump water heater → cools and dehumidifies the room it's in → either it's in unconditioned garage (cold climate problem) or it becomes a free dehumidifier in conditioned space
- All-electric → no atmospheric vents → no make-up air for combustion appliances, but range-hood make-up air still required
- Tight house + range hood + interior basement door open → can depressurize basement, pull soil gases

**Designing a high-performance house is a coupled optimization problem.** Treat it like one.

### 1.5 The Energy Hierarchy

This order is non-negotiable for cost-effectiveness:

1. **Reduce loads first** — envelope, glazing area, orientation, shading
2. **Right-size efficient systems** — heat pumps, HRV, LED, induction
3. **Renewable energy last** — solar PV, battery storage

Why this order? Every $1 spent on insulation/airtightness reduces the required PV system by $3–5. Adding PV to a leaky, under-insulated house is putting a Tesla battery in a Yugo. You can also never "overshoot" load reduction — comfort and resilience always benefit.

A typical mistake is the inverse: clients ask for "solar panels" first, the GC sells panels, and the house still has thermal bridges everywhere, single-pane windows, and ducts in a 140°F attic. The PV pays for the leaks instead of being the icing.

### 1.6 The Five Control Layers of the Building Enclosure

Every assembly (wall, roof, foundation) must continuously manage five things, in this order of importance:

| Layer | Function | Typical material |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Water (bulk) | Sheds rain | WRB (Tyvek, Solitex, ZIP, fluid-applied) + flashings |
| 2. Air | Stops air leakage | Taped sheathing, fluid-applied membrane, interior poly, smart membrane |
| 3. Vapor | Manages diffusion | Class I/II/III retarder appropriate to climate |
| 4. Thermal | Stops heat flow | Cavity insulation + continuous exterior insulation |
| 5. Structural | Holds it up | Framing, sheathing, fasteners |

**Continuity** is the test. Take a fat marker and try to draw the air barrier all the way around the building section without lifting the pen. If you can't, neither can the building. Every transition — sill plate to slab, wall to roof, window to rough opening, top plate to ceiling — is a potential break.

### 1.7 Thermal Bridging

A 2x6 wood-framed wall with R-21 cavity insulation has a *whole-wall* R-value of about R-14 to R-16, not R-21. The studs, plates, headers, and corners conduct heat around the insulation. Typical framing factors:

- Standard 16" o.c. framing: 23–27% wood by area
- Advanced 24" o.c. framing: 15–18%
- Double-stud wall (offset framing): <5% wood by area (no continuous bridges)

**Solutions, ranked**:

1. **Continuous exterior insulation** — 1.5–4" of rigid mineral wool, polyiso, or EPS on the outside of sheathing. Eliminates thermal bridges through studs. Industry standard for serious performance.
2. **Double-stud walls** — two 2x4 walls with a gap, often 9–12" total thickness. Common in cold climate Passive House. Cheaper than thick foam, but takes more floor area.
3. **Advanced framing (OVE)** — see §7. Reduces framing factor but does not eliminate bridges.
4. **Larsen trusses / Mooney walls** — engineered exterior insulation cavity systems.
5. **SIPs (structural insulated panels)** — eliminates studs but has joint-detailing issues.

**Common bridges builders miss**:
- Slab edge (uninsulated concrete loses massive heat to soil) — solve with 2" XPS or sub-slab insulation extending up
- Cantilevered floors (bay windows, overhangs) — air-seal and insulate underside
- Steel lintels, beams, columns penetrating envelope — thermal breaks required
- Window/door rough openings — Z-flashed continuous insulation around opening
- Garage-to-house wall and ceiling — often the worst-detailed assembly in the house

### 1.8 Hygrothermal Analysis: Why Insulation Placement Matters

Insulation placement determines where the **dew point** sits inside the wall assembly. If the dew point lands inside a moisture-sensitive material (OSB, plywood, wood framing) during winter, you get condensation, then mold, then rot.

**Rule of thumb (cold-side temperature ratio)**:
For climate zones 5+ (Sierra, Tahoe, far Northern CA), the IRC requires that exterior continuous insulation be at least:
- 7.5% of total R-value in CZ 5 (e.g., R-5 of R-21 cavity → wall meets ratio at R-1.6 minimum)
- 23% in CZ 6 (e.g., R-7.5 of R-21)
- 38% in CZ 7 (e.g., R-10 of R-21)

Below those minimums, you must use a Class II vapor retarder on the interior (kraft-faced batts, smart membrane).

For most California climate zones (1–9, 11–13), this is rarely binding — but for CZ 14–16 it matters.

**Vapor drive in California**:
- Cooling-dominated climates (CZ 6, 7, 10, 13, 15): vapor drives **inward** in summer when AC is running. Don't use interior poly. Use a vapor-permeable interior finish.
- Heating-dominated climates (CZ 14, 16): vapor drives **outward** in winter. Use a Class II smart membrane on interior, or rely on enough exterior insulation to keep sheathing warm.
- Mixed (most of CA): use a **smart vapor retarder** (e.g., MemBrain, Intello) that is open in summer and closed in winter.

WUFI or similar hygrothermal simulation is appropriate for any wall above R-30 or where the assembly differs from prescriptive code. Most Passive House consultants will run WUFI on the wall section as part of certification.

## 2. Passive House (Passivhaus)

Passive House is the most rigorous voluntary residential standard in the world. Two competing flavors:

### 2.1 PHI (Germany) vs. PHIUS (United States)

**Passivhaus Institute (PHI)** — Darmstadt, Germany. The original. Single global metric:
- Annual heating demand ≤ 15 kWh/m²/yr (4.75 kBTU/ft²/yr)
- Annual cooling demand ≤ 15 kWh/m²/yr
- Primary energy (PER) ≤ 60 kWh/m²/yr (PHI 9th gen, classic / plus / premium tiers)
- Airtightness ≤ 0.6 ACH50
- No more than 10% of hours over 25°C indoor

PHI is climate-agnostic; the same targets apply in Darmstadt, Sacramento, or Phoenix. This is its biggest critique — it's much harder (and arguably uneconomical) to hit 15 kWh/m² heating in a Maine winter than in Sacramento.

**Passive House Institute US (PHIUS)** — Chicago, USA. Climate-specific. Adjusts targets per location.
- **Phius CORE 2021** — primary "passive" standard
- **Phius ZERO 2021** — CORE plus full electrification plus offset operational energy with on-site or off-site renewables

**Phius 2021 climate-specific targets**:
- Annual heating demand and cooling demand: vary by climate; for Sacramento (CZ 12 / IECC 3B), heating is around 6.0 kBTU/ft²/yr and cooling around 10–15 kBTU/ft²/yr
- Peak heating and peak cooling load: in W/ft² (peak loads are the more binding constraint for system sizing)
- **Airtightness: 0.06 CFM50/ft² of total enclosure area** (roughly 0.6 ACH50 for a typical house, but the area-normalized metric is what governs)
- Source energy targets reduced for ZERO tier

For California, PHIUS is usually the right call. PHI is appealing for the brand cachet but the targets don't reflect that Berkeley and Sacramento don't actually need 16" of exterior wall insulation.

### 2.2 The Five Passive House Principles

1. **Super-insulation** — R-40 to R-60 walls, R-60 to R-80 roofs, R-20 to R-40 slabs
2. **Thermal-bridge-free construction** — every detail designed for ψ (psi) value < 0.01 W/m·K
3. **High-performance windows** — U-factor ≤ 0.14 (PHI) or climate-specific (PHIUS), triple-pane with insulated frames
4. **Airtight envelope** — verified by blower door at 0.6 ACH50 or PHIUS area-normalized equivalent
5. **Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery** — balanced HRV or ERV, 75–95% sensible recovery efficiency

### 2.3 Super-Insulation Options

| Assembly | Typical R-value | Cost premium | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2x6 + R-23 batt + R-15 ext. mineral wool | R-30 effective | $$ | Common Passive House wall in mild climate |
| 2x6 + R-23 + 4" polyiso | R-44 nominal | $$ | Higher R per inch, watch for thermal degradation of foam at cold temps |
| Double 2x4 wall, 12" cavity, dense-pack cellulose | R-44 | $$$ | Most thermal-bridge-free, no foam, excellent hygric buffer |
| Larsen truss / exterior dense-pack | R-50+ | $$$$ | Cold-climate Passive House standard |
| SIPs (8.25" EPS core) | R-32 | $$ | Fast to erect, joint sealing is critical |

**Foam choices**:
- **Open-cell spray foam (ocSPF)** — ~R-3.7/in, vapor open, cheaper, no thermal short-circuit. *Avoid in roof assemblies in cold climates without exterior insulation* — risk of condensation on sheathing.
- **Closed-cell spray foam (ccSPF)** — ~R-6/in, vapor closed, structural, air barrier, water-resistant. Required for sub-slab and exterior-of-sheathing in cold climates. Higher embodied carbon (most blowing agents are still HFCs unless HFO-blown — specify HFO).
- **Polyiso** — ~R-5.7/in nominal but degrades to R-4.5/in below 25°F. Best for warm climates.
- **GPS (graphite polystyrene)** — ~R-4.7/in. Linear performance across temperatures. Good choice for cool/cold sites.
- **Mineral wool (Rockwool ComfortBoard 80/110)** — R-4.2/in. Vapor open, fireproof, hydrophobic, much lower embodied carbon than foam. Industry favorite for exterior continuous insulation when budget allows.

### 2.4 Thermal-Bridge-Free Detailing

The signature Passive House moves:

**Window installation** — the window sits in the plane of the insulation, not at the inside face of the rough opening. Methods:
- Outie install: window frame flush with exterior cladding plane (and the continuous insulation), strapped/screwed back to the structural rough opening
- Buck-frame extensions: plywood or PVC bucks extend the rough opening out into the foam plane
- Innie-with-thick-jamb returns: window in the structural plane, with deep insulated returns to bring it into the conditioned plane (rare in PH)

**Slab edge** — sub-slab insulation (4" EPS minimum) extends up the inside face of the foundation wall, with a thermal break between footing and slab. Often a 2" XPS strip at slab edge, plus exterior foundation insulation to grade.

**Wall-to-roof transition** — continuous exterior insulation wraps from wall up over the wall plate; air barrier is continuous from wall sheathing to ceiling membrane.

**Corners** — two-stud or three-stud corners (advanced framing) eliminate the four-stud corner block that's a thermal short.

**Headers** — single-ply LSL or LVL with foam to the exterior of the header, not double 2x10s solid.

Every Passive House project uses **THERM** or similar 2D heat flow software to model edge details and prove ψ ≤ 0.01 W/m·K.

### 2.5 Airtight Construction

The 0.6 ACH50 target is *7–10x tighter than code minimum*. Strategies:

**Approach A — Taped exterior sheathing (most common in CA)**:
- ZIP System sheathing + ZIP Liquid Flash or ZIP tape at all seams
- All penetrations sealed with liquid flash or compatible tape
- Sheathing-to-foundation transition: liquid-applied flashing, peel-and-stick down to the slab/foundation
- Window/door rough openings: liquid-applied around the entire RO before window installation

**Approach B — Fluid-applied WRB/air barrier**:
- Sto Gold Coat, Prosoco R-Guard / Cat 5, Henry Blueskin VP100, or similar
- Spray or roll on plywood/OSB sheathing
- Excellent for irregular geometry and complex flashing details
- More expensive material; pays back in labor for complex buildings

**Approach C — Interior air barrier (Airtight Drywall Approach)**:
- Gaskets at all wall-to-ceiling, wall-to-floor, electrical box transitions
- Drywall acts as the air barrier
- Requires meticulous detailing; vulnerable to future remodels (someone cuts a hole)
- Common in retrofits, less so in new construction

**Approach D — Membrane behind drywall**:
- Smart vapor retarder (Intello Plus, MemBrain) hung continuously, taped at seams and penetrations
- Common in double-stud walls with dense-pack cellulose
- Becomes the air barrier and the vapor control layer

### 2.6 Blower Door Testing

Run blower door tests in three stages, not just at final:

1. **Pre-drywall blower door** — sheathing/membrane phase. This is when you can FIND leaks and FIX them. Smoke pencil, thermal camera, hand on every penetration. Target ≤ 1.0 ACH50 at this stage to be on track for 0.6 at final.
2. **Post-mechanical / pre-drywall verification** — after all mechanical penetrations are sealed, before drywall hides everything.
3. **Final test** — for certification documentation.

A typical first-time Passive House builder will fail the pre-drywall test by 2–3x. The fix list is always: top plate to attic, bottom plate to slab, electrical penetrations, plumbing penetrations, recessed lights (don't use them), HVAC boots, dryer vent, range hood vent, kitchen sink drain through rim joist.

**Reference numbers**:
- 0.6 ACH50: Passive House (PHI)
- 0.05–0.08 CFM50/ft² shell: PHIUS area-normalized
- 1.0 ACH50: DOE Zero Energy Ready Home minimum, very tight by US standards
- 3.0 ACH50: 2018 IECC code minimum in CZ 3–8 (most of US)
- 5.0 ACH50: 2018 IECC code minimum in CZ 1–2
- 7–10 ACH50: typical existing pre-2000 US house

A 2,500 ft² house at 0.6 ACH50 leaks about 25 CFM at 50 Pa. A code-minimum new home at 5 ACH50 leaks about 200 CFM. The difference at natural pressures is roughly 10x.

### 2.7 HRV vs. ERV

**HRV (Heat Recovery Ventilator)**: exchanges sensible heat only. Incoming and outgoing air pass through a heat exchanger; energy is transferred but moisture is not.
- Best for: cold/dry climates, where interior humidity is already low and you don't want to recycle bathroom moisture
- California zones 14, 16 (mountains): HRV preferred

**ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator)**: exchanges sensible heat *and* latent heat (moisture). Membrane allows water vapor to move between airstreams.
- Best for: hot/humid summer climates (rejects incoming moisture) and dry winter climates (retains indoor humidity from cooking, breathing, plants)
- California zones 1–13: ERV preferred — better humidity control year-round

**Specification**:
- Sensible Recovery Efficiency (SRE): aim for ≥ 75% (Passive House requires ≥ 75% per PHI; PHIUS varies)
- Total Recovery Efficiency for ERV: ≥ 60%
- Specific Fan Power: < 0.45 W/CFM for Passive House (means efficient EC motors)
- ASHRAE 62.2 compliant airflow: typically 0.03 CFM/ft² + 7.5 CFM per occupant

**Distribution**:
- Dedicated ductwork (Passive House best practice): smaller diameter, often 4–6" rigid, supply to bedrooms/living, return from kitchen/baths
- "Tied into the heating ductwork" (poor practice): mixing causes short-circuiting, can't run ventilation independently
- "Spot ventilation in lieu of HRV" (not Passive House): doesn't recover energy; OK for code but not for performance

**Top units in current CA market**: Zehnder ComfoAir series (premium, made in Switzerland), Broan AI series (mid-market, made in US), Mitsubishi Lossnay (compact), Renewaire (commercial-grade, common in multifamily).

### 2.8 Passive House Windows

Window performance dominates envelope heat loss in any cold-leaning climate. Targets:

**Passive House targets**:
- Whole-window U-factor ≤ 0.14 BTU/hr·ft²·°F (≤ 0.80 W/m²·K) for PHI
- PHIUS climate-specific: U-0.17 to U-0.28 typical for CA climates
- SHGC: variable by orientation. South: 0.40+ (free winter gain). East/West: 0.25 (block summer gain). North: 0.30 (modest balance).
- Air leakage: ≤ 0.04 CFM/ft² (Class AW windows)

**Construction**:
- Triple-pane insulated glazing, argon or krypton fill, two low-E coatings
- "Warm edge" spacer (foam or stainless, not aluminum)
- Insulated frame: fiberglass (Cascadia, Inline, Alpen), wood-clad-aluminum with thermal break, or pultruded composite
- Tilt-turn operation (European standard) for compression seal — much tighter than US double-hung

**Sources**:
- European imports: Optiwin, Internorm, Schüco, ProgressionPH, Zola. Premium, often 4–6 month lead times
- Domestic Passive House-capable: Alpen High Performance Products (Colorado), Cascadia Windows (BC/WA), Inline Fiberglass (Ontario), Logic (PA)
- Mid-market high-performance (not full PH but close): Marvin Essential, Andersen 100 with triple pane, Pella Reserve triple pane

**Installation in thick walls**:
- Outie install with bucks: window screwed to bucks at the exterior face, sealed with backer rod and sealant at the inside, expanding foam (low-expansion) at the gap
- Use Tremco Illbruck I3 system or 3M 8067 flashing tape with primer
- Sloped exterior sill (1:12 minimum) — this gets missed
- Drainage plane integration: WRB/peel-and-stick laps OVER the head flashing, UNDER the window at jambs/sill

## 3. Net Zero Energy and Near-Net-Zero

### 3.1 Site Net Zero vs. Source Net Zero

**Site Net Zero**: kWh produced on-site (typically via PV) over a year ≥ kWh consumed on-site. The metric most people mean when they say "net zero." Easier to hit.

**Source Net Zero** (a.k.a. primary energy net zero): accounts for the upstream losses of grid electricity (combustion at the power plant, transmission losses). Source factors are typically 2.5–3.0 for grid electricity vs. ~1.05 for natural gas. Much harder to achieve.

Why it matters: under source accounting, electrifying everything *increases* your source energy unless your grid is clean. California's grid was 52% renewable + zero-carbon as of 2023, with a source factor closer to 1.7–2.0. Under PHIUS ZERO and the DOE Zero Energy Ready Home program, source-based accounting is increasingly the standard.

**Practical implication for the GC**: if you size PV to cover *site* annual kWh, you're delivering site net zero. If your client wants to be a "grid asset" or sees the home as part of a low-carbon future, you'll oversize PV by 30–50% (also good for battery autonomy).

### 3.2 HERS Index

The Home Energy Rating System (RESNET) is the residential energy benchmark in the US. Scale:

| HERS | Performance |
|---|---|
| 150 | Existing pre-1980 home |
| 100 | 2006 IECC reference home (baseline) |
| 70 | Typical new California home (Title 24 minimum) |
| 50 | ENERGY STAR Certified Home |
| 30 | DOE Zero Energy Ready Home (before PV) |
| 0 | Net Zero (with PV) |
| <0 | Net positive (producing more than consuming) |

Passive House homes typically come in at HERS 10–25 *before* PV, and 0 to negative with PV.

Title 24 has been requiring HERS-verified measures (duct leakage, refrigerant charge, QII insulation, blower door) since 2008. **All Title 24 homes have a HERS rater** at final — they're just not called out on the listing.

### 3.3 Load Reduction (Non-Passive House)

If you're building "high performance but not Passive House," a defensible target stack for California:

| Component | Target |
|---|---|
| Walls | R-23 cavity + R-5 to R-10 continuous = R-28 to R-33 effective |
| Attic/roof | R-60 (CZ 14, 16) to R-49 (most of CA), unvented preferred |
| Slab | R-10 perimeter, R-5 sub-slab if budget allows |
| Windows | U-0.25 or better, dual-pane low-E + argon (triple-pane in CZ 16) |
| Air sealing | ≤ 1.5 ACH50 |
| Ducts | Inside conditioned space (best) or R-8 with ≤ 4% leakage |
| Water heating | Heat pump water heater (HPWH), 65+ gallon, UEF ≥ 3.5 |
| Cooking | Induction range, not gas |
| Cooling/heating | Heat pump (variable-speed mini-split or central HP), HSPF2 ≥ 8.5, SEER2 ≥ 16 |
| Ventilation | Balanced HRV or ERV with SRE ≥ 75% |
| Lighting | 100% LED, controls for daylighting |
| Appliances | All ENERGY STAR Most Efficient where available |

This package will land HERS 30–40 without PV, and HERS 0 with a typically-sized PV array (4–8 kW for a 2,000–3,000 ft² home).

### 3.4 All-Electric Buildings

California reach codes (Berkeley, Oakland, San Francisco, San Jose, Santa Monica, Sacramento, etc.) require or incentivize all-electric new construction. As of 2026, the statewide CEC standard incentivizes heat pumps for both space and water heating in all CA climate zones; many jurisdictions have outright gas bans for new construction.

**Why all-electric**:
- No combustion appliances → no atmospheric venting → tighter envelope possible
- No gas infrastructure → save the $10–20K of gas line, meter, and trunk runs
- Indoor air quality: no NO₂, no benzene from gas stoves, no combustion byproducts
- Path to net zero: electrons can be solar, methane cannot
- Long-term operating cost: heat pumps are 2.5–4x more efficient than gas furnaces; HPWH 3–4x more efficient than gas tank

**Sizing the electric service**:
- 100A: adequate for code-minimum, modest house, no EV
- 200A: standard. Heat pump + HPWH + induction + EV charger (Level 2 at 40A) usually fits
- 400A: large homes, two EVs, large mini-split system, pool, hot tub. Often required by builders who don't load-calc properly
- **Load calc** (NEC Article 220, optional method 220.82 for dwellings): most all-electric California homes can fit on 200A with proper load management — smart panels (Span, Schneider Square D Energy Center, Lumin) are the new tool. They time-shift loads so you don't need to upsize service.

**Heat pump water heater performance**:
- Best COP at 50–80°F ambient. California garages, utility rooms, basements all work well.
- COP drops below 40°F (mountain climates) — needs supplemental electric resistance, defrost cycles
- Cools and dehumidifies the room it's in (~1 ton of cooling). In CZ 14, 16 garage installations, the heat exhaust can chill the garage to uncomfortable levels in winter
- Models: Rheem ProTerra, AO Smith Voltex, Bradford White Aerotherm, Sanden CO2 (split-system, premium)
- Recovery is slower than gas — size by first-hour rating, not just tank capacity. 65 gallon HPWH ≈ 50 gallon gas in real-world delivery
- Ducted exhaust kit lets you dump cool exhaust outside (summer) or into AC return (year-round)

### 3.5 Solar PV Sizing

Quick sizing for California residential:

```
PV system kW (DC) = annual kWh consumption / (production factor × shading factor × DC-AC derate)
```

Production factor for California:
- CZ 13, 15 (Central Valley, desert): 1,650–1,750 kWh/kW/yr
- CZ 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 12 (LA basin, Sacramento Valley, coastal-inland transition): 1,500–1,650
- CZ 1, 3, 4, 5 (coastal, foggy): 1,300–1,500

Example: 2,500 ft² all-electric home in Sacramento uses ~7,000 kWh/yr → 7,000 / 1,600 = 4.4 kW system. Add 20% for NEM 3.0 self-consumption strategy and battery losses → 5.3 kW.

**Roof area required**: roughly 65–70 ft² per kW of modern panels (400+ W modules). A 5.3 kW system = ~350 ft² of unshaded south/southwest roof.

**DC/AC ratio**: oversize the DC array relative to the inverter. 1.15–1.30 is standard. Clips peak but captures more on cloudy days and morning/evening. NEM 3.0 makes this even more attractive — peak production isn't valued highly anyway.

**Inverter topology**:

| Type | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| String inverter | Cheapest, simplest | Whole-string failure mode, no module-level monitoring, shade hurts whole string |
| String + DC optimizer (SolarEdge) | Module-level optimization, single inverter point of failure | Inverter is the warranty risk (10-year std); rapid shutdown built-in |
| Microinverter (Enphase IQ8) | Module-level conversion, redundant, simple permitting | Cost premium ($0.10–0.20/W); many small electronics on the roof |

For California with NEM 3.0 + battery + complex roofs (multiple azimuths, dormers, shading), microinverters are the default residential choice. Enphase ecosystem also gives clean battery integration with IQ Battery.

### 3.6 Battery Storage

Under NEM 3.0 (in effect since April 2023, sustained on appeal in 2026), export rates fell roughly 75% from NEM 2.0. Average export ≈ $0.05/kWh; peak (summer 4–9 PM) up to $3.50/kWh; off-peak near zero. **Self-consumption with batteries is now the economic play, not export.**

Reference: [Solar.com NEM 3.0 explainer](https://www.solar.com/learn/nem-3-0-proposal-and-impacts-for-california-homeowners/), [EnergySage NEM 3.0 guide](https://www.energysage.com/blog/net-metering-3-0/), [Solar Power World on the 2026 court ruling](https://www.solarpowerworldonline.com/2026/03/california-court-strikes-down-nem-3-0-reform-appeal/).

**Battery products**:

| Battery | Usable kWh | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tesla Powerwall 3 | 13.5 kWh, 11.5 kW continuous | Integrated solar inverter (97.5% efficient), good arbitrage |
| Enphase IQ Battery 5P | 5 kWh (modular) | Stack up to 80 kWh; matches Enphase microinverter system; LFP chemistry |
| FranklinWH aPower 2 | 15 kWh, 12 kW | Newer entrant; AC-coupled |
| Sonnen ecoLinx | 10–20 kWh | Most expensive, longest warranty, robust battery management |
| SolarEdge Home Battery | 10 kWh stackable | Integrates with SolarEdge inverter |

**Sizing strategies**:

1. **Self-consumption** (most common): cover the typical evening load 4 PM – midnight. Usually 10–15 kWh = one Powerwall.
2. **Whole-home backup**: cover essential loads for 12–24 hours during outage. 20–40 kWh = 2–3 batteries.
3. **Off-grid resilience**: 3–7 days autonomy plus PV. 40–80 kWh. Rare; expensive.

**NEM 3.0 implications for the GC**:
- New solar-only installs have ~10–14 year paybacks vs. 5–8 years under NEM 2.0
- Solar + battery installs have 7–10 year paybacks and dramatically better lifetime ROI
- **Sell solar+battery together as a single system**, not as add-ons
- Pre-NEM 2.0 customers (interconnected by April 14, 2023) keep NEM 2.0 for 20 years and can add batteries without losing it. Some pre-existing solar customers should NOT replace their array (it would force them onto NEM 3.0)
- Existing homes built post-April 2023 are already on NEM 3.0; design for self-consumption from day one

## 4. LEED for Homes

LEED for Homes (USGBC, current version v4.1) is the most internationally recognized residential green building standard. Largely a "checklist plus prerequisites" structure.

### 4.1 Rating Structure

Total 110 points possible. Certification levels:

| Level | Points |
|---|---|
| Certified | 40–49 |
| Silver | 50–59 |
| Gold | 60–79 |
| Platinum | 80+ |

**Prerequisites (mandatory, no points)**:
- Integrative process (early team meeting)
- Floodplain avoidance
- Minimum energy performance (ENERGY STAR equivalent)
- Indoor water-use reduction (≥ 20% below baseline)
- Whole-house ventilation per ASHRAE 62.2
- Combustion venting (sealed combustion only)
- Construction activity pollution prevention

### 4.2 Credit Categories (and point ranges)

| Category | Points | Key credits for a residential GC |
|---|---|---|
| Integrative Process | 2 | Early energy modeling, team charrette |
| Location and Transportation | 15 | Site selection, transit, density, walkability |
| Sustainable Sites | 7 | No-disturbance setbacks, rainwater management |
| Water Efficiency | 12 | Indoor (1.5 GPM showerheads, dual-flush toilets), outdoor (drip irrigation), water metering |
| Energy and Atmosphere | 38 | Largest category. HERS index, renewable energy, advanced framing |
| Materials and Resources | 10 | Whole-building LCA, environmental product declarations (EPDs), regional materials, FSC wood |
| Indoor Environmental Quality | 16 | Low-emitting materials, ventilation, MERV-13 filtration, garage isolation |
| Innovation | 6 | Beyond-credit performance, exemplary performance, LEED AP |
| Regional Priority | 4 | Climate/state-specific bonus points |

### 4.3 The Provider/Green Rater System

LEED for Homes is verified by a two-tier system:

- **LEED for Homes Provider**: organization contracted by USGBC for your region. They oversee the rating process, maintain QA, and submit the project for certification.
- **Green Rater**: trained field verifier (often also a HERS Rater) who visits the site at rough-in and final, runs performance tests, and signs off on credits.

**Cost to certify a single-family home**:
- Provider fee: $1,500–$3,500 depending on home size and target level
- Green Rater fees: $1,500–$3,000 (includes 2–3 site visits, blower door, duct test)
- USGBC registration: $250
- USGBC certification fee: $300–$600

**Total: roughly $3,500–$6,000 in certification soft costs**, on top of the construction premium.

### 4.4 Most Impactful Credits for a Residential GC

In rough order of "biggest bang per dollar":

1. **EA prerequisite — ENERGY STAR equivalence**: usually drives the whole envelope and mechanical design
2. **EA c.1: Annual energy use** — up to 29 points based on HERS-style performance modeling
3. **IEQ c.2: Low-emitting materials** — paints, adhesives, flooring, insulation, composite wood
4. **MR c.3: Construction waste management** — 50% (1 pt), 75% (2 pt), 90% (3 pt)
5. **EA c.4: Air infiltration** — points for ACH50 below code minimum
6. **WE c.3: Indoor water use** — 35–50% reduction (use EPA WaterSense fixtures)
7. **IEQ c.4: Enhanced ventilation** — MERV-13 filter, balanced ventilation
8. **MR c.4: Environmentally preferable products** — FSC wood, EPDs, regional sourcing

### 4.5 Certifying vs. Building to the Standard

The honest builder conversation with a client:

> "We can either build to LEED Gold and not certify, which saves about $5,000 in fees but means no plaque on the wall, no MLS marker, and no third-party verification. Or we certify, which costs about $5,000 and gives you documentation, a transferable certificate, and accountability for me to deliver what I promised. The construction cost is the same either way."

Most clients with the budget for a custom home choose to certify if they're already going to do the work. The third-party verification is the value-add. The buyers who balk at certification fees often also balk at the actual envelope and mechanical upgrades — those are not your high-performance clients.

## 5. Construction Waste Management

### 5.1 CalGreen 50% Diversion (Mandatory)

CalGreen § 4.408 requires 50% diversion of construction and demolition waste by weight (or volume, but weight is standard). This is **mandatory** on all new residential and substantial alterations.

Documentation required at COO:
- Receipts/manifests from C&D recyclers showing recycled tons
- Documentation of source-separated recycling
- Final waste audit showing total tons generated, tons diverted, % diversion

Many local jurisdictions push this higher (San Francisco: 65–75%; Berkeley: 75%; LA: 75%).

### 5.2 Major Waste Streams on a Residential Project

By weight, a typical 2,500 ft² single-family build generates roughly:

| Material | % by weight | Recyclable? |
|---|---|---|
| Wood (framing scrap, formwork) | 30% | Yes (chip for mulch, biomass) |
| Drywall | 15% | Yes (back to manufacturer or gypsum recycler) |
| Concrete/masonry | 15% | Yes (crushed for aggregate) |
| Metal (rebar, ducts, flashing, copper) | 5% | Yes (high value scrap) |
| Cardboard/paper | 10% | Yes (curbside) |
| Insulation scrap | 3% | Limited (some manufacturers take back) |
| Plastic (vapor barriers, wrap, packaging) | 5% | Limited |
| Roofing (asphalt shingles) | 5% | Yes (asphalt road base) |
| Mixed/non-recyclable | 12% | No |

### 5.3 Sorting Strategies

**On-site source separation** (highest diversion %, more labor):
- Separate bins for wood, metal, drywall, concrete, cardboard, mixed trash
- Requires site footprint (4–6 bins) and crew discipline
- Best for large projects (>3,500 ft²) with longer schedules

**Mixed C&D with sorted facility** (lower diversion %, simpler logistics):
- One big roll-off, sorting happens at the recycler
- Facility provides diversion certificate based on their average sort rate (typically 60–75%)
- Easier on small lots; lower labor cost
- Most common for typical 2,500–3,500 ft² builds in CA

**Hybrid** (most common):
- Separate roll-offs for the high-volume streams (wood, concrete during foundation work)
- Mixed C&D bin during finish phases
- Cardboard kept separate (easy curbside or own truck)

### 5.4 Deconstruction vs. Demolition

For tear-downs, deconstruction sells materials and dramatically improves diversion:

**Deconstruction** (manual disassembly, salvage):
- Time: 3–4x longer than demo
- Cost: usually 2–3x demolition cost gross, but tax deduction for donated materials (Habitat ReStore, The ReUse People) often offsets 50–100% of the premium
- Salvage typical from a 1950s tract home: lumber (Douglas fir is high-value), doors, windows, hardwood floors, fixtures, cabinets
- Recovered value: $5,000–$25,000 for typical home

**Demolition with sorted recycling** (mechanical, but recyclables pulled):
- Time: 1.5–2x conventional demo
- Cost: 1.2–1.5x demo
- Diversion: 60–80% achievable

**Pure demolition** (loader and roll-off):
- Cheapest, fastest
- 30–50% diversion if you mix C&D and use a sorted facility
- Does not meet many local thresholds

### 5.5 C&D Recyclers in California

**Finding facilities**:
- CalRecycle Recycling Locator: https://www2.calrecycle.ca.gov/wheretorecycle/
- Local jurisdiction waste authority (most have approved-hauler lists)
- Build It Green has C&D recycler directories by region

**Major California players**:
- Recology (Bay Area)
- Athens Services (LA, San Gabriel Valley)
- Republic Services (statewide)
- Waste Management (statewide)
- Local independents (e.g., Zanker Recycling in San Jose — one of the highest diversion rates in the state)

**What they accept**:
- Most accept: wood, drywall, concrete, metal, cardboard, asphalt shingles
- Many do not accept: insulation, mixed plastics, hazardous (paint, solvents, treated wood)
- Treated wood (CCA, ACQ) goes to a Class II landfill (treated wood waste) — separate manifest

### 5.6 Waste Reduction by Design

The biggest waste reduction comes before the first piece of lumber is delivered:

- **Advanced framing** (§7): 25–35% less lumber
- **Design to standard dimensions**: 4-ft, 8-ft, 16-ft increments → no scrap. A 24x36 house is more efficient than 23x37.
- **Panelized framing**: factory-cut and assembled wall panels arrive ready to set. ~50% less scrap, faster, but premium cost.
- **Just-in-time delivery**: less site storage waste, less weather damage
- **Material take-offs by experienced estimators**: most lumber overrun comes from sloppy take-off, not field waste

## 6. Healthy Materials and Indoor Air Quality

### 6.1 VOC Limits (CalGreen Mandatory)

CalGreen § 4.504 sets mandatory VOC limits for:

| Product | Limit (g/L) |
|---|---|
| Architectural coatings (flat paint) | 50 |
| Non-flat paint | 100 |
| Anti-corrosive coating | 250 |
| Stains | 250 |
| Indoor adhesives (general) | 50 |
| Floor adhesive | 50 |
| Construction adhesive | 70 |
| Sealants (general) | 250 |
| Sealants (architectural) | 250 |
| Composite wood — see §6.2 | varies |

**Verification**: keep MSDSs / SDSs for every product used. Manufacturers must declare VOC content. Look for products explicitly labeled "CalGreen Compliant" or "SCAQMD Rule 1113 compliant" (the underlying air district rule).

**Beware**: "low-VOC" is not regulated; "zero-VOC" usually refers to the base only. Colorants add 10–150 g/L. Specify zero-VOC base AND zero-VOC tints (e.g., Benjamin Moore Natura with Gennex colorants, Sherwin-Williams Harmony, Behr Premium Plus Ultra No-VOC, ECOS).

### 6.2 Formaldehyde and Composite Wood

CARB ATCM Phase 2 (2009, amended) and EPA TSCA Title VI (2016, fully effective 2018) set the same emission limits for:

| Product | Emission limit (ppm) |
|---|---|
| Hardwood plywood (veneer core) | 0.05 |
| Hardwood plywood (composite core) | 0.05 |
| Particleboard | 0.09 |
| MDF | 0.11 |
| Thin MDF (≤ 8 mm) | 0.13 |

All composite wood and finished goods sold in the US since March 22, 2019 must be certified compliant by an EPA-recognized third-party certifier (TPC). Reference: [EPA Formaldehyde Emission Standards for Composite Wood Products](https://www.epa.gov/formaldehyde/formaldehyde-emission-standards-composite-wood-products).

**Beyond mandatory**:
- **NAF (No Added Formaldehyde)**: uses no urea-formaldehyde binders. Soy-based (e.g., PureBond plywood) or MDI binders. Emits at or below ambient outdoor formaldehyde (~0.01 ppm).
- **ULEF (Ultra-Low Emitting Formaldehyde)**: uses formaldehyde resins but at very low emission levels (typically <50% of TSCA limits).
- **Practical pick**: NAF plywood and MDF for any millwork, cabinetry, or interior finish work. Cost premium 10–25%; worth it for IAQ.

OSB sheathing is almost universally low-emitting (uses MDI/PMDI binder, not urea-formaldehyde). Not a primary IAQ concern.

### 6.3 Carpet and Flooring

| Material | Certification to specify |
|---|---|
| Carpet | Carpet and Rug Institute (CRI) Green Label Plus |
| Carpet cushion/pad | CRI Green Label |
| Resilient flooring (LVT, vinyl, linoleum) | FloorScore |
| Hardwood | FSC + low-VOC factory finish or site-finished with water-based |
| Tile | Inherently low-VOC; check the thinset/grout |
| Adhesive for flooring | CalGreen Table 4.504.1 |

**Avoid in tight homes**:
- Wall-to-wall carpet in primary living areas — sink for dust, allergens, and (in older specs) formaldehyde from backing
- Vinyl with phthalate plasticizers — specify phthalate-free
- Engineered hardwood with formaldehyde-bonded core — specify NAF

### 6.4 Paint

The marketing terms vs. the truth:

- **Zero-VOC**: base paint contains ≤ 5 g/L VOC. Colorants typically add 10–150 g/L. A medium tint in zero-VOC base can yield 50–100 g/L final paint.
- **Low-VOC**: ≤ 50 g/L final paint
- **GreenGuard Gold**: tested final product (with tints), more stringent than VOC limit alone — also tests for other chemicals

**Specify**: "GreenGuard Gold certified zero-VOC interior latex with low-VOC colorants" — and require the specifying contractor (you) to verify the can label, not the spec sheet.

### 6.5 Insulation Health Considerations

| Insulation | Worker safety | Occupant IAQ |
|---|---|---|
| Fiberglass batt | PPE during install (skin/lung irritant); formaldehyde-free options now standard (Owens Corning Pink, Knauf EcoBatt) | Inert once installed; no off-gassing |
| Mineral wool (Rockwool, Thermafiber) | PPE during install; not as itchy as fiberglass | Inert; vapor-open; fire-resistant; preferred for sound and fire |
| Open-cell spray foam | Major: isocyanate exposure during install (24–72 hr re-entry); ventilation required during cure | Trace amine off-gassing if mis-applied; mostly stable |
| Closed-cell spray foam | Same install hazards; HFO blowing agents (preferred) vs. legacy HFC (high GWP) | Stable when properly mixed and applied |
| Cellulose (dense-pack) | Dust during install; relatively benign | Borate fire retardants — non-toxic; some find it dusty |
| Wood fiber (Steico, GUTEX) | Benign | Inert, vapor-open, premium material |
| EPS / GPS | Benign | Stable; vapor-semipermeable |
| XPS | Benign | Higher embodied carbon (HFC blowing agents being phased out for HFO) |

**Practical pick for a healthy assembly**: mineral wool in walls and roof, cellulose in attics, EPS (or HFO-blown closed-cell) below grade.

### 6.6 HVAC Filtration

ASHRAE 52.2 rates filter efficiency by MERV.

| MERV | What it captures | Pressure drop |
|---|---|---|
| 6–8 | Lint, dust, pollen (basic) | Low (0.1" wg) |
| 11 | Smaller dust, mold spores | Moderate (0.2" wg) |
| 13 | Bacteria, smoke, PM2.5 (LEED, ASHRAE 62.2 standard for high-IAQ homes) | High (0.3–0.5" wg) |
| 16 | Most viruses, sub-micron PM | Very high (0.5–0.8" wg) |
| HEPA | Virtually all particles | Requires dedicated fan |

**MERV-13** is the IAQ industry standard for "healthy home" filtration. Critical to size the air handler and ductwork for the pressure drop — a MERV-13 in a system designed for MERV-8 will starve airflow, freeze the coil, and trip a high-static cutoff.

**Builder coordination**:
- 4–5" deep media filter cabinet, not 1" filter slot (lower pressure drop, longer change interval — 6–12 months)
- Air handler ECM motor with sufficient static pressure capacity (0.8" wg total external static, not 0.5")
- Wildfire smoke addition: dedicated MERV-13 or HEPA recirculating unit (IQAir, AustinAir) in primary bedroom

### 6.7 Post-Construction Air Quality Testing

For owners who want verification (or have chemical sensitivities):

**Common tests**:
- Formaldehyde: 24-hr passive badge sample (around $150–300 per location). Action level: 27 ppb (California OEHHA acute) or 7 ppb (chronic).
- TVOCs: photoacoustic or PID monitor, or 24-hr sorbent tube sent to lab. Action level varies — most healthy-home programs target ≤ 500 µg/m³ TVOC.
- Particulate (PM2.5): real-time monitor (PurpleAir, Awair); good for HVAC verification
- Radon: 90-day passive test required by most green programs in CZ 14, 16 (rare elsewhere in CA but increasing in mountain areas)

**Pre-occupancy bake-out**: run the HVAC at 80°F+ for 48–72 hours with full ventilation before testing — accelerates off-gassing and gives a realistic reading.

## 7. Advanced Framing (Optimum Value Engineering)

### 7.1 Principles

OVE was developed by the NAHB Research Center in the 1970s and has been refined ever since. Core moves:

| Element | Conventional | Advanced |
|---|---|---|
| Stud spacing | 16" o.c. | 24" o.c. |
| Plate stacking | Random | In-line (rafter/truss aligns with stud, aligns with floor joist) |
| Corners | 3-stud or 4-stud with drywall blocking | 2-stud corner with drywall clip or 3-stud with rotated middle stud |
| T-intersections (interior wall to exterior) | 3-stud channel | Ladder blocking or drywall clips |
| Headers | Double 2x10 with cripples | Sized for actual load; often single LVL or sized header with insulated cavity above |
| Cripples and jacks | Multiple | Engineered, minimum required |
| Top plates | Double 2x | Single 2x with metal plate strap, where allowed |
| Floor joist sized to load | Often oversized | Engineered I-joists at calculated spacing |

### 7.2 Material Savings

Typical results (from NAHB and DOE Building America studies):

- **Lumber: 25–35% reduction** in board-feet
- **Labor: 5–10% reduction** (fewer pieces to install, but more cuts at headers)
- **Insulation: 5–10% more cavity volume**, plus reduced thermal bridging

A typical 2,500 ft² home: conventional framing ≈ 14,000 bf; advanced framing ≈ 9,500–10,500 bf. At $0.70–1.10/bf for #2 SPF or DF, that's $2,500–$5,000 in lumber savings.

### 7.3 Structural Implications

24" o.c. framing requires:

- **Sheathing**: 7/16" OSB or 1/2" plywood is standard. At 24" o.c., must be installed with long edge perpendicular to studs and blocked at unsupported edges (or use 5/8").
- **Drywall**: 5/8" ceiling drywall at 24" o.c. trusses (already standard); 1/2" wall drywall at 24" o.c. studs can show "fastener telegraphing" if you use 1/2"; specify 5/8" wall drywall on 24" o.c. for premium finish.
- **Wind/seismic**: hold-downs and shear walls must be designed for the spacing. Most California Class A engineers can handle this; structural calcs are typically required by AHJ.
- **Wall height**: studs ≥ 9' may need to be upgraded (#1 grade or doubled) — check IRC Table R602.3(5).

### 7.4 Coordination

Advanced framing is not a "field substitution" decision the GC can make alone:

- **Structural engineer**: should design the project for OVE from the start. Truss spacing, beam sizing, hold-downs all change.
- **Architect**: must align floor-to-floor stacking, window placement on stud module
- **Truss/I-joist supplier**: typically already designs for 24" o.c.
- **Permit**: most CA AHJs accept OVE under IRC R602; some require engineered design (more rigorous calc, beneficial anyway)
- **Crew**: foreman buy-in critical. Crews used to 16" o.c. will revert under pressure. Pre-mark studs, walk the layout with the framer.

- Energy
- Indoor Air Quality / Health
- Resources (materials, waste)
- Water
- Community

- Less brand recognition outside CA — affects resale to relocating buyers

### 8.3 ENERGY STAR New Homes

The baseline of "verified efficient." Requires:

- HERS rater verification at rough-in and final

- Quality-installed insulation (Grade I per RESNET)
- Minimum HVAC and water heating efficiencies
- ENERGY STAR appliances and lighting
- Blower door and duct leakage testing

In California, ENERGY STAR's national requirements are often lower than Title 24, so the "ENERGY STAR" label is sometimes a no-op — Title 24 compliance achieves it automatically. But the **documentation, third-party verification, and marketing label** still have value.

### 8.4 What Clients Actually Care About

Surveys (NAHB, RESNET, USGBC) consistently show that home buyers care, in order:

1. **Lower utility bills** (top reason, ~80%)
2. **Healthier indoor air** (especially post-COVID, post-wildfire smoke)
3. **Comfort** — no drafts, no hot/cold rooms, quiet
4. **Resilience** — backup during outages, fire-resistant
5. **Resale value** (HERS index now on MLS in many California markets)
6. *Then* — certifications and environmental claims

**Marketing implication**: lead conversations with bills, air, comfort, resilience. The certification is a third-party verification of those claims, not the claim itself.

### 8.5 Pricing Premium and ROI

What the market supports in California:

| Performance level | Typical cost premium (over Title 24 minimum) |
|---|---|
| Title 24 + better air sealing + ENERGY STAR appliances | 1–2% |

Recovery on operating costs (utility bill reduction): typically 7–15 years on the envelope/mechanical premium; PV payback varies 8–14 years under NEM 3.0 (faster with battery).

**Resale**: a 2020 California Department of Real Estate study showed certified high-performance homes selling at a 4–9% premium and 30–50% faster than comparable non-certified homes in the same neighborhoods.

### 8.6 Finding Green Building Clients

- **Architect referrals** — Passive House and LEED-credentialed architects need GCs who won't fight them. Be the GC who *gets it*. AIA local chapters have committees on Energy/Sustainability.

- **Trade associations**:
  - **Phius Certified Builder** (free training, listed in Phius directory)
  - **Certified Green Professional (CGP)** through NAHB
  - **LEED AP Homes** (USGBC credential — modest study commitment, big credibility)
  - **Build It Green Certified GreenPoint Rater** — be the rater, not just the builder, on small projects
- **Lead with a project portfolio**: one well-photographed, well-documented Passive House or Net Zero project does more than ten years of brochure copy
- **Energy audit / consulting as a foot in the door**: offer existing-home energy retrofits as a service line; many turn into new-build clients

## 9. Climate-Specific Considerations for California

California's 16 Title 24 climate zones span more variation than most other US states. Reference: [California Energy Commission climate zone tool](https://www.energy.ca.gov/programs-and-topics/programs/building-energy-efficiency-standards/climate-zone-tool-maps-and).

### 9.1 Climate Zone Cheat Sheet

| CZ | Region | Characteristic | Dominant load |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | North coast (Crescent City, Eureka) | Cool, very foggy | Heating |
| 2 | North coast valley (Santa Rosa, Napa) | Mild, fog-influenced | Mixed |
| 3 | SF Bay Area coast/inner (SF, Oakland, San Mateo) | Mild, marine | Mixed (mostly heating in shoulder seasons) |
| 4 | Central coast inland (San Jose) | Mild, mild summers | Mixed |
| 5 | Central coast (Santa Barbara, San Luis Obispo) | Mild marine | Mixed (low both) |
| 6 | LA Basin coast (Santa Monica, LAX) | Mild marine | Mixed (low both) |
| 7 | San Diego | Mild marine | Cooling-leaning |
| 8 | LA inland coast (Pasadena, Long Beach) | Warm-mild | Cooling |
| 9 | LA inland (Burbank, San Bernardino flats) | Hot summer | Cooling |
| 10 | San Bernardino/Riverside (Riverside, Ontario) | Hot summer | Cooling |
| 11 | Northern Valley (Redding, Red Bluff) | Hot summer, cool winter | Both |
| 12 | Sacramento Valley (Sacramento, Stockton, Davis) | Hot summer, cool winter | Both |
| 13 | Central Valley (Fresno, Bakersfield) | Very hot summer, cool winter | Cooling-dominant |
| 14 | High desert (Palmdale, Lancaster, Mojave) | Hot summer, cold winter | Both, with cold snaps |
| 15 | Low desert (Palm Springs, El Centro) | Extremely hot summer | Cooling-dominant |
| 16 | Mountain (Tahoe, Truckee, Mammoth) | Cold winter, mild summer | Heating-dominant |

### 9.2 Hot-Dry Climates (CZ 9–13, 15)

**Strategies**:
- **Exterior shading** is far more cost-effective than glass upgrades. Deep overhangs (2:1 H:D for south), exterior shades, awnings for E/W.
- **High-SHGC restriction on east/west glass**: SHGC ≤ 0.22 on east/west, ≤ 0.30 on south (per Title 24 prescriptive in many zones). Triple-glazed isn't required; the SHGC is.
- **Cool roofs**: white or high-albedo membrane, light-colored tile, or aluminum-pigmented coatings. Roof aged solar reflectance ≥ 0.55 is the typical target.
- **Radiant barriers**: foil-faced sheathing or under-sheathing barrier in vented attic; reduces summer ceiling heat gain by 5–10%.
- **Unvented conditioned attic** (preferred for HVAC inside conditioned space): closed-cell foam or netted blown insulation at the roof deck; bring ducts inside the envelope.
- **Night flush ventilation**: low summer humidity allows large temperature swings; open windows at night to pre-cool thermal mass (works only in non-tight, non-AC homes — limited Passive House applicability).
- **Heat pump sizing**: cooling-dominant; pick HSPF2 ≥ 8.5 and SEER2 ≥ 16, variable-speed.

### 9.3 Marine Climates (CZ 1, 3, 6)

**Strategies**:
- Heating dominates; cooling load is small or negligible.
- **South-facing solar gain** is free heat — but glass must be balanced against summer overheat. Use deep overhangs sized for 2 ft above grade summer sun angle.
- **Smaller cooling systems**: often a single mini-split head serves the whole house. No need for full AC.
- **Watch for moisture in tight envelopes**: marine air is humid year-round. Vapor-open assemblies, ERV with bypass for cool nights, no interior vapor barriers.
- **Cool-roof exemption**: in CZ 1, 3, cool roofs are not required (and sometimes counterproductive for heating dominance).

### 9.4 Mixed-Humid (CZ 11, 12)

**Strategies**:
- Both heating and cooling matter; design has to balance.
- **ERV preferred over HRV**: summer humidity rejection, winter humidity retention.
- **Balanced exterior insulation**: continuous mineral wool or polyiso outside the studs, vapor-open or smart membrane interior.
- **Heat pump with proper sizing**: variable-speed inverter ducted or ducted-mini-split (Mitsubishi PVA/PUMY, Fujitsu Halcyon, Daikin) — the Central Valley shoulder seasons need part-load efficiency more than peak capacity.

### 9.5 High-Fire-Risk Zones (Cal Fire SRA, especially CZ 14, 16, and parts of 9, 11)

CalFire's Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) requirements (Chapter 7A of the CRC) apply to construction in State Responsibility Areas and Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones. Intersects with sustainable building in important ways.

**Required (Chapter 7A)**:
- Class A roofing (the WUI rule — slate, tile, metal, asphalt Class A rated)
- Ember-resistant vents (Brandguard, Vulcan, OHagin) — 1/16" mesh, baffled
- Decking: composite, metal, or ignition-resistant treated wood
- Siding: non-combustible (fiber cement, stucco, metal) or ignition-resistant
- Windows: tempered exterior glass (one pane) or dual-pane with one tempered
- Eaves: enclosed (soffit) or ignition-resistant
- Skylights: tempered glass, no plastic dome

**Intersection with high-performance**:
- **Fiber cement siding** over mineral wool continuous insulation: ideal — both non-combustible. Avoid foam in WUI zones unless protected behind non-combustible cladding.
- **Tape and membrane choice**: in WUI, some peel-and-stick membranes are ASTM E84 Class A rated; many are not. Check.
- **Closed-cell spray foam under roof deck**: still allowed in WUI; the roof deck itself must meet Class A and the assembly is rated as a system.
- **Ventilation intake**: HRV/ERV intake must be locatable to avoid ember intrusion — fine-mesh screens, intake hood with closable damper for active fire events (some HRV brands now offer "fire mode" or smoke-sensing dampers).
- **Defensible space (Zones 0–2, per CalFire/PRC 4291)**: 100 ft total — Zone 0 (0–5 ft, ember-resistant non-combustible), Zone 1 (5–30 ft, lean-clean-green), Zone 2 (30–100 ft, fuel reduction). Zone 0 went into effect January 2025 — no mulch, no combustible plants, no firewood within 5 ft of structure.
- **Safety vs. tightness**: a tighter, well-filtered (MERV-13 or HEPA) home is a *better* shelter-in-place during wildfire smoke events than a leaky house. Passive House and high-performance homes are *fire-resilient* assets, not liabilities.

## 10. Sequencing and Quality Control on a High-Performance Build

A condensed jobsite playbook. The differences from conventional construction are mostly *order* and *verification*, not new tasks.

### 10.1 Pre-construction

- Energy model (Passive House: WUFI Passive or PHPP; LEED/ZERH: REM/Rate or Ekotrope)
- Whole-wall, roof, foundation R-value calculations including framing factor
- Air barrier plan: a marker drawing on the section showing the continuous air barrier
- Window schedule with U-factor, SHGC, frame material, installation type
- Mechanical design with Manual J load calc (not just rule-of-thumb sizing)
- HRV/ERV specification with duct layout and supply/exhaust per ASHRAE 62.2
- Pre-construction meeting with subs: framer, sheathing crew, HVAC, electrical, plumbing — walk the air barrier plan

### 10.2 Foundation / Slab

- Sub-slab insulation (2–4" EPS) before vapor barrier
- Sub-slab vapor barrier (15 mil cross-laminated, e.g., Stego Wrap) lapped 12", taped, sealed at penetrations
- Slab-edge insulation, capillary break at footing-to-slab
- Anchor bolts pre-installed (no drilling and breaking the air barrier later)

### 10.3 Framing

- Advanced framing layout pre-marked on plates
- Single top plates where allowed; insulated headers
- Rim joist sealed (foam or membrane) before backfill
- Wall-to-roof and wall-to-floor blocking installed for air barrier continuity

### 10.4 Sheathing and WRB

- ZIP sheathing or plywood + fluid-applied: choose one strategy and execute end-to-end
- All seams taped or liquid-flashed
- Rough openings flashed *before* window install (sill pan, jamb wraps, head flashing pre-positioned)
- First blower door test now — pre-drywall, pre-mechanical (target ≤ 1.0 ACH50)

### 10.5 Windows and Doors

- Set in plane of insulation (or with bucks)
- Backer rod + sealant interior; expanding low-expansion foam in cavity; sealant exterior over flashing tape
- Flashing tape laps over head, under jambs, over sill — never reverse

### 10.6 Continuous Exterior Insulation

- Mineral wool or rigid foam over WRB (or under WRB depending on system)
- Furring strips (rainscreen) at 16–24" for cladding attachment; long screws engaging structural framing through foam
- Special detailing at window/door bucks, parapets, deck attachments

### 10.7 Mechanical Rough-in

- Ducts and HRV/ERV inside the conditioned envelope (in soffits, dropped ceilings, conditioned attic) — not in vented attic or crawlspace
- All penetrations through the air barrier sealed (electrical boxes with airtight boots, plumbing penetrations with foam + tape, HVAC chases sealed at top and bottom)
- Second blower door test — pre-drywall, post-mechanical

### 10.8 Insulation

- Quality-installed (RESNET Grade I): every void filled, every cavity to nominal R, batts cut around wires and plumbing, not stuffed
- Inspector or Green Rater verification before drywall
- Dense-pack cellulose monitored for density (3.5 lb/ft³ minimum)

### 10.9 Drywall, Finish, Commissioning

- Low-VOC drywall mud (avoid the cheap "lightweight" that has formaldehyde-bearing binders)
- Low-VOC paint applied with HVAC running and ventilation high
- Final blower door test (certification)
- Final duct leakage test (Title 24)
- HRV/ERV commissioning: measured supply and exhaust airflows at every register
- Manual J vs. measured cooling/heating performance verification
- Owner manual: how to operate the HRV, change filters, maintenance schedule, what NOT to do (don't add a wood stove; don't install a 1,200 CFM range hood)

## 12. Bottom-Line GC Guidance

If you remember nothing else from this module:

1. **Build the envelope first. Everything else is downstream.** Air-sealing and continuous insulation are not upgrades; they are the foundation of a high-performance home.
2. **Test, don't trust.** Blower door at rough-in. Duct leakage. Filter pressure drop. Refrigerant charge. Measured airflows. Real numbers, real verification.
3. **Right-size the mechanicals.** A house with R-30 walls and 1.0 ACH50 needs a fraction of the HVAC capacity of a code house. Don't let the HVAC sub install the same 4-ton system they use everywhere.
4. **Ventilate intentionally.** Tight houses require balanced ventilation. Period. HRV or ERV is not optional.
5. **All-electric is the trajectory.** Design for it. The houses you build in 2026 will operate for 80+ years. Gas pipes are obsolete infrastructure.
6. **Detail the transitions.** Wall-to-roof, wall-to-foundation, window rough opening, every penetration. 90% of high-performance failures are at transitions, not in the field of an assembly.
7. **Verify materials, don't trust marketing.** "Zero-VOC" base + high-VOC colorants is not zero-VOC paint. Check the SDS, not the brochure.
8. **Climate-specific judgment matters.** A Sacramento Passive House and a Mendocino coastal Passive House are different buildings — vapor strategy, glazing, mechanicals, all differ.
9. **Document for certification — but build for the client, not the plaque.** The right answer for most clients is "build to LEED Gold or Phius CORE and certify only if the client values it."
10. **High performance is a system, not a product.** No single window, insulation, or heat pump makes a house high-performance. The discipline of detailing, sequencing, and verification makes it.
