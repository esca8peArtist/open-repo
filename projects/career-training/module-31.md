# Prevailing Wage and Federal Labor Compliance

> **Purpose.** Winning a public works contract without understanding prevailing wage is one of the most expensive mistakes a GC can make. Violations result in civil penalties, back-wage assessments, and debarment from future public contracts. This module covers the two main compliance frameworks — federal Davis-Bacon and California Prevailing Wage Act — and the documentation systems required to prove compliance when audited.

> **Scope.** This module focuses on compliance procedures for general contractors and their subcontractors. It does not cover union negotiations or collective bargaining agreements, which are a separate body of law. For project types not covered here (federal tribal lands, DOE projects, specific grant-funded programs), consult a labor compliance specialist.

## 0. Mental Model — Why Prevailing Wage Exists and What It Actually Requires

Prevailing wage laws emerged from the observation that federal and state construction contracts, funded by taxpayer money, were being awarded to contractors who undercut labor costs by paying below market wages — undercutting local union and non-union contractors who paid reasonable rates, and suppressing wages in entire regions.

The solution: require contractors on government-funded projects to pay workers the **prevailing wage** — the wage and fringe benefit rates that prevail in the local area for each trade classification.

What prevailing wage does NOT require:
- Hiring union workers (it requires union-equivalent wages; the workers need not be union members).
- Following union work rules or jurisdictional agreements.
- Using specific subcontractors or vendors.

What it DOES require:
- Paying each worker in each trade classification at least the prevailing wage rate for their classification in the county where the work is performed.
- Paying prevailing fringe benefits (or equivalent in cash if no fringe plan is in place).
- Maintaining and submitting certified payroll records weekly.
- Posting the wage determination on site.
- Flowing the requirements down to every sub and sub-sub on the project.

## 1. Davis-Bacon Act — Federal Projects

### 1.1 Scope and Coverage

The **Davis-Bacon Act** (40 U.S.C. §§3141–3148) applies to:
- All federal construction contracts over **$2,000** (essentially all construction).
- Federally assisted construction through grants, loans, and contracts where the Davis-Bacon Act is specifically required by the program's authorizing legislation.

Federally assisted programs with Davis-Bacon requirements include:
- Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) — highway and bridge projects
- HUD-funded housing and community development (CDBG)
- Federal Transit Administration (FTA) — transit construction
- Department of Energy projects
- FEMA disaster recovery construction
- Clean Water Act State Revolving Fund projects in many cases

**Not covered by federal Davis-Bacon** (but California Prevailing Wage may apply):
- State-funded projects (schools, water agencies) without federal money — covered by California law only
- Private construction not using federal funds

The **Davis-Bacon Related Acts** (DBRA) are approximately 70 federal statutes that extend Davis-Bacon requirements to federally assisted programs. When you bid a federal grant project, read the contract specifications carefully — the specific prevailing wage statute will be identified.

### 1.2 Wage Determinations — How to Find and Apply Them

The Department of Labor (DOL) publishes wage determinations by county and construction type. There are two types:

**General Wage Determinations:** Published for common construction activities; updated annually. Available at sam.gov (formerly beta.SAM.gov) and at dol.gov/agencies/whd/davis-bacon.

**Project Wage Determinations:** Issued specifically for a project when general wage determinations don't cover the work type or when the contracting agency requests a specific determination.

**Construction types for Davis-Bacon purposes:**
| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Building** | Schools, hospitals, offices, housing |
| **Heavy** | Dams, sewage treatment plants, major water projects |
| **Highway** | Roads, highways, bridges, airport runways |
| **Residential** | Single-family homes, apartment buildings under 4 stories |

The wage determination will be **incorporated into the contract specifications**. The contracting agency is required to include it. If you don't see it, request it before bidding.

**Reading a wage determination:**
- Lists trade classifications (e.g., Carpenter, Electrician, Plumber, Laborer) with:
  - Basic hourly rate
  - Fringe benefits (health, pension, vacation, training — can be paid as cash supplements if no formal plan)
  - Total (basic + fringe) = **total package**

The contractor must pay at least the listed rate for each worker's **actual trade classification**. Misclassifying a carpenter as a laborer (lower rate) to pay less is the most common Davis-Bacon violation — and the most heavily audited.

### 1.3 Key Davis-Bacon Requirements

**Posting:** Post the wage determination and DOL Poster WHD-1321 at a site location accessible to all workers.

**Pay frequency:** Workers must be paid at least **weekly** on Davis-Bacon projects.

**Certified payrolls:** Submit weekly (see Section 3.3).

**Fringe benefits:** The contractor may pay fringes in cash, through a bona fide benefit plan, or a combination. If paying fringes as cash, those cash amounts are included in the hourly rate calculation.

**Apprentice rates:** Apprentices registered in a DOL-approved apprenticeship program may be paid apprentice rates. Workers not enrolled in such a program must be paid the journeyman rate — even if they are trainees.

**No kickbacks:** Contractors cannot require workers to kick back any portion of wages. The Copeland Act (a companion statute) makes wage kickbacks a federal crime.

## 2. California Prevailing Wage Act — State and Local Projects

### 2.1 Scope and Coverage

California Labor Code §1720 et seq. (the Prevailing Wage Act) applies to:
- All public works contracts awarded by the **State of California**.
- All public works contracts awarded by any **California political subdivision** (counties, cities, school districts, water districts, transit agencies, etc.).
- Contracts of any dollar amount — there is **no minimum contract threshold** in California for state/local prevailing wage, unlike the $2,000 federal threshold.

"Public works" under California law is broadly defined (Labor Code §1720):
- Construction, alteration, demolition, installation, or repair work done under public works contract.
- Maintenance work is specifically excluded — routine maintenance is not prevailing wage.
- The work must be paid for in whole or in part out of public funds.

**Projects that often surprise contractors:**
- School construction or modernization — always covered.
- City-funded streetscaping and parks — covered.
- Redevelopment and housing projects using public subsidies — often covered; check with the awarding agency.
- LAUSD, SFUSD, or other school district contracts — covered.
- Work on publicly owned buildings performed under a private contract — check whether public funds are flowing.

**Projects typically NOT covered:**
- Privately funded affordable housing (unless also receiving a public subsidy).
- Work performed under a grant that is not routed through a public agency as contracting party.
- Agricultural work.
- Work performed by owner-operators on their own property with no public funding.

### 2.2 California Wage Determinations

The **California Department of Industrial Relations (DIR)** publishes prevailing wage determinations by county and craft. Available at dir.ca.gov/oprl/dprewagedetermination.htm.

**Key differences from Davis-Bacon:**
- California rates are generally **higher** than Davis-Bacon rates because California labor costs are higher.
- California publishes separate rates for **overtime and holiday** work — these must be tracked.
- California wage determinations include a **training contribution** component (to support state-approved apprenticeship programs).
- California defines specific **craft classifications** that may differ from federal classifications.

**Apprentice rates in California:**
Workers must be enrolled in a **California-approved apprenticeship program** to be paid apprentice rates. Labor Code §1777.5 requires contractors to employ apprentices on public works projects at a ratio of one apprentice per five journeymen per trade. Contact the applicable Joint Apprenticeship Training Committee (JATC) or DIR-approved apprenticeship program to enroll apprentices.

**Penalty for failing to use apprentices:** Civil penalty plus restitution of wages owed.

### 2.3 California-Specific Requirements Beyond Davis-Bacon

**DIR Registration (Labor Code §1725.5):**
All contractors and subcontractors on California public works must be **registered with the DIR** as a public works contractor. Unregistered contractors cannot bid or work on public works. Registration:
- Apply at dir.ca.gov
- $400 registration fee (as of 2025)
- Renew annually
- All subs must also be registered — GC is responsible for verifying subs' registration

**Electronic Certified Payroll (eCPR):**
California requires **electronic submission** of certified payroll reports through the DIR's online portal (see Section 3.3). Paper submission is not accepted on most California public works projects after 2016.

**Apprenticeship requirements (Labor Code §1777.5):**
GC must:
1. Contact the applicable JATC or apprenticeship program at least 72 hours before commencing work to request apprentices.
2. Employ apprentices at the required ratio.
3. Document the request and the response (even if no apprentices are available).

**Penalty for non-compliance with §1777.5:** $100 per day per journeyman who should have been paired with an apprentice.

## 3. Wage Determinations and Certified Payroll Documentation

### 3.1 Classifying Workers Correctly

Correct worker classification is the core prevailing wage compliance obligation and the source of most violations. Every worker on a covered project must be assigned a **trade classification** that reflects the actual work they perform.

| Classification | Typical Work | Common Misclassification Trap |
|---|---|---|
| **Carpenter** | Framing, forming, rough carpentry, finish carpentry | Misclassifying carpenter as laborer to pay lower rate |
| **Cement Mason** | Finish concrete work, flatwork, curb and gutter | Misclassifying as laborer |
| **Electrician (Inside)** | All electrical work inside buildings | Using IBEW apprentice rates for non-enrolled workers |
| **Operating Engineer** | Operating equipment — cranes, excavators, compactors | Operating engineer rate significantly higher than laborer |
| **Laborer** | General labor, cleanup, traffic control (if properly classified), demolition | Over-using the laborer classification for skilled work |
| **Plumber/Pipefitter** | All plumbing and mechanical piping work | |
| **Teamster** | Truck driving to/from and on site | Misclassifying a truck driver as a laborer |
| **Iron Worker** | Structural steel, rebar | |
| **Painter** | All painting | |

**If a worker performs work in two classifications on the same day:** Pay the higher rate for all hours worked that day, OR track hours by classification and pay each at the applicable rate (the latter requires meticulous time records).

### 3.2 Fringe Benefits

Prevailing wage rates include a **fringe benefit package** in addition to the base hourly rate. Fringe benefits typically include:
- Health and welfare
- Pension/retirement contributions
- Vacation pay
- Training contributions

**Options for satisfying fringe benefit obligations:**
1. **Bona fide benefit plan:** Contribute to a union trust fund, a company health plan, or another qualifying benefit plan. Contributions count dollar-for-dollar against the fringe requirement.
2. **Cash in lieu of benefits:** If no qualifying plan exists, pay fringes as additional cash. This is simpler administratively but more expensive because fringe-as-cash is subject to payroll taxes; fringe-as-benefit plan typically is not.
3. **Combination:** Contribute what you can to a qualifying plan; pay the remainder as cash.

Keep records of all benefit plan contributions — they must be documented in certified payrolls.

### 3.3 Certified Payroll Reports

**Federal (Davis-Bacon):** Submit **WH-347** (Payroll Form and Statement of Compliance) to the contracting agency weekly, within 7 days of payroll. Available at dol.gov.

**California:** Submit weekly **eCPR** (electronic Certified Payroll Report) via the DIR's online portal at dir.ca.gov/pwc/ecprsystem.htm.

**What a certified payroll must show, per worker:**

| Field | Required Content |
|---|---|
| Name | Legal name |
| Address | Home address |
| Work classification | Trade classification per the wage determination |
| Hours worked each day | Daily breakdown by trade classification |
| Total hours worked | This week |
| Pay rate | Basic hourly rate |
| Fringe benefit contributions | Per hour or per week |
| Gross pay | Before deductions |
| Deductions | Federal/state taxes, Social Security, Medicare, voluntary deductions |
| Net pay | Take-home |
| Employer signature | Owner or authorized officer certifying under penalty of perjury |

**The Statement of Compliance** (included on WH-347 or the California eCPR equivalent) is a sworn statement that the certified payroll is correct and complete, that workers have been paid the required wages, and that no kickbacks have been made. Signing a false statement of compliance is perjury.

**Common certified payroll errors:**
- Wrong trade classification
- Omitting fringe benefit contributions
- Omitting workers who are on site for short periods (delivery drivers, crane operators)
- Failing to report overtime hours at the prevailing overtime rate
- Submitting late (must be within 7 days of the close of the payroll period)

### 3.4 Posting Requirements

**Post on site, in a conspicuous location:**
- Federal Davis-Bacon: DOL Poster WHD-1321, plus the applicable wage determination.
- California: California wage determination for the project, DIR prevailing wage poster.
- Both: "EEOC Notice of Right to be Free from Discrimination" and Cal/OSHA Job Safety and Health poster.

Inspectors will check for proper posting. Missing posters are an immediate violation — easy to avoid.

## 4. Penalties and Audit Procedures

### 4.1 Federal (Davis-Bacon) Penalties

**Back wages:** Any worker paid less than the prevailing wage rate is owed the difference for all hours worked at the insufficient rate. The contractor (and potentially the subcontractor) must pay this back immediately on finding a violation.

**Civil money penalties (Davis-Bacon Act, as implemented by DOL WHD):**
- **Debarment:** Willful or repeated violations can result in being placed on the **Ineligible (Debarred) Parties List** for 3 years — barring the firm from all federal contracts and federally assisted projects. This is the most severe sanction and is career-ending.
- **Contract termination:** The contracting agency can terminate the contract.
- **Withholding from contract payments:** The contracting officer may withhold payment to cover back-wage liability.

**Criminal penalties (Copeland Act violations — kickbacks):** Fine and/or imprisonment.

### 4.2 California Prevailing Wage Penalties (Labor Code §§1775–1777.7)

California's penalties are specific and significant:

| Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Paying below prevailing wage rate | **$200 per day per worker** for each day the violation continues, plus restitution of all wages owed |
| Failure to employ apprentices (§1777.5) | $100 per day per journeyman who should have been paired |
| Failure to submit certified payroll (Labor Code §1776) | $100 per day per worker for each day of failure |
| Willful misclassification of workers | Enhanced civil penalties; DIR may refer for criminal prosecution |
| Debarment | Contractor debarred from all California public works for up to 3 years |

**California DIR audit:** The DIR's Labor Enforcement and Compliance Unit (LECU) conducts audits. Triggers for audit include:
- Worker complaints (workers can and do file complaints anonymously).
- Random selection.
- Pattern of late or incomplete certified payroll submissions.
- Large dollar contracts.
- Industry-wide enforcement campaigns (DIR targets high-violation industries — typically framing, drywall, and concrete on public works).

**Audit procedure:**
1. DIR sends a Notice of Investigation.
2. Request for records (payrolls, worker time records, subcontracts).
3. Worker interviews (DIR may interview workers independently from contractor).
4. Preliminary findings issued; contractor has opportunity to respond.
5. Final determination; penalties assessed; appeal rights available (Office of Administrative Hearings).

### 4.3 Audit Survival — What Auditors Look For

| Audit Focus Area | What They Check | How to Be Ready |
|---|---|---|
| Trade classifications | Do classifications on payroll match the actual work performed? | Keep contemporaneous work records by classification; photographs of work performed |
| Fringe benefit contributions | Are benefit plan contributions documented and accurate? | Contribution statements from benefit plan trustees |
| Apprentice ratios | Are apprentices employed at required ratios? | Request letters to JATC; apprentice registration documents |
| Sub payroll compliance | Have all subs submitted certified payrolls? | Subcontract requires CPR; GC maintains copies of all sub CPRs |
| Overtime | Is OT paid at the correct prevailing overtime rate? | Payroll records showing OT hours and rates |
| On-call and travel time | Are workers paid for travel to remote work sites? | Review DOL guidance on portal-to-portal rules |

## 5. Project Eligibility and Reporting Requirements

### 5.1 Determining Whether a Project Requires Prevailing Wage

**Ask before bidding.** Do not rely on the owner telling you — some owners don't know their own requirements. Review:

1. **Funding source:** Is there any federal or state money? Grant funding? Bond proceeds from a school or transit authority?
2. **Project specification Section 00800 or similar** — prevailing wage requirements, if any, will be stated here.
3. **California public agency:** All public agencies in California must pay prevailing wage on covered work — but what is "covered" has nuances. If in doubt, contact the DIR at dir.ca.gov.
4. **CUCP (California Unified Certification Program):** For transit projects with FTA funds, check if Davis-Bacon and California prevailing wage both apply (they frequently do — pay the higher of the two rates).

**Federal funds + California public agency = both Davis-Bacon and California law apply.** Pay whichever rate is higher for each classification — typically California rates are higher.

### 5.2 Prevailing Wage in the Bid Process

Prevailing wage requirements significantly affect bid preparation:

**Labor cost calculation:**
```
Total labor cost per worker = 
  (Hours × Basic wage rate) 
  + (Hours × Fringe benefit rate)  
  + Employer-side payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA, SUTA, SDI)
  + Workers compensation premium (based on total payroll including fringes paid as cash)
```

Fringe benefits paid through a qualifying plan are NOT subject to FICA and FUTA — this is a significant cost advantage of using a union benefit trust fund or a qualifying fringe plan over paying fringes as cash.

**Bid math example (Los Angeles County, 2025 — approximate rates):**
- Journeyman Carpenter base rate: ~$52/hr
- Fringe benefits: ~$34/hr
- Total package: ~$86/hr
- Employer taxes (FICA, FUTA, SUTA): ~12% of base = ~$6.24/hr
- Workers comp (Carpentry, CA rate): ~$12–18/hr equivalent
- **Fully burdened labor cost: ~$104–$110/hr per journeyman carpenter**

Compare this to an unverified prevailing wage bid from a contractor using workers at $35/hr cash — the underbidder may not understand their legal obligations, or they may intend to violate them. If you lose a public works bid to a number that seems impossibly low, consider filing a labor compliance complaint with DIR.

### 5.3 Prevailing Wage on Projects with Public Subsidies

A growing gray area: **affordable housing projects** and **economic development projects** that receive public tax credits, grants, or other subsidies but are not direct public works.

California Labor Code §1720(b) specifically includes: "The construction, alteration, demolition, installation, or repair work done under contract and paid for in whole or in part out of public funds."

"Paid for... out of public funds" has been interpreted broadly by the DIR to include:
- Tax increment financing
- Community Development Block Grants
- Some forms of low-income housing tax credits (LIHTC) when paired with direct public funding
- Public land contributed at below-market value

If your project involves any of these, seek a prevailing wage coverage determination from DIR before you bid. Do not assume private funding structures exempt you.

## 6. Record-Keeping and Compliance Best Practices

### 6.1 Payroll Records Retention

**Federal (Davis-Bacon):** Retain payroll records for **3 years** after the project completes. (29 CFR §5.5)

**California:** Retain payroll records for **3 years** after completion. (Labor Code §1776)

**Practical standard:** Retain for 5 years to cover any audit within the statute of limitations.

**What to retain:**
- All certified payroll reports (WH-347 / eCPR) for GC and all subs
- Worker time records (time cards, sign-in sheets) by day and classification
- Payroll registers
- Fringe benefit contribution statements
- Apprentice registration documents and JATC request letters
- Worker classification justification (for any non-standard classifications)

### 6.2 Pre-Project Compliance Setup

**Do this before work begins:**

1. **Obtain the correct wage determination** — verify you have the current version for the project county and construction type.
2. **Register with DIR** — GC and all subs; confirm DIR registration numbers are valid.
3. **Request apprentices** — contact applicable JATCs in writing (email is fine; keep the confirmation) at least 72 hours before work begins.
4. **Brief all subs** — provide each sub with the applicable wage determination; require a written acknowledgment that the sub understands their prevailing wage obligations.
5. **Set up payroll system** — configure your payroll software (Sage, QuickBooks, Buildertrend Payroll, LCP Tracker, or similar) to track hours by trade classification and compute prevailing wage rates automatically.
6. **Appoint a Labor Compliance Officer** — even on small projects, someone must own CPR submission and audit response. On larger public works projects, some awarding agencies require a formal LCO designation.

### 6.3 Subcontractor Compliance — Your Responsibility

**As the prime contractor, you are responsible for your subcontractors' prevailing wage compliance.** If a sub violates prevailing wage, the DIR can assess penalties against the prime contractor, not just the sub.

**Protect yourself:**

1. **Include prevailing wage obligations in every subcontract** on covered projects (see Module 30, Section 2.3 for flow-down requirements).
2. **Require subs to submit certified payrolls to you weekly** before you forward them to the contracting agency.
3. **Review sub certified payrolls** for obvious errors — wrong classification, wrong rate, missing workers.
4. **Withhold sub payments** if the sub has not submitted certified payrolls — make this a subcontract term.
5. **Include a prevailing wage indemnification clause** in the subcontract: sub indemnifies GC for any prevailing wage violation attributable to the sub's work.

### 6.4 LCP Tracker and Compliance Software

Most large California public works projects now use **LCP Tracker** — an electronic labor compliance platform that automates certified payroll submission, tracks apprentice ratios, and flags potential violations. Some school districts and transit agencies require its use.

Other software options: **Elation Systems**, **eMars**, **Procore Workforce Management** (with prevailing wage module).

Budget for compliance software on public works projects over $2M — the cost ($3,000–$15,000 per project) is trivial compared to violation exposure.

## 7. Compliance Checklist

### Pre-Bid Checklist

- [ ] Confirm whether project requires Davis-Bacon, California Prevailing Wage, or both
- [ ] Obtain correct wage determinations from sam.gov (federal) and dir.ca.gov (California)
- [ ] Calculate fully burdened labor costs using prevailing rates
- [ ] Verify DIR registration current for GC; plan to verify for all subs before mobilization
- [ ] Review apprentice ratio requirements for all trades on the project

### Pre-Mobilization Checklist

- [ ] DIR registration verified for GC and all subs (check dir.ca.gov public works contractor search)
- [ ] Wage determination posted on-site in conspicuous location
- [ ] Required labor posters posted (DOL WHD-1321 and/or California prevailing wage poster)
- [ ] JATC apprentice requests sent in writing; confirmation received
- [ ] Subcontracts include prevailing wage flow-down clause
- [ ] Payroll software configured for prevailing wage rate tables and certified payroll output
- [ ] Labor compliance officer designated; certified payroll submission account created at DIR eCPR portal

### Weekly Compliance Checklist

- [ ] Time cards collected from all workers; hours recorded by trade classification
- [ ] Certified payroll completed and submitted to contracting agency within 7 days of payroll week close
- [ ] Sub certified payrolls received; reviewed for accuracy; forwarded to contracting agency
- [ ] Fringe benefit contributions verified (or cash fringe payments confirmed on payroll)
- [ ] Apprentice ratio verified for each trade this week

### Post-Project

- [ ] All payroll records archived for 5 years (digital backup in at least two locations)
- [ ] Final certified payroll submitted within 7 days of last payroll period
- [ ] DIR final audit waiver requested (optional; available after project closes out; prevents future audit on that project)
