# Multi-Family and Light Commercial Construction Fundamentals

> **Purpose.** The curriculum's strongest tracks are single-family residential (Modules 07–28) and heavy industrial (Modules 01–04). The middle market—multi-family housing (Type III and V wood-frame; Type I concrete), tenant improvement, retail build-out, small office, and light commercial—is where most California GCs actually work most of the time. This market has different occupancy classifications, fire-separation requirements, accessibility obligations, MEP coordination demands, and contract structures that differ fundamentally from residential construction. A residential GC who wins their first 20-unit apartment project faces an unfamiliar code environment and formal contract structures the curriculum has not addressed.

> **Distinction from Module 07.** Module 07 teaches residential GC scope and management (single-family homes). This module teaches the code, structural, and contracting framework that governs multi-family and light commercial projects—a different regulatory and structural environment.

## 0. Mental Model — Multi-Family and Commercial Are Different Code Environments

Most residential GCs have built single-family homes under California's Residential Code. They know about stud framing, roof trusses, drywall, and single inspection points. When they bid their first multi-family or light commercial project, they encounter:

- **Occupancy classifications** that drive fire-resistance requirements and material choices
- **Fire-separation assemblies** that require design review and code compliance verification
- **Accessibility requirements** that extend beyond single-family ADA basics
- **Formal contract structures** (AIA A201) that define the design professional's authority and the GC's obligations
- **MEP coordination complexity** that requires systematic management, not informal coordination

The GC who treats a 20-unit wood-frame apartment like a large single-family home will discover code violations at final inspection, schedule delays from rework, and contract disputes with the designer over who was responsible for coordination. The GC who understands the distinct requirements of multi-family/commercial work avoids these problems.

This module teaches the fundamentals that separate residential GCs from commercial GCs.

## 1. Occupancy Classifications and Construction Types

### 1.1 The IBC Occupancy Groups

The International Building Code (IBC) organizes occupancies into groups based on use. California adopts IBC with amendments in the California Building Code (CBC). The groups most relevant to this module:

**R-2: Multi-Family Residential**
- Two or more dwelling units (apartments, condos, townhouses).
- Includes hotels with sleeping units and cooking facilities.
- Code requirements differ significantly from single-family (R-3).

**A: Assembly**
- Buildings for gathering of 50+ people (restaurants, theaters, conference rooms, churches).
- Requires emergency egress planning, occupant load calculations, and accessible seating.

**B: Business and Professional Services**
- Offices, doctor's offices, outpatient clinics, banks.
- Generally lower risk than assembly; requires accessible routes and emergency egress.

**M: Mercantile**
- Retail stores, shopping malls, showrooms.
- Requires accessible shopping aisles, checkout counters, dressing rooms.

**S-1 and S-2: Storage**
- Warehouses, storage facilities.
- S-1: high-hazard materials (flammable, explosive).
- S-2: general storage.

### 1.2 Construction Types and Fire-Resistance Ratings

The IBC defines construction types (I, II, III, IV, V) based on the required fire-resistance ratings of structural elements. The fire-resistance rating is the time (in hours) a building element resists fire exposure: 1-hour, 2-hour, 3-hour, 4-hour ratings are common.

**Type I-A and I-B: Non-Combustible**
- All structural elements are non-combustible (steel, concrete).
- Type I-A: 3–4 hour fire-resistance throughout.
- Type I-B: 2 hour fire-resistance throughout.
- Used for: tall buildings, high-rise residential, large commercial projects.

**Type II-A, II-B, II-C: Partially Protected Non-Combustible**
- Structural frame is non-combustible; some non-structural elements may be combustible.
- Type II-A: 2-hour fire-resistance on structural elements.
- Type II-B: 1-hour fire-resistance on structural elements.
- Type II-C: 0-hour fire-resistance (unprotected non-combustible).
- Used for: mid-rise office, smaller commercial projects.

**Type III-A and III-B: Partially Protected Combustible**
- Exterior walls are non-combustible (concrete, masonry); structural frame may be wood or protected steel.
- Type III-A: 2-hour fire-resistance on structural frame; 2-hour exterior walls.
- Type III-B: 1-hour fire-resistance; 1-hour exterior walls.
- Used for: commercial buildings with masonry facades, some multi-family.

**Type IV: Heavy Timber**
- Structural frame is solid or laminated wood (minimum 8-inch dimension), with non-combustible exterior walls.
- Used for: mill buildings, warehouses, sometimes converted to commercial/residential lofts.

**Type V-A and V-B: Wood-Frame**
- Structural frame, floors, roof, and most elements are wood or wood-based.
- Type V-A: 1-hour fire-resistance on structural elements.
- Type V-B: 0-hour fire-resistance (unprotected wood).
- Used for: single-family residential, small multi-family (up to 3 stories), light commercial.

### 1.3 Multi-Family Construction Types in California

The dominant multi-family construction type in California is **Type V-A** (wood-frame with 1-hour fire-resistance). The 1-hour fire-resistance requirement is achieved through:
- Type X drywall (fire-rated gypsum board with added fibers) on the underside of floor/ceiling assemblies.
- Fire-blocking and draft-stopping in walls and attics.
- Fire-rated door assemblies separating units (party walls).

For taller buildings (4+ stories) or high-occupancy projects, **Type III-A** (non-combustible exterior walls with fire-rated interior frame) is used. The most common version: concrete or masonry exterior walls with a Type V wood-frame interior.

**Podium construction** (Type I concrete base with Type V wood above) is California's most popular multi-family form:
- Ground floor + one parking level: concrete (Type I-B or II-A).
- Residential floors above: wood-frame (Type V-A).
- This maximizes parking efficiency and keeps residential costs low while meeting fire code for mixed-occupancy.

### 1.4 Sprinkler Requirements

Sprinkler systems are mandatory or discretionary depending on occupancy, height, and construction type:

- **Multi-family R-2, Type V-A, 3+ stories:** Automatic sprinkler system required per CBC Section 903.2.8. NFPA 13, 13R, or 13D applies.
  - NFPA 13: Full sprinkler coverage (all areas).
  - NFPA 13R: Residential sprinkler system (simplified design; wet pipes in common areas, dry pipes in individual units).
  - NFPA 13D: Single-family or duplex (minimal design).

- **Retail/mercantile with <5,000 sq ft:** Sprinklers usually discretionary; depends on occupant load and construction type.

- **Type I and II buildings:** Sprinklers may be required based on size and occupancy, regardless of construction type.

If sprinklers are discretionary, the designer and owner make the decision. However, sprinkler installation affects the timeline and cost; the GC must budget for it in the estimate.

### 1.5 High-Rise Triggers

Any building more than 55 feet above grade is considered "high-rise" under CBC. High-rise requirements include:
- Automatic sprinkler systems (mandatory).
- Two independent emergency stairwells (both enclosed and pressurized).
- Emergency voice/alarm communication system.
- Advanced fire detection and alarm systems.
- Backup emergency power systems.

Most multi-family projects in California are 55 feet or less (5–6 stories of wood-frame). Understanding the 55-foot threshold is important because it triggers significant cost and complexity if exceeded.

## 2. Fire Separation Requirements and Assemblies

### 2.1 Party Walls and Fire-Rated Assemblies

A **party wall** is the assembly separating two adjacent units in a multi-family building. Party walls must achieve a fire-resistance rating that typically depends on occupancy and construction type. For R-2 occupancy (multi-family), Type V-A construction, the party wall must achieve **1-hour fire-resistance rating**.

A 1-hour fire-resistance party wall typically consists of:
- Wood stud frame (2×4 or 2×6).
- Type X drywall on both sides (minimum 5/8" thickness).
- Sound damping insulation (optional but common).
- Acoustic sealant at all penetrations.

**Fire-resistance rating testing:** UL (Underwriters Laboratories) and other testing labs conduct standard fire-resistance tests. A wall assembly receives a rating (1-hour, 2-hour) if it withstands the fire test for that duration. Architectural specifications reference these tests by UL number: "UL 468" might be a 1-hour wood-stud party wall assembly.

**The GC's role:** Ensure the wall is constructed exactly per the tested assembly specification. This means:
- Drywall thickness (5/8", not 1/2").
- Number of layers (2 layers on at least one side, for many 1-hour assemblies).
- Framing type and spacing.
- Acoustic sealant at every penetration (electrical outlet, pipe, duct).

If the wall is constructed differently—using 1/2" drywall or skipping sealant—the 1-hour rating is void, and the wall does not meet code.

### 2.2 Corridor Assemblies and Exit Paths

Corridors serving multiple units must achieve a **1-hour fire-resistance rating** (for Type V-A, R-2 occupancy). This is often called a "1-hour corridor assembly" and is achieved through:
- Drywall on walls (typically 5/8" Type X).
- Fire-rated door assemblies separating the corridor from individual units.
- Fire-stopping at all penetrations (where pipes, ducts, or wiring penetrate the corridor walls).

**Fire-rated doors:** Any door opening into a 1-hour corridor must be a 20-minute or 45-minute fire-rated assembly (depending on code requirements; 20-minute is more common). Fire-rated doors include:
- The door frame (metal, fire-rated).
- The door leaf (solid-core wood or steel).
- Automatic door closer (door must self-close).
- Automatic latch (door must latch on closure).

A standard residential door (hollow-core wood, standard frame) does not meet fire-rating requirements. Installing the wrong door is an inspection failure.

### 2.3 Fire Blocking and Draft Stopping

**Fire blocking** is the placement of fire-resistant materials in concealed spaces (walls, floors, attics) to slow fire spread and prevent drafts that accelerate fire. Fire blocking is required:
- At the line where a wall intersects a floor or roof (vertical concealed space in the wall must be blocked horizontally by a solid material, typically 2×4 wood).
- In concealed spaces above dropped ceilings (must be fire-blocked to prevent fire travel through the plenum).
- Around chimneys and pipes (gap between the pipe and framing must be fire-blocked with non-combustible material).

**Draft stopping** is similar but applies to concealed spaces in concealed spaces (floor cavities in multi-story buildings). If the floor cavity of a multi-family building is not blocked, fire can travel from one unit's floor cavity through to an upper unit's floor cavity—a dangerous condition.

The most common GC error: assuming the framing automatically provides fire blocking. In reality, the framing creates many gaps; fire blocking must be explicitly installed and inspected. A framing inspection checklist should verify fire blocking completion before drywall coverage.

### 2.4 Shaft Walls and MEP Penetration Assemblies

An **MEP shaft** is a vertical opening containing mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems (ducts, pipes, conduit, wiring). Shafts must achieve a **2-hour fire-resistance rating** (for Type V-A, R-2 occupancy), which is more stringent than the 1-hour party wall requirement.

A 2-hour shaft wall typically consists of:
- Steel stud frame (not wood; wood cannot achieve 2-hour rating in a shaft assembly).
- Type X drywall, typically 2 layers on both sides (5/8" per layer).
- Fire-caulking at every pipe, duct, or wire penetration.

**UL-listed shaft assemblies:** Just like party walls, shaft assemblies must be tested and UL-listed. A common 2-hour shaft assembly: UL 1999 (a specific tested assembly). Deviating from the tested assembly voids the rating.

**The mechanical and drywall sub conflict:** The mechanical sub wants to penetrate the shaft with pipes and ducts; the drywall sub wants to seal the shaft completely. The GC must manage this conflict by establishing the shaft dimensions and penetration locations early (in the framing coordination meeting), before framing is closed.

### 2.5 Common GC Errors in Fire Separation

**Error 1: Treating multi-family like single-family.**
Single-family homes (R-3 occupancy) don't require party wall fire-resistance ratings or corridor assemblies. The first time a GC builds a 10-unit apartment using single-family construction methods, they discover fire-blocking requirements, fire-rated doors, and shaft walls were missed. Correcting these issues at final inspection (when drywall is up) is expensive.

**Error 2: Installing the wrong drywall.**
Standard drywall (regular gypsum board) looks identical to Type X fire-rated drywall. The GC's framing crew orders drywall by dimension ("1/2" drywall," "5/8" drywall") without specifying Type X. The drywall supplier delivers standard drywall. The drywall is installed; at inspection, it fails because it's not Type X.

**Error 3: Skipping acoustic sealant at penetrations.**
Acoustic sealant (a caulk-like material) must be applied at every electrical outlet, pipe penetration, and duct opening in a fire-rated wall. The sealant prevents fire from spreading through the gap around the penetration. A framing crew without multi-family experience often skips this, creating a path for fire to spread through the wall.

**Error 4: Accepting a fire-rated door that doesn't close automatically.**
A fire-rated door assembly requires an automatic door closer. A subcontractor who installs the fire-rated frame and door but forgets the closer has installed 90% of the assembly. The missing closer is a code violation and an inspection failure.

## 3. Accessibility and Path-of-Travel Obligations

### 3.1 The Two Frameworks: Federal ADA and California CBC Chapter 11B

**Federal ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act):**
- Enforced by the DOJ, DOT, and HUD.
- Establishes minimum accessibility standards (2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design).
- Applies to all buildings open to the public and to housing constructed with federal funding.

**California CBC Chapter 11B:**
- California's state accessibility standards.
- Often stricter than federal ADA.
- Enforced by local building departments (not federal agencies).
- Applies to all buildings in California (not just federally funded projects).

For any California project, CBC Chapter 11B applies. When CBC Chapter 11B is stricter than ADA, CBC controls. The GC must be familiar with both but must comply with CBC at minimum.

### 3.2 What "Accessible Path of Travel" Means for Tenant Improvement

An **accessible path of travel** is the continuous, accessible route from the public right-of-way to an accessible entrance and through the building to accessible facilities (restrooms, drinking fountains, exits).

For **tenant improvement (TI) projects**, the ADA and CBC include a key rule: if the total cost of the TI work is less than 20% of the building's replacement cost (called the "disproportionate cost test"), the owner does not have to upgrade the entire building's path of travel. However, if the TI cost is 20% or more, the full path of travel must be made accessible.

**Example:**
- Building replacement cost (estimated): $1M.
- 20% threshold: $200,000.
- TI scope: new interior fit-out of one tenant space, estimated cost $150,000.
- Result: The $150,000 is less than 20%; the TI does not trigger path-of-travel upgrades.

But if the TI scope is $250,000, the 20% threshold is exceeded; the path of travel from parking to the accessible entrance must be upgraded to full ADA compliance.

### 3.3 Multi-Family Accessibility Requirements

Multi-family housing under CBC Chapter 11B must provide:

**Accessible units:**
- At least 7% of dwelling units must be accessible and adaptable (the percentage varies based on unit count and occupancy).
- Accessible units are those with no steps to entry, accessible kitchens and bathrooms, accessible doors and hallways.

**Type A and Type B units:**
- **Type A units:** Fully accessible (designed for wheelchair use, with accessible bathroom, kitchen, hallways, etc.). These are rare; typically only 1–2 per building.
- **Type B units:** Adaptable (not fully accessible at construction, but readily adaptable to accessibility by the tenant through installation of grab bars, accessible fixtures, etc.). These are more common.

**Common area accessibility:**
- Accessible parking spaces (minimum 1 per 25 units, with accessible route to the building entrance).
- Accessible main entrance (no steps; ramp if needed).
- Accessible hallways and corridors (36" minimum width).
- Accessible community rooms and facilities.

### 3.4 Accessible Path-of-Travel in Tenant Improvement

When a TI triggers path-of-travel upgrades, the GC is typically responsible for coordination and execution. Typical upgrades include:

- **Parking lot improvements:** Creating or restriping accessible parking spaces (9-foot space + 8-foot access aisle), installing accessible space signage.
- **Approach ramp:** If the accessible entrance is not at grade level, a ramp must be constructed (slopes no steeper than 1:12 ratio; 5-foot level landings every 30 feet of rise).
- **Entrance door:** Replacing the entrance door with one that has a maximum opening force of 5 pounds (for a single action door; higher for double-action doors).
- **Accessible route:** Ensuring the route from parking to accessible entrance is at least 36 inches wide, with maximum cross-slopes of 2% and maximum running slopes of 5%.
- **Signage:** Installing International Symbol of Accessibility signs at accessible parking and accessible entrances.

### 3.5 GC Obligations and Liability

A critical distinction: **The ADA and CBC hold the building owner responsible for accessibility compliance, not the GC.** However, the GC is responsible for constructing what the drawings and specifications require. If the drawings do not show accessible path-of-travel work, but the building department later requires it, the cost is typically an owner cost (change order), not the GC's responsibility.

**But the GC's liability risk arises when:**
1. The GC knows path-of-travel work is required but fails to include it in the estimate or schedule.
2. The GC constructs the TI without flagging to the owner that path-of-travel work was required.
3. The GC's work creates a new accessibility violation (e.g., a new entrance that the TI created must be accessible, but the GC didn't make it accessible).

**The GC's protection:** Coordinate with the designer early to determine whether path-of-travel upgrades are required. If required, ensure they are shown on the contract drawings and included in the TI scope. Document your communication to the owner in writing.

### 3.6 When to Call the DSA (Division of the State Architect)

California's Division of the State Architect (DSA) oversees accessibility compliance for public schools, community colleges, and state-funded projects. DSA has stricter accessibility requirements than CBC and performs its own inspections separate from the building department.

If your TI is a school or state-funded project, DSA approval is required before occupancy. The GC should budget for DSA review, testing, and inspection in addition to the building department review. Accessibility deficiencies that the building department missed are often caught by DSA, resulting in rework and schedule delays.

## 4. Multi-Family Project Sequencing

### 4.1 Zone-Based Sequencing vs. Single-Building Sequencing

Single-family residential construction is straightforward: build one house, move to the next. Multi-family construction (especially mid-rise or high-rise) requires **zone-based sequencing.**

A multi-family project is divided into zones (typically by floor or wing). Construction proceeds zone-by-zone through a standard sequence:
1. Framing and structural work complete in Zone 1.
2. MEP rough-in work starts in Zone 1 while Zone 2 framing begins.
3. Inspections occur by zone (framing inspection, MEP rough-in inspection).
4. Drywall installation proceeds zone-by-zone.
5. Finish work (painting, fixtures, flooring) proceeds zone-by-zone.

This creates a "wave" across the building. At any given time, different zones are at different phases, allowing multiple trades to work simultaneously without interfering with each other.

### 4.2 Critical Inspection Hold Points

Multi-family projects have inspection hold points that differ from single-family construction. Before proceeding to the next phase, an inspection must be passed:

**Shear wall nailing inspection:** Before drywall coverage, the inspector verifies that shear walls are nailed per the architectural/structural drawings. Missing or improper nailing means the wall doesn't provide the intended lateral bracing capacity—a serious code violation. This inspection often reveals that framing contractors did not install nails in the correct pattern (they "eyeballed" it instead of following the drawing).

**Fire blocking inspection:** Before drywall coverage, the inspector verifies that fire blocking is installed at all required locations (horizontal blocking at floor/ceiling lines, blocking around pipes, etc.). Missing fire blocking is a common deficiency and must be corrected before drywall coverage.

**MEP rough-in inspection:** After MEP rough-in but before drywall, the inspector verifies that all ducts, pipes, and conduit are installed correctly and per the drawings. This inspection is critical for catching coordination errors (e.g., a duct that collides with a structural beam) before drywall.

If any of these inspections fail, the project cannot advance to drywall. Failing these inspections is expensive because corrective work must occur with framing already complete (more labor-intensive than correcting during initial installation).

### 4.3 Managing Multiple Trade Crews in the Same Building

Unlike single-family construction (where one trade completes, then the next trade begins), multi-family construction requires simultaneous work by multiple trades. This creates safety and coordination challenges:

**Vertical separation:** Framing work on floors 3–4 should not create safety hazards for finish work on floors 1–2 (falling debris, dust, noise). The GC must implement dust containment (plastic barriers between zones) and establish work windows (framing works 6:00 AM–2:00 PM on upper floors; finish work 2:00 PM–6:00 PM on lower floors).

**Material staging:** Different trades require different material storage. Framing requires wood deliveries; mechanical requires pipe and equipment. The GC must designate material storage areas so trades don't conflict over space.

**Sequencing interdependencies:** Electrical rough-in must start after framing rough is done (so electricians know where to run conduit), but before drywall coverage (so electrical boxes can be mounted on studs). If the sequence slips, multiple trades can become blocked simultaneously.

### 4.4 MEP Shaft Coordination

The MEP shaft is the most critical coordination point in multi-family construction. The shaft is a vertical opening carrying all mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems for multiple units. If the shaft layout is not coordinated before framing closes it, conflicts are discovered too late (when framing is already complete and moving a duct means rebuilding the framing).

**Best practice:** Establish the shaft layout in a dedicated coordination meeting before framing starts:
1. Mechanical sub shows the locations of major ducts and pipes (supply air, return air, chilled water, hot water).
2. Electrical sub shows the locations of panels and major conduit runs.
3. Plumbing sub shows the locations of drain stacks and supply lines.
4. These are overlaid physically or on a drawing.
5. Conflicts are resolved (e.g., "the supply duct and the drain stack collide at the 4th-floor level; we'll move the duct 6 inches to the west").
6. The agreed shaft layout is documented on a revised drawing, stamped "For Construction."

This coordination meeting, done early, prevents rework and schedule delays. Neglecting it is one of the costliest mistakes on multi-family projects.

## 5. AIA A201 Contract Administration

### 5.1 How AIA A201 Differs from Residential Owner-Contractor Agreements

A residential contract (like AIA A106) gives the owner and GC relatively equal standing. Both can make requests; changes are negotiated as change orders.

**AIA A201** (General Conditions of the Contract for Construction, used on design-bid-build commercial projects) gives the **design professional (architect or engineer) significant authority.** The design professional:
- Interprets the contract documents (if the drawings and specs are ambiguous, the architect decides what was intended).
- Reviews all submittals from the GC and subs; approves or rejects them.
- Issues certificates for payment (formal approval that work is substantially complete and payment is due).
- Can require rework if work doesn't comply with the drawings and specs.

### 5.2 Submittals and the Approval Chain

A **submittal** is any request by the GC for approval of a product, process, or method before installation. Common submittals include:
- Product data sheets (confirming the product specified is being used).
- Shop drawings (detailed drawings showing how something will be installed).
- Samples (actual material samples for color/finish approval).
- Certificates (testing certificates confirming products meet code).

**The submittal process under A201:**
1. Sub prepares the submittal and sends to the GC.
2. GC reviews for compliance with contract documents; signs the submittal form.
3. GC sends to the architect.
4. Architect reviews (typically 10–14 days turnaround time per A201).
5. Architect returns one of three responses:
   - "Approved as submitted" — sub can proceed with installation.
   - "Approved as noted" — sub must make the noted changes before installation.
   - "Rejected" — submittal does not comply; resubmit after revision.

**A critical distinction:** An "approved as noted" submittal is not a free pass. The sub must address the notes before installation. If the sub proceeds with installation and ignores the architect's notes, the sub is in breach of the contract.

### 5.3 RFIs (Requests for Information)

An **RFI** is the GC's formal request for clarification when the drawings or specs are ambiguous or conflicting. Example: "The electrical plan shows a 20-amp circuit, but the equipment data sheet specifies 30 amps. Which is correct?"

**RFI process under A201:**
1. GC (or sub) submits the RFI to the architect.
2. Architect has 7–10 days to respond (per A201).
3. Architect responds in writing, resolving the conflict.

**Critical: An RFI response from the architect is a contract interpretation.** If the GC proceeds based on the RFI response and it turns out to be wrong, the GC is not responsible—the architect is. This is why RFIs are important: they create a written record of decisions and allocate risk.

**How to write an RFI that protects the GC:** Include a statement like: "This RFI requests clarification of an ambiguity in the contract documents. Proceeding with installation pending the architect's response. If the architect's response requires rework, we reserve the right to a time extension and cost adjustment per A201 Section 3.7."

### 5.4 Certificates for Payment

Before the GC can invoice the owner for payment, the architect must issue a **Certificate for Payment.** The certificate is the architect's formal attestation that:
- Work is substantially complete (or complete to the percentage claimed in the pay app).
- Work complies with the contract documents.
- Work qualifies for payment.

An architect can reduce a certificate amount if work is non-conforming. Example: the GC claims $50,000 for electrical rough-in, but the electrician installed wire in unapproved locations. The architect might certify only $45,000, withholding $5,000 until the wire is relocated.

**GC response to a disputed certificate:** The GC can submit a detailed response explaining why the work complies (with photos, RFI responses, and architect approvals as supporting evidence). If the GC and architect cannot resolve the dispute, the contract provides an Initial Decision Maker (IDM) process before escalation to arbitration or litigation.

### 5.5 Initial Decision Maker (IDM) Process

If the GC and architect (or owner) disagree on a contract interpretation, payment, or a deficiency claim, A201 requires an "Initial Decision Maker" process before litigation. The IDM (often a neutral third party, such as the project engineer or a construction manager) hears the dispute and issues a binding decision.

Most GCs are unfamiliar with the IDM process and jump to litigation when disputes arise. Understanding that A201 requires IDM review first (and that IDM decisions are binding unless either party objects in writing within 30 days) can save a dispute from escalating to costly arbitration.

## 6. MEP Coordination Without a Dedicated MEP Coordinator

### 6.1 What a GC Needs to Manage MEP Coordination

On a large commercial project ($10M+), a dedicated MEP coordinator (often a separate consultant) produces detailed coordination drawings and BIM models showing all trades' rough-in locations. On a smaller multi-family or light commercial project ($1M–$5M), there's no MEP coordinator. The GC must manage coordination.

The minimum required for effective coordination:
- A single floor-by-floor coordination drawing (or multiple drawings by zone).
- This drawing overlays mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems on the same base plan.
- Clashes (where two systems occupy the same space) are identified on the drawing.

### 6.2 Running a Coordination Meeting Without BIM

If the project doesn't have a BIM model, coordination can be done with paper:

1. **Print translucent overlays:** Print the mechanical plan on translucent paper. Print the electrical plan on a second sheet of translucent paper. Print the plumbing plan on a third sheet.

2. **Physically overlay them:** Place the mechanical sheet over the base architectural plan. Place the electrical sheet on top. Place the plumbing sheet on top. Look at the overlaid sheets and visually identify clashes.

3. **Mark conflicts:** Where a duct, pipe, and conduit overlap in space, mark the conflict with a red circle and note. "Conflict at grid D-4: HVAC supply duct conflicts with electrical 4-inch conduit. Resolution: Move duct 12 inches to the north."

4. **Document and distribute:** Create a marked-up coordination drawing documenting all conflicts and proposed resolutions. Distribute to all MEP subs.

5. **Walk the field:** Before installation begins, walk the project with each MEP sub, pointing out their rough-in locations and showing them the coordination drawing. Answer questions: "Where exactly should my duct go? How much vertical clearance do I have?"

### 6.3 Establishing Elevation Rules and Hierarchies

When space is tight and trades cannot easily avoid each other, establish an elevation hierarchy:

**Standard hierarchy (from lowest to highest priority to maintain intended location):**
1. Plumbing (drain stacks are the most difficult to relocate because slope is critical).
2. Mechanical (ducts can be relocated but at some cost).
3. Electrical (conduit and wiring can be relocated most easily).
4. Low-voltage (data, AV cabling can be relocated easily).

This hierarchy means: if plumbing and mechanical conflict, mechanical moves. If mechanical and electrical conflict, electrical moves.

### 6.4 The Shaft Review Meeting

The shaft review meeting is critical and should occur before framing is complete. Attendees: GC, mechanical sub, electrical sub, plumbing sub, architect (if available).

**Agenda:**
1. Review the shaft drawing (showing all penetrations and their locations).
2. Confirm the locations of all major systems penetrating the shaft (supply air duct, return air duct, chilled water lines, hot water lines, electrical panel, drain stacks).
3. Confirm sleeve sizes (the holes that will be built into the concrete or drywall to allow pipes and ducts to pass through).
4. Resolve any conflicts identified during the walk-through.
5. Confirm the agreed layout on a marked-up drawing.

This meeting produces a finalized "shaft layout drawing" that each sub signs. This becomes the construction document for the shaft.

### 6.5 What Triggers a BIM Coordination Requirement

BIM (Building Information Modeling) is a 3D digital model showing all building systems. It's valuable for coordination but costly ($5,000–$50,000+ depending on project complexity).

BIM is typically required when:
- **Project size:** Exceeds $5M (large enough to justify the cost).
- **Specification requirement:** The architect's specifications explicitly require BIM coordination.
- **Owner requirement:** The owner (often a large corporate entity) requires BIM as part of their internal project standards.
- **Complexity:** MEP systems are particularly complex and space is constrained.

If BIM is required, budget for it and hire a BIM coordinator early (ideally during design, so coordination issues are caught before construction begins).

### 6.6 GC Liability When Coordination Fails

A critical point: **If two subs install conflicting work because the GC failed to coordinate, the GC pays for the rework, not the subs.**

Example: The mechanical sub installs a supply duct; the electrical sub installs conduit in the same location. They conflict. Who pays?
- If there was no coordination drawing or coordination meeting, the GC typically bears the cost (the GC failed to coordinate).
- If there was a coordination drawing showing the duct location, but the electrical sub installed conduit anyway, the electrical sub bears the cost (they didn't follow the coordination drawing).

This distinction makes the coordination process contractually important, not just operationally important. A documented coordination meeting and signed coordination drawing protect the GC.

## Case Study 1: The Tenant Improvement Accessible Path-of-Travel Trap

**Project context:**
- 8,500 sq ft retail tenant improvement in an existing strip center.
- New tenant: fast-casual restaurant with counter service and table seating.
- Lease negotiations: landlord will pay for structural/MEP upgrades; tenant pays for interior fit-out.
- TI scope and contract value: $320,000 (interior demolition, new walls, kitchen equipment, flooring, paint, MEP terminations).

**The issue:**
During the plan-check process, the building department reviews the TI plans and issues a requirement: "Accessible path-of-travel upgrades required per CBC Chapter 11B. Scope includes: (a) new accessible parking space (the existing lot has no dedicated accessible space); (b) ramp from parking to the building entrance (the entrance is 8 inches above grade); (c) new fire-rated door at the restaurant entrance (existing door is standard, not fire-rated for the restaurant occupancy change); (d) widened corridor from the entrance to the dining area (minimum 36 inches per CBC)."

Estimated cost: $68,000 ($18,000 for accessible parking and ramp; $8,000 for fire-rated door; $42,000 for interior circulation improvements).

**The dispute:**
The tenant says: "Path-of-travel upgrades are not part of our TI scope. The landlord should pay for them because they affect the common area."

The landlord says: "Your TI triggered the accessibility requirement. The building department said the accessibility upgrades are triggered by your occupancy change from generic retail to a restaurant. You're responsible."

The GC is caught in the middle. The contract is ambiguous: the TI contract doesn't explicitly list path-of-travel as the tenant's responsibility or the landlord's responsibility.

**What does the 20% disproportionate cost rule actually mean here?**

The rule states: "If the cost to upgrade the accessible path of travel exceeds 20% of the alteration cost, the alteration is disproportionate, and the tenant is not required to pay for path-of-travel upgrades exceeding 20%."

Calculation:
- TI cost (interior fit-out only): $320,000.
- 20% of TI cost: $64,000.
- Path-of-travel cost: $68,000.

The path-of-travel cost ($68,000) exceeds 20% of TI cost ($64,000) by $4,000. So the restaurant tenant must pay only $64,000 toward path-of-travel upgrades; the remaining $4,000 is disproportionate and is the landlord's responsibility.

**Can any of the path-of-travel work be deferred?**

Some path-of-travel work can be deferred if the tenant doesn't immediately need occupancy. However, the building department typically requires all path-of-travel work to be complete before the building department issues an occupancy permit. Deferral is not a realistic option for most TIs.

**The GC's response:**
The GC immediately researches the contract and builds department requirements. The GC prepares a written memo to both the tenant and landlord:

> "Path-of-Travel Accessibility Analysis — Building department review has identified accessibility upgrades required per CBC Chapter 11B. Total path-of-travel cost: $68,000. Per the 20% disproportionate cost rule, the tenant is required to contribute $64,000 (20% of the $320,000 TI contract); the landlord is responsible for the remaining $4,000 that exceeds the 20% threshold. A breakdown by cost category is attached. The tenant's architect and the landlord's facilities team should coordinate with the building department to confirm the work scope and approach timeline. The GC will include the tenant's $64,000 cost in a revised contract addendum and will coordinate with the landlord on the $4,000 landlord cost as a separate allowance."

This memo clarifies the contractual responsibility and moves both parties toward resolution.

**Key lessons:**
1. **Path-of-travel requirements are triggered by occupancy changes, not always by cost.** Changing a retail space to a restaurant (different occupancy classification) can trigger path-of-travel work even if the interior fit-out cost is small.
2. **Understand the 20% rule before signing the TI contract.** If the GC understands the rule, they can alert the tenant and landlord early and include path-of-travel work in the initial contract scope, avoiding surprises at plan check.
3. **Path-of-travel work is almost never deferred.** The building department won't issue an occupancy permit until path-of-travel is complete. Budget for it in the timeline.
4. **The GC must serve as the honest broker.** The tenant and landlord have competing interests. The GC's role is to understand the code requirement, explain it clearly to both parties, and facilitate a resolution.

## Case Study 2: The Missing Fire Blocking

**Project context:**
- 24-unit Type V-A wood-frame multi-family project (residential construction, 3 stories).
- Fire-resistance requirement: 1-hour party walls, 1-hour corridor assembly, fire blocking at all floor/ceiling lines and penetrations.
- Framing sub: experienced in residential construction but first multi-family project.

**The problem:**
Pre-drywall inspection by the building department framing inspector. The inspector is specifically checking for fire blocking compliance (a common deficiency on multi-family projects).

Inspection findings: Missing fire blocking in 14 of 24 units. The framing sub installed 2×4 blocking at some locations (exterior walls, where they understood it was required) but missed the code-required locations throughout the units:
- Attic/upper-floor interface (where the top plate of the first-floor wall meets the second-floor joist).
- Interior wall/ceiling interfaces (where partition walls meet the soffit above).
- Cavities above dropped ceilings.

**The deficiency:**
The framing inspector issues a notice of non-compliance. The framing must be corrected before drywall can proceed. Correcting fire blocking after framing is complete is labor-intensive: interior studs must be cut to access the cavity, blocking installed, studs reinstalled/patched.

Estimated correction cost: $8,000–$12,000 (additional labor for accessing and installing blocking in 14 units).

**Responsibility for the cost:**
The framing sub argues: "This is an interpretation issue. Fire blocking requirements vary by location; we installed blocking where we understood it was required. The inspector is being overly strict."

The GC's subcontract with the framing sub states: "Framing shall be installed per the architectural drawings and per California Building Code. All work shall pass inspections by the authority having jurisdiction."

**Who is responsible?**

The framing code explicitly requires fire blocking at all concealed vertical spaces and at floor/ceiling junctions. This is not an interpretation issue; it's explicit code language. The framing sub's failure to install blocking at all code-required locations is a subcontract breach.

The framing sub is responsible for the cost of correcting the deficiency. However, if the GC did not clearly communicate fire-blocking requirements to the framing sub (e.g., no fire-blocking checklist, no pre-construction meeting to explain multi-family-specific requirements), the GC shares some responsibility for clarity/communication.

**What would have prevented this?**

1. **Pre-construction meeting with the framing sub:** The GC should walk the framing sub through multi-family-specific code requirements before the sub starts work, including a marked-up plan showing all fire-blocking locations.

2. **Fire-blocking checklist:** Provide the framing sub with a simple checklist of fire-blocking locations (verified against the architectural/structural drawings).

3. **Framing inspection for fire blocking before drywall:** Rather than waiting for the building department inspection, the GC should conduct their own framing inspection specifically checking fire blocking. If deficiencies are found, they can be corrected before drywall coverage (less costly than correction after framing is "complete").

4. **Framing subcontract clarity:** Specify in the subcontract: "All fire blocking required per CBC Sections 720.1 and 722.1 shall be installed before drywall coverage. Fire blocking inspection shall be scheduled and passed before drywall proceeding."

**The GC's response:**
The GC meets with the framing sub and building department inspector. The GC confirms: "Fire blocking is required at all locations identified by the inspector. The framing sub is responsible for correction per the subcontract. Work must be corrected within 5 days; we'll re-inspect before proceeding to drywall."

The GC documents this conversation in writing. The framing sub corrects the deficiencies; the building department re-inspects and passes. Drywall proceeds on schedule (only a 5-day delay instead of a 2-week delay if rework were deferred).

**Key lessons:**
1. **Multi-family fire-blocking requirements are strict and non-negotiable.** The GC must ensure the framing sub understands them before work begins.
2. **A pre-construction framing inspection by the GC (not just the building department) catches these issues early.** Correcting during framing is cheaper than correcting after framing.
3. **Subcontract language must be clear.** The contract should explicitly reference the fire-blocking locations and the requirement to pass inspection before drywall coverage.
4. **Document and communicate.** When addressing deficiencies, document the communication in writing so there's no ambiguity about responsibility.

## Summary and Key Takeaways

Multi-family and light commercial construction is not "bigger residential construction." It's a different regulatory and structural environment with distinct code requirements, formal contract structures, and coordination complexity that residential GCs must understand to succeed in commercial work.

The fundamentals:
1. **Occupancy classifications** drive fire-resistance requirements and system choices.
2. **Fire-separation assemblies** require design review and systematic code compliance.
3. **Accessibility obligations** extend beyond single-family ADA basics and include path-of-travel requirements.
4. **Multi-family sequencing** requires zone-based planning and coordination of multiple trades.
5. **AIA A201 contracts** give the design professional significant authority and require systematic submittal/RFI discipline.
6. **MEP coordination** can be managed on smaller projects without BIM, but requires disciplined processes.

GCs who master these fundamentals transition successfully from residential to commercial work and avoid costly rework, schedule delays, and contract disputes.
