# Hillside and Difficult-Site Construction in California

> **The hard truth.** A flat-site GC moving to hillside work who prices it like flat work will lose money on the first job, lose more on the second, and either change companies or change businesses by the third. Hillside residential construction in California routinely runs 1.8x–3.5x the cost-per-square-foot of equivalent flat-site work, and the cost variance from project to project is enormous. The difference between a profitable hillside builder and an insolvent one is not skill at framing or finish — it is geotechnical literacy, drainage discipline, access planning, and the willingness to walk away from sites that should not be built on at the price the owner is willing to pay.
>
> This guide is the reference you should read before bidding any site with slope above 15%, expansive clay, high groundwater, fault proximity, or constrained access. It will not make you a geotechnical engineer. It will keep you from getting hurt by the things geotechnical engineers warn about.

## 1. Site Challenges and Risk Assessment

### 1.1 The five killers on a difficult site

Before any further reading, internalize these. Every line in this guide ties back to one of them.

1. **Water** — surface, subsurface, and groundwater. Drainage is the most commonly under-engineered, under-built, and under-warranted system on hillside projects. Water failures are the #1 cause of catastrophic loss.
2. **Soil that moves** — landslides, debris flows, expansive clay heave, settlement of poorly compacted fill, liquefaction.
3. **Access** — what you cannot get to the site, you cannot build with. Crane reach, concrete pump distance, truck turnaround.
4. **Seismic geometry** — hillside split-levels with downhill open faces are inherently weak-story unless deliberately braced. Faults under your pad are non-negotiable setbacks.
5. **Time** — every difficulty above costs schedule, and schedule on hillside work compounds: shoring rentals, dewatering, winterization, finance charges, and crew demoralization.

### 1.2 Site risk categories

| Risk Category | Tier 1 (Standard) | Tier 2 (Moderate) | Tier 3 (Difficult) | Tier 4 (Severe) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Slope (rise/run)** | 0–15% (gentle) | 15–30% (moderate) | 30–50% (steep) | >50% (very steep) |
| **Soil type** | Dense sand/gravel, well-graded native | Silty sand, lean clay (CL) | Fat clay (CH), loose fill, peat lenses | Bay mud, deep uncontrolled fill, slide debris |
| **Expansion Index (EI)** | 0–20 (Very Low) | 21–50 (Low) | 51–90 (Medium) | 91–130+ (High/Very High) |
| **Groundwater depth** | >20 ft below grade | 10–20 ft | 5–10 ft | <5 ft or perched at footing |
| **Seismic** | SDC C, no fault zone | SDC D, >1 mile from active fault | SDC D/E, near fault, hillside SDC modifier | Alquist-Priolo zone, liquefaction zone, landslide zone |
| **Erosion potential** | Low (flat, vegetated) | Moderate (some slope, mixed cover) | High (steep, sparse vegetation) | Very high (bare steep slope, prior slides) |
| **Access** | 24 ft+ paved road, ample lay-down | Narrow paved, modest lay-down | Single-lane road, no lay-down, switchbacks | Helicopter, hand-carry, or extended pump only |
| **Utility constraints** | All utilities at street, gravity sewer | Long sewer lateral, OH electric | Septic required, well, propane | No utilities, off-grid required, OWTS engineering |

A site rated Tier 3 or 4 on **any two** categories should be priced as a hillside/difficult-site project. A Tier 4 on water OR slope alone is enough.

### 1.3 Pre-bid site assessment checklist

Walk the site **twice** — once dry (summer/fall) and once after rain if at all possible. Bring a clinometer or use a slope app, a GPS, a camera, a measuring wheel, and a notepad. Photograph everything.

| # | Item | Notes / Red flags |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Slope measured at multiple cross-sections | Average is meaningless; pad area, driveway, and septic area each matter |
| 2 | Visible seeps, springs, wet spots, rushes/sedges/horsetails growing | All indicate groundwater within rooting depth — typically <3 ft |
| 3 | Existing cracks in driveway, walks, neighbor's foundations | Pattern (vertical, diagonal, stair-step) tells story of soil movement |
| 4 | Leaning trees, "pistol-butt" curved trunks, J-shaped trees | Classic creep indicators — slope is moving slowly |
| 5 | Hummocky ground, scarps, tension cracks at slope crest | Active or dormant landslide signature |
| 6 | Drainage paths during and after rain | Where does water go? Where does it pond? Where does it concentrate? |
| 7 | Neighbor's drainage discharges | Does uphill neighbor's downspout dump on this pad? |
| 8 | Existing retaining walls — condition, height, drainage | Walls bulging, cracking, weep holes blocked = future liability |
| 9 | Access road — width, gradient, surface, weight limits, switchbacks | Measure tightest corner; can a 40 ft concrete truck make it? |
| 10 | Overhead constraints — wires, tree canopy, structures | Crane height clearance, pump boom clearance |
| 11 | Soil sample observation (auger or hand-dug pit) | Color, texture, moisture, plasticity. Fat clay rolls into ribbon. |
| 12 | Bedrock outcrops, rock fragments in soil | Could mean shallow rock — good for piers, bad for excavation |
| 13 | Distance and elevation to nearest sanitary sewer | Sewer ejector pumps? Gravity not feasible? |
| 14 | Setbacks from top-of-slope and toe-of-slope | Local code typically requires 2H:1V from top, 1H:1V from toe; varies |
| 15 | Fire hazard zone (CAL FIRE FHSZ map) | Very High FHSZ triggers WUI requirements + fire access |
| 16 | FEMA flood zone (FIRM map) | A, AE, V, VE zones require Floodplain Development Permit |
| 17 | Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zone (CGS maps) | 50 ft setback from trace; full geologic investigation required |
| 18 | Seismic Hazard Zone (liquefaction/landslide) | CGS Seismic Hazards Zonation Act requires geotechnical study |
| 19 | Coastal Zone | CDP required; bluff setback often 100+ ft, geologic-life basis |
| 20 | Heritage / protected trees | Inventory and survey; permit before removal |
| 21 | Existing easements (utility, drainage, equestrian, access) | Plot on survey; cannot build over and often cannot grade over |
| 22 | Historic aerial photos (Google Earth historical layer, UCSB FrameFinder) | Was this site a fill? A quarry? A drainage channel? |

Document with date-stamped photos. **Condition surveys of neighboring properties are essential** before you start — photograph every visible crack on every neighbor's house within the zone of influence (typically 1.5x excavation depth from cut face).

### 1.4 The geotechnical report — what to require

On any difficult site, the geotechnical report is the most important document in your bid set after the structural plans. **Do not bid without one.** If the owner has not commissioned one, your bid is conditioned on the owner obtaining one and on review of its findings.

**Scope to require** (this is what you tell the owner to ask for from the geotech):

- Minimum 2 borings, more typical 3–5 for hillside residential, located at the proposed footprint corners
- Borings to minimum 25 ft or 5 ft into competent bearing material, whichever is deeper
- SPT (Standard Penetration Test) blow counts (N-values) at 2.5 ft intervals
- Soil classification per USCS, including Atterberg limits (LL, PL, PI) on clay strata
- Expansion Index test per ASTM D4829 on near-surface soils
- Moisture content and dry density on representative samples
- Sulfate and chloride content (corrosivity to concrete and reinforcement)
- R-value or CBR if any new pavement
- Groundwater depth at time of drilling AND seasonal high estimate
- Slope stability analysis with Factor of Safety calculations (static AND pseudo-static for seismic)
- Recommendations for: foundation type, bearing pressures, lateral earth pressures, settlement, retaining walls, drainage, site preparation, compaction specs

### 1.5 How to read a geotechnical report

Most GCs skim the cover letter and flip to the recommendations section. Do not be that GC. Read the report in this order:

| Section | What to look for | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Project description** | Footprint, finished floor elevations, retaining wall heights | Did the geotech evaluate the actual project? If FFE changed, the report may be invalid |
| **Site description & geology** | Mapped geologic unit, formation name, surficial deposits | Franciscan mélange vs. dune sand vs. bay mud are totally different problems |
| **Field investigation** | Boring locations relative to building, depths, hand-auger holes | Were borings actually inside the footprint? Common shortcut: drillers couldn't access, borings are 50 ft away |
| **Boring logs** | Layer-by-layer description, N-values, water table | This is the actual data. Recommendations are interpretation. Reading the logs lets you challenge interpretation |
| **Laboratory results** | EI, Atterberg limits, sulfate, density, shear strength | Match these against the recommendations |
| **Slope stability analysis** | FS values for static and seismic, critical surface location | FS < 1.5 static / 1.1 seismic = problem regardless of what the rec section says |
| **Geohazard section** | Faults, liquefaction, landslides, debris flow, lateral spreading, settlement | This is where the deal-breakers live |
| **Recommendations** | Foundation, walls, drainage, site prep, fill specs, inspection | The "spec" portion you will build to |
| **Limitations & assumptions** | The disclaimers | Tells you what the geotech is NOT taking responsibility for |
| **Plates / cross-sections** | Cross-section through the site with subsurface profile | Single most useful drawing — pin it to your office wall |

**Reading SPT N-values (uncorrected):**

| N (blows/ft) | Sand | Clay (consistency) |
|---|---|---|
| 0–4 | Very loose | Very soft |
| 4–10 | Loose | Soft |
| 10–30 | Medium dense | Medium stiff |
| 30–50 | Dense | Stiff to very stiff |
| >50 | Very dense | Hard |

Spread footings on sand generally want N>15 in the bearing layer; on clay, want N>10 with confirmed cohesion. Piers in bedrock typically want refusal (N>50/6") at tip elevation.

**Bearing capacity** quoted in the report (e.g., "1,500 psf allowable, may be increased to 2,000 psf for combined dead+live+seismic") is what your structural engineer designs footings to. **Lateral bearing** (passive pressure, typically 150–350 pcf equivalent fluid pressure) is used for sliding resistance and pier design. **Active/at-rest pressures** (35–65 pcf typical for non-expansive backfill behind retaining walls) drive wall reinforcement.

### 1.6 Slope stability fundamentals

A slope is stable when the resisting forces (soil strength, root reinforcement, support at toe) exceed the driving forces (gravity acting on the soil mass, water in the soil increasing weight and reducing effective stress).

**Factor of Safety (FS) = Resisting / Driving.** California new construction generally requires FS ≥ 1.5 static, ≥ 1.1 to 1.2 pseudo-static (seismic). LA City and County typically require FS ≥ 1.5 static / ≥ 1.1 seismic.

**Angle of repose** (approximate, dry conditions):

| Soil | Angle of Repose | Slope (rise:run) |
|---|---|---|
| Dry sand, well-graded | 30–35° | ~1:1.5 |
| Wet sand | 15–30° | ~1:2 to 1:4 |
| Gravel, angular | 35–45° | ~1:1 to 1.4:1 |
| Stiff clay (short-term, dry) | 50–80° | nearly vertical |
| Soft clay (long-term, wet) | 0–15° | 1:4 to flat (it will flow) |
| Bedrock, jointed | 45–90° | varies — depends on joint orientation |
| Engineered fill, compacted | 26.5° (2:1) maximum per CBC standard | 2H:1V |

**California's standard maximum graded slope** for new construction without an engineered specific design is **2:1 (horizontal:vertical, 50%, 26.5°)**. Anything steeper triggers slope stability analysis and typically retaining systems.

**Role of vegetation:** Established native vegetation with deep roots (oaks, chamise, manzanita) can add 5–25% to apparent cohesion in the rooting zone (top 3–6 ft). Shallow grass roots contribute much less. **Clearing established vegetation reduces stability** — this is why bare graded slopes slide in the first big rain.

**Role of groundwater:** Saturation does three things, all bad: (1) adds weight to the driving force, (2) reduces effective stress (and hence frictional strength), (3) generates pore pressure that pushes outward. **The vast majority of California landslides are triggered by sustained winter rainfall raising the groundwater table**, not by single storms.

**Historic slide evidence on a property:**
- Hummocky topography
- Closed depressions
- Sag ponds
- Tilted trees, fences, walls
- Disrupted drainage
- Curved scarps at the head
- Toe bulges
- Cracks parallel to contour

If you see ANY of these, the site has moved before. It will move again. Get a geologist on the property before you bid.

### 1.7 California-specific seismic and geohazard maps

These are public, free, and you must consult them at the pre-bid stage:

| Resource | What it shows | URL/access |
|---|---|---|
| **CGS Earthquake Hazards Zone Application (EQZApp)** | Alquist-Priolo zones, Seismic Hazard zones (liquefaction, landslide) | maps.conservation.ca.gov/cgs/EQZApp |
| **CGS Landslide Inventory** | Mapped historic and active landslides | conservation.ca.gov/cgs/landslides |
| **USGS Q-Faults** | Quaternary faults nationally | usgs.gov/programs/earthquake-hazards/faults |
| **CAL FIRE FHSZ** | Fire Hazard Severity Zones | osfm.fire.ca.gov |

## 2. Hillside Foundation Systems

### 2.1 When standard spread footings fail

A continuous footing or pad footing is the cheapest residential foundation. It works when:

- Slope <10–15% (so footing can step modestly or follow flat pad)
- Native soil bearing capacity ≥ 1,500 psf at footing depth
- Expansion Index < 50 (Low)
- Groundwater >5 ft below footing
- No setback / daylight problems (toe of footing within 1:1 line from face of cut)

When ANY of these fails, you go to a deep foundation, a stepped foundation, or major over-excavation. The most expensive mistake on a hillside is trying to make spread footings work on a marginal site.

### 2.2 Drilled piers (cast-in-drilled-hole — CIDH)

Drilled piers are the workhorse of California hillside foundations. A vertical hole is drilled with a truck-mounted or tracked auger rig, a reinforcement cage is lowered in, and concrete is placed. The pier transmits load through skin friction along its length and end bearing at its base.

**When piers are used:**
- Slopes >20–25%
- Expansive soils where founding on stable layer below the active zone is desired
- Sites with downslope creep, where transferring load below the creep zone is required
- Sites with high seismic loads needing uplift resistance
- Sites with poor surface soils overlying competent bedrock or dense sediment

**Typical sizing for residential hillside:**

| Parameter | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Diameter (shaft) | 18", 24", 30", 36" (24" is most common residential) |
| Depth | 15–30 ft (deeper on steep sites with thick colluvium; can reach 40–60 ft) |
| Embedment in competent material | Minimum 5 ft, often 8–10 ft |
| Reinforcement | 6–10 #6 to #9 vertical bars, #4 ties at 12" o.c., full-length cage |
| Concrete | f'c = 3,000 psi minimum, often 4,000 psi; pump-grade slump 4–6" |
| Spacing | Typically 8–16 ft on center along grade beam |
| Bell at base | 2x to 3x shaft diameter (rare in CA hillside — bells are difficult to inspect and have largely been replaced by deeper straight shafts) |

**Drilling equipment access:**
- Truck-mounted rigs (Texoma, Watson) need ~12 ft wide flat-ish working pad and 12–14 ft headroom
- Tracked limited-access rigs (Mobile B-50, CME-45, Diedrich D-50, Soilmec mini) fit through 6–7 ft gates and operate on 30% slopes — but cost 1.5–2x truck rig daypower
- Crane-suspended ("hanging") rigs for extreme access

**Inspection** is non-negotiable and is performed by the **geotechnical engineer of record** or a **deputy inspector** (registered geologist or PE):

1. **Pre-pour observation of bottom**: Bearing material verified, depth recorded, hole cleaned of cuttings, water depth measured (<3" or use tremie). Drill hole sidewalls observed for caving.
2. **Cage inspection**: Reinforcement matches drawings, cover spacers in place, cage straight and centered.
3. **Concrete placement**: Continuous pour, tremie if wet hole, slump tested, cylinders made for 28-day breaks. Concrete placed within 90 min of batch.
4. **Pier log**: Every pier gets a log: depth, time started, time ended, slump, cylinders, observations, signed by inspector.

**Common pier mistakes:**
- Drilling, then waiting overnight, then placing concrete → hole sloughs, pier defective
- Placing concrete with water in the bottom by free-fall → segregated concrete, weak tip
- Skimping on inspection because owner balked at cost → pier rejected at building department, redo at GC expense
- Letting the structural drawing's pier depth substitute for the geotech's depth-to-bearing call (the geotech's call governs)

### 2.3 Grade beams

A grade beam is a continuous reinforced concrete beam spanning between piers. It supports the structure above and ties the piers together. It is **not** a footing — it should not rest directly on expansive soil; it is suspended between piers with a void (often 4–6" of cardboard void-form, e.g., Sure-Void) below it.

**Typical grade beam dimensions for residential hillside:**

| Parameter | Typical |
|---|---|
| Width | 12"–18" |
| Depth | 18"–36" (depends on span between piers and load) |
| Top reinforcement | 2–4 #6 bars |
| Bottom reinforcement | 2–4 #6 bars |
| Stirrups | #4 at 6"–12" o.c. |
| Pier-to-beam connection | Pier vertical bars dowel up into beam, sometimes hooked or with mechanical splice |

**Common grade beam mistakes:**
- Beam rests on soil instead of void form → expansive soil heaves the beam, cracking the structure
- Pier dowels don't extend into beam → connection fails in seismic
- Beam steel not properly developed at corners and ends → cracking at re-entrant corners

### 2.4 Post-and-beam (cripple wall on pier) substructure

A wood or steel post sits on a concrete pier or pad and supports a wood beam, which carries the floor framing. The structure literally stands above the natural grade on stilts. This is the classic mid-century hillside house pattern.

**When it's used:**
- Moderate to steep slopes (15–50%)
- Minimal site disturbance desired
- Architectural goals: deck/floor over slope, light footprint

**Considerations:**
- Posts on piers transmit only vertical (gravity) load efficiently. Lateral load (seismic, wind) requires **bracing** — moment frames at post bases, knee braces between post and beam, or a separate lateral system (shear walls).
- **Soft-story risk** at the downhill open face: this is the #1 retrofit need on California hillside post-and-beam houses. New construction should have moment frames or specially-designed shear walls at the downhill face.
- **Maintenance access** under the floor is critical: pest inspection, plumbing, dry rot, sometimes seismic retrofit work. Minimum 18" clear is code, but 36"+ is what you actually want for serviceability. If access is bad, future O&M costs spike.
- **Galvanic corrosion** at post bases: galvanized steel post bases in damp environments fail in 20–30 years. Use stainless, properly drained, and elevated above splash.

### 2.5 Stepped foundation

The footing follows the slope in a series of horizontal "treads" and vertical "risers." Each step is engineered. The structural function is essentially a series of connected continuous footings.

**Code limitations (CRC R403.1.5 / CBC 1809.5):**
- Bottom of footing always horizontal
- Top of footing slopes no more than 10% (1:10) — beyond that, stepped footing required
- Steps: at least 6" of horizontal at top of footing between steps; vertical step typically matches block course (e.g., 8") for masonry stem walls

**Practical limits:**
- Stepped foundation works well to about 25% slope
- Beyond 25%, the number of steps becomes uneconomical and the soil volume that has to be cut for level pads at each step becomes large
- Each step is a potential cold joint, drainage issue, and rebar continuity challenge

**Forming challenges:**
- The combination of stepped wall + stepped footing + slope drainage makes formwork complex and slow
- Inside corners at each step are hard to vibrate concrete into
- Rebar must be continuous across each step (lap or bend) — careful detailing required

### 2.6 Helical piers

Steel shafts (typically 2-7/8" to 4.5" diameter pipe or 1.5" solid square) with one or more helical plates welded to the lead section. Installed with hydraulic torque motor; torque correlates to capacity via empirical equations.

**Strengths:**
- Low vibration — good adjacent to existing structures
- Moderate equipment access — installer can fit through a 36" gate with a mini-excavator-mounted unit
- Quick installation — 10–30 piers/day depending on conditions
- Immediate load capability — no concrete cure
- Verifiable capacity from torque

**Weaknesses:**
- Typical residential capacity per pier: 20–60 kips (vs. 100–300+ kips for CIDH)
- Corrosion concern in aggressive soils; galvanized typical, but service life questions in 100-yr exposure
- Less common for new construction whole-building — more common for additions, retrofits, deck supports, and underpinning

**Building department acceptance**: ICC-ES evaluation reports (e.g., Chance, Magnum, Ram Jack) make permitting routine but always confirm with the local jurisdiction.

### 2.7 Concrete slab on steep sites

Slab-on-grade is the cheapest floor system but is rarely appropriate on slopes >15% without major site work. The fundamental problems:

1. **Fill section.** Bringing the pad to a flat elevation requires either deep cut (expensive, exposes daylight retaining problem) or deep fill (more expensive, requires engineered compaction and time). On a 30% slope, a 40 ft wide pad needs 6 ft of fill at the downhill side.
2. **Compaction on slope.** Compaction near the fill edge ("daylight") is hard to achieve because there's no confinement. Edge of slab subsidence is the typical failure.
3. **Drainage.** Water sheets toward the slab from uphill; subdrain at the cut-back is mandatory; underslab drainage (perforated pipe + gravel + filter fabric) is added insurance.
4. **Expansive soil.** A slab over expansive clay will crack and lift. Either over-excavate and replace with 24–48" of non-expansive engineered fill, OR go to a structural slab on piers (more like a grade beam mat).

**If slab on grade is used:**
- Geotechnical-engineered subgrade preparation (over-ex, moisture conditioning, compaction)
- Capillary break: minimum 4" clean gravel (3/8" pea gravel or #57)
- Vapor barrier: 10–15 mil Class A vapor retarder (Stego, Vapor Block) — lapped, taped, sealed at penetrations
- Reinforcement: #4 bars at 12"–18" o.c. each way (do not rely on welded wire mesh in mid-slab — it inevitably ends up on the bottom)
- Control joints at 12–15 ft o.c. maximum (saw cut within 12 hr or tooled at finish)
- Underslab drainage: perforated pipe in gravel along the uphill perimeter, daylighted

## 3. Grading and Earthwork on Difficult Sites

### 3.1 Cut vs. fill strategy

Every cubic yard of earth you move on a hillside costs money twice — once to excavate, once to handle (compact, haul, or place). The cheapest pad is the one nearest natural grade.

**Strategy hierarchy:**
1. **Step the structure into the slope** so the building footprint absorbs the grade. Daylight basement / split-level / multi-level pad.
2. **Balance cut and fill on-site** so neither hauling-out nor hauling-in is required.
3. **Cut only**, haul-off excess. Safer than fill (you're founding on native, not constructed material).
4. **Fill only** (cantilever pad over downhill side). Most expensive, most risky, most regulated.

A common error is to design a flat pad that requires both deep cut and deep fill — both of the most expensive operations on the same project.

### 3.2 Fill compaction on slopes — benching

When new fill is placed against an existing slope, the contact surface between native and new must be **benched** — cut into the slope in steps — so the fill cannot slide down on the old surface. Without benching, the new fill rides like a wedge on the old slope and slips during the first big rain or earthquake.

**Bench geometry (typical, confirm with geotech):**
- Bench width (horizontal): minimum 8 ft, often 10–15 ft
- Bench height (vertical): typically equal to one compaction lift (8"–12")
- Bottom keyway: at the toe of fill, a deeper key cut 4–6 ft below natural grade, 10–15 ft wide, with a subdrain
- Keyway extends down to firm bearing (not topsoil, not colluvium) — confirmed by geotechnical observation

**Compaction specifications (typical residential structural fill):**

| Location | Relative Compaction | Test Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Below structural footings / slabs | 90% modified Proctor (ASTM D1557), 95% in some jurisdictions | 1 test per 500 CY per lift, minimum 2 per lift |
| Below pavement / drives | 90–95% | 1 test per 500 CY per lift |
| Below landscape / non-structural | 85–90% | 1 test per 1,000 CY |
| Trench backfill, utilities | 90% (95% under hardscape) | 1 test per 50 LF per lift |

**Compaction equipment by lift:**

| Equipment | Suitable for | Lift depth (loose) |
|---|---|---|
| Smooth drum vibratory roller | Sand, gravel, well-graded soils | 12" |
| Sheepsfoot / padfoot roller | Clay, silt | 8"–10" |
| Plate compactor (jumping jack/rammer) | Cohesive, trench backfill, edges | 4"–6" |
| Plate compactor (vibratory plate) | Granular, around utilities | 6"–8" |

**Moisture conditioning** is the most-skipped step: clay won't compact dry, sand won't compact too wet. Optimum moisture content (OMC) from a Proctor curve is the target, typically +/- 2–3%.

### 3.3 Expansive soil management

Expansive clay (EI > 50, plasticity index > 15, fat clay CH) swells when wet and shrinks when dry. The differential between wet center and dry edges of a foundation generates heaving and cracking. This is the #1 cause of residential foundation distress in California outside earthquake.

**Management options, in order of typical cost:**

1. **Avoid the soil**: drilled piers below the active zone (typically 3–7 ft deep), grade beams on void-form, suspended slab. This is the gold standard and what most California hillside projects use.
2. **Over-excavate and replace**: remove 24"–48" of expansive native, replace with non-expansive engineered fill (EI < 20, often clean granular import). Conventional spread footings then bear on engineered fill. Most common on flatter expansive sites (Central Valley).
3. **Moisture conditioning + maintain**: pre-wet subgrade to above OMC, compact to 85–88% (intentionally low — over-compaction in expansive clay creates more swell potential), then maintain consistent moisture with a perimeter watering system and impermeable surface treatment. Works but requires owner discipline forever.
4. **Lime stabilization**: 4–6% hydrated lime by weight mixed into the top 12"–18" of clay reduces plasticity and expansion. Common in roadwork, less so in residential because of dust, cure time, and pH issues with landscape.
5. **Post-tensioned slab**: rigid mat that floats over modest expansion. Common in Texas hillside, much less common in California except some Central Valley tract work.

### 3.4 Landslide repair grading

Sites with prior landsliding require remedial grading designed by a **geotechnical engineer in responsible charge** (signed and stamped plans, continuous observation during construction).

**Typical remediation:**
1. **Remove the failed mass**: excavate slide debris down to undisturbed native or bedrock
2. **Keyway**: cut a key 8–15 ft deep into bedrock or competent native at the toe, 15–20 ft wide
3. **Subdrains**: perforated pipe in keyway and at bench backs, daylighted to surface — collects and removes infiltrating water
4. **Engineered fill**: place compacted fill in benched lifts back up to grade, 90%+ R.C.
5. **Surface drainage**: brow ditches above the repair, terrace drains, no concentrated runoff onto the repaired slope
6. **Surface protection**: hydroseed, erosion blanket, sometimes shotcrete or rock blanket on steep faces

Landslide repairs are expensive ($150K–$2M+ for residential-scale), require Special Inspection at every step, and are not insurable in the same way as conventional construction. **If you are bidding work on a property with a prior slide, the slide repair is its own project**, not a line item.

### 3.5 Import and export

| Issue | Considerations |
|---|---|
| **Haul truck capacity** | Standard end-dump 10–14 CY; transfer truck 18–22 CY; double-belly 16–18 CY |
| **Loaded truck weight** | 60,000–80,000 lbs gross — many hillside roads not engineered for this |
| **Haul route approval** | Many jurisdictions require pre-approved haul route, especially Bay Area & LA hillside communities |
| **Hours of operation** | Typically 7am–5pm M-F, 9am–4pm Sat, no Sunday |
| **Dust control** | SCAQMD Rule 403 (or BAAQMD, SJVAPCD equivalent) — water trucks, soil binders, covered loads |
| **Tracking control** | Stabilized construction entrance (rock pad 50 ft x 14 ft, 3"–6" rock); street sweeper |
| **Neighbor impacts** | Vibration complaints common; pre-construction crack survey of neighbors recommended |
| **Disposal fees** | Class III landfill rates $30–80/CY in CA; clean inert fill $5–20/CY at recyclers |
| **Import quality** | Test imported fill for contamination, sulfate, expansion before placing |

A 1,000 CY export project = ~75–100 truck trips. On a switchback hillside road, that's a major neighborhood event.

### 3.6 SWPPP and erosion control

A project disturbing **1 acre or more** triggers California's Construction General Permit (CGP), requiring a SWPPP (Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan) prepared by a QSD (Qualified SWPPP Developer) and implemented by a QSP (Qualified SWPPP Practitioner). **Hillside sites under 1 acre often still require local erosion control plans.**

**BMP toolkit for steep slopes:**

| BMP | Use | Typical cost |
|---|---|---|
| Hydroseeding | Stabilize bare slopes 2:1 or flatter, after final grade | $0.15–0.40/SF |
| Erosion control blanket (straw, coir, jute) | Steeper slopes, channels, immediately after grading | $1.50–4.00/SY |
| Bonded fiber matrix (BFM/SMM) | Steep slopes, fast cover | $0.40–0.80/SF |
| Fiber rolls / wattles | Slope interruption every 15–25 ft vertical | $2–4/LF |
| Silt fence | Downhill perimeter of disturbed area | $2–4/LF |
| Check dams in channels | Slow flow in temp ditches | $50–150 each |
| Sediment basin | Concentrated runoff collection | varies, often $5K–25K |
| Gravel bags | Temporary check dams, inlet protection | $3–6 each |
| Stabilized construction entrance | Mandatory at site access | $1,500–4,000 |

**Winterization (Oct 1 – Apr 30):** California enforces strict rainy-season requirements. Plan to have all disturbed slopes stabilized before October. If you can't, plan for major standby and rework.

## 4. Retaining Walls and Slope Stabilization

### 4.1 When walls are required

Any time you cut or fill and need to hold the soil at a slope steeper than 2H:1V (or whatever the geotech allows for the soil type), you need a retaining structure.

**Permit thresholds (typical California, confirm locally):**
- Wall <3 ft retained height (measured from bottom of footing to top of retained soil): often no separate permit, sometimes builder-designed allowed
- Wall 3–4 ft retained height: permit required, engineered design typically required, especially with surcharge
- Wall >4 ft any condition: permit + engineered design + Special Inspection
- Wall with **surcharge** (building, driveway, pool) at any height: engineered design required
- LA City: walls ≥4 ft from top of footing to top of wall require engineering and permit; walls supporting surcharge always

### 4.2 Cantilever concrete retaining walls

A vertical concrete stem on a wide concrete base footing, reinforced to resist overturning and sliding. Footing has a "heel" (extending under the retained soil, providing dead-load stability) and often a "toe" (extending out from the wall, mobilizing passive resistance and reducing soil pressure on the wall face).

**Typical residential proportions:**

| Wall height (H) | Stem thickness top | Stem thickness base | Footing width | Heel | Toe |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 ft | 8" | 8" | 3 ft | 18" | 6" |
| 6 ft | 8" | 10" | 4–5 ft | 30" | 12" |
| 8 ft | 8" | 12" | 5–6 ft | 36" | 18" |
| 10 ft | 10" | 14" | 6–8 ft | 48" | 24" |
| 12 ft | 12" | 16"–18" | 8–10 ft | 60"+ | 24" |

These are starting points. Actual design depends on soil parameters (active pressure, friction), surcharge, seismic increment (Mononobe-Okabe), drainage condition. **Always have a structural or geotechnical engineer design any wall >4 ft.**

**Drainage detailing — the most missed item in residential retaining wall failures:**
- Perforated pipe (4" SDR-35 or HDPE) at base of stem, in 12" wide x 18"+ tall column of #2 or 3/4" drain rock
- Geotextile filter fabric (Mirafi 140N or equivalent) wrapped around the drain rock column to prevent fines migration
- Weep holes through wall every 8 ft maximum at base (with screen and gravel pocket behind)
- Daylight the drain pipe to a positive outlet — NEVER tie to sanitary sewer, never blind-end
- Surface drainage above wall to prevent water concentrating into backfill

### 4.3 CMU (Concrete Masonry Unit) retaining walls

CMU walls are common for residential up to about 10 ft retained height. The block is grouted solid and reinforced vertically and horizontally.

**Typical residential:**
- 8" CMU for walls to 6 ft
- 12" CMU for walls 6–10 ft
- Vertical reinforcement: #5 @ 16" o.c. min, typically more
- Horizontal reinforcement: bond beam every 24"–32" with #4 or 9-gauge ladder/truss every course
- Cells with vertical bars grouted solid
- Stem-to-footing dowels at every vertical bar

**Inspection points:**
- Footing rebar before pour
- Cleanouts at base of CMU before initial grout (high-lift grouting)
- Vertical reinforcement and ties before grouting
- Grout slump and aggregate size (fine grout 8–11" slump for small cells; coarse grout for >2" minimum cell dimension)
- Special Inspection per CBC Section 1705.4 (engineered masonry)

CMU is forgiving for moderate residential walls. The most common failure is again drainage — CMU walls without weeps and back-of-wall drainage will efflorescence, then crack, then fail just like concrete.

### 4.4 Segmental block (MSE) retaining walls

Mechanically Stabilized Earth walls use dry-stacked precast concrete blocks (Allan Block, Versa-Lok, Keystone, Redi-Rock) with horizontal geogrid reinforcement layers embedded in the compacted backfill. The wall acts as a coherent gravity mass.

**Strengths:**
- Cost-effective for tall walls (10–25 ft)
- No formwork, fast construction
- Tolerates settlement
- Architectural finish options

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires a **wide reinforced zone behind the wall** — typically 0.7H to 1.0H (wall height) of horizontal space for geogrid. On a hillside cut, that means cutting much further back into the slope than a cantilever wall.
- Drainage detailing still critical
- Geogrid placement is hidden work — Special Inspection essential

**Heights without engineering** (manufacturer's tables, no surcharge, level backfill): typically 3–4 ft.
**Engineered** (with geogrid): commonly 20–25 ft, exceptional cases higher.

### 4.5 Soldier pile and lagging walls

Steel H-piles (W8x40 to W14x90 typical residential) installed by driving or drilled-and-grouted into pre-augered holes at 6–10 ft on center, with timber lagging (3x or 4x rough-sawn) or shotcrete panels installed between piles as excavation proceeds.

**Used for:**
- Tight cuts adjacent to existing structures where a cantilever or MSE wall would require too much backfill room
- Deep cuts where a cast-in-place wall would be too slow
- Sites where neighbor's foundation is at risk during excavation

**Tieback anchors** for walls >12–15 ft retained height: high-strength steel strands or bars drilled into stable ground behind the failure wedge, post-tensioned to lock the wall.

**Cost**: $80–$200/SF of wall face (vs. $40–$100/SF for cantilever concrete) — premium for difficult-access and adjacent-structure work.

### 4.6 Sheet pile

Interlocking steel sheets (PZ, AZ profiles) driven vertically. Used for deep cuts in soils with high groundwater where a soldier pile system would leak. Rare in residential except for high-water-table coastal/marina projects. Removal often impractical; sheet piles often left in place.

### 4.7 Rock slope protection

Cut slopes in marginal materials that can't realistically be re-vegetated are commonly protected with:

| System | Description | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Rip-rap / rock blanket** | Large rock (D50 = 12"–24") placed loose over filter fabric | Toe protection at streams, channel armor, light slope protection |
| **Gunite / shotcrete slope** | Sprayed concrete 3"–6" thick, often with mesh | Vertical or near-vertical cut faces in jointed rock |
| **Soil nail wall with shotcrete** | Drilled, grouted nails (#8–#11 bar) at 4–6 ft o.c. with shotcrete facing | Deep cuts where excavation precedes wall, no room for tieback rig |
| **Rockfall netting** | Drape mesh anchored at top, sometimes with energy-dissipating panels | Rock face above building pad |

### 4.8 Drainage behind walls — the universal failure point

Walls don't fail because the concrete is weak. Walls fail because water builds up behind them and the wall must resist 2–3x the design lateral pressure. Hydrostatic pressure on a 10 ft wall adds about 3,125 lb/lf of horizontal force — equivalent to designing for a much taller wall.

**Universal back-of-wall drain detail:**
1. Wall face: dampproofing or waterproofing membrane (asphalt emulsion, fluid-applied)
2. Drainage composite or 12"+ column of clean drain rock (3/4" minus gravel, washed, no fines)
3. Filter fabric (Mirafi 140N or equivalent) between drain rock and backfill
4. 4" perforated pipe at base, holes down, in drain rock
5. Solid pipe outlet to daylight (not sewer, not blind-end)
6. Weep holes at 8 ft o.c. max as backup
7. Impermeable cap at top (12" of clay or pavement) to keep surface water out of backfill

Photograph this detail before backfill. It is the single most-litigated detail on residential retaining walls.

## 5. Access and Logistics on Difficult Sites

### 5.1 Equipment access requirements

Plan access in this order: (1) the biggest piece of equipment you'll bring, (2) concrete delivery, (3) materials delivery, (4) workers.

| Equipment | Min Road Width | Max Grade | Turning Radius | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Concrete truck (10 CY mixer) | 12 ft, 14 ft preferred | 15%, 10% on curves | 40 ft outside | Loaded weight 66,000 lbs; chute reach ~16 ft |
| Concrete pump truck (32 m) | 12 ft, 14 ft preferred | 12% | 35 ft outside | Outriggers extended ~22 ft each side; needs flat pad |
| Concrete pump truck (52 m) | 14 ft | 10% | 45 ft outside | Outriggers ~28 ft each side; serious pad required |
| Excavator (CAT 320, 22-ton) | 10 ft for truck delivery on lowboy; mini-ex fits 6 ft | walks 30% | varies | Track damage to driveways — lay plywood/mats |
| Bobcat / skid steer | 6 ft | 25% | 20 ft outside | The hillside workhorse |
| Mini-excavator (3–5 ton) | 5–6 ft | 35% | small | Tracked, low ground pressure |
| Drill rig (CIDH, truck-mounted) | 12 ft | 8% | 50 ft outside | Heavy, single-rear-axle pivot tough |
| Drill rig (limited access, tracked) | 7 ft | 25% | small | Premium daypower but huge access advantage |
| Crane (rough-terrain 30-ton) | 12 ft road; 25 ft x 25 ft pad | 5% on pad | 40 ft | Outriggers fully extended for picks |
| Lumber/material truck (boom) | 12 ft | 12% | 40 ft | Boom reach typically 30–40 ft |
| Dumpster delivery (40 yd) | 12 ft | 8% | 40 ft | Needs clear overhead |
| Fire apparatus (engine/ladder) | 20 ft (CFC 503) | 15% (often less) | 28 ft inside, 48 ft outside | Code minimum for access; often the binding constraint on driveway design |

**The binding constraint is usually fire access.** A road good enough for fire engines (20 ft wide, 15% max, 75,000 lb capable, turnaround) is good enough for everything else. A road good enough for the concrete truck may not satisfy fire — and fire signs off on the permit.

### 5.2 Crane picks

When the building footprint is below the road, when materials cannot be carried in safely, when prefab elements (panelized walls, trusses, modular floors) must be set — a crane is required.

**Crane planning:**

| Item | Considerations |
|---|---|
| **Crane size** | Determined by reach (radius) and load. Use the manufacturer's load chart: max capacity drops dramatically with radius. A 30-ton crane at 60 ft radius may pick only 3,000 lb. |
| **Pick radius** | Distance from crane center pin to load. Always plan for the worst pick (farthest, heaviest). |
| **Pad** | Crane outriggers need solid, level support — typically 3'x3' steel plates or timber cribbing. On soft ground, engineered crane mats. Outrigger reactions can be 30,000–80,000 lb on a small crane. |
| **Overhead clearance** | Power lines: Cal/OSHA Section 1611 — 10 ft min clearance from 50 kV or less, more for higher voltage. Most residential service is 12 kV — keep 10 ft. Plan the boom swing path. |
| **Lift plan** | Required for critical lifts (>75% of chart, multiple cranes, blind picks). Written, signed, distributed to crew. |
| **Rigging** | Slings, shackles, spreader bars rated for load. Inspect daily. Tag lines mandatory on every pick. |
| **Permits** | Most jurisdictions require an oversize/oversweight load permit for crane mobilization, sometimes a separate crane permit for road closure or staging. |

**Crane day cost**: $2,500–$8,000 per day including operator and oiler for residential rough-terrain or hydraulic; specialty cranes (long-boom, lattice) much higher. Add mobilization in/out ($1,500–$5,000 each way).

### 5.3 Concrete pumping

A pump truck makes hillside concrete possible. Two configurations:

| Configuration | Reach | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Boom pump (truck-mounted)** | 32 m (~105 ft), 42 m, 52 m (~170 ft), 58 m+ | Standard residential hillside; sets up at road, reaches across |
| **Line pump (trailer)** | 200–500+ ft via steel/rubber hose | Extreme reach, around corners; slower; multiple line couplings |

**Mix design for pumping** is different from placement:
- Slump 4"–6" for boom pump (4" typical, 5–6" if long reach)
- Maximum aggregate size limited to 1/3 of smallest line dimension (typically 1" max for 4" line, 3/4" for 3" line)
- Mix must be cohesive — too much sand or fines, it segregates and plugs; too coarse, it plugs the line
- Slump for placement: 5–7" typical on slope, 6–8" for tall pours
- **Get the mix design approved by the pumper before the pour day** — pumpers know what plugs

**Pump considerations:**
- Maximum vertical reach typically 100–150 ft; horizontal much further
- Set up on level ground with outriggers; CANNOT pump on slope
- Cleanup water disposal — never on neighbor's property, never into storm drain (it's hazardous waste pH 12+)
- Pump operator typically charges $200–$400/hr with 4 hr minimum

### 5.4 Material storage and just-in-time delivery

No flat space = no laydown yard. Strategies:

1. **Just-in-time scheduling** — material arrives day of installation. Requires reliable suppliers and tight schedule discipline.
2. **Temporary storage platform** — small lumber lift / framing platform built early, repurposed multiple times during the build.
3. **Off-site staging yard** — rent yard space near site, shuttle daily with a small truck.
4. **Hoisting** — material hoist (CTL boom truck, Spider lift, or fixed scaffolding hoist) for vertical movement on steep sites.
5. **Conveyor** — for bulk material (dirt, gravel, mud-sill nails / mortar), conveyor belt from staging to site.

### 5.5 Worker access and Cal/OSHA on slopes

| Topic | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Walking-working surfaces** | Cal/OSHA Title 8 § 3273 — non-slip, clear of debris; on slopes >2:1, stairs or ramps with handrails required |
| **Fall protection** | 7.5 ft trigger height for construction (Title 8 § 1670); 100% tie-off above this with PFAS (personal fall arrest system) — harness + lanyard or SRL + anchor rated 5,000 lb |
| **Scaffolds anchored to slope** | Title 8 § 1644 — must be designed for the loading, anchored to resist tipping; on slopes >10% baseplate leveling jacks and base plate boards required |
| **Excavation access** | Egress every 25 ft of excavation length; ladders 3 ft above top of excavation; protective systems (shoring/sloping/shielding) for excavations >5 ft |
| **Confined spaces** | Below-grade rooms during construction may be confined spaces — atmospheric testing, retrieval, permit |
| **Ladders** | Type 1A (300 lb capacity) for construction; 4:1 ratio (1 ft out for every 4 ft up); secured top and bottom |
| **Hot work** | Permit, fire watch, extinguishers — particularly important in FHSZ |
| **Heat illness prevention** | Title 8 § 3395 — shade, water, rest, training, acclimatization. Bare hillsides bake in summer. |

A useful rule: if a worker would slip, fall, or roll an ankle just walking the site, you need engineered access. Build temporary stairs, fence drop-offs, route paths around hazards.

### 5.6 Neighbor impacts and pre-construction surveys

The single most underrated risk on hillside projects: **neighbor lawsuits**. Excavation vibration, runoff onto downhill property, dust, noise outside permitted hours, drainage that worsens after construction — these become claims even when not your fault, because the timing correlates with your work.

**Pre-construction protection:**
1. **Photographic condition survey** of every adjacent property's visible features — cracks, settlements, drainage, vegetation. Date-stamped, signed by surveyor, copy to neighbor (offered, not forced).
2. **Vibration monitoring** if pile driving, sheet pile, blasting, or large rock breakers within 100 ft of neighbor structures. Seismograph rental ~$300/wk.
3. **Drainage documentation** before, during, after — note where neighbors' downspouts discharge, where slope drains daylight.
4. **Written notification** to neighbors before start: scope, schedule, hours, contact person, complaints procedure.
5. **Insurance** — confirm builders risk + GL covers off-site property damage; consider Owner Controlled Insurance Program (OCIP) on larger projects.

## 6. Drainage and Waterproofing Below Grade

### 6.1 The hillside drainage principle

Water on a hillside has only one direction: downhill, toward your foundation. Your job is to intercept it before it reaches the foundation, collect what does reach it, and discharge it to a positive outlet.

A complete hillside drainage system has FOUR independent layers, each one a backup for the others:

1. **Surface drainage** — grading, swales, brow ditches above the structure, gutters and downspouts off the structure
2. **Subsurface drainage above the structure** — interceptor drains at the cut-back, French drains, slope drains
3. **Foundation perimeter drainage** — footing drain at the base of all below-grade walls
4. **Below-slab drainage** — perforated pipe under slab in gravel layer, daylighted

Skip any one of these and you transfer risk to the others. Skip two and you have water in the basement.

### 6.2 Foundation drainage detail

The "footing drain" — at the base of every below-grade wall, exterior side:

```
                  Backfill
                  (free-draining when possible)
                     |
   Wall face --->    |     Filter fabric envelope
                     |     wraps drain rock column
   Waterproofing     |     to prevent fines clogging
   membrane          |
                     |
                   /====\
                   ||  ||  <-- 12-18" tall column of
                   ||  ||      clean drain rock (3/4")
                   ||O ||  <-- 4" perforated pipe,
                   ||  ||      holes DOWN, on top of
                  -------     2" rock bed
   Wall footing -----------
                   (slope min 1% to daylight)
```

**Pipe slope:** Minimum 0.5%, 1% recommended. The pipe MUST slope continuously to outlet — flat or back-pitched sections collect water and freeze/plug.

**Outlet:** ALWAYS daylight, never sewer. Discharge to:
- Curb cut at street (with city approval)
- Slope drain to natural watercourse (with regulatory approval if a Waters of the U.S.)
- Dry well / dispersion bed (only when soil percolation tested and approved)
- Sump and pump (if no gravity outlet available)

### 6.3 Below-grade waterproofing

**Dampproofing vs. waterproofing:**
- **Dampproofing** (asphaltic emulsion, asphalt parging) — resists moisture vapor and damp soil. NOT for hydrostatic conditions.
- **Waterproofing** — resists liquid water under pressure. Required wherever groundwater contact is possible.

**Waterproofing systems (positive-side, exterior application):**

| System | Description | Pros | Cons | $/SF residential |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **HDPE sheet** (Delta-MS, Platon) | Dimpled membrane mechanically attached; acts as drainage layer also | Tough, fast install, drainage built in | Mechanical fasteners can leak; seams need attention | $2–4 |
| **Self-adhered sheet** (Bituthene, CCW MiraDRI) | Rubberized asphalt sheet with poly carrier, peel-and-stick | Proven, continuous bond, well-known | Cold weather install issues; punctures at backfill | $3–6 |
| **Fluid-applied** (Tremco TremProof, Carlisle CCW-525, polyurethane) | Trowel/spray/roll on; cures to monolithic membrane | Conforms to complex geometry, no seams | Application thickness control critical; UV degradation if left exposed | $4–8 |
| **Bentonite** (Volclay, CETCO Voltex) | Sodium bentonite panels; swells in water to self-seal | Self-healing minor punctures, environmentally friendly | Requires confinement (cannot work without overburden); $$$ | $5–9 |
| **Crystalline integral** (Xypex, Kryton) | Admixture or surface-applied chemicals that grow crystals in concrete | No membrane to puncture | Cracking still bypasses; cracks must be detailed separately | $1–3 (admixture) + repair |

**Drainage board over membrane** is standard practice: dimpled HDPE panel (Delta-Drain, Carlisle CCW-MD, Mirafi G100N) protects the membrane during backfill AND provides a drainage plane that conducts water down to the perimeter drain.

**Inspection before backfill** is mandatory. Photograph every wall, every penetration, every termination. Once backfilled, defects are invisible and very expensive to repair.

### 6.4 Sump pumps

When the footing drain cannot reach daylight (basement below adjacent road, level lot, very low site), a sump pump is the next option.

**Design:**
- **Sump pit**: 18"–24" diameter, 24"–36" deep precast or plastic basin set below slab elevation
- **Pump**: submersible centrifugal, 1/3 HP to 3/4 HP residential, capacity 30–80 GPM
- **Backup pump**: a second pump in the same pit, on a higher float — runs if primary fails. Or battery backup pump.
- **Battery backup**: 12V deep-cycle battery + DC pump; runs for 4–8 hours of intermittent pumping during power outage. Critical in storm scenarios where power and water arrive together.
- **Alarm**: high-water alarm (audible + ideally cellular/WiFi) — alerts owner before water reaches finished floor
- **Check valve** on discharge line — prevents backflow into pit
- **Discharge**: solid pipe to a positive outlet, again NEVER to sewer (NPDES violation, sewer overload). Daylight or storm drain (with permit).

**Maintenance reminder** for owner: pumps fail. Test annually. Service every 5–7 yr. Replace at 10–12 yr.

### 6.5 Window wells and below-grade egress windows

CRC R310 requires emergency escape and rescue openings (EERO) in every sleeping room. Below-grade bedrooms need window wells.

| Requirement | Code (CRC R310) |
|---|---|
| Net clear opening | 5.7 sq ft (5.0 sq ft for grade-floor windows) |
| Minimum opening height | 24 in |
| Minimum opening width | 20 in |
| Maximum sill height | 44 in above floor |
| Window well horizontal projection | 36 in min from wall |
| Window well width | Allow window to fully open |
| Window well area | 9 sq ft min, projection 36 in min |
| Window well depth >44 in | Ladder or steps required, 12 in proj max, not encroaching more than 6 in into required well dimensions |
| Cover | Permitted if operable from inside without keys/tools, and supports 40 PSF live load |
| Drainage | Window well must drain — connect to footing drain or have separate drain |

A window well in clay soil without drainage becomes a bathtub in the first big rain. Always tie window well drain to the perimeter foundation drain.

## 7. Seismic Considerations on Hillsides

### 7.1 Why hillside houses are seismically harder

Three issues stack:

1. **Split-level geometry creates weak/soft stories.** The uphill side of a split-level has full-height walls into soil/foundation; the downhill side often has tall stilts, garage openings, view glass. Stiffness is concentrated uphill, mass is concentrated centered, and lateral force concentrates at the soft downhill face. Result: rotation, then collapse, in a major earthquake.

2. **Long, narrow plans on hillside lots.** Many hillside houses are long perpendicular to the slope, with garage at one end and bedrooms at the other. The plan aspect ratio creates diaphragm flexibility issues and torsion under seismic load.

3. **Foundation on slope.** Posts on tall piers act as inverted pendulums. Even if individually braced, the global P-delta and overturning at the downhill end is severe.

### 7.2 Hillside ordinances

| Jurisdiction | Hillside Provisions |
|---|---|
| **LA City** | LAMC 91.1613 (Hillside Design), 91.7006 (Methane Zones), LADBS Information Bulletin P/BC 2020-049 (Hillside Construction) — defines Hillside Area, requires special studies, restricts grading, mandates engineered foundations and pad certification |
| **LA County** | County Building Code Chapter 18 Hillside Management Area provisions |
| **San Francisco** | Slope Protection Act (SPAS) — slopes >20% trigger geotechnical review |
| **Berkeley, Oakland** | Hillside Overlay Districts with grading limits, view ordinances, fire access |
| **Bay Area cities (Tiburon, Mill Valley, Belvedere, Sausalito)** | Significant slope/view/landslide ordinances |
| **Santa Monica Mountains** | Local Coastal Programs with severe slope/ESHA limits |

LADBS Information Bulletin P/BC 2020-049 is the closest thing to a statewide "hillside textbook" and is worth reading even outside LA.

### 7.3 Soft-story / weak-story on hillsides

**Soft story:** A story with lateral stiffness <70% of the story above (or <80% of the average of the three above).

**Weak story:** A story with lateral strength <80% of the story above.

Hillside houses commonly have both: a tall, open downhill ground level (carport/garage/view) above which sit full-walled upper stories. Per ASCE 7, when soft/weak story irregularities exist, design force amplification (Section 12.3.3) and additional design provisions apply. **The economics nearly always justify designing the irregularity out**, by adding steel moment frames or stiffened narrow shear walls at the open downhill face.

**New construction options:**
- Steel moment frames (Simpson Strong-Frame, MiTek SidePlate, custom) at openings
- Narrow proprietary shear walls (Simpson Strong-Wall, Hardy Frame)
- Concrete walls at downhill face (when concrete substructure is already present)
- Cross-bracing in light-frame walls

**Retrofit of existing hillside houses:** Each city's voluntary or mandatory retrofit standard differs. LA's "Hillside Bracing" guidelines are a useful reference.

### 7.4 Alquist-Priolo and CEQA

The Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zoning Act (1972) prohibits new occupied structures across the trace of an active fault. CGS publishes the maps. Within an A-P zone:

- A fault investigation is required for any new occupied structure
- Investigation includes detailed mapping, trenching, age-dating of disrupted layers
- A 50 ft setback from the located trace is the typical minimum
- The Engineering Geologist signs the report

Outside A-P zones but in **Seismic Hazard Zones** (liquefaction or earthquake-induced landslide), a different study under the Seismic Hazards Mapping Act applies — typically conducted as part of the geotechnical report.

**CEQA** triggers when an entitlement is required (subdivision, design review, conditional use permit, variance) — environmental review must address seismic hazards, often via a Mitigated Negative Declaration. CEQA can add 6–18 months to schedule.

### 7.5 Liquefaction on hillsides

Liquefaction requires (1) saturated soil, (2) loose granular soil, (3) strong ground shaking. Hillside sites typically have low water tables and don't liquefy. **Exceptions:**
- Sites at the toe of slopes near streams/wetlands
- Sites with shallow perched groundwater in fill
- Sites in former drainages, swales, infilled gulches

If liquefaction is identified in the geotech report, mitigation can be drilled piers below the liquefiable zone, ground improvement (stone columns, deep dynamic compaction), or — usually — relocate the building.

## 8. Difficult Access Construction Methods

### 8.1 Panelized framing

Walls pre-built off-site as panels (typically 8–24 ft long), trucked to site, craned into place. Reduces on-site framing labor by 40–60%. Particularly valuable on hillside sites because:

- Eliminates on-site material storage (panels arrive on truck)
- Reduces fall exposure (panels stood up, no working at heights piece-by-piece)
- Faster (panels go up in days, not weeks)
- Quality controlled in shop conditions

**Considerations:**
- Site must accept truck and crane (planning constraint moves earlier in the schedule)
- Tolerance for panel size from shop must align with site-built foundation tolerances — coordinate carefully
- Crane day is expensive: every panel must be ready and labeled

### 8.2 Modular and offsite construction

Full volumetric modules (90% complete in factory) trucked to site and craned onto foundation. Two California vendors of note: Plant Prefab (Rialto), Connect Homes (Los Angeles). Pricing typically $300–600/SF turnkey for the module portion.

**Difficult-site benefits:**
- Minimal on-site work — perfect for steep, remote, or sensitive sites
- Schedule compression: foundation work and module build happen in parallel

**Difficult-site challenges:**
- Module dimensions (often 14–16 ft wide x 60–70 ft long x 12 ft tall) require road geometry that hillside roads often don't have
- Crane size escalates: a 4-module house may need a 200-ton crane and a custom transport route
- "Last mile" of access — backup truck up a switchback driveway — is the binding constraint

### 8.3 Helicopter lifts

Used for extreme terrain — remote mountain cabins, ridgetop sites with no road. Heavy-lift helicopters (Erickson Sky Crane, Columbia Helicopters CH-47, Sikorsky S-64) can lift 10,000–25,000 lb.

**Planning:**
- FAA Part 133 external load operations — certified pilot, certified rigger
- Lift plan: load weights, attachment points, swing path, drop zones, abort plan
- Ground crew with radio, fire suppression, spotter
- Weather windows: helicopters fly in <25 kt wind, good visibility, no precip
- Cost: $5,000–$25,000 per flight hour (5–30 lifts/hr depending on cycle time)

Helicopter is a last resort. If a road can be built, build it. If not, helicopter is the only path and the project's cost premium is enormous.

### 8.4 Phased excavation

When a single deep excavation would be too unstable, too large for equipment, or too disruptive, phase the cut:

1. Excavate Phase 1 (one corner or one side)
2. Install walls/piers in Phase 1, backfill where stable
3. Excavate Phase 2, working off the completed Phase 1
4. Continue until complete

Particularly common in hillside basement work adjacent to neighbors — the unexcavated soil buttresses the standing slope on neighbor's side while you work on yours.

### 8.5 Temporary shoring and underpinning

When excavating below the foundation level of an adjacent existing structure, support must be provided to prevent that structure's foundation from sliding into your excavation. Failure to provide adequate support is the #1 hillside lawsuit.

**Shoring options:**

| Method | Description | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Sloped excavation** | Cut back at safe angle, no structure | Open sites, no adjacency |
| **Soldier pile + lagging** | Steel H-piles + timber/shotcrete lagging | Most common urban hillside |
| **Soldier pile + tiebacks** | Same + post-tensioned anchors into stable ground | Tall walls, no internal bracing room |
| **Soil nail + shotcrete** | Drilled grouted nails + shotcrete face, top-down | When tieback rig won't fit |
| **Secant or tangent piles** | Overlapping or touching CIDH piles, often steel-reinforced | Deep + groundwater |
| **Underpinning (pit method)** | Sequential excavation under existing footing in 3-4 ft sections, dry-pack concrete each pit | Restoring support to settled structure or extending existing foundation deeper |
| **Helical pier underpinning** | Driven steel piers and brackets transferring load below existing footing | Settled foundation repair, light additions |
| **Micropile underpinning** | 6"–8" small-diameter drilled, grouted, reinforced piles | Heavy loads, limited access |

**Critical workflow rule:** never excavate adjacent to an existing foundation without:
1. A signed shoring/underpinning plan stamped by the engineer of record
2. Continuous Special Inspection during the work
3. Vibration and settlement monitoring (survey points on the neighbor's structure)
4. Written documentation of pre-existing conditions

## 9. Permitting and Regulatory Issues

### 9.1 Grading permits

Most California jurisdictions require a separate **grading permit** beyond the building permit when:

| Trigger | Typical threshold |
|---|---|
| Volume of cut or fill | >50 CY, sometimes >150 CY |
| Depth of cut or fill | >1 ft, sometimes >2 ft |
| Slope created | Any new manufactured slope steeper than 5:1 |
| Disturbed area | >1,000 SF typical, varies |
| Any work in hillside overlay | Often: any grading at all |
| Any work in geologic hazard area | Often: any grading at all |

The grading permit application typically requires: grading plan signed by a civil engineer, geotechnical report, erosion control plan, and (in hillside areas) slope stability analysis.

### 9.2 Geological review

In hillside areas and seismic hazard zones, the building department's geologic review is a separate gate. Documents required:

- **Engineering Geology Report** by a Certified Engineering Geologist (CEG)
- **Geotechnical Engineering Report** by a Geotechnical Engineer (GE) or Civil Engineer with geotechnical experience
- **Slope stability analysis** with FS calculations
- **Site-specific seismic hazard analysis** (often)
- **Cross-sections** showing subsurface conditions and proposed work
- **Special Inspection / Continuous Observation plan** during construction

City geologists typically take 4–12 weeks for first plan check. Comments are detailed and require formal response. Budget 2–4 plan check cycles.

### 9.3 California Coastal Commission

Within the **Coastal Zone** (varies, typically 1,000 yards to 5 miles inland, mapped), all development requires a **Coastal Development Permit (CDP)** from the Coastal Commission or a city/county with a certified Local Coastal Program (LCP).

| Issue | Implications |
|---|---|
| **Bluff setback** | Often 100 ft from bluff edge or 75-year erosion line; sometimes "geologic life of structure" basis (often 100 yr) |
| **ESHA** | Environmentally Sensitive Habitat Area — no development; buffers (50–300 ft) around ESHA |
| **Public access** | Lateral access along coast; vertical access from road to beach; cannot be obstructed |
| **View impacts** | Designated viewsheds protected; building height limited; landscape screening required |
| **Shoreline armoring** | Seawalls, riprap: severely restricted; new armoring nearly impossible since 2018 sea-level rise policies |
| **Septic / OWTS** | Restricted near ESHA, waterways; advanced treatment often required |

CDPs in appealable areas can be appealed to the Coastal Commission within 10 working days of local approval — adding 4–6 months of risk to schedule.

### 9.4 FEMA floodplain development

If the site is in a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA — Zones A, AE, V, VE):

| Zone | Description | Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| **A, AE** | 1% annual chance flood (100-yr floodplain), inland flooding | Lowest floor at or above Base Flood Elevation (BFE) + freeboard (1–3 ft per jurisdiction); residential basements typically prohibited |
| **V, VE** | 1% annual chance flood with wave action (coastal) | Lowest horizontal structural member at or above BFE + freeboard; piling/column foundations required; breakaway walls below BFE; no fill |
| **X (shaded)** | 500-yr floodplain | No FEMA requirements, but lender may require flood insurance |
| **X (unshaded)** | Outside floodplain | No requirements |

A Floodplain Development Permit (from the local floodplain administrator) is required for any work in A, AE, V, VE. Elevation certificate required at completion. Fill in floodways is generally prohibited.

### 9.5 Tree protection

Most California jurisdictions have heritage / protected tree ordinances. Common protections:

| Species (varies by jurisdiction) | Threshold | Protection |
|---|---|---|
| Native oak | 6"–10" DBH (diameter at breast height) | Permit to remove; protection zone during construction |
| Redwood | Often all | Permit + arborist report |
| Heritage / specimen trees | Listed by city | Permit + may require Council approval |
| Public street trees | All | City Forestry permit + replacement |

**Tree Protection Zone (TPZ):** Typically the dripline (canopy radius) plus 5 ft, or 1 ft of radius per inch of DBH. Within TPZ:
- No grading, no compaction, no storage
- Fence with construction fence 6 ft tall, signage
- Arborist on call for any required work within TPZ
- Hand-dig only within TPZ

Removal of a protected tree without permit: fines from $5,000 to $50,000+ per tree, plus mandatory replanting (often 5:1 or 10:1).

### 9.6 Fire access

California Fire Code (CFC, Title 24 Part 9) Chapter 5, plus CAL FIRE regulations for State Responsibility Areas:

| Item | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Access road width** | 20 ft clear (CFC 503.2.1); sometimes 26 ft if buildings >30 ft tall |
| **Vertical clearance** | 13'6" min |
| **Gradient** | 12% max (some jurisdictions 15%); ascending grades up to 20% allowed for short distances if surface is approved |
| **Surface** | All-weather, designed for 75,000 lb fire apparatus |
| **Turnaround** | Dead-ends >150 ft require approved turnaround (hammerhead, cul-de-sac 96 ft diameter, or other) |
| **Bridges/culverts** | Engineered for 75,000 lb |
| **Gates** | Knox box; clear opening 20 ft; auto-open on power loss or fire signal |
| **Defensible space (WUI)** | Zone 1 (0–5 ft, ember-resistant); Zone 2 (5–30 ft, lean and clean); Zone 3 (30–100 ft, reduced fuel) per CAL FIRE PRC 4291 |
| **Fire flow** | 1,000–2,000 GPM at 20 psi residual, varies by building size |

Many hillside driveways do not naturally meet these. Solutions include:
- Driveway redesign with switchbacks and lower grade
- Fire turnouts every 400 ft on one-lane roads
- Approved alternates (sprinkler upgrade in lieu of access)
- Separate fire apparatus access road if main driveway is too steep

## 10. Pricing Difficult Sites

### 10.1 Premium cost categories

Each item below is typically priced AS AN ADDER over a flat-site baseline, not absorbed into general overhead.

| Category | Typical Premium Range (% of flat-site cost) | Pricing notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Geotechnical investigation** | $8K–$50K (often owner-direct) | Make clear in contract whether GC or owner pays |
| **Special foundation** (pier + grade beam vs. spread footing) | 2–6x foundation cost | Drilled pier residential: $250–700/LF of pier + cage + concrete; grade beam: $200–400/LF |
| **Retaining walls** | $80–$300/SF wall face | Concrete cantilever ~$60–120/SF; CMU ~$60–100/SF; segmental block ~$50–80/SF; soldier pile ~$120–250/SF; soil nail ~$150–300/SF |
| **Drainage system** | $15K–$80K residential | Foundation drain + waterproofing + interceptors + outlets |
| **Earthwork import** | $50–120/CY delivered + placed + compacted | Includes engineered fill, testing, compaction |
| **Earthwork export** | $40–100/CY excavated + hauled + tipped | Includes Class III disposal fees |
| **Concrete pump** | $1,500–$4,000/day + $20–40/CY | Required virtually always on hillside |
| **Crane** | $2,500–$8,000/day + mob $1,500–$5,000 each way | One day per major lift event |
| **Equipment mobilization premium** | $5K–$30K | Tracked equipment, limited-access drilling rig, etc. |
| **Productivity impact** | 1.2x–1.8x labor hours per task | Working on slope, hauling material to point of use, fall protection time |
| **Erosion control / SWPPP** | $0.50–$3.00/SF disturbed | BMPs + monitoring + winter rework |
| **Dust control** | $3K–$15K | Water truck, soil binder |
| **Temporary facilities** | $10K–$40K | Construction power on hillside, generators, water, restrooms, security |
| **Special Inspection** | $15K–$80K | Pier observation, soils compaction testing, masonry, concrete sampling, weld inspection |
| **Pre-construction surveys** | $2K–$10K | Neighbor condition surveys, vibration monitoring baseline |
| **Schedule extension** | varies | Hillside work runs 1.3x–2.0x flat-site duration; G&A and overhead scale with time |

### 10.2 Contingency

Contingency on flat-site residential is typically 3–5%. **On difficult sites, plan for 15–25% contingency**, and explain why to the owner before contract:

1. **Subsurface uncertainty** — the geotech is a sample; reality is between borings. Differing site conditions are normal, not exceptional.
2. **Weather sensitivity** — work disrupted by rain costs schedule, rework, BMP repair.
3. **Regulatory variability** — plan check comments add scope; geologic review can require additional studies, design changes.
4. **Access / logistics changes** — neighbor complaints, road closures, supply chain disruptions amplified by single-point-of-failure access.
5. **Discovered conditions** — buried debris, prior slide debris, abandoned utilities, archaeological resources.

**How to explain to owners:** "On flat-site construction, we know within a few percent what something will cost because the variables are limited. On hillside construction, we know within 20% because the ground, the weather, the regulators, and the schedule all interact. The contingency isn't padding — it's our shared insurance against the variables. Unused contingency reverts to you at closeout."

### 10.3 Unit price contracts for earthwork

**Lump sum earthwork on a difficult site is a recipe for litigation.** Once you start digging, conditions diverge from assumptions. With lump sum, every divergence becomes a claim. With unit price, divergence is priced into the contract.

**Recommended structure:**

| Line item | Unit | Unit price |
|---|---|---|
| Mobilization | LS | $ |
| Stripping topsoil | CY | $ |
| Cut to fill | CY | $ |
| Cut to export | CY | $ |
| Engineered fill import + placement | CY | $ |
| Rock excavation (>0.5 CY rock) | CY | $ (premium) |
| Hardpan / unanticipated excavation | CY | $ (premium) |
| Dewatering | day or LF of trench | $ |
| Compaction testing | per test | $ (or owner-direct) |

A "**changed conditions clause**" — typically based on AIA A201 §3.7.4 or ConsensusDocs equivalent — entitles the GC to additional compensation when conditions encountered differ materially from those indicated in the contract documents.

### 10.4 Differing site conditions and RFIs

On a difficult site, **document everything**. When the conditions encountered differ from those documented in the geotechnical report or shown on plans, you have a potential claim:

| Type | Definition (per FAR 52.236-2, used as model) |
|---|---|
| **Type I** | Subsurface or latent physical conditions differing materially from those indicated in the contract documents |
| **Type II** | Unknown physical conditions of an unusual nature, differing materially from those ordinarily encountered |

**Documentation protocol when you hit a changed condition:**
1. STOP the affected work
2. Photograph the condition with date stamp
3. Notify owner and architect/engineer in writing within 24 hr (or per contract — typically 7–14 days but document immediately regardless)
4. Request inspection by geotechnical engineer
5. Issue an RFI describing the condition and requesting direction
6. Track time/labor/equipment cost from the moment of discovery
7. Submit Change Order request with documentation

**Common changed-condition triggers on hillside sites:**
- Bedrock encountered shallower than predicted (good — pier shorter, but unit price for rock excavation if it must be removed)
- Bedrock encountered deeper than predicted (bad — pier longer, more concrete, more time)
- Groundwater encountered when geotech said dry
- Soft layer / debris / fill encountered in supposedly native ground
- Existing utilities or structures not on as-builts
- Slide debris, abandoned infrastructure, contamination
- Archaeological / paleontological / cultural resources (CA AB 52, NAGPRA — may halt work for days/weeks)

### 10.5 Schedule pricing reality

Hillside residential typically runs 18–30 months for a custom home that would take 12–18 months on flat ground. Drivers:

- Geotechnical investigation and design: 2–6 mo before construction
- Permit + plan check (hillside): 4–9 mo (vs. 2–4 mo flat-site)
- Foundation: 4–10 wk (vs. 2–3 wk flat)
- Earthwork: 2–8 wk (vs. 1–2 wk flat)
- Retaining walls: 4–12 wk (often parallel with foundation but sometimes critical path)
- Framing-finish: similar to flat-site, but with productivity impact from access

Carry GC general conditions and overhead at the longer duration. A common pricing mistake is to estimate G&A at flat-site duration and then "absorb" the extra months.

## Appendix A — Critical Inspection Hold Points

These are the moments when work MUST stop for inspection — by building department, geotechnical engineer, special inspector, or all three. Missing any of them creates uncertainty about the condition of buried work and is the most common source of certificate-of-occupancy delays.

| # | Hold Point | Inspector | Documentation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pre-grading meeting and site walk | Geotech + civil + Building Dept | Site readiness report |
| 2 | Stripping complete, before fill | Geotech | Subgrade approval, photos |
| 3 | Keyway excavation complete, before fill | Geotech | Bedrock contact verified, photos |
| 4 | Subdrain installation, before backfill | Geotech | Pipe slope, outlet, gravel coverage |
| 5 | Each compaction lift (during fill) | Geotech | Density test reports |
| 6 | Pad subgrade complete, before foundation | Geotech + Building Dept | Pad certification letter |
| 7 | Pier hole bottom (every pier) | Geotech / deputy inspector | Pier log |
| 8 | Pier reinforcement, before pour | Building Dept + Special Inspector | Photos, cage inspection |
| 9 | Footing / grade beam reinforcement | Building Dept | Photos |
| 10 | Retaining wall steel before grout/pour | Building Dept + Special Inspector | Photos |
| 11 | Damp/waterproofing complete, before backfill | Building Dept | Photos of entire wall surface, penetrations |
| 12 | Foundation drain installed, before backfill | Building Dept + Geotech | Pipe slope, outlet verified, photos |
| 13 | Underslab gravel + pipe, before slab pour | Building Dept | Photos, compaction reports |
| 14 | Shear wall nailing (framing) | Building Dept | After lath/before drywall (lath at exterior, drywall at interior) |
| 15 | Hold-down installation | Building Dept + Special Inspector if engineered | Embed, anchor bolt torque |
| 16 | Final site drainage operational | Civil + Building Dept | All outlets, swales, downspouts tied in |

## Appendix B — Document Set on a Hillside Job

Every hillside job should have a controlled set of documents on site, accessible to the field:

1. **Geotechnical report** (full, with logs and plates) — paper copy at site, digital with PM
2. **Civil drawings** — site, grading, drainage, erosion control
3. **Architectural plans + structural drawings + structural calcs**
4. **Hillside permit set** including plan check correction stamps
5. **Site survey** (existing conditions, boundary, topography)
6. **Soils compaction test reports** — collected during fill, indexed by date and elevation

8. **Special Inspection reports** — daily, indexed by item
9. **SWPPP / erosion control plan** — with weekly inspection logs
10. **Pre-construction condition surveys** — neighbor properties, with photographs
11. **Vibration monitoring data** — if applicable
12. **RFI log** — open, closed, pending
13. **Change Order log** — including differing site condition claims
14. **Crane lift plans + helicopter lift plans** — if applicable
15. **Emergency action plan** including landslide response and evacuation

## Final Word

The hillside builder who survives long-term has internalized three habits:

1. **Bid the site you have, not the site you wish you had.** Walk it twice, read the geotech end-to-end, price the premiums, and carry contingency. If the owner won't accept the price the site requires, walk away — you cannot value-engineer a landslide.

2. **Water is the enemy.** Drainage is the single highest-leverage system on the project. Spend on it generously. Inspect it before backfill. Photograph everything.

3. **Document like you'll be in court.** On hillside work, you might be — but more importantly, the discipline of documentation protects you, the owner, the neighbors, and the engineer-of-record. The best risk management on a hillside project is a complete file.
