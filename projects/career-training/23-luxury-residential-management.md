# High-End and Luxury Residential Project Management

> A practical operations manual for the residential GC moving from standard construction (single-family homes, ADUs, mid-range remodels at $300K–$800K) into the luxury market ($1M–$10M+ construction budgets). Luxury residential is not just "expensive construction" — it is a different business, with different clients, different vendors, different schedules, different contracts, and different failure modes. The goal of this document is to prepare you to operate at the luxury level without getting burned by what you don't know you don't know.

## 1. What Makes Luxury Residential Different

The biggest mistake a successful standard-residential GC makes when moving into luxury is assuming it is the same job at a higher price point. It is not. The economics, the client expectations, the trade ecosystem, the schedule logic, and the failure modes are fundamentally different. Understanding these differences before you sign your first $3M contract is the single most valuable thing you can do.

### 1.1 The Client Profile

Luxury clients are not the same as the move-up buyer in a $700K renovation. The dominant client archetypes you will encounter:

| Archetype | Description | What They Want From You |
|---|---|---|
| **The Self-Made Entrepreneur** | Tech founder, business owner, hedge fund principal. Time-poor, decisive, demands quality and certainty. Tends to be direct. | Speed, certainty, transparency. Will pay a premium for predictability. |
| **The Inherited Wealth Client** | Old money. May be polite but reserved; uses an estate manager or family office as the day-to-day point of contact. | Discretion, deference, understated quality. Loyalty to vendors who handle them well. |
| **The Celebrity / Athlete** | Public figure with privacy concerns. Often a manager or business manager makes the decisions. Schedule pressure (touring, season schedules). | Confidentiality (often NDAs), security awareness, accommodation of erratic availability. |
| **The Design-Forward Couple** | Often two professional spouses, deeply involved in design, may have hired a name-brand architect or designer. Frequent revisions. | Collaboration, patience with iteration, sophistication on detail. |
| **The Foreign Buyer** | International client; the house may be a secondary residence; deals through a U.S.-based representative. | English-language documentation, time-zone responsiveness, willingness to handle long absences. |
| **The Developer-Client** | Building a spec luxury home for resale. Different priorities — ROI per square foot, market appeal, certainty of delivery. | Schedule certainty, value engineering without obvious compromise, market-appropriate finishes. |

**Key behavioral patterns common across luxury clients:**

- **They have opinions and they are not shy.** They have looked at design magazines, traveled to fine hotels, lived in beautiful homes. Their taste is real and their feedback is specific.
- **They are surrounded by professionals.** You will rarely deal only with the principal. Expect to interface with an estate manager, a family-office CFO, a personal attorney, an interior designer, an architect, a landscape designer, a kitchen designer, a lighting designer, an A/V integrator, and sometimes a separate "owner's representative." Each has their own agenda and their own opinions about you.
- **They expect access.** Luxury clients want the principal of the GC firm available — not just the foreman. They will text you on weekends. They expect a response within hours, not days.
- **They are intolerant of unprofessionalism.** A trash-strewn site, a Porta-Potty visible from the street, a sub smoking on the curb, a junior PM who can't answer a question — these things end relationships.
- **They talk to each other.** Your reputation in a wealthy neighborhood or social circle is a single chain of word-of-mouth. One great project produces five referrals; one disaster blacklists you across an entire ZIP code.

### 1.2 Budget Is Not the Constraint — But Overruns Still Damage the Relationship

This is the single most counterintuitive thing about luxury work. The client has the money. They will pay for what they want. **But that does not mean you can be sloppy about cost.**

What luxury clients actually optimize for:

1. **Quality** — non-negotiable. This is why they hired you.
2. **Certainty** — they want to know what they are getting, when, and for how much.
3. **Speed** — they want their house. Within reason, they will pay for time.
4. **Experience** — the process itself must be pleasant. They have other things to do than babysit you.
5. **Price** — last. But still real.

The trap is concluding "budget doesn't matter." It does. A 30% overrun on a $4M project is $1.2M, and even a client who can write that check will resent it. The damage is not financial; it is to trust. They will not refer you. They may not pay your final invoice without a fight. They will tell their friends you "weren't honest about the numbers."

**The discipline:** Luxury budgets are not constraints to optimize against — they are commitments you make and must defend. Bring overrun risks up early, in writing, with options. Never surprise.

### 1.3 Quality Standards Are Tighter, Period

Luxury tolerances are not industry tolerances. The standard residential "looks good from six feet" rule does not apply. Specific examples:

| Trade | Standard Residential | Luxury Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Drywall finish | Level 4 | Level 5 throughout (smooth) |
| Tile lippage | 1/16"–1/8" acceptable | 1/32" target, 1/16" maximum on rectified tile |
| Paint finish | One coat primer + 2 finish | Skim coat + Level 5 + 2 primer + 3 finish on enamel work |
| Millwork miters | 1/16" gap acceptable | 1/32" maximum on visible joints; many GCs target zero |
| Floor flatness (FF) | FF 20–25 | FF 35+ for hardwood, FF 50+ for polished stone |
| Reveal consistency | "Close" | Within 1/32" across a run |
| Wood grain matching | Not required | Sequence-matched, book-matched, slip-matched as specified |
| Stone vein matching | Not required | Book-matched, vein-flow continued across slab transitions |
| Hardware alignment | Within 1/8" | Aligned to within 1/32" across a run of doors or drawers |

"Close enough" does not exist on a luxury job. If a tile is off, it comes out. If a paint finish shows roller marks under raking light, it gets redone. The cost of rework is built into the fee. Plan for it.

### 1.4 The Project Team Is Larger and More Complex

A standard $600K remodel might involve a homeowner, an architect, a structural engineer, and you. A $5M luxury project routinely involves:

- Owner (and frequently their spouse, each with separate opinions)
- Owner's representative or estate manager
- Architect (often two: design architect + architect of record)
- Interior designer (sometimes with a separate procurement specialist)
- Landscape architect
- Kitchen designer (separate from interior designer on many projects)
- Lighting designer
- A/V and smart home integrator
- Acoustic consultant (home theater, music rooms)
- Pool and spa consultant
- Security consultant
- Structural engineer
- MEP engineers (often three: M, E, P separately)
- Civil/geotechnical engineer
- Energy/Title 24 consultant
- LEED or green building consultant
- Code consultant (in difficult jurisdictions)
- Owner's attorney
- Owner's accountant or family-office CFO
- The GC and team

You coordinate all of them. The GC is the operational center of the project; nobody else has the daily visibility into how decisions hit the field. Your value to the owner is, in large part, that you make the rest of this team function.

### 1.5 Long Lead Times Dominate the Schedule

On a standard project, the schedule is driven by trade sequencing. On a luxury project, the schedule is driven by **what has been ordered and when it arrives.** Typical luxury lead times (assume 2026 baseline; ranges widened post-pandemic and have not fully normalized):

| Item | Typical Lead Time | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Custom cabinetry (high-end shop) | 16–24 weeks | From shop drawing approval to delivery |
| Custom interior doors | 14–20 weeks | Stile-and-rail, paint-grade or veneered |
| Custom windows (Quantum, Bonelli, Loewen, Marvin Ultimate) | 12–20 weeks | Steel windows (Crittall, Optimum) 20–32 weeks |
| Imported decorative tile (Italy/Spain/Morocco/Japan) | 12–20 weeks | Plus container shipping; add 4–6 weeks for ground transit |
| Domestic decorative tile (Ann Sacks, Walker Zanger, Heath) | 6–12 weeks | Custom colors longer |
| Stone slabs (selection to fabrication) | 4–8 weeks | Selection at slab yard, plus 3–5 weeks fabrication |
| Imported plumbing fixtures (Vola, Dornbracht, THG) | 12–24 weeks | Custom finishes longer |
| Domestic plumbing fixtures (Waterworks, Kallista, Watermark) | 8–16 weeks | Special finishes 14–20 weeks |
| Sub-Zero / Wolf appliances | 8–16 weeks | Built-in refrigeration runs longer |
| Miele appliances | 6–12 weeks | |
| La Cornue range | 24–36 weeks | Custom colors and configurations |
| Lacanche range | 16–24 weeks | |
| Decorative hardware (Nanz, E.R. Butler, Sun Valley Bronze) | 12–20 weeks | Custom patinas and finishes |
| Lighting fixtures (Apparatus, Roll & Hill, Allied Maker) | 12–24 weeks | Many made-to-order |
| Lutron HomeWorks system | 8–16 weeks | Includes design and programming time |
| Crestron whole-home AV | 12–20 weeks | Rack equipment and shading lead times longer |
| Motorized shades (Lutron Sivoia QS Wireless or wired) | 8–14 weeks | Larger fabric counts longer |
| Steel doors and railings (custom fabrication) | 16–28 weeks | |
| Reclaimed wood flooring | 12–20 weeks | Curing time after delivery |
| Specialty plaster (Venetian, tadelakt, Roman clay) | Trade lead time 8–16 weeks | Booking the artisan, not the material |

**Implication for scheduling:** Decisions on long-lead items must be made in pre-construction or very early in construction. The traditional residential pattern of "decide as we go" does not work. If the client has not selected the kitchen tile by month 4 of a 14-month project, the project will be delayed.

This is also why **pre-construction services** are central to luxury work — you cannot wait for permits to start procurement planning.

### 1.6 Multiple Revisions Are Normal

The architect will refine details. The designer will revise the finish schedule. The client will change their mind about the powder room. This is normal. It is not failure. It is the way luxury design works.

Your job is to accommodate revisions **without losing your shirt.** That means:

- Pricing change orders quickly (within 48–72 hours for most)
- Tracking design evolution with a finish-revision log and a drawing revision log
- Pushing back when a late change creates demolition or schedule consequences — with information, not attitude
- Never doing field-directed work without written authorization, even from a trusted client

### 1.7 Communication Is More Demanding

Standard residential: weekly walk-throughs and a phone call if there's a problem. Luxury residential: **weekly written reports, photo updates, schedule updates, budget updates, and direct access to the principal.** The owner (or their representative) expects:

- A weekly written progress report (template in §11)
- Photo documentation of progress (often shared via a portal — Buildertrend, Procore, CompanyCam, or a private cloud folder)
- Monthly cost reports with line-item detail
- Same-day responses to written questions; same-week resolution to issues raised
- Access to the principal of the GC firm (not just the PM or foreman) when needed

Treat communication as a deliverable. It is not "soft" work; it is what the client is paying for, alongside the building.

## 2. Qualifying and Winning Luxury Clients

The luxury market is not won at bid time. It is won in the years before bid time — through relationships, portfolio, and reputation. Understanding how luxury work is sourced and awarded is essential before you spend money chasing it.

### 2.1 Referral vs. Competitive Bid

Roughly speaking, luxury residential work is sourced in this proportion:

| Source | Approximate Share | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Architect referral | 35–45% | The single most important channel. Architects refer GCs they trust to protect their design. |
| Interior designer referral | 15–25% | Designers control client trust on the finish side. |
| Past client referral | 20–30% | The compounding asset of a luxury practice. |
| Open competitive bid | 5–15% | Often a bad sign — client is shopping price, or the design team has no preferred GC. |
| Developer / repeat institutional | 0–15% | Spec luxury work, or repeat work for a family that builds multiple homes. |

**Implication:** Architects and interior designers are your most important business development channel. Cultivating them takes years, not months. The economics:

- A single relationship with a productive architect can deliver $5M–$30M+ of work per year, indefinitely.
- The cost of cultivating an architect is essentially free — show up, do great work, make them look good, never pitch them, never undercut them, never argue with them in front of a client.

**Working with architects: the unwritten rules:**

1. **Their drawings are gospel. If you find a problem, raise it through an RFI, not by changing it in the field.**
2. **Make them look good in client meetings.** Defer to them on design questions. Take heat on cost questions.
3. **Never bypass them to talk directly to the client about design.** Even if the client invites you to.
4. **Be visible at their other projects' final walkthroughs.** Be useful. Be referrable.
5. **Pay invoices to design consultants on time** if you are managing the contract — it gets back to the architect immediately.
6. **Don't pitch them for work.** Pitch them by doing good work elsewhere that they hear about.

### 2.2 Portfolio Presentation

What luxury clients (and the design team) actually evaluate:

| Element | What to Show | What to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Project photography | Professional photos (architectural photographer, not iPhone), 8–15 images per project, daylight + twilight, detail shots that prove craft | Construction-progress photos, blurry photos, photos with logos or watermarks |
| Project list | 6–12 strongest projects with budget range, square footage, duration, designer, architect | Long list of bargain projects, projects under $1M as your top examples |
| References | 3–5 past clients willing to take a call; signed releases from each | Stale references from 5+ years ago, references where the relationship soured |
| Process documentation | Sample pre-con deliverables, sample monthly report, sample long-lead log — show how you work | Generic boilerplate, marketing fluff with no substance |
| Team bios | Project manager and superintendent — these are who the client will work with | Only the principal's bio (client wonders who is actually on-site) |
| Awards / publications | Houses published in Architectural Digest, Dwell, Veranda, Robb Report, local design magazines | Builder-trade awards the client has never heard of |
| Insurance and licensing | Current COI, license number, bond | Stale documents |
| Financial credibility | Bank reference, surety letter, audited financials if available | Vague claims about "stability" |

**The portfolio standard:** Your top 6–8 projects should look like they could be published. If they cannot, you are not ready to compete at the luxury level — or you need to invest in professional photography of completed work before pitching.

### 2.3 Pre-Construction Services: Charging for Them

At the luxury level, pre-construction is paid work. The argument is straightforward:

- Pre-construction takes 200–600 hours of senior staff time
- It requires estimating, subcontractor pricing, schedule development, constructability review, value engineering, long-lead procurement planning, and design coordination
- These hours have real cost
- The client benefits from a better-defined project before they commit to construction
- Free pre-construction creates perverse incentives (lock in the client, hide costs)

**Typical pre-construction structures:**

| Structure | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed fee | $25,000–$150,000 depending on project scope | Most common; predictable for both sides |
| Hourly with cap | Billed at rates ($150–$300/hr senior, $100–$175/hr junior) up to a cap | When scope is uncertain |
| Percentage of construction (estimated) | 1–2% of estimated construction cost | Less common; can feel like a slot machine |
| Credit against construction fee | Pre-con fee credited to construction fee if you are awarded the project | Client-friendly; reduces apparent cost |

**Typical pre-construction deliverables:**

1. Constructability review of design documents (markup of conflicts, missing info, code issues)
2. Detailed estimate at each design milestone (SD, DD, CD), with categorized line items
3. Schedule (CPM, with long-lead procurement integrated)
4. Long-lead procurement plan
5. Subcontractor pre-qualification and trade pricing
6. Value engineering options (cost and quality implications, not just "cheaper")
7. Logistics plan (site access, staging, neighbor considerations, hours of work)
8. Mock-up plan
9. Cash flow projection
10. Risk register

**Pricing pre-construction:** Roughly $15–$30 per square foot of project on a typical $1M–$5M project, or 0.75%–2.5% of estimated construction value. Adjust upward for difficult sites, multiple design revisions, or distant locations.

### 2.4 The First Meeting

The goal of the first meeting is mutual qualification, not the sale. You are evaluating whether you can deliver this project profitably and well. They are evaluating whether they can work with you.

**Bring:**
- Portfolio (printed or tablet — do not assume Wi-Fi)
- Project list and reference list
- Sample monthly report (redacted)
- Sample long-lead log (redacted)
- Insurance certificate and license info
- Business card and signed NDA if requested

**Ask:**
- What is the design status? (SD / DD / CD / not yet engaged)
- Who is the architect? Interior designer?
- What is the construction budget? (If they refuse to answer, this is a yellow flag)
- What is the desired schedule? Move-in target?
- Have you built before? What did you like and dislike about it?
- Who else are you talking to?
- Are there any prior consultants or contractors who walked off this project? Why?
- What is your decision-making process? Who else is involved?

**Show:**
- Your process (pre-con through closeout) — not your sales pitch
- Examples of similar projects (budget, scope, complexity)
- How you communicate (the monthly report is more persuasive than a brochure)
- Calibrated humility — you do not know what you do not know about their project yet

**Avoid:**
- Giving a number in the first meeting (estimate the budget if asked, but do not commit)
- Trash-talking other GCs they may be considering
- Promising a schedule before seeing the design
- Discounting your fee to win the conversation

### 2.5 Red Flags: Clients to Decline

| Red Flag | Why It Matters | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| Shops exclusively on price | Will treat you as a commodity, fight every change order, blame you for design ambiguity | Politely decline or quote conservatively |
| No design team in place | You will be doing design coordination work without authority | Insist on architect and IID being on board before contract |
| Rushed timeline with no design development | Decisions will be made under pressure; quality and budget both suffer | Counter-propose a realistic schedule; walk if rejected |
| Has fired previous GCs (multiple) | The pattern is the problem, not the previous GCs | Call the previous GCs. Believe what they tell you. |
| Financial distress signals | Slow to pay deposit, asks about deferred payment, family business in the news | Verify funding source (bank letter, attorney confirmation) |
| Spouse disagreement | Two principals with conflicting visions and no decision protocol | Insist on a single designated decision-maker, or written conflict-resolution protocol |
| Demands lump-sum on conceptual design | Wants certainty without committing to design completion | Decline lump sum; offer cost-plus with GMP |
| "We're going to live there during construction" on a major remodel | Hugely complicates everything; client will be frustrated daily | Negotiate temporary housing in budget, or accept extended schedule and reduced margin |
| Doesn't sign NDA / refuses references' calls | Doesn't want their previous experience scrutinized | Walk |
| Treats you with condescension in first meeting | This is how they treat the world; it will get worse | Walk |
| Lawyer present in first meeting making demands | Adversarial tone is set | Bring your own attorney to the next meeting; reset tone |
| Wants you to start before contract | Magical thinking about how this will work | Refuse |

### 2.6 Contract Negotiation with Sophisticated Clients

The client's attorney will review your contract. Many luxury clients use real estate or corporate attorneys who are not construction specialists; some use specialist construction counsel. Either way, expect:

- 4–12 weeks of negotiation
- Multiple redline rounds
- Provisions you have never seen in standard residential
- Pressure on indemnification, insurance limits, dispute resolution, warranty, lien waivers

**Provisions commonly negotiated on luxury contracts:**

| Provision | Owner Position | GC Position |
|---|---|---|
| Indemnification | Broad, including consultants and design team errors | Limit to GC's own negligence; mutual indemnity |
| Insurance | $5M–$25M umbrella, OCIP if large | Match standard limits; price OCIP as separate cost |
| Liquidated damages | Per-day delay damages | Resist or cap at 0.5–1% per week, with carve-outs for owner-caused delay |
| Consequential damages waiver | Often want to remove | Insist on mutual waiver |
| Warranty period | 2 years standard, 5–10 years for some items | 1 year standard, 2 years for major systems, manufacturer pass-through for the rest |
| Audit rights | Full audit, including subcontractor records | Allow within scope of open-book provisions; protect proprietary GC overhead detail |
| Termination for convenience | Wants ability to terminate without cause | Accept, with payment for work in place + demobilization + reasonable lost profit (5–10% of remaining fee) |
| Dispute resolution | Some prefer litigation; others arbitration | AAA Construction Industry Arbitration is the typical compromise |
| Confidentiality / NDA | Very common at celebrity / UHNW level | Sign, but carve out portfolio rights with photo approval |
| Photo and publicity rights | Want strict control | Negotiate photo rights with anonymity protections |

**Get a construction attorney.** Do not negotiate luxury contracts without one. A specialist costs $2,500–$15,000 per contract and saves multiples of that.

## 3. Contracts and Fee Structures at the Luxury Level

The dominant contract model in U.S. luxury residential is **cost-plus with a guaranteed maximum price (GMP)** under an open-book arrangement. Lump-sum (stipulated sum) is uncommon because the design is rarely complete enough to price meaningfully, and the project will change too much during construction for a lump-sum to hold without massive contingency.

### 3.1 Why Cost-Plus + GMP Dominates

| Contract Type | When Used | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lump sum (stipulated sum)** | Complete CDs, no expected changes | Predictable cost to owner; GC carries all risk | Requires complete design (rare in luxury); huge contingency hidden in number; adversarial change orders |
| **Cost plus fixed fee, no cap** | Trust-rich relationship, evolving design | Aligned incentives, transparent | Owner has open-ended exposure |
| **Cost plus % fee, no cap** | Rare in modern luxury | Simple | Perverse incentive (GC earns more if costs more); not recommended |
| **Cost plus fixed fee with GMP** | Default for $1M–$10M+ luxury | Owner cost cap, GC transparency, supports design evolution | Requires sophisticated cost tracking; GMP must be set at the right design stage |
| **Construction management at risk (CMAR)** | Larger projects, multi-prime | GC engaged in pre-con, then converts to GMP | Same as above, more formal |

**The cost-plus + GMP structure in practice:**

- Owner pays **actual cost** of construction (labor, materials, subs, equipment, general conditions)
- GC adds a **fixed fee** (often called construction management fee) — flat dollar or percentage
- GMP is the cap: actual cost + fee cannot exceed GMP without owner-approved change order
- If actual cost + fee is **below GMP**, savings are returned to owner (sometimes shared 50/50 or 75/25)
- If actual cost + fee exceeds GMP without approved CO, **GC absorbs the overrun**

This structure aligns incentives: GC is paid for actual work, has no incentive to inflate costs, and owner has a cap. The GMP is set at a defined design milestone (typically 90–100% CDs) once enough information exists to price reliably.

### 3.2 Open-Book Accounting

"Open book" means the owner has visibility into actual costs — invoices, subcontract amounts, vendor pricing. It does NOT mean access to the GC's proprietary overhead or other clients' information.

**What is open:**
- Subcontract amounts and invoices
- Vendor invoices for materials
- Labor hours and rates for direct labor
- General conditions costs (with detail)
- Permit and consultant pass-through costs
- Change order pricing detail

**What is NOT open:**
- GC home-office overhead structure
- Profit detail on labor burden
- Negotiated subcontractor rebates or volume discounts (negotiate this up front)
- Other clients' pricing or projects
- Internal GC labor costs above what's loaded in general conditions

**Running an open-book project:**

1. Use construction accounting software (Sage 300, Foundation, Buildertrend, Procore, CMiC) — not QuickBooks general ledger only
2. Set up a job-cost structure that mirrors the budget categories (so reports tie out)
3. Establish billing protocol: monthly application with backup (invoices, lien waivers, labor reports)
4. Establish audit protocol: owner or owner's rep can request backup on any line; respond within 5 business days
5. Document the line between open-book detail and proprietary internal data, in the contract

### 3.3 GC Fee Structure

Fee on luxury residential is typically expressed as a percentage of direct construction costs. **Typical ranges in 2026:**

| Project Size | Typical Fee Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $1M–$2M | 15–20% | Smaller projects carry higher fixed overhead per dollar |
| $2M–$5M | 12–17% | Most common range |
| $5M–$10M | 10–15% | Volume efficiency |
| $10M–$20M | 8–12% | Larger projects, may include dedicated staff |
| $20M+ | 6–10% | Institutional-scale residential |

Fee can be structured as **fixed dollar** (preferred — predictable, not influenced by cost growth) or **percentage** (less common at the luxury level, because it creates the wrong incentives).

**Justifying the fee:** When pushed on fee, the answer is the value provided:

- Dedicated PM and superintendent
- Pre-construction work (often partially included)
- Procurement management (long-lead tracking, vendor coordination)
- Quality control program (mock-ups, pre-installation conferences, daily walks)
- Coordination of design team (RFI process, submittal management)
- Reporting (weekly + monthly)
- Warranty and post-completion service
- Risk underwriting (GMP exposure)
- Office overhead (accounting, scheduling, executive oversight)

**General conditions vs. fee:** General conditions (project-specific costs — site office, dumpster, port-a-johns, project manager labor, superintendent labor) are usually itemized as direct project costs, not fee. Fee is the GC's net of profit + home-office overhead allocation. Be explicit in the contract about which is which; otherwise GC's labor on the project becomes a fight.

### 3.4 Contingency Ownership

Contingency on a luxury project typically lives in two places:

| Contingency | Owned By | Released By | Typical Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GC contingency / construction contingency** | GC | GC, used to cover small overruns, minor coordination issues, unforeseen field conditions within scope | 3–7% of construction cost |
| **Owner contingency** | Owner | Owner, used to cover scope additions, design changes, allowance overruns, unforeseen above a threshold | 8–15% of construction cost |

**Best practice:** Both contingencies are shown on the monthly report. GC contingency usage is reported but does not require approval below a per-use threshold (often $5K–$10K). Owner contingency is drawn only by approved change order.

**Common dispute:** "What is GC contingency for vs. what is a change order?" Define in contract. Typical framing:

- GC contingency covers: minor field coordination, subcontractor coordination, small unforeseen conditions consistent with construction documents
- Change order required for: scope additions, design changes, allowance overruns, unforeseen conditions inconsistent with documents (e.g., undisclosed structural conditions)

### 3.5 Allowances

Luxury projects run extensive allowances because design selections happen during construction. **Be careful here — allowance management is one of the biggest budget-leakage paths on luxury projects.**

**Categories that are typically on allowance:**

| Category | Typical Allowance Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cabinetry | $400–$1,500+ per linear foot | High-end millwork shops easily $800–$2,000/LF |
| Stone slabs (material only) | $40–$300+/SF | Quartzite, marble, exotic stones |
| Decorative tile (material only) | $20–$200+/SF | Imported, hand-painted, mosaics much higher |
| Plumbing fixtures (per fixture) | $1,500–$15,000+ | Vola, Dornbracht, Volevatch high end |
| Decorative lighting | $50–$200+/SF | Excludes recessed, includes pendants, sconces, chandeliers |
| Appliances | $50,000–$250,000+ | Full luxury package |
| Decorative hardware | $300–$1,500+ per opening | Nanz, Sun Valley Bronze, E.R. Butler |
| Flooring | $20–$100+/SF | Wide-plank, reclaimed, exotic species |
| Paint | $4–$10/SF | Higher for specialty finishes |
| Audio/visual | $150,000–$1M+ | Whole-home systems |
| Smart home / automation | $50,000–$500,000+ | Lutron, Crestron, Control4 |
| Landscape and hardscape | Separate budget, $100K–$2M+ | Often a separate contract |
| Pool and spa | $150K–$1M+ | Separate contract typical |

**Setting realistic allowances:**

1. **Anchor in the client's design intent.** "Italian marble countertops at $80/SF" is meaningless. "Calacatta Gold or similar, polished, 3cm, from XYZ slab yards, fabricated and installed at $X/SF total" is meaningful.
2. **Use historical data from completed projects.** Track allowance vs. actual on every project; build a database.
3. **Distinguish material allowance from installed allowance.** Cleaner to express allowances as material + installed separately, since labor varies with material complexity.
4. **Quote allowance with explicit assumptions.** "Stone slab allowance: $X covers material + fabrication + installation for slabs in the $Y–$Z/SF price range; selections above this range require a CO."
5. **Tighten allowances as decisions are made.** Convert allowance line to fixed line item as soon as selection is approved. Allowance management is a moving target — make it a fixed target as quickly as possible.

**Managing against allowances:**

- Track allowance status weekly: selected, ordered, delivered, installed
- Report monthly: allowance amount, selection amount, delta, status
- Convert to firm cost as soon as selection is confirmed
- Treat allowance overruns as change orders (additive) and underruns as credit COs (deductive)

### 3.6 Change Order Culture

Change orders are routine on luxury projects. They are not a sign of failure; they are how the project responds to design refinement and client preference change. **The discipline is process, not avoidance.**

**A functional CO process:**

| Step | Owner | Timeline | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Change identified | Anyone (architect, designer, client, GC, sub) | Day 0 | Verbal request or RFI |
| 2. Change Order Request (COR) issued | GC | Day 0–3 | Written description, scope, sub pricing if needed |
| 3. Pricing developed | GC, with subs | Day 3–10 | Detailed cost breakdown, schedule impact |
| 4. COR submitted | GC to owner / architect | Day 10–14 | Pricing, schedule impact, contingency draw vs. CO |
| 5. Decision | Owner | Day 14–21 | Approve, reject, or modify |
| 6. CO executed | GC | Day 21–28 | Signed CO, work proceeds |

**For small COs (under $5K–$10K, no schedule impact):** Simplified process — same-day verbal, written within 48 hours, owner approval by email.

**Red flag pattern:** Pending COs piling up — work proceeding without authorization. This is a path to disputes. Enforce the discipline:

- **Never do work in the field without written authorization.** Even from a client. Especially from a client.
- **Document verbal directives in writing immediately.** Confirm by email.
- **Track aging of pending COs.** Anything over 14 days unanswered escalates to the owner directly.

**Common client objection to CO:** "Why is this a change order? It seems obvious it would be needed." Answer with grace: walk through the design documents, show what was specified vs. what is being requested, and explain why the additional scope or change drives the cost. Never argue. Document. Move on.

## 4. Procurement and Long-Lead Management

If there is one place where luxury residential operationally differs from standard residential, it is procurement. **Luxury projects are as much about buying as building.** The schedule does not run from "demo to punch list" with materials arriving when needed — it runs from "specification approval to selection to order to delivery to install," with each item tracked independently.

### 4.1 The Procurement-Driven Schedule

A simplified view of how procurement drives schedule on a typical $4M project:

| Month | Decision / Selection | Order Triggered | Field Activity (parallel) |
|---|---|---|---|
| -3 (pre-con) | Cabinetry shop selected, custom windows specified | Cabinetry shop drawing process initiated, window order placed | Demolition, site prep |
| -2 | Tile selections final, stone slabs viewed at yard | Tile orders placed (imported), stone slabs tagged | Foundation, framing |
| -1 | Plumbing fixtures, decorative hardware selected | Plumbing/hardware orders placed | Framing, MEP rough |
| 0 (start of construction) | Permitted, mobilized | Final selections in progress | Framing, MEP rough |
| 1 | Lighting fixtures selected, AV/smart home programmed | Lighting orders placed, AV equipment ordered | MEP rough, exterior cladding |
| 2 | Appliances confirmed | Appliances ordered (delivered last) | Drywall, insulation |
| 3 | Paint colors approved | Paint scheduled | Drywall, paint primer |
| 4 | — | — | Cabinetry installation begins (delivered) |
| 5 | — | — | Tile, stone fabrication and install |
| 6 | — | — | Finish trades, fixtures install |
| 7 | — | — | Punch list, appliance install |

Notice that the major procurement decisions happen **before** the field activity that requires them. This is by design. If you wait until you "need" the cabinetry to order it, you are 16–24 weeks late.

### 4.2 The Long-Lead Log

The single most important procurement tool. A spreadsheet (or module in your project management system) tracking every long-lead item. Reviewed weekly. Reported monthly. Updated continuously.

**Minimum columns:**

| Column | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Item description | What it is |
| Spec / cut sheet reference | Linked to submittal |
| Vendor | Who is supplying |
| Required by date | When needed on-site (back from install date) |
| Lead time (weeks) | Quoted lead time |
| Order date | When ordered |
| Expected ship date | When vendor will ship |
| Expected delivery | When it arrives on-site |
| Status | Selected / Ordered / In production / Shipped / Delivered / Installed |
| Selection responsible | Client / Designer / GC |
| Approval status | Pending / Approved |
| Notes | Anything unusual |
| Risk | Green / Yellow / Red |

**Typical entries on a $4M project:** 80–150 line items. Review weekly with the PM and superintendent. Issues flagged to the design team and owner immediately.

### 4.3 Stone Slab Selection

Natural stone selection is a deceptively complex process. The owner sees "we picked the slabs." What actually happens:

1. **Quarry** — Block is quarried (Italy, Brazil, India, etc.). Limited control.
2. **Cutter** — Block cut into slabs at a stone factory. Slabs from a single block are sequenced (book-matched pairs).
3. **Importer** — Slabs imported to U.S. by a distributor.
4. **Slab yard** — Distributor or local yard inventories slabs. Client visits yard, walks slabs, tags chosen slabs.
5. **Hold / reservation** — Slab yard holds tagged slabs (typically 2 weeks free, then storage fee).
6. **Fabricator** — Fabricator picks up tagged slabs, brings to shop. Templates the field with laser or paper.
7. **Fabrication** — Cut, polish, edge profile (chamfer, eased, bullnose, ogee, mitered apron, etc.).
8. **Delivery + install** — Heavy crew, often with crane or A-frame.

**GC's role at each step:**

- **Coordinate slab yard visits.** Designer + client + GC superintendent. Tag in person.
- **Confirm slab quantity covers the scope plus waste factor.** Slab yields ~80% on simple layouts, 60–65% on complex layouts (large islands, full slabs, book-matching). Always check.
- **Confirm book-matching strategy with fabricator.** Slabs from the same block — confirm fabricator can read the vein direction and match cuts.
- **Manage hold fees.** Slabs reserved beyond the free hold accrue fees. Build into budget.
- **Coordinate template date.** Fabricator templates the cabinetry, so cabinets must be installed first. Schedule accordingly.
- **Receive and inspect.** Slabs ship from fabricator to site. Check for damage at delivery — claims are time-limited.
- **Install conditions.** Cabinets installed, plumbing rough complete, dishwasher cleat in place, range/cooktop dimensions confirmed.
- **Protect after install.** Plywood over stone tops for the remainder of construction.

**Book-matching:** Mirror-image pairing of two slabs cut sequentially from the same block. Common on bathroom walls, fireplace surrounds, kitchen island panels. Requires:
- Sequence-tagged slabs
- Fabricator who understands the assembly direction
- Field installer who keeps slabs in order
- Site protection so no slab is "wasted" through damage

### 4.4 Custom Cabinetry

Custom millwork is the highest-stakes long-lead item on most luxury projects. A few rules:

1. **Never give nominal dimensions to a custom cabinet shop.** Always provide actual field dimensions, taken after framing and rough MEP are complete, and after any wall straightening or shimming. Custom millwork is dimensionally unforgiving.
2. **Shop drawing approval is sequential.** Designer reviews, then architect, then GC, then owner. Multiple round trips are normal. Build 4–8 weeks of shop drawing review into the schedule.
3. **Approve elevation by elevation.** Do not approve a 200-page shop drawing set as a single batch. Approve room by room, finalizing rooms whose long-lead components (hardware, hinges, slides) need to be ordered.
4. **Confirm hardware before approval.** Hinges (Blum, Salice, Grass), slides (Blum Tandembox, Knape & Vogt, Accuride), pulls and knobs (specified by designer) — all confirmed before the shop builds.
5. **Confirm species, finish, grain direction.** Quarter-sawn vs. plain-sawn, rift-cut, sequence-matched, slip-matched, book-matched. Confirm on every visible elevation.
6. **Site visit by shop.** Have the cabinet shop visit the site for measurement, not the GC measuring on behalf of the shop. Their dimensional tolerance is their responsibility.
7. **Delivery and storage.** Cabinets typically arrive as flat-packed components or partially-assembled cases. Storage in active construction is a problem — best to deliver in waves, install immediately, and protect.
8. **Protection after install.** Cardboard panels secured with painter's tape, then moving blankets over them. Any sub working on or near cabinets uses protection. The cabinet shop sends a touch-up technician at punch list.

**Tolerance on custom millwork:**

- Visible miters: 1/32" maximum gap
- Reveal consistency: 1/32" across a run
- Door alignment: aligned within 1/32" across a row
- Drawer fronts: aligned within 1/32"
- Cabinet face plumb and level: 1/16" across a run

### 4.5 Decorative Tile (Imported)

Imported decorative tile from Italy, Spain, Morocco, Portugal, Japan, and Turkey is standard on luxury projects. Common pitfalls:

- **Lead times are long and variable.** Quoted 12 weeks easily becomes 16–20. Container shipping adds 4–6 weeks. Customs delays add 1–4 weeks.
- **Sample approval is required.** Order 2–3 SF samples for client approval before placing main order. Color and tone vary batch to batch.
- **Quantity estimation requires waste factor.** Standard tile: 10–15% waste. Decorative pattern, mosaic, herringbone: 15–20%. Heavily-figured marble mosaic: 25%+.
- **Always order spare tile for repair.** Future repairs from a different production run will not match. Order 5–10% extra above waste — usually 50–200 SF — and store at the owner's location after closeout.
- **Match material across batches.** If the project requires more tile than a single production run, confirm batch matching with the supplier and segregate batches by location.
- **Confirm thickness and substrate compatibility.** Imported tile may be unusual thickness (10mm, 12mm, 15mm); requires substrate adjustment, transitions, threshold details.
- **Sourcing through trade reps vs. directly.** Most reputable importers (Ann Sacks, Walker Zanger, Artistic Tile, Exquisite Surfaces, Domus, Made A Mano) have trade reps. Build the relationship; their service on damages, replacements, and back-orders is what you are paying for.

## 5. Quality Control at the Luxury Level

Quality control on luxury work is a program, not an attitude. It involves measurable tolerances, mock-ups, pre-installation conferences, daily walkthroughs, and protection systems. The cost of building this program into your operation is non-trivial; the cost of not having it is catastrophic.

### 5.1 Tolerances — Measurable, Not Vague

| Category | Standard | Tools |
|---|---|---|
| Drywall finish | Level 5 throughout (skim coat) | Raking light inspection, smooth touch |
| Paint — enamel | No brush marks visible under raking light; consistent sheen | Light + magnification |
| Paint — wall | No roller stipple, lap marks, or holiday | Raking light |
| Wood floor flatness | 1/8" in 10 feet (FF 35+) | 10-ft straightedge |
| Wood floor — gap between boards | 1/64" maximum on unfinished, after acclimation | Feeler gauge |
| Tile — lippage | 1/32" preferred, 1/16" max on rectified | Lippage gauge |
| Tile — grout line consistency | Within 1/16" across a run | Eye + caliper |
| Tile — pattern alignment | Symmetric layout, full tiles centered | Pre-installation layout |
| Stone — joint width | Per spec; typically 1/16" or less; mitered edges on edges | Eye |
| Stone — vein matching | Per design; book-matched, slipmatched | Approval from designer at fabrication |
| Cabinetry — reveal | 1/8" reveal, consistent to 1/32" | Tape, eye |
| Cabinetry — door alignment | Aligned within 1/32" | Eye + light |
| Cabinetry — drawer slide | Smooth full-extension, soft-close engaged | Hand check every drawer |
| Hardware — alignment | Centerlines aligned within 1/16" across a run | Tape |
| Trim — miter | 1/32" maximum gap on visible joints | Eye + magnification |
| Trim — wood grain | Continuous direction across run | Visual |

**Document the standard in the project Quality Control Plan.** Reference the tolerances in subcontract scope of work. Use a checklist for each trade.

### 5.2 Mock-Ups

Mock-ups are standard practice on luxury projects. They prevent expensive errors and establish the "approved" standard before full installation.

**Typical mock-ups:**

| Element | Mock-Up | Approval From |
|---|---|---|
| Tile pattern | 8' × 8' wall section or full wall in a small room | Designer + client |
| Stone slab installation | Vertical sample of intended slab use | Designer + client |
| Paint color and sheen | 4' × 4' minimum, in the actual room with actual light | Designer + client |
| Specialty plaster (Venetian, tadelakt) | 4' × 4' minimum | Designer + client |
| Millwork details | Door style, reveal, hardware mock-up for one cabinet door | Designer + client |
| Stone fireplace surround | Dry-stacked at fabricator or 1:1 paper template | Designer + client |
| Wood floor stain | 4' × 4' minimum, in the actual room | Designer + client |
| Exterior cladding | 4' × 8' minimum, on the actual wall | Architect + designer |
| Window/door installation | One opening fully installed and finished | Architect |
| Lighting test | Temporary fixtures in actual position to confirm beam, cone, glare | Lighting designer |

**Mock-up protocol:**

1. Identify mock-ups required in pre-construction
2. Schedule mock-ups before the related procurement is committed (because mock-up failure may require re-selection)
3. Build mock-up on-site or at the trade's shop
4. Invite designer, architect, client to review at scheduled time
5. Document approval in writing — photo + sign-off
6. Preserve mock-up on-site as the "standard" until trade is complete

**Mock-ups are not free.** Budget $5K–$25K for mock-ups on a major project. The cost is a small fraction of redoing a full installation.

### 5.3 Pre-Installation Conferences

Held before each major trade begins. Attendance:

- GC PM and superintendent
- Trade foreman (the actual person running the work)
- Designer or designer's representative for finish trades
- Architect for design-sensitive scopes
- Sometimes the owner or owner's rep

**Agenda:**

1. Scope of work and quantities
2. Schedule (start, key milestones, completion)
3. Standards (reference the QC tolerances, mock-up, samples)
4. Coordination with adjacent trades (who is before, after, in parallel)
5. Logistics (access, hours, storage, dust control)
6. Submittal and approval status
7. Long-lead status (material on site or arrival date)
8. Safety
9. QC protocol (daily walks, hold points, inspection sign-offs)
10. Questions

Document with meeting minutes. Distribute to attendees within 24 hours.

### 5.4 Daily Quality Walkthroughs

The GC PM or superintendent walks the entire site daily. This is not optional. The walk:

- Inspects in-progress work against the standard
- Identifies defects before they are covered by subsequent work
- Identifies coordination issues between trades
- Identifies safety issues
- Photographs conditions
- Generates a punch list of items to be addressed

**Format:** A walking checklist by area and trade. Many GCs use mobile apps (PlanGrid/Autodesk Build, Procore Field, Buildertrend) to log issues with photos and assign to subs.

**Discipline:** Issues identified are tracked to closure. A daily-walk log that doesn't close items is decoration; the value is in the closure.

### 5.5 Finish Protection Program

Luxury finishes are easy to damage and impossible to perfectly restore. Protection is a line item, not a hope.

| Surface | Protection | Cost (relative) |
|---|---|---|
| Hardwood floors (during construction) | Masonite or 1/4" plywood + 6-mil poly underneath | Moderate |
| Hardwood floors (after finish) | Ram Board + corner protection | Moderate |
| Marble / stone floors | 1/2" plywood, taped at seams, no direct foot traffic | High |
| Stone countertops | 1/2" plywood, no items on top, no work over | Moderate |
| Custom cabinetry | Cardboard panels (manufacturer typically supplies), moving blankets over, painter's tape | Moderate |
| Decorative tile (after grout) | Cardboard panels, taped, no traffic for 48 hours minimum | Low |
| Walls (in finished rooms) | Ram Board or cardboard, painters tape at top | Moderate |
| Windows | Painter's tape and paper masking, or factory film left on | Low |
| Custom doors | Hardboard wraps, corner protection | Moderate |
| Stairs | Treads covered with Masonite, secured with tape | Moderate |
| Plumbing fixtures (after install) | Bagged with poly, painter's tape, "Do not use" sign | Low |
| Light fixtures (after install) | Plastic bags or factory packaging retained | Low |
| Appliances (after install) | Cardboard + moving blankets + tape | Moderate |
| Bathtubs and showers (after install) | Cardboard + tub protection film | Low |

**Protection program ownership:** The superintendent owns this. Every sub working in a protected area is briefed on the protection and is responsible for maintaining it. Protection that is removed is replaced before that sub leaves the site.

**Common failures:**
- Floor protection seams come loose, dust and grit migrate under, scratches develop
- Cabinet protection is removed by a sub for access, not replaced
- Plumbing fixtures are bagged but the bag is torn during install of adjacent trim
- Counters are used as work surfaces despite signs

The PM walks daily to verify protection. Treat protection failures the same as quality failures.

### 5.6 Defect vs. Acceptable

There will be defects on every luxury project. The question is which get corrected and which are accepted. A pre-established standard helps.

**Frame the conversation around three categories:**

1. **Must correct.** Defect that violates the approved standard (e.g., tile lippage exceeding 1/16", a paint finish with visible roller marks). GC corrects at GC cost; no negotiation.
2. **Negotiated.** Defect within the tolerance band but visible to a critical eye. Discuss with designer and client. Options: accept as-is, partial credit, full correction.
3. **Acceptable.** Within tolerance, normal industry variation. Accepted without correction.

**Document this in the QC plan, with examples.** When a defect comes up, refer to the document, not your opinion. This depersonalizes the conversation.

**Punch list management:**

- Pre-punch by GC (superintendent walks every area)
- Punch by designer
- Punch by architect
- Punch by owner
- Combine into a single list with location, description, responsible party, due date
- Close items with photos and inspection
- Final walkthrough only after punch is closed

Luxury punch lists are extensive. 200–500 items on a $4M project is normal. Build 4–8 weeks of closeout time into the schedule.

## 6. The Design Team Ecosystem

Coordinating the design team is one of the GC's most valuable functions on a luxury project. Each designer has a domain; each has authority within their domain; each has opinions outside their domain. Your job is to make them function as a unit.

### 6.1 Interior Designer — Your Most Important Relationship

The interior designer specifies essentially everything the client touches: cabinetry, hardware, plumbing fixtures, tile, stone, paint, lighting fixtures, flooring, drapery, furniture. The designer also has the closest day-to-day relationship with the client.

**Working effectively with interior designers:**

- **Coordinate early.** During pre-construction, sit down with the designer to align on long-lead procurement, mock-up plan, and submittal review process. Don't wait for problems.
- **Honor their specifications.** If the designer specifies a particular plumbing fixture, you order that fixture, not "equal." Substitution requires designer approval.
- **Make their installations beautiful.** The designer is judged by how their selections look installed. Your installation is a major part of their portfolio.
- **Defer on aesthetic decisions.** When the designer and the client disagree about finish, you stay out of it. When the architect and the designer disagree, you facilitate but don't take sides.
- **Communicate weekly.** A standing weekly call with the designer, in addition to the broader OAC (Owner-Architect-Contractor) meeting.
- **Manage RFIs to the designer separately from architect.** Many GCs combine these — bad practice. The designer responds to RFIs on her scope; the architect to his.
- **Protect their fee on changes.** When a designer-driven change generates a CO, do not mention to the client that "the designer changed her mind." Frame as project evolution.

**Common conflict pattern:** Designer specifies something the architect's drawings don't accommodate (e.g., a recessed niche in a structural location). Resolve through RFI, not by picking sides. Document.

### 6.2 Architect — Design Authority

The architect has interpretive authority over design intent. On most luxury projects, the architect has signed an AIA B101 (or similar) with the owner and has duties including:

- Construction administration
- Submittal review
- Site visits and observation
- Pay application certification
- Substantial completion and final acceptance
- Change order review

**Working effectively with architects:**

- **Use the RFI process.** Field questions about drawings go through formal RFI, with sequential numbering, response deadlines, and a log. Verbal answers are not enforceable.
- **Submit submittals on time.** Architects review submittals; late submittals slow the architect and slow you.
- **Document field conditions that vary from drawings.** Architects need this for record drawings and for closeout.
- **Be visible at OAC meetings.** Weekly OAC meeting is standard. PM and superintendent attend.
- **Respect the architect's authority on aesthetic interpretation.** If the architect says the trim profile must match a detail you can't quite execute, escalate through cost and schedule discussion, not by ignoring it.
- **Make the architect look good.** Same principle as the designer: their reputation is partly built on the quality of your installation.

### 6.3 Kitchen Designer (Sometimes Separate)

On many luxury projects, a specialty kitchen designer is engaged — separate from the interior designer. Kitchen designers specialize in workflow, appliance integration, ventilation, and millwork.

**Coordination points:**

- Cabinetry shop drawings — kitchen designer reviews along with interior designer
- Appliance specifications and rough-in dimensions
- Plumbing rough-in (especially pot fillers, prep sinks, multi-zone faucet locations)
- Electrical rough-in (under-cabinet lighting, switched receptacles, dedicated circuits for appliances)
- Ventilation (hood CFM, makeup air, duct sizing)
- Stone slab selection and template (kitchen designer often present for slab selection)

### 6.4 Lighting Designer

A dedicated lighting designer is now standard on $3M+ projects. The lighting designer is **separate from the electrician.** The designer:

- Designs the lighting scheme (general, task, accent, decorative)
- Specifies fixtures (often unique, custom, or high-end)
- Specifies beam angles, color temperature, CRI
- Designs control zones and dimming protocol
- May specify Lutron, Crestron, or other control system

**GC's role:**

- Rough-in to the lighting designer's specs
- Coordinate with electrician on box sizes, ceiling depth, fixture support
- Mount fixtures per spec, with adjustment
- Mock-up critical lighting (cove lighting, sconces, downlights) before paint
- Final aim and focus session with lighting designer

**Common failures:**

- Electrician installs standard 4" recessed boxes when designer specified 2" or 3" precision aperture
- Trim ring color (white, black, gold) wrong, replacement requires re-fixturing
- Cove lighting recess undersized, throw not as intended
- Fixture support inadequate for the weight of a chandelier
- Dimming protocol mismatch (forward-phase vs. reverse-phase, ELV vs. MLV)

### 6.5 A/V and Smart Home Integrator

Discussed in detail in §7. Critical coordination points during design:

- Conduit sizes and routing
- Rack room location, size, power, cooling
- Speaker and display locations (before drywall)
- Network infrastructure (Cat6A, fiber)
- Integration with HVAC, lighting, security, shading

### 6.6 Landscape Architect

Often a separate contract from the GC's. Coordination interface:

- Hardscape connection to building (waterproofing, flashings)
- Drainage (foundation drains tie to site drainage)
- Irrigation rough-in through foundation
- Outdoor lighting (low-voltage and line-voltage transitions)
- Outdoor kitchen (gas, water, electrical, hood)
- Pool and spa equipment vault location and access
- Site utilities (water, gas, electrical, telecom, sewer)

Even if the landscape architect is separate, the GC must coordinate. Schedule joint walks during framing, before exterior cladding, before final grading.

## 7. Smart Home and Low-Voltage Systems

Smart home and low-voltage have become extensive scopes on luxury projects. A $4M home easily includes $200K–$600K of low-voltage work — lighting control, motorized shades, A/V, security, networking, access control, climate. The GC does not design or program these systems — but coordinates the rough-in, ensures site readiness, and manages the closeout.

### 7.1 The Scope

A typical luxury smart home includes:

| System | Sub-system |
|---|---|
| Lighting control | Dimmer panels, keypads, occupancy sensors, scene programming |
| Motorized shading | Roller shades, drapes, exterior shades |
| HVAC | Zoned thermostats, sensors, integration |
| Audio/Video | Whole-home audio, theaters, distributed video, displays |
| Security | Alarm, intrusion, glass break, cameras, monitoring |
| Access control | Smart locks, intercom, gate control |
| Network | Wired, wireless, fiber backbone, ISP termination |
| Surveillance | Cameras (IP), NVR, viewing displays |
| Communication | VoIP, intercom, paging |
| Automation | Centralized control (Crestron, Control4, Savant) |

### 7.2 Lighting Control (Lutron, the dominant brand)

**Lutron RadioRA 3** — wireless-based control system, hybrid wired/wireless. Mid-tier; serves homes up to ~10,000 SF or so. Uses Clear Connect Type X wireless.

**Lutron HomeWorks QSX** — flagship wired system. Used on larger or more complex homes. Wired control to dimmer panels in a rack, with low-voltage keypads at switch locations.

**Critical rough-in requirements:**

- **Neutral wire at every switch location.** Standard residential often doesn't include neutral at switch boxes. Lutron requires it.
- **Lutron-specific low-voltage wiring.** Cat5e/Cat6 or QS Link cable, depending on system. Routed in low-voltage conduit, not co-mingled with line voltage.
- **Larger gang boxes at keypad locations.** Lutron keypads have specific box requirements; standard single-gang is often inadequate.
- **Dimmer compatibility with fixtures.** ELV (electronic low-voltage), MLV (magnetic low-voltage), forward-phase, reverse-phase, 0-10V, DALI, DMX. The lighting designer specifies the fixture; the dimmer must match. Confirm in coordination.

**Sub vs. dealer:** Lutron systems are programmed by certified Lutron dealers/integrators. The electrician installs wiring and devices; the dealer programs and commissions. Establish the relationship early.

### 7.3 Whole-Home Automation (Crestron, Control4, Savant)

| Platform | Position |
|---|---|
| Crestron | Highest-end, most customizable, requires dedicated programmer; common on $5M+ homes |
| Control4 | Mid-luxury, dealer-installed, lower programming cost; common on $2M–$5M |
| Savant | Mac-like simplicity, higher-end, growing share |
| Loxone, RTI, URC | Less common at top luxury |

**Rack room requirements:**

- **Location.** Centrally located, easily accessible for service. Often a closet on the main floor or basement.
- **Size.** Minimum 4' × 6' for a major system; 6' × 8' or larger for whole-home with theaters
- **Power.** Dedicated 20A circuits (often 4–8); UPS-protected outlets; isolated ground in some designs
- **Cooling.** Active cooling required. Either ducted from main HVAC with dedicated supply and return, or a dedicated mini-split. Heat load 1,500–5,000 BTU/hr depending on equipment.
- **Ventilation.** Even with cooling, dedicated supply and return to keep cycling air. Equipment fails when ambient is over 85°F.
- **Lighting and outlets.** Service lighting in rack room; ample outlets for service.
- **Cable management.** Wall-mounted patch panels; ladder rack overhead; conduit entry below.
- **Acoustic isolation.** Some racks are loud (fans, drives). Consider an acoustically-treated wall if rack is adjacent to occupied space.

**GC coordination:**

- Identify rack room location in design phase. Architects often miss this.
- Pull electrical permit for rack power early.
- Size HVAC to include rack room cooling.
- Coordinate conduit pathways from rack to every distribution point.
- Provide service door (3' wide minimum).
- Provide structured wiring runs to every room before drywall — once drywall is up, you cannot add Cat6A.

### 7.4 Network Infrastructure

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| Cabling | Cat6A minimum to every room; Cat6A or fiber to AP locations |
| Wi-Fi access points | One AP per 1,500–2,500 SF; mounted in ceilings or high walls |
| Fiber backbone | If multiple buildings or large home with separate wings |
| ISP termination | Dedicated location, often in rack room |
| Redundancy | Some clients want dual ISP (cable + fiber) with failover |
| Switch | Managed, PoE+ for AP power, sized for total drop count |
| Patch panel | Rack-mounted, labeled to room and location |

Design the network in conjunction with the AV integrator. Pull a minimum of two Cat6A drops to every room (one for TV, one for spare). Pull four to media rooms and home offices.

### 7.5 Motorized Shading

Standard at the luxury level. Major brands:

- **Lutron Sivoia QS Wireless** — integrates with Lutron lighting; wireless
- **Lutron Sivoia QS** — wired version
- **Hunter Douglas PowerView** — wireless, integrates with other systems
- **Crestron motorized shades** — wired, integrates with Crestron lighting and AV
- **MechoSystems / Lutron Triathlon** — commercial-grade options for very large windows

**Rough-in requirements:**

- Low-voltage to every shaded window (one cable per shade)
- Conduit from shade location to closest junction box if wireless
- Power supply locations (rack room or distributed)
- Window head detail: shade pocket vs. surface-mounted; coordinate with the architect
- Drape track mounting for motorized drapes — backing required, not just drywall

### 7.6 Audio/Video

| Sub-scope | Considerations |
|---|---|
| Whole-home audio | Sonos (simpler) vs. distributed audio (matrix amp + ceiling speakers); 8–16 zones common |
| Home theater | Dedicated room with projector, screen, surround sound, riser, lighting, acoustic treatment |
| Media rooms | Less formal than theater; TV, soundbar or in-wall speakers, lighting |
| Distributed video | Matrix switching from rack to every display location |
| Displays | TV size and location specified by designer; mounting backing required |
| Speakers | In-ceiling, in-wall, freestanding; cable routing matters |

**Home theater specific:**

- Riser construction (10"–14" tall, decoupled from main floor for acoustics)
- Acoustic treatment (panels, diffusion, bass traps)
- Lighting (low-glare, dimmable, scene-controlled)
- Projector mount and screen
- HVAC (dedicated zone, quiet diffusers, return path)
- Door (solid core minimum, often acoustic seal)
- Wall and ceiling treatment (sometimes resilient channel or double drywall for isolation)
- Wiring (HDMI, network, speaker — pulled in conduit for future flexibility)

### 7.7 Security

| Element | Considerations |
|---|---|
| Alarm system | Wired sensors at every door and window; glass break; motion |
| Cameras | IP, PoE-powered, multiple locations; NVR with adequate storage (30–90 days) |
| Door locks | Schlage Encode, August, Yale, Latch; integration with automation |
| Intercom | At main entry and gate; video; integration |
| Gate control | Sliding or swing; automation tie-in; battery backup |
| Panic / duress | Some clients want panic buttons in master, panic room provisions |
| Monitoring | Third-party monitoring (ADT, Vector, Bay Alarm) or self-monitored |

Coordinate with the architect on sightlines for cameras, conduit pathways, and aesthetic concealment.

### 7.8 GC Coordination Role

The GC does not design or program the smart home. The GC:

1. **Coordinates rough-in during framing.** Pull every low-voltage cable while walls are open.
2. **Confirms backing.** TV displays, projector mounts, drape tracks, speaker brackets — all need solid backing.
3. **Manages the rack room.** Power, cooling, ventilation, finishes (epoxy floor or sealed concrete, painted walls, service door).
4. **Coordinates conduit.** Low-voltage conduit from rack room to every distribution point, with pull strings.
5. **Schedules integrator visits.** During framing for rough-in, during drywall to fish remaining cables, during finish for device install, during punch for commissioning.
6. **Manages closeout.** As-built drawings of low-voltage, system documentation, user training, warranty pass-through.

**Common GC failures:**

- Treating low-voltage as an afterthought
- Letting the AV integrator dictate field changes without GC awareness
- Not coordinating rack cooling with the HVAC contractor
- Not providing backing for ceiling speakers and projector mounts (drywall alone is inadequate)
- Failing to manage the integrator's punch list at closeout

## 8. Staffing a Luxury Project

Luxury projects require senior staff and continuity. The economics support it; the client demands it.

### 8.1 Dedicated Project Manager

The PM on a luxury project is dedicated to that project — not splitting time across four jobs. They:

- Are on-site daily or nearly daily
- Manage subs, schedule, budget, submittals, RFIs, change orders
- Run weekly OAC meetings
- Author the weekly client report
- Manage the long-lead log
- Coordinate with the design team
- Walk the project for QC daily

PM workload: a major project occupies 60–80% of a senior PM's time. Lighter projects can share PMs; $3M+ generally cannot.

### 8.2 Superintendent Quality

The superintendent is on-site every day and is the most visible person to the client and the design team. They must be:

- **Articulate.** Capable of explaining technical issues to a non-technical client without condescension.
- **Organized.** Site is clean, paperwork current, schedule visible.
- **Presentable.** Clean clothes, friendly, professional.
- **Technically deep.** Knows means and methods across every trade.
- **Decisive.** Makes field calls without escalating every issue.
- **Discreet.** Some clients are public figures; the superintendent never speaks about them outside the project.

The market rate for a luxury residential superintendent is $130K–$220K base, often with project completion bonuses. A great super is the single highest-leverage hire in your firm.

### 8.3 Subcontractor Calibration

Not every subcontractor can deliver luxury quality. Pre-qualify subs for craftsmanship:

| Criteria | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Portfolio | Photos and references at the luxury level |
| Foreman quality | Lead person on your project specifically — confirm before contracting |
| Crew stability | Same crew on multiple projects, not constantly turning over |
| Pace | Capable of slow, careful work — not just fast |
| Submittals | Can produce submittals (some trades, like millwork) |
| Cleanliness | Site discipline visible at their other projects |
| Reputation | Other GCs at the luxury level know them |

**Building the bench:**

- Start with the trades most critical to luxury quality: tile, plaster, millwork, painting, hardwood floor finishing.
- Visit current projects of prospective subs — luxury work requires reference visits, not phone calls.
- Pay subs well and on time. Luxury subs do not chase money; if you make them, they walk.
- Don't beat them up on price. The cheap luxury sub is a contradiction in terms.

**Trades where the standard residential sub may not work:**

| Trade | Why |
|---|---|
| Tile | Lippage, layout, miters all at higher standard |
| Painting / finishing | Level 5 finish, enamels, raking light tolerance |
| Plaster (Venetian, tadelakt, Roman clay) | Specialty technique, very few qualified |
| Millwork installation | Field-fit precision, scribing, finishing |
| Hardwood floor finishing | Wide plank, rift-cut, custom stain — different skill |
| Stone fabrication and install | Mitered edges, book-matching, large slab handling |
| Specialty masonry | Limestone veneer, dry-stack, lime mortar |

For trades where reliable luxury subs are not available, some GCs keep direct labor on staff (next section).

### 8.4 Direct Labor for Finishing

Some GCs keep skilled finish carpenters and painters on staff for luxury projects. Reasons:

- Quality control is direct, not contractual
- The crew develops trade knowledge specific to the firm's projects
- Better scheduling flexibility
- Better margin on direct labor than subcontracted

Tradeoffs:

- Fixed overhead between projects
- Workers' comp and benefits exposure
- HR and labor compliance burden
- Requires a constant project pipeline

Decision: If you do 4+ projects per year at $2M+ and have one or two trades where the local market is weak, direct labor on those trades makes sense. Otherwise, contract.

### 8.5 Key Staff Continuity

Luxury clients bond to people. The PM and superintendent that started the project should be the same ones at closeout. Staff turnover mid-project is a serious failure.

**Practical protections:**

- Pay key staff well; benchmark and adjust
- Project completion bonuses (significant — 5–15% of base salary for major projects)
- Career path within the firm (PM → Senior PM → Project Executive)
- Limit number of concurrent projects to avoid burnout
- Defer marginal pursuits to protect existing project staff

If a key staff member must leave mid-project (life event, illness), introduce the replacement to the client personally, with the departing person present. Don't ghost.

## 9. Client Communication and Relationship Management

Communication is the deliverable, not the byproduct. Build a system.

### 9.1 Weekly Written Progress Report

Distributed every Friday or Monday. Roughly 4–8 pages. Template:

1. **Header.** Project name, week ending date, distribution list.
2. **Executive summary.** Half-page narrative of the week.
3. **Schedule status.** Current week activities completed; next week planned; overall schedule status (on track / behind / ahead, with explanation).
4. **Budget status.** High-level (full detail in monthly report).
5. **Decisions needed from owner / design team.** Itemized, with due dates.
6. **Pending RFIs and submittals.** Status, with aging.
7. **Long-lead procurement update.** Highlight any changes.
8. **Photos.** 6–12 images, organized by area.
9. **Safety / incidents.** Anything to report.
10. **Site visit notes (if owner visited).** Documented.

**Format:** PDF, distributed by email or shared portal. Photo quality matters — use a real camera or recent smartphone with good light. Annotate photos with brief captions.

### 9.2 Monthly Budget Reporting

Discussed in §3.7. Distributed with monthly pay application. The reporting itself is a major deliverable.

### 9.3 Site Visits

Luxury clients visit often. Protocol:

- **Advance notice preferred** (24 hours), but accommodate spontaneous visits — within reason.
- **Safety briefing on first visit:** hard hat (if any active overhead work), closed-toe shoes, awareness of hazards.
- **Foreman or PM accompanies the client.** Never let a client walk an active site alone.
- **Discussion vs. direction:** Listen. If the client wants to direct work, gently redirect: "Let me write that up so we can confirm with the designer and price it out."
- **Document the visit.** Note what was discussed in the weekly report.
- **Never criticize a sub in front of the client.** Note the issue, address privately, follow up with the client when resolved.

### 9.4 Problem Communication

Bad news on a luxury project is inevitable. The art is delivering it well.

**Principles:**

1. **Be proactive.** Tell the client about a problem before they discover it. Always.
2. **Tell in person or by phone first, written second.** Email a serious problem cold and you are guaranteed an adversarial response.
3. **Bring a solution.** "We discovered X problem; here are options A, B, C, with cost and schedule implications."
4. **Own it.** If it's your fault, say so. The instinct to blame a sub or a designer reads badly.
5. **No surprises in the monthly report.** Anything significant in a written report has been verbally discussed first.

**Common problem categories and approaches:**

| Problem | Approach |
|---|---|
| Schedule delay | Discuss as soon as visible; offer recovery plan; identify owner-side decisions that would help |
| Cost overrun | Identify cause (scope addition, design change, allowance overrun, unforeseen condition); present in writing with options |
| Quality defect | Inspect, document, develop correction plan; present plan to client with cost and schedule impact |
| Subcontractor problem | Address privately; do not involve the client unless the sub must be replaced |
| Design ambiguity | RFI to architect / designer; track to resolution |
| Permit / inspection issue | Inform client immediately, with corrective plan |
| Personal client conflict (e.g., between spouses) | Stay neutral; defer to whomever is the designated decision-maker; document agreements |

### 9.5 The "Principal" vs. the "Estate Manager"

On UHNW projects, there is often a professional intermediary — estate manager, family-office staff, business manager. The day-to-day communication runs through them; the principal sees a summary.

**Calibration:**

- **Identify the decision-maker structure.** Who can approve scope? Who can approve change orders? What is the threshold?
- **Communicate to the intermediary at the operational level.** Daily decisions, schedule, low-stakes decisions.
- **Reserve principal communication for significant items.** Major decisions, significant cost movements, major schedule events.
- **Confirm the intermediary's authority.** Get it in writing. Some intermediaries overreach; some withhold information from the principal. Both are problems.
- **Build the relationship with the intermediary.** They are your bridge. A great estate manager makes the project; a poor one breaks it.

If the principal is the day-to-day point of contact (as is common with founder-entrepreneurs), the protocol is direct — but be respectful of their time. Don't bury them in detail; consolidate decisions.

## 10. Profitability on Luxury Projects

Luxury can be highly profitable — or destroy a firm. The difference is operational discipline.

### 10.1 Why Luxury Can Be Highly Profitable

- **Higher fees.** 12–20% of construction cost vs. 8–12% on standard residential.
- **Less price competition.** Selection is on capability and reputation, not bid number.
- **Better clients.** Decisive, well-resourced, professionally supported.
- **More interesting work.** Attracts and retains top staff.
- **Long-term relationships.** Repeat business and referrals over a 10–30 year horizon.
- **Premium ancillary services.** Pre-construction, warranty, design coordination — all billable.
- **Lower bad debt.** Sophisticated clients pay on agreed terms.

### 10.2 Why Luxury Can Destroy Profitability

- **Scope creep.** Design evolves, COs proliferate, every CO has admin cost.
- **Rework.** Mock-ups not approved, client changes mind after install, quality miss requires redo.
- **Extended duration.** Projects that go 6 months over budget consume site overhead and PM time.
- **Warranty callbacks.** Luxury clients call about things standard clients ignore. Years after closeout.
- **High administrative burden.** Reporting, meetings, documentation, design coordination.
- **Difficult subs.** Specialty subs charge premium and demand premium scheduling.
- **Client relationship cost.** Hand-holding, calls, visits, lunches, gifts at holidays — soft costs that add up.
- **Marketing and BD cost.** Portfolio photography, awards submissions, magazine pitches, designer events.

### 10.3 Protecting Margin

The disciplines that protect margin on luxury projects:

| Discipline | Practice |
|---|---|
| Weekly cost tracking | Run a cost report every week, not just at month-end |
| Early warning on overruns | If a category is trending over, flag in week 1 of the overrun, not after it has compounded |
| Capture all CO scope | Every field-directed change → COR within 48 hours, no exceptions |
| Manage subcontractor change orders | Subs will pad COs; review and negotiate before passing to owner |
| Manage allowance underruns | If an allowance underruns, the credit goes to the owner; don't try to absorb |
| Document everything | Verbal directives confirmed in writing same day |
| Manage long-lead | Late delivery often becomes GC cost (acceleration); avoid by managing procurement |
| Schedule discipline | Every week of overrun is GC cost (PM, super, site overhead); push to milestone completion |
| Sub payment discipline | Pay on time; subs price you accordingly on the next project |
| Closeout aggressively | Last 5% of project consumes 20% of duration if undisciplined; punch list management is critical |

### 10.4 The Math of a Luxury Project

A simplified example: $4,000,000 construction cost, 14% fee, 14-month duration.

| Line | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Construction direct cost | $4,000,000 | Subs, labor, materials, equipment, GC |
| GC fee (14%) | $560,000 | Fixed fee |
| General conditions (in direct cost) | $480,000 | PM, super, site office, dumpster, etc. — 12% of direct |
| GC home office overhead allocation | (Estimated) $200,000 | From the fee |
| GC profit (target) | (Estimated) $360,000 | From the fee, ~9% of direct cost |
| Effective net margin | 9% of direct, 15% of revenue | If executed cleanly |

If the project goes 3 months over and consumes an additional $90K of PM/super time and $40K of site overhead, the GC absorbs $130K, dropping net profit to $230K — a 36% margin erosion.

If the project also has $80K of rework or quality misses not chargeable to subs, net profit drops to $150K — a 58% margin erosion.

**The discipline is operational, not contractual.** A clean execution protects the margin; a sloppy execution gives it back.

### 10.5 The Referral Network

A satisfied luxury client is a multi-decade asset.

| Source | Lifetime Value (approximate) |
|---|---|
| One satisfied client | 3–8 referrals over 10 years, often $1M–$5M of work each |
| One satisfied architect | 1–3 projects per year over 10–20 years, $2M–$10M each |
| One satisfied interior designer | 2–5 projects per year over 10–20 years, $1M–$5M each |
| One unsatisfied client | Negative referrals across a social circle — possibly 5–15 projects lost |

The math justifies expensive client service. A $5K holiday gift, a $10K photoshoot of their completed home, $20K of warranty work in year 3 done graciously — all trivial against the lifetime value.

**Active referral cultivation:**

- Send a thank-you card and a meaningful gift at substantial completion
- Photograph the completed project (with client permission); offer them prints
- Submit the project for publication (with client and designer permission)
- Check in 6 months and 1 year after move-in
- Address warranty items quickly and without argument
- Invite past clients to relevant industry events (gallery openings, design fairs)
- Stay in touch with the designer and architect — they remember you when they have new work

### 11.9 Common Pitfalls — Things That Get Luxury GCs in Trouble

| Cheap subs on critical trades | Pay for quality; don't penny-pinch on millwork, tile, plaster, paint |
| Inadequate protection | Treat protection failures as quality failures |
| Closeout punch list neglected | Build punch into schedule, not as an afterthought |
| Communication failure with one stakeholder | Standing weekly meeting with each major design party |
| Ignoring estate manager / business manager | Calibrate the right level of communication to each |
| Not photographing the completed work | Hire an architectural photographer at closeout |
| Walking away from a client at closeout | One-year and two-year check-ins; respond to warranty calls |
| Letting key staff burn out | Project completion bonuses; limit concurrent projects |

## Closing Note

Luxury residential is a different business, not a bigger version of the same business. The skills that made you successful at $400K projects — efficient sequencing, strong subs, clean books, honest dealing — are necessary but not sufficient. Layered on top are: pre-construction sophistication, procurement-driven scheduling, open-book financial transparency, mock-up culture, daily QC discipline, design team coordination, smart home and low-voltage management, professional communication, and durable client relationships.

The reward, for those who build the operation properly, is a high-margin practice with a long referral horizon. The penalty, for those who try to handle luxury work with standard-residential muscle memory, is loss of money and reputation.

Build the operation. Hire the people. Cultivate the design relationships. Master the procurement. Manage the communication. The rest follows.
