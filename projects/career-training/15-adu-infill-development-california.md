# ADU and Infill Development in California — A GC's Reference Guide

> **Reading note**: ADU law in California is a fast-moving target. The legislature has passed material amendments to Gov. Code §§ 65852.2 (ADU) and 65852.21 / 66411.7 (SB 9) almost every year since 2017. Cite this guide as a starting point, but **always verify against the current HCD ADU Handbook and your local jurisdiction's adopted ADU ordinance** before pulling a permit. Where this guide notes a "state mandate," it means the locality must allow at least that much; the locality may be **more permissive** but not more restrictive on the listed dimensional standards.

---

## 1. ADU Law in California — Current State (2025)

### 1.1 ADU Types — Definitions and Differences

California recognizes four functional types of ADUs in Gov. Code § 65852.2 and § 65852.22, with materially different rules for each:

| Type | Definition | Max Size (state min) | Independent Utility | Owner-Occupancy |
|------|------------|----------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Detached ADU** | Freestanding structure on a lot with a primary dwelling | 1,200 SF (or larger if local code permits) | Owner's option | None (post-AB 976) |
| **Attached ADU** | Shares one or more walls with the primary dwelling | 50% of primary up to 1,200 SF; locals must allow at least 850 SF (1,200 SF if ≥2 BR) | Owner's option | None |
| **Internal / Conversion ADU** | Within the existing footprint of the primary dwelling or an accessory structure (e.g., basement, attic) | Must allow conversion of entire existing structure; minimum 800 SF "expansion" allowed | Owner's option | None |
| **Junior ADU (JADU)** | ≤500 SF within the walls of an existing or proposed **single-family** dwelling; may share a bathroom with primary | 500 SF maximum (state cap, not floor) | Usually shared with primary | **Required** (state mandate retained) |

Key distinctions for the GC:

- **JADU vs. Internal ADU**: A JADU is *carved out of* a single-family residence, has a separate exterior entrance, can share sanitation with the primary, and includes an "efficiency kitchen." An internal/conversion ADU is fully independent (separate bath, full kitchen) and can sit inside any existing residential or accessory structure.
- **Garage conversion**: Common confusion — converting a detached garage with full bath/kitchen creates a **detached ADU**, not a JADU. Converting an attached garage **within the home's walls** to a small unit with shared bath can be a JADU. The classification drives owner-occupancy, parking, and fee rules.
- **"Movable tiny home" units** are not ADUs under state law unless they are permanently attached on a permanent foundation, properly classified, and meet CBC/CRC. HUD-code manufactured homes can qualify if installed under HCD permit.

### 1.2 State Preemption — What Recent Bills Did

State ADU law preempts most local ADU regulation. The state floor is a **floor, not a ceiling**, and locals must allow at least the state minimums. The major reform bills:

- **AB 68 (2019), AB 881 (2019), SB 13 (2019)** — established the modern preemption framework, killed most owner-occupancy requirements, killed most parking requirements near transit.
- **AB 2221 (2022, eff. 1/1/2023)** — closed loopholes locals were exploiting:
  - Required local agencies to identify deficiencies in writing on first review (no successive new objections)
  - Front setbacks cannot be used to preclude an 800 SF ADU at 16 ft height
  - "Passageway" requirements (paths to ADUs) eliminated
  - Detached ADUs in the front yard allowed if other code paths are infeasible
- **SB 897 (2022, eff. 1/1/2023)** — raised the state height ceiling:
  - 18 ft for detached ADU on a lot within ½ mile of major transit stop or high-quality transit corridor (HQTC), **+2 ft to match primary roof pitch** = up to 20 ft
  - 18 ft for a detached ADU on a lot with an existing/proposed multistory multifamily building
  - **25 ft (or primary dwelling height limit, whichever lower) for attached ADUs**
  - Existing non-conforming zoning conditions on the primary cannot be required to be cured as a condition of ADU approval
  - Fire sprinklers cannot be required for an ADU if not required for the primary dwelling, with limited exceptions
- **AB 976 (2023, eff. 1/1/2025)** — made **permanent** the prohibition on local owner-occupancy requirements for ADUs (the prior 2025 sunset was repealed). **JADUs still require owner-occupancy** of either the primary or the JADU.
- **AB 1033 (2023, eff. 1/1/2024)** — authorizes local opt-in ordinances allowing **separate sale of ADUs as condominiums**. Local jurisdiction must affirmatively adopt; most have not yet. Each ADU sold separately must comply with the Davis-Stirling Act and lender consent on existing mortgages.
- **SB 1211 (2024, eff. 1/1/2025)** — multifamily lots:
  - Up to **8 detached ADUs** per multifamily lot (was 2), capped at the number of existing primary units
  - **No replacement parking** required when uncovered parking is demolished for an ADU
- **SB 684 (2023, eff. 7/1/2024)** — ministerial approval for **up to 10 unit infill projects** on qualifying multifamily-zoned parcels ≤5 acres (see §8.2)

### 1.3 By-Right ("Ministerial") Approval

For ADUs meeting the state standards, the local agency **must** process the application ministerially:

- **No discretionary review** (no design review board, no architectural review committee veto)
- **No public hearings**
- **No conditional use permit**
- **No CEQA review** (statutorily exempt under Pub. Res. Code § 21080.17)
- **60-day shot clock**: agency must approve or deny within 60 days of a complete application; otherwise **deemed approved**
- **Objective standards only**: setbacks, height, size — measurable standards. Subjective standards like "compatible with neighborhood character" are not enforceable in ministerial review.
- **First-pass completeness**: under AB 2221, the agency must list **all** deficiencies on first review; it cannot keep finding new ones in successive submittals.

**Practical GC implication**: if a planner tries to send your ADU to design review, push back in writing citing § 65852.2(a)(3) and request the matter be ministerial. Document everything.

### 1.4 Maximum Sizes — State Floors

The state requires localities to allow at least:

- **Detached new ADU**: 1,200 SF
- **Attached new ADU**: 850 SF (1+ BR studio); 1,000 SF (1 BR); **1,200 SF if 2+ BR** — local cap can be 50% of primary or 1,200 SF, whichever is greater
- **JADU**: 500 SF (this is both the floor and the legal definitional ceiling)
- **Conversion of existing space**: no size limit — entire existing structure can be converted, plus up to 150 SF for ingress/egress
- **800 SF safe harbor**: locals **cannot** apply size, setback, lot coverage, FAR, or open space standards in a way that prevents at least an 800 SF, 16 ft tall, 4-ft setback ADU on any qualifying lot

### 1.5 Setbacks

- **State minimum**: 4 ft side and rear setbacks for new detached or attached ADUs
- **Front setbacks**: locals may keep their underlying front setback **unless** doing so would preclude an 800 SF ADU, in which case the front setback yields (AB 2221)
- **No setback required** for an ADU built within an existing structure or replacing an existing structure in the same footprint
- **No setback required** for a JADU (it's inside the existing walls)
- **Building code separation** (CRC R302) still applies between separate buildings on the lot — this is independent of zoning setback.

### 1.6 Height

| Situation | State Max (locals must allow at least) |
|-----------|------------------------------------------|
| Detached ADU, default | 16 ft |
| Detached ADU within ½ mile of major transit / HQTC | **18 ft** (+2 ft if matching primary roof pitch = 20 ft) |
| Detached ADU on lot with existing/proposed multistory multifamily | 18 ft |
| Attached ADU | **25 ft** or height of primary dwelling, whichever is lower |
| JADU / Internal | Existing structure height |

"Major transit stop" and "HQTC" are defined in Pub. Res. Code § 21155 and § 21064.3. HQTC = bus or rail corridor with ≤15 minute peak headways. Practical workflow: check your county's Sustainable Communities Strategy map or the HCD AB 2097 map layer.

### 1.7 Parking

State law **prohibits** parking requirements for an ADU if **any** of these apply:

1. Within ½ mile **walking distance** of public transit (includes bus stops)
2. Within an architecturally and historically significant district
3. Part of a proposed or existing primary residence or accessory structure (i.e., conversion)
4. On-street parking permits are required but not offered to the ADU occupant
5. A car share vehicle is located within one block
6. Within one block of a car share pick-up location

For most California urban/suburban lots, at least one of these triggers applies. **Practical reality**: parking is rarely required for urban ADUs.

**SB 1211 (2025)**: if existing uncovered parking is demolished to build the ADU, no replacement parking can be required. (Garage demolition for an ADU has long been "no replacement" — SB 1211 extends to uncovered.)

### 1.8 Lot Coverage, FAR, Open Space

Locals cannot apply these standards in a way that precludes an **800 SF, 16-ft, 4-ft setback ADU**. So if your client has a small or already-developed lot, the 800 SF safe harbor wins over a 35% lot coverage rule.

### 1.9 Owner-Occupancy — Current Status (2025)

- **ADUs**: NO owner-occupancy requirement — made permanent by AB 976 (no sunset)
- **JADUs**: Owner-occupancy of **either** the primary dwelling OR the JADU is **still required** (state mandate). This is recorded as a deed restriction.
- **SB 9 lot splits**: applicant must sign a 3-year owner-occupancy affidavit for the lot being split

### 1.10 Number of ADUs Allowed

**Single-family lot** (state floor — locals can be more permissive):
- One detached or attached ADU **PLUS** one JADU = 3 units total (primary + ADU + JADU)
- A JADU can only be created from a single-family residence; multifamily lots cannot have a JADU.

**Multifamily lot**:
- **Conversion ADUs**: at least one, or 25% of existing units (whichever is greater), inside non-livable space (storage, boiler room, garage, attic, basement)
- **Detached ADUs**: up to **8 per lot** under SB 1211 (effective 2025), capped at the number of existing primary units. **Two stories** allowed.

### 1.11 SB 9 — Two Units / Lot Split in SFR Zones

SB 9 (eff. 1/1/2022, Gov. Code § 65852.21 and § 66411.7) allows ministerial approval of:

- **Two units** on an existing SFR-zoned lot (duplex, or two detached units, or primary + ADU)
- **Lot split** of an SFR lot into two lots, each ≥1,200 SF and ≥40% of the original lot size
- **Combination**: lot split + two units per lot = **4 units across two lots**

**Eligibility filters**:
- Single-family zoning only
- Within an urbanized area or urban cluster (per Census)
- Not in a coastal high hazard, very high fire severity zone (unless mitigated), historic district, prime farmland, wetlands, hazardous waste site, or earthquake fault zone
- Not requiring demo or alteration of housing that has been rented in the prior 3 years (anti-displacement)
- Not within a designated historic district or on the National Register
- Owner must sign affidavit of intent to occupy one unit for 3 years (lot splits only — does not apply to two-unit projects without a lot split)
- Cannot have been used in a prior SB 9 split by the same owner or an adjacent owner with shared ownership

**Practical**: SB 9 has had **far lower uptake** than originally projected — UC Berkeley Terner Center data shows fewer than 200 lot splits statewide in the first two years. Major friction points: lender resistance to financing on newly split lots, lack of pre-approved plan libraries, owner-occupancy affidavit, and aggressive local objective standards (e.g., 4-ft setback to lot line, 800 SF unit max) that local agencies have layered on.

## 2. ADU Feasibility Analysis for the GC

### 2.1 Property Research — First Pass (Free, 1–2 hours)

Before charging for a feasibility study, run this checklist:

1. **Zoning** — pull the city/county zoning map. Confirm SFR or multifamily zone. Confirm ADU is allowed (it is, in every residential zone, by state law).
2. **Parcel data** — county assessor for lot dimensions, square footage, existing improvements
3. **Aerial + Street View** — Google Earth Pro for setbacks, garage existence, side-yard access width (matters for crane/material handling)
4. **Transit overlay** — is the parcel within ½ mile of a major transit stop? (Drives height, parking, fees) Use HCD's AB 2097 statewide map layer or the city's GIS.
5. **Hazard overlays** — Cal FIRE Fire Hazard Severity Zone, FEMA flood maps, Alquist-Priolo earthquake fault zones, liquefaction (CGS), high fire severity (impacts WUI requirements and SB 9 eligibility)
6. **Easements and setbacks** — preliminary title report from the owner or order one ($300–$500); look for utility easements, drainage easements, recorded restrictions
7. **HOA / CC&Rs** — even if state law preempts most HOA ADU restrictions (Civil Code § 4751), check for design review and notification obligations
8. **Historic** — local historic register, Mills Act contracts, National Register, California Register, conservation districts
9. **Permit history** — pull permit history on the primary structure. Unpermitted additions are a feasibility killer (or a separate workstream).

### 2.2 Utility Capacity

Utility upgrades are the #1 surprise cost on ADU projects. Audit early.

**Water service**:
- A new ADU **cannot** be required to install a new or separate water service unless explicitly requested by the owner (state law preempts mandatory upgrade for ADUs ≤750 SF)
- For ADUs >750 SF, fees must be proportional and capacity can be reviewed
- A 3/4" or 1" meter typically serves 1 SFR + 1 ADU; check the meter and service line size on site
- Replacing a meter typically requires water purveyor inspection, sometimes a hot-tap or saddle on the main

**Sewer**:
- ADUs generally connect to the existing sewer lateral; no new lateral required by state law
- Check lateral material and condition — older clay or Orangeburg laterals may need replacement regardless
- For septic systems: capacity analysis required, may need percolation test and system upgrade — significant cost ($25k–$60k for a new septic field). Some counties (Sonoma, Marin) have streamlined septic ADU rules.

**Electrical**:
- 100A service is the minimum modern default; 125–200A typical. ADU adds heat pump, EV charger pressure → consider 200A panel as baseline.
- Detached ADU: dedicated subpanel fed from main, or new service drop. New service drop = PG&E/SCE/SDG&E coordination, 2–6 month lead time — start application **at permit submittal**, not at framing.
- **Load calculation** under NEC 220 or 230.83 — required by plan check. Often shows existing service is adequate without upgrade.
- For ADU >750 SF: utility may require dedicated meter; for ≤750 SF, owner can choose shared meter.

**Gas**:
- Most new ADUs in California are **all-electric** by default — Title 24 2022 and 2025 cycles push heat pumps, and many cities (Berkeley line was struck down by 9th Cir., but reach codes vary) require electrification.
- If gas is provided, capacity check on existing meter required.

### 2.3 Structural Assessment of an Existing Garage (JADU / Conversion ADU)

The garage looks like the cheap path. It often is. It's also where you'll lose money on hidden conditions. Pre-construction checklist:

1. **Foundation**:
   - Slab thickness (often 3.5" — minimum for habitable is 4" with vapor barrier and base course)
   - Cracks > ¼" or differential settlement → engineer evaluation
   - Stem walls / footing: many older garages have shallow footings (12" or less) that don't meet current frost/seismic
   - Check for capillary moisture — slab without vapor barrier needs a poured-over membrane (Schluter Ditra, Mapei Mapelastic Aquadefense) or new slab with VB
2. **Framing**:
   - 2x4 walls @ 24" o.c. were common — adequate for occupancy load but may need stiffening for new openings (windows for egress/light)
   - Header conditions at the garage door opening — typically remove door, infill with framed wall, new structural opening for window/door
   - Roof framing: 2x4 or 2x6 rafters often undersized for added ceiling insulation dead load; verify with engineer
3. **Roof**:
   - Asphalt comp roofs reaching end-of-life — bake replacement into the conversion estimate
   - Skylights or new vents need flashing details that match new finished interior
4. **Seismic**:
   - Cripple wall bracing if elevated above slab — likely needed retrofit (Plan Set A / Earthquake Brace + Bolt)
   - Garage living space conversion often triggers full seismic retrofit assessment — engineer can scope based on lateral analysis
5. **Ceiling height**:
   - Habitable rooms require **7-ft minimum** (CRC R305) — many older garages with low headroom (especially with sloped slab to door) need slab leveling or floor build-up
6. **Required upgrades — almost always**:
   - Insulation (walls, ceiling, slab perimeter)
   - Smoke and CO alarms
   - Egress window in sleeping room
   - Bathroom ventilation
   - 2x4 → R-15 cavity insulation (Title 24 will often require continuous exterior insulation as well)
   - Mechanical ventilation (whole-house, typically 50 CFM exhaust + supply or HRV)

### 2.4 Fire Sprinklers

**State rule (SB 897 codified)**: an ADU shall not require fire sprinklers if the primary residence is not required to have them. This is a hard preemption.

**Practical caveats**:
- If the primary already has sprinklers (e.g., post-2011 new construction), the ADU is typically required to extend the system or have its own.
- Some cities try to require sprinklers for ADUs over 500 SF or two-story detached ADUs — this is increasingly challenged successfully under SB 897 if the primary lacks sprinklers.
- WUI zones (Chapter 7A CRC) still apply — exterior fire-resistant assemblies, ember-resistant vents, etc. Not the same as interior sprinklers.

### 2.5 Impact Fees

This is one of the biggest cost variables city-to-city, and one of the biggest legal protections in state ADU law:

- **ADU ≤750 SF: EXEMPT from all impact fees** — state mandate. Includes development impact fees, capacity charges, school fees on units under 500 SF, traffic/park fees.
- **ADU 751–1,200 SF**: fees must be **proportional** to the size of the ADU vs. the primary dwelling. Example: 1,000 SF ADU + 2,000 SF primary → ADU pays 50% of the impact fees a new SFR would owe.
- **School fees**: separate exemption — school districts **may** charge fees for ADUs >500 SF (Ed. Code § 17620). Many districts charge $5–$8/SF.
- **Connection / capacity charges**: water and sewer agencies still charge connection fees, but state law caps them at proportional rates for ADUs >750 SF.

**GC action**: pull a written fee estimate from the city building department at the pre-application meeting. Don't quote a number to the owner you don't have in writing.

### 2.6 Historic Districts and HOAs

**HOAs**: Civil Code § 4751 voids HOA covenants that "effectively prohibit or unreasonably restrict" ADU construction. HOAs can still impose **reasonable** design standards (matching color, roof material). They cannot require discretionary architectural review for an ADU that meets objective standards.

**Historic resources**:
- National Register / California Register / local landmark: locals may impose discretionary design review — this is one of the few zones where ministerial preemption yields
- Mills Act properties: contract terms govern
- Conservation overlay districts: gray area; check with HCD if local impose discretionary review on a non-listed property

### 2.7 Pre-Application Meeting — How to Use It

Most California jurisdictions offer (some require) a pre-app meeting for ADUs, $200–$1,500.

Front-load these decisions:
1. Confirm ministerial path (not discretionary)
2. Confirm setback, height, lot coverage application
3. Get written fee estimate (impact fees, plan check, building permit, school fees)
4. Confirm utility connection path and any required upgrades
5. Confirm meter / address process
6. Confirm fire-sprinkler requirement (in writing)
7. Confirm if pre-approved plans are available
8. Identify the building plan check route (separate ADU planner vs. general)
9. Confirm CalGreen, Title 24, accessibility scope
10. Identify special inspections required (soil, structural, etc.)

Bring: site plan, basic floor plan/sketch, primary building permit history printout, photos. The hour-long meeting saves weeks of plan-check cycles.

## 3. Design Considerations for ADU Construction

### 3.1 Detached ADU

**Site planning**:
- Honor 4-ft side/rear setback; if the lot dimensions force a creative placement, plan for SB-897 ½-mile transit height (18–20 ft) if eligible to allow a 2-story footprint
- Fire access: most jurisdictions require an unobstructed path from public way to the ADU front door (some require minimum 3-ft width). Note: AB 2221 eliminated the "passageway" requirement, but emergency access remains.
- Easements: don't build over them. Period.
- Solar access of primary: a 2-story ADU can shade the primary's solar — coordinate with PV system or future system reservation
- Tree protection: many cities have tree ordinances (protected oaks, heritage trees) that survive ministerial review

**Foundation**:
- Flat lots: slab on grade is fastest and cheapest
- Sloped lots: stepped footing or pier-and-beam — soils report likely required for slope >10% (and almost certainly for hillside areas)
- Expansive clay (much of inland CA): post-tensioned slab or stiffened mat — coordinate with geotech
- Hillside: anchor block and grade beam, drilled piers; budget +25–40% over flat-lot foundation

**Exterior**:
- Match-versus-contrast — let the architect drive. Match cuts design review hassle to zero in non-historic neighborhoods. Contrast can be permitted ministerially if it meets objective standards (color limits, roof material limits).
- Cladding: fiber cement (Hardie) is the workhorse — fire-resistive, durable, fits WUI Chapter 7A
- Roof: WUI areas need Class A roofing assemblies — comp shingle with rated underlayment is the default; metal is also Class A and matches modern aesthetic

### 3.2 Garage Conversion JADU (and Garage→ADU)

**Foundation/floor**:
- Slab adequacy: 4" min, 4" base course, 10 mil VB. Older garage slabs without VB are common — overlay strategies:
  - Cementitious self-leveler with integrated vapor barrier
  - Sleeper system 2x4 over 6 mil poly + rigid foam (loses 3–4" of head height — verify CRC 7-ft min after build-up)
  - Demo and re-pour (most expensive but cleanest)
- Slope: garage slabs slope ¼" / ft toward the door. Habitable slabs must be level. Leveling self-leveler is required.

**Fire separation (R302.6) — critical**:
- If **any** portion of the garage remains for vehicle parking (rare in conversion, common in JADU where half-garage remains), the wall and ceiling between garage and habitable space requires **5/8" Type X gypsum** on the garage side
- Door from garage to habitable space: 1-3/8" solid-core, self-closing, self-latching (or 20-minute fire-rated)
- If garage is fully converted (no vehicle parking remains), R302.6 doesn't apply
- Detached garage conversion that is now fully ADU: no R302.6 concern internally

**Thermal envelope**:
- Walls usually need to be furred 2x4 → 2x6 effective to fit R-15 + continuous insulation (Title 24 typically requires R-13 cavity + R-5 continuous for converted spaces in most climate zones)
- Existing wall finish often unfinished — easiest to add insulation in cavity, drywall, vapor retarder (climate-dependent)
- Ceiling: blown cellulose or batt; for low-pitch garage roof, may need rigid foam atop sheathing
- Slab: perimeter insulation (R-10 to 24" deep) — often overlooked, required by Title 24 in most CZs

**Ceiling height (CRC R305)**:
- 7-ft min for habitable
- 6-ft 8-in for bathrooms, halls, kitchens (with some exceptions)
- 6-ft 4-in for sloped portions (≥50% of room must be at 7 ft)
- Garage rolling door header is often at 7 ft — losing 4–6" for finished floor + insulation can put you under code. Plan early.

### 3.3 Attached ADU

**Structural connection**:
- New foundation typically required at the ADU side, tied into existing — engineer detail for differential settlement and lateral connection
- Shear wall coordination — opening through existing wall may interrupt existing shear path; lateral analysis required
- Roof framing: typically a new shed or gable framed off the primary; flashings and step-flashing at the wall-to-roof are leak-prone — pay attention to detail

**Sound separation**:
- State law requires only that the unit be habitable; CBC § 1206 requires STC 50 (45 if field-tested) between dwelling units — this is a hard code item
- Wall assembly: staggered-stud 2x4 on 2x6 plate, R-15 batt, RC channels + 5/8" drywall = STC ~55
- Floor/ceiling assemblies (if stacked): IIC 50 also required — resilient channel ceiling, gyp-crete topping, or engineered floor systems

**Egress**:
- Separate exterior entrance required (state mandate)
- Each sleeping room: egress window per CRC R310 (5.7 SF, min dimensions, sill ≤44")
- Smoke + CO alarms interconnected within the ADU (do not need to interconnect with primary)

### 3.4 Internal ADU

**Within existing footprint**:
- Conversion ADU in an existing house basement or attic — no setback applies
- Separate exterior entrance required (often the trickiest design challenge — adding a basement walk-out is significant excavation)
- Egress, natural light, ventilation all per CRC

**Separate address**:
- USPS assigns; locals usually issue with the building permit
- Required for emergency services routing and utility billing

### 3.5 Universal Design — Why It's an Easy Upsell

ADUs are often built for multigenerational living or to age-in-place. Building in accessibility from day one is cheap; retrofitting later is expensive.

Low-cost moves:
- **Step-free entry**: no-threshold or low-threshold exterior door, gentle ramp under-cladding
- **Wider doors**: 32" clear (36" leaf) on bathroom, bedroom, and main entry
- **Bathroom blocking**: 2x10 backing at 33–36" AFF in all bathroom walls for future grab bars
- **Curbless shower**: linear drain, sloped slab — easier to spec at rough-in
- **Hallway widths**: 42" preferred (36" minimum)
- **Outlet/switch heights**: 18" / 44" AFF
- **Lever hardware** vs. knobs throughout
- **Reinforced bedroom walls** for future ceiling lift mounting (optional)

### 3.6 Title 24 Energy Compliance

- All ADUs (new and conversion) must comply with **Title 24, Part 6** (energy) and **Part 11 (CalGreen)** — though some conversion projects qualify for partial CalGreen exemptions
- **Performance path** (CBECC-Res software) is more flexible than the prescriptive path — typical for compact ADUs with constrained envelope
- **HERS-tested measures**: duct leakage, envelope tightness (blower door), refrigerant charge for HVAC — typical detached ADU will require ≥2 HERS measures
- **Electrification**: 2022 Title 24 cycle pushed all-electric as baseline; 2025 cycle continues. Heat pump space heating, heat pump water heater, electric induction cooking — what you should be spec'ing by default.
- **Solar PV**: required on new detached ADUs per Title 24, **sized to the ADU's annual energy use** (not the full-house mandate). Common: 1.5–3 kW system. Conversion ADUs are typically exempt from new PV.

## 4. Permitting ADUs

### 4.1 Application Package

A complete ADU application typically includes:

1. **Application form** + applicant/owner authorization
2. **Site plan** — to scale, showing existing structures, setbacks, easements, ADU location, parking (if required), trees, drainage
3. **Floor plan** — dimensioned, with room labels and sizes
4. **Elevations** — all four sides, dimensioned heights, window/door schedule
5. **Cross-section** — at least one, showing wall, floor, ceiling, roof assemblies
6. **Structural details / engineering** — calcs and details for foundation, lateral, framing
7. **Title 24 Part 6 compliance documentation** (CF1R) — by certified energy consultant or CEA
8. **HERS rater identified** for required field tests
9. **CalGreen checklist** (CF1R for energy is separate)
10. **Soils report** (if hillside, expansive, or required by local)
11. **Utility plan** — electrical riser, plumbing schematic, gas (if any)
12. **Fees** — plan check at submittal; impact and permit fees at issuance

### 4.2 Processing Time Mandates

State law (Gov. Code § 65852.2(b)) gives locals **60 days** from a complete application to act:

- Days 1–30: completeness review
- Days 31–60: technical review and decision
- If the agency does not act by day 60, the application is **deemed approved**

In practice:
- "Complete" is contested — agencies declare incomplete to restart the clock. AB 2221 limits this: agency must identify **all** deficiencies on the first review.
- Combined with a primary dwelling permit (e.g., remodel + ADU), the 60-day rule applies only to the ADU portion if a stand-alone ADU application is filed.
- Deemed-approval is rarely invoked — most owners want a paper approval to record for financing. Use it as leverage, not as the goal.

### 4.3 Pre-Approved ADU Plans

Many cities now maintain a library of pre-approved ADU plans (state encouraged this in AB 434 / SB 13 follow-on). Examples:

- City of San José, San Diego, San Francisco, LA City, LA County, Sacramento, Oakland, Berkeley, San Mateo County
- HCD maintains an aspirational pre-approved plan model

**GC advantage**:
- Plan check on a pre-approved plan: 1–2 weeks vs. 6–12 weeks for custom
- Plan check fees reduced (often 50%)
- Saves $5k–$15k in architectural design fees
- Some cities (e.g., San José) offer **across-the-counter** issuance for pre-approved + standard site conditions

**Trade-off**: limited customization. Make sure the lot conditions support the chosen plan's foundation and orientation.

### 4.4 JADU Permitting

JADUs typically follow a simplified path:

- Many cities have a **JADU checklist** (1–3 pages) rather than full plan-check
- Single-story interior remodel scope dominates
- Owner-occupancy deed restriction is recorded — bring a sample restriction form to the pre-app meeting
- Plan check: 2–4 weeks typical
- Fees: often $500–$2,500 total (vs. $10k–$30k for a detached ADU)

### 4.5 Meter and Utility Decisions

**Owner's choice** for a detached ADU (state mandate per § 65852.2(g)):
- Separate water/sewer/electric meter, or
- Shared meter with primary

**Practical implications**:
- Shared meter = simpler install ($3k–$8k savings), but rental complications (one bill to split)
- Separate meter = clean rental accounting, easier future sale (under AB 1033, if locally allowed)
- Sub-metering: a software/hardware setup (e.g., Submeter Solutions) is an alternative — bills are split by usage without separate utility meter

### 4.6 Address Assignment

- ADUs must have a separate address (state mandate)
- Process: local building or planning department assigns; USPS confirms
- Format usually: 1234 Main St + 1234 A Main St (or 1234½) — locality decides
- Required for 911 routing, utility billing, package delivery
- Recommend the owner update homeowners insurance to reflect the new unit before occupancy

### 4.7 CalGreen Scope

**New construction ADUs**: full CalGreen Tier 1 (mandatory) applies
- Construction waste management plan (65% diversion)
- Low-VOC paints, adhesives, sealants
- Water-efficient fixtures (low-flow)
- Documentation submitted with permit closeout

**Conversion ADUs**: many CalGreen provisions don't apply to existing structures — but new construction portions (e.g., new bathroom) trigger applicable sections.

## 5. Construction Specifics — Detached ADU

### 5.1 Foundation

- **Slab on grade**: fast, cheap on flat lots; 4" slab on 4" Class 2 AB on 10 mil VB; #3 @ 18" o.c. each way (or fiber-reinforced); thickened edges at bearing walls
- **Stem wall with crawl**: better for clay soils, allows easier plumbing/electrical access; 18" min crawl height; full vapor barrier ground cover required
- **Pier and beam**: hillside or floodplain; drilled or driven piers with grade beams; soils report drives design
- **Soils report**: required for hillside (>10% slope), expansive clay, liquefaction zones, or by local mandate. Plan for $2k–$6k for a single ADU soils report.

### 5.2 Framing

- Standard platform framing; 2x6 walls increasingly common for energy compliance (cavity insulation capacity)
- High wall-to-floor area ratio in a compact ADU = more wall area per SF than a typical house = more time per SF
- Sequencing in a tight yard:
  - Material staging: confirm side yard access width at feasibility. Anything <4 ft means hand-carry; <6 ft means no Bobcat.
  - Crane / boom truck for trusses: confirm clear airspace and setbacks; often pre-stage with neighbor coordination
  - Concrete pump for slab: required if side yard <8 ft for ready-mix truck access
- Engineered lumber (LVL beams, I-joists) for long spans in compact plans

### 5.3 Exterior Envelope

- **WRB**: house wrap (Tyvek, Typar) or fluid-applied (Henry, Prosoco) — fluid-applied gaining share for tight envelopes
- **Cladding**:
  - Fiber cement (Hardie) — workhorse, fits WUI
  - Stucco — California staple, slow trade, requires hardline waterproofing detailing at penetrations
  - Wood — only with rainscreen, not WUI-compliant without ignition-resistant treatment
  - Metal panels — modern aesthetic, premium pricing, fast install
- **Roofing**:
  - Comp shingle Class A — default
  - Standing seam metal — premium, Class A, low-maintenance
  - Flat / TPO — modern look, requires careful detailing
- **Flashings**: critical at wall-to-roof, window heads, deck-to-wall — detail-heavy in compact plans

### 5.4 Windows and Doors

- **Egress** (CRC R310): every sleeping room needs an emergency escape opening
  - Min net clear opening: 5.7 SF (5.0 SF if ground floor)
  - Min height: 24"
  - Min width: 20"
  - Sill height: ≤44" from finished floor
- **Light and ventilation** (CRC R303):
  - Glazing area ≥8% of floor area
  - Openable area ≥4% of floor area
  - Or mechanical ventilation + artificial light if windows insufficient
- **Energy compliance**: U-factor ≤0.30, SHGC depending on climate zone — most California production windows meet this; verify on NFRC label

### 5.5 MEP Coordination

A 600 SF ADU has the same MEP elements as a 2,500 SF house (kitchen, bath, HVAC, electric panel, water heater), just compressed. Coordination is harder per SF, not easier.

- Mechanical: ductless mini-split is the default — 1–2 indoor heads, outdoor unit on exterior wall, no duct chases needed
- Heat pump water heater: requires 700+ CF ambient air or ducted intake — verify utility closet sizing at design
- Electrical: panel placement — exterior wall accessible (NEC 230.70); plan for future EV charger and PV inverter
- Plumbing: kitchen + bath on one wet wall = lowest plumbing cost; back-to-back plumbing minimizes pipe runs

### 5.6 Title 24 Field Verification

- **Duct leakage test**: required for any system with ducts — total leakage ≤6 CFM25 per 100 SF conditioned floor area (mini-splits exempt)
- **Envelope leakage (blower door)**: required for new ADU with prescriptive path — ≤5 ACH50 typical; some cities require 3.0 ACH50
- **Refrigerant charge test**: required for HVAC > 1 ton (mini-splits typically auto-charged at factory)
- **HERS rater coordination**: schedule rough-in (insulation inspection, duct test) and final (blower door, refrigerant) tests; budget $400–$800 per ADU

## 6. Construction Specifics — Garage Conversion (JADU)

### 6.1 Structural

- **Slab**: check thickness (drill core sample or visual inspection at edge); 4" min; if thinner, overlay or replace
- **Vapor barrier**: 10 mil min under new slab; for existing slab, surface-applied membrane or sleeper system over poly
- **Footings**: existing garage footings may be 8–12" — code-compliant for the existing load but may need underpinning if new openings change load paths
- **Lateral**: garage door opening removal becomes a major shear wall opportunity — sheath new infill wall as a shear wall with hold-downs
- **Engineer involvement**: almost always required; budget $2k–$5k for stamped plans + calcs

### 6.2 Thermal Envelope

- Existing 2x4 garage walls: typically uninsulated; insulate to R-15 batt + continuous R-5 if Title 24 requires
- Furring options: 1.5" rigid foam exterior, or 1" interior strapping with foam infill
- Ceiling: blown cellulose to R-30 to R-49 depending on climate zone; rafter framing may need ventilation baffles
- Slab: perimeter insulation R-10 to 24" deep at exterior face (Title 24 mandate for most CZs); typically achieved with 2" XPS

### 6.3 HVAC

**Recommendation**: ductless mini-split, always. Reasons:
- No duct chases needed (garage ceilings are usually low)
- No tie-in to existing system (which is sized for the primary)
- Heat pump efficient + meets Title 24 electrification preference
- Single outdoor unit, 1–2 indoor heads for typical JADU footprint
- Cost: $4k–$7k for 12–18k BTU system installed

**If extending primary system**:
- Verify capacity (Manual J calc on existing + new load)
- Existing duct trunk extension — often requires additional return air
- Furnace replacement may be needed if undersized — at which point ductless costs less

### 6.4 Electrical

- Panel: check existing service capacity; load calc on existing + new
- JADU adds: 1 kitchen circuit (20A), 1 bath circuit (20A), 1 small-appliance + lighting circuits, water heater, HVAC condenser, dryer (if), range/cooktop
- Typical JADU adds 40–60A of load — most 100A services need upgrade; 125A and 200A services usually OK with load calc
- Code: AFCI all 15/20A 120V circuits; GFCI all wet locations; tamper-resistant receptacles

### 6.5 Plumbing

- Rough-in: cut slab if no existing drain — biggest single cost driver in a conversion
  - Saw-cut + jackhammer per fixture; trench drain to existing main, sand bed, glue PVC, repour
  - Budget $4k–$10k for full bath + kitchen plumbing rough-in (slab cut)
- If wet wall can be located near existing primary plumbing on the other side of a shared wall, drain lines can run in the wall to existing — cheapest path
- Water heater: tankless or heat pump tank (50 gal); locate inside conditioned space (heat pump WH needs ambient air); vent per manufacturer

### 6.6 Egress

- Existing garage door opening: ideal location for new full-lite door or door + sidelight window
- If sleeping room created, must have an egress window meeting CRC R310 (see §5.4)
- Existing windows may be too high — replace or add a new opening

### 6.7 Fire Separation — Don't Skip This

R302.6 applies when **any** vehicle parking remains in a garage that abuts habitable space:

- Wall between garage and habitable space: 5/8" Type X gypsum on garage side, all the way to roof sheathing (continuous)
- Ceiling above garage (if habitable space above): 5/8" Type X gypsum (typically with batten or hat channel)
- Connecting door: solid-core 1-3/8" min, self-closing, self-latching, OR 20-minute fire-rated assembly
- Penetrations through fire-rated assembly: fire-stop sealant, fire-rated boxes

If the entire garage is converted (no vehicle parking remains), R302.6 does not apply — but the local AHJ may still require the wall separation if the structure is connected to the primary residence and used as a separate dwelling unit (R302.3 dwelling unit separation, 1-hour rated).

## 7. Financing ADUs — What the GC Needs to Know

### 7.1 Owner Financing Options

The financing path drives the GC's payment schedule and risk exposure. Common paths:

| Option | Mechanism | Typical Use Case | GC Implication |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Cash** | Owner pays from savings | <$100k JADU, partial fund | Standard draw schedule |
| **Cash-out refinance** | Refinance primary mortgage, pull equity | Strong equity, decent rates | Owner has funds upfront — best case for GC |
| **HELOC** | Home equity line | Phased / smaller ADUs | Draws as needed; rate variable |
| **Construction loan** | Stand-alone construction-to-perm or fix loan | Detached ADU > $200k | GC manages draw requests with lender |
| **RenoFi / specialty ADU loan** | Loan against post-completion value | High-cost ADU, low equity owner | Lender requires GC license verification, draw schedule, lien releases |
| **CalHFA ADU Grant Program** | Up to $40k pre-development grant | LMI homeowners | Status: funding paused (verify current — past rounds: Q4 2022, Q1 2023 exhausted within hours) |
| **State/local incentive programs** | Various, evolving | Region-specific | Check Casita Coalition tracker |

**Verify program status** at HCD and CalHFA before quoting an owner.

### 7.2 GC's Role in the Financing Process

If the owner is using a construction loan:

1. **Pre-construction**:
   - GC submits W-9, contractor license, insurance certs (GL + WC), bond rider
   - Detailed cost breakdown / schedule of values matching loan budget
   - Construction schedule with milestone dates
2. **Draw schedule**:
   - Typical 4–8 draws (foundation, framing, MEP rough-in, drywall, finishes, completion)
   - Each draw: invoice + photo evidence + lender inspector visit + lien waivers from subs
   - Title company funds disbursement after lender approval
3. **Lien releases**:
   - Conditional waiver on progress payment (CA Civil Code § 8132)
   - Unconditional waiver on progress payment received (CA § 8134)
   - Final unconditional waiver (CA § 8138)
   - Get them from every sub and supplier on every draw, every time
4. **Title searches**: lender may run title at each draw to verify no mechanic's liens; GC's job is to keep liens off

**Critical**: never start work without a signed contract, a clear draw schedule, and confirmation of loan funding. ADU loans regularly stall in underwriting; "we're approved, start framing" is a red flag.

### 7.3 Owner as ADU Investor — Cash Flow Math

If the owner is building to rent, basic math the GC can run as a sales tool:

- Construction cost: $250k all-in for a 700 SF detached ADU
- Market rent: $2,500/mo = $30k/yr gross
- Less operating (taxes, insurance, maintenance, vacancy): 30% = $9k
- NOI: $21k/yr
- Cap rate (against marginal construction cost): $21k / $250k = 8.4% — strong yield
- Payback period (simple): $250k / $21k = ~12 years
- Tax: increased property value adds to assessment for the ADU portion only (Prop 13 protects the rest)

This isn't financial advice — it's a back-of-envelope conversation starter that closes deals.

### 7.4 SB 9 Monetization

A GC can guide an owner through:
1. SB 9 lot split (planning/survey) + objective standards review
2. Build a second SFR + ADU on the new lot
3. Sell the new lot's house (or rent it)
4. Sale unlocks equity that funded construction; owner retains primary

Frictions: lender resistance to financing on newly created lots; CC&Rs sometimes prohibit subdivision; 3-year owner-occupancy affidavit binds owner.

### 7.5 Spec ADU Construction

GCs increasingly buy SFR lots, build primary + ADU (or use SB 9 to split), and sell with both. Considerations:

- Capital: $500k–$1.2M+ tied up per project; partnership equity or hard money usually required
- AB 1033 separate sale: only viable where locality has adopted opt-in ordinance (currently <5 jurisdictions)
- Holding period: typical 9–14 months land-to-sale
- Profit: target 15–20% gross margin; California land basis squeezes this

## 8. Small Multifamily Infill — Duplexes, Triplexes, Small Apartments

### 8.1 SB 9 in Practice

SB 9 enables up to 4 units on a former SFR lot:
- Lot split: 2 lots
- Two units per lot: 4 total
- Each unit can be a detached unit, attached unit, or duplex configuration
- Owner-occupancy affidavit required for the lot split (3 years on one of the new parcels)

Practical SB 9 build patterns:
- **Cottage cluster**: 2 small detached units per lot
- **Front + back**: existing SFR retained + new ADU-style unit at rear
- **Two duplexes**: most aggressive; common in low-density urban infill

### 8.2 SB 684 — 10-Unit Ministerial Infill

SB 684 (eff. 7/1/2024) is a sleeper bill that may rival ADU law in importance for small infill GCs:

**Eligibility**:
- Multifamily-zoned parcel (not SFR)
- ≤5 acres
- Substantially surrounded by qualified urban uses
- Served by public water and sewer
- No demolition of recently-rented housing
- Not on prime farmland, wetlands, hazardous sites, flood zones, fire severity zones, etc.

**What you get**:
- Ministerial approval (no CEQA, no public hearings, no discretionary review)
- 60-day decision shot clock
- Up to 10 units of housing
- Limitations on local objective standards that would preclude density "naturally suitable" for low-income (10–30 units/acre depending on jurisdiction)
- Average unit size ≤1,750 SF

**GC implication**: SB 684 + SB 423 (streamlined approval for multifamily on infill sites) + density bonus law = an emerging path for small infill developers to build 6–10 unit buildings without the entitlement risk that historically killed these projects. Watch this space.

### 8.3 Multifamily Construction Differences vs. SFR

**Fire separation**:
- CBC § 708 — fire partitions between dwelling units: 1-hour rated
- CBC § 711 — horizontal assemblies (floor/ceiling between stacked units): 1-hour
- Townhouse / 2-unit configuration: CBC § 706 — 2-hour fire wall (or 1-hour if sprinklered)
- Penetrations: fire-stopping per ASTM E814 — required at every penetration of rated assembly
- These details add 10–20% to MEP and framing time vs. a single-family build

**Egress**:
- Each unit needs independent egress to a public way
- Common corridors trigger Group R-2 occupancy rules — 1-hour corridor rating, panic hardware on common doors, illuminated exit signs

**Accessibility (Type B units under Fair Housing Act)**:
- Buildings with 4+ dwelling units served by common entrance: **all** ground-floor units must be Type B (basic accessibility)
- Type B requirements: 32" clear doors, accessible kitchen/bath clearances, reinforced walls for grab bars, accessible route to building
- CBC Chapter 11A (housing) and 11B (public accommodation) apply
- This is a federal requirement under FHA — California also enforces through CBC

**Sprinklers**:
- NFPA 13R typical for 4-story or less R-2 multifamily
- NFPA 13D for townhouse-style with separate egress
- 5+ stories or larger: full NFPA 13

### 8.4 Stacked Flat vs. Townhouse

| Aspect | Stacked Flat | Townhouse |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Fire separation** | Horizontal 1-hour floor/ceiling | Vertical 2-hour wall |
| **Foundation** | Single foundation system | Often separate; each unit on own footing |
| **Roof** | Single roof | Separate or split roof |
| **MEP** | Stacked plumbing chases easy; HVAC requires per-unit | Each unit fully separate MEP |
| **Cost per SF** | 5–10% lower (shared elements) | 5–10% higher (separations) |
| **Subdivision** | Condo plan required for separate sale | Can be SFR-style on separate lots (after lot split) |
| **Construction sequencing** | All units in one push | Can phase unit-by-unit |

### 8.5 Separate Utilities

**Sub-metering vs. master meter**:
- California PUC rules (PU Code § 739.5) require sub-metered tenants pay at the same rate the master would pay; cannot mark up
- **Individual meters**: each unit has direct utility account; cleanest for the owner
- **Master + sub-meters**: one utility account, owner bills tenants per usage; requires sub-meter compliance and CPUC tariff filings for some utilities
- **Allocation billing** (RUBS): for water, increasingly disallowed in CA (water sub-meter mandates per SB 7)

Practical: install individual meters when feasible. Trade-off is service drop fees ($1k–$5k per meter from utility).

## 9. The GC as ADU Specialist — Business Strategy

### 9.1 Market Drivers (2025)

- **Housing shortage**: California is short ~1M units; ADUs accounted for ~17% of new housing permits statewide in 2023, ~25% in LA County
- **Rental income**: $2k–$4k/mo in most metros; high-yield investment vs. stock market
- **Multigenerational living**: aging parents, adult children, family flexibility
- **Aging in place**: owners building ADUs to downsize into and rent main house
- **AB 1033 separate sale**: emerging market once more cities adopt
- **SB 9 + SB 684**: small infill is the new frontier

ADU permits issued in CA: ~10,000 (2018) → ~28,000 (2023). Growth has plateaued in some regions due to construction cost; cost-conscious products (prefab, conversion) are gaining share.

### 9.2 Positioning Yourself

**Productize your offering**:
- Pre-approved plan partnerships (3–5 architects you trust with permit-ready plans)
- Tiered pricing tiers: budget, mid, premium with clear scope inclusions
- Sample contracts, draw schedules, change order forms ready to send
- Photo library of past work organized by type (JADU, detached, conversion)

**Build a referral network**:
- Realtors selling SFR with ADU potential — lead source #1
- Estate planning attorneys — multigenerational and post-death property uses
- Mortgage brokers and ADU lenders (Renofi, Mosaic, A&D Mortgage)
- Architects and designers specializing in ADU
- Solar installers (cross-promotion: build the ADU + add PV)
- Local YIMBY chapters (advocacy + lead source)

**Online presence**:
- Project pages with cost, timeline, photos
- Google Business Profile with reviews — ADU buyers research heavily
- Casita Coalition member listing if you serve California broadly

### 9.3 Typical Project Cost Ranges (California 2025)

| Type | Range | Drivers |
|------|-------|---------|
| **JADU (garage half conversion, no slab work)** | $40k–$80k | Minimal MEP, no new foundation, no exterior changes |
| **Full garage to ADU conversion** | $100k–$180k | Slab cut, full MEP, insulation, possible exterior changes |
| **Attached ADU** | $150k–$250k | New construction tied to existing; engineer scope |
| **Detached ADU (600–800 SF)** | $200k–$400k | Full new construction; site work; foundation; finishes drive top end |
| **Detached ADU (1,000–1,200 SF, premium finish)** | $350k–$550k | High-end finishes; hillside; complex roof |
| **2-story detached ADU on multifamily lot (SB 1211)** | $300k–$500k | Engineering complexity; access constraints |
| **SB 9 lot split + duplex** | $700k–$1.2M | New construction across 2 lots; survey + entitlement; utility splits |

**Cost per SF reality check** (turnkey, 2025):
- Conversion: $200–$400/SF
- New detached: $400–$650/SF
- Premium new detached (high-design, slope, accessibility): $700–$900/SF

These exceed primary SFR construction $/SF because of compact-plan inefficiency (more mechanical, finish, and finish-trim per SF). Set owner expectations early.

### 9.4 Timeline

| Phase | JADU | Detached ADU | SB 684 (10 unit) |
|-------|------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Feasibility & design** | 2–6 weeks | 4–12 weeks | 8–16 weeks |
| **Permit (submittal to issuance)** | 4–10 weeks | 8–16 weeks | 12–24 weeks |
| **Construction** | 8–14 weeks | 16–28 weeks | 8–14 months |
| **Total (start of design to occupancy)** | 4–6 months | 7–12 months | 18–30 months |

Plan for the longer end. The 60-day ministerial deadline gets blown more than half the time because of completeness disputes, plus utility coordination (PG&E new service can be 6 months).

### 9.5 Risk Management

**Scope creep on garage conversions** — the single biggest profit killer:
- Hidden structural issues (rotted plates, undersized foundation, drainage problems)
- Slab not level enough — adds days of self-leveler work
- Existing electrical service inadequate
- Existing sewer lateral failed
- Mitigation: open-up allowance in contract ($5k–$15k contingency); written change-order procedure; demolition-phase walk-through with engineer

**Owner financing delays**:
- Construction loan stalls in underwriting; appraiser inexperience with ADU comps
- Cash-out refi rate-lock expires
- HELOC frozen by lender mid-project (rare but happens)
- Mitigation: don't break ground without loan in funded escrow; verify draw process before mobilization

**Utility upgrade surprises**:
- PG&E/SCE service upgrade lead times (3–6 months); easement requirements; trenching scope
- Sewer lateral discovery requires replacement
- Water meter upgrade not anticipated
- Mitigation: utility check at feasibility; written confirmation from utility before bid

**Permit risk**:
- Plan check kickbacks on items that seem straightforward
- AHJ inspector interpretation differences (Title 24 HERS testing, R302.6 details)
- Mitigation: pre-application meeting + relationship with local plans examiner

**Subcontractor capacity**:
- ADU work is high-touch low-revenue per project; many subs deprioritize small jobs
- Mitigation: dedicate a "small project" sub bench; offer schedule flexibility; build relationships before you need them

**Contractor's license / insurance**:
- ADU work is residential, B license adequate; some structural work needs C-5 framing sub
- WC mandatory in CA for any employees; verify all sub WC
- General liability: $1M/$2M minimum; many lenders require $2M aggregate

### 9.6 Building a Repeat Engine

ADU clients refer at 2–3× the rate of SFR remodel clients because:
- Personal investment story they share
- Visible, photo-worthy outcome
- Rental cash flow story to neighbors

Capture this:
- Photo + video on completion (release form)
- Owner testimonial (written and video)
- 6-month and 12-month follow-up call (NPS + referrals)
- Annual newsletter to past clients with policy updates (new laws each year keep you relevant)

## 12. Closing — Why ADU Is the Right Specialty

For a residential GC in California in 2025:

- **Demand is structural** — California housing shortage isn't going away; ADU is permitted and incentivized policy direction
- **Margins are healthier than SFR remodel** — per-project complexity supports premium pricing
- **Regulatory tailwind** — state law expands every year, locals have less discretion to slow projects
- **Recurring referral engine** — ADU owners refer at high rates
- **Optionality** — JADU work feeds detached ADU work feeds SB 9/SB 684 small infill feeds spec development. A GC can climb the value chain over a 5–10 year horizon, from $40k JADU projects to $1M+ infill developments, with the same regulatory knowledge base.

Master the law and the typical conversion failure modes. The rest is execution — and execution is what GCs are for.
