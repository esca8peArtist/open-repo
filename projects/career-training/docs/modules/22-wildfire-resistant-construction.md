# Wildfire-Resistant Construction for the California General Contractor

> **Why this matters now**: After the January 2025 Palisades and Eaton fires (the most expensive wildfire event in U.S. history), the FAIR Plan reported roughly **$4 billion in losses** and pushed for a 36% rate increase. Private carriers continue to non-renew in WUI zip codes. Your client's question is no longer "Will this pass code?" — it is "Will this be insurable in five years?" Code-minimum is the floor; insurable-minimum is the target. A GC who can speak fluently to Chapter 7A, the IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home standard, and the California "Safer from Wildfires" discount matrix is a *trusted advisor* — not a price-takers in a commodity bid.

---

## 1. The California Wildfire Context for Builders

### 1.1 The WUI defined

The **Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI)** is any area where human development meets or intermingles with undeveloped wildland fuels. In California regulation, "WUI Fire Area" is a regulatory designation that drives whether **Chapter 7A** (or, beginning 2026, the new **California Wildland-Urban Interface Code, Title 24 Part 7**) applies. WUI status is determined by two overlapping concepts:

1. **Responsibility area** (who has fire-protection jurisdiction):
   - **State Responsibility Area (SRA)** — CAL FIRE has primary wildland fire protection responsibility (mostly rural and unincorporated lands).
   - **Local Responsibility Area (LRA)** — cities and local fire districts hold primary responsibility (most incorporated areas).
   - **Federal Responsibility Area (FRA)** — federal land (USFS, BLM, NPS).
2. **Fire Hazard Severity Zone (FHSZ)** — the hazard rating assigned to a parcel: **Moderate, High, or Very High**.

### 1.2 Determining if a property is WUI: the FHSZ lookup

The single most important first step on any project in a hill, canyon, foothill, or rural parcel is **pulling the FHSZ for the address**.

- **Primary tool:** CAL FIRE Fire Hazard Severity Zone Viewer (ArcGIS): https://experience.arcgis.com/experience/03beab8511814e79a0e4eabf0d3e7247
- **Hotline / questions:** (916) 633-7655 or `FHSZinformation@fire.ca.gov`
- **Print the map** showing the parcel and FHSZ designation; include it in your project file. Plan checkers and underwriters will both ask for it.

**Current FHSZ effective dates (critical — these maps were just updated):**

| Map | Adopted / Effective |
|---|---|
| SRA FHSZ (current adopted map) | Effective **April 1, 2024** |
| LRA FHSZ Phase 1 | Released Feb 10, 2025 |
| LRA FHSZ Phase 2 | Released Feb 24, 2025 |
| LRA FHSZ Phase 3 | Released March 10, 2025 |
| LRA FHSZ Phase 4 | Released March 24, 2025 |
| **LRA FHSZ effective date** | **July 1, 2025** |

The 2025 LRA update was the **first major remap since 2011** and expanded mapped Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ) acreage substantially in many counties. **A parcel that was previously unmapped may now be in a designated High or VHFHSZ.** Always re-verify on the current viewer — do not rely on a prior survey or NHD report older than mid-2025.

### 1.3 SRA vs. LRA — how it affects your permit and code stack

| Aspect | State Responsibility Area (SRA) | Local Responsibility Area (LRA) |
|---|---|---|
| Wildland fire protection | CAL FIRE | City / county / local fire district |
| Chapter 7A triggered by | **Moderate, High, or Very High** FHSZ in SRA (per PRC §4291 + CBC) | Locally adopted **VHFHSZ** (and in some jurisdictions, High) |
| Defensible space enforcement | PRC §4291 (CAL FIRE inspections) | Local ordinance, locally enforced |
| Plan review | Local building department; CAL FIRE may review for road/access | Local building dept + local fire authority having jurisdiction (AHJ) |
| Common AHJs | County building dept; CAL FIRE Unit | City building dept; municipal/district fire marshal |

**Practical takeaway:** in SRA, Chapter 7A is keyed to the FHSZ; in LRA, you must check whether the local jurisdiction has adopted Chapter 7A *and* whether they applied it only to VHFHSZ or extended it to High FHSZ. Many LRA cities also impose **local amendments** (e.g., Class A roofs *everywhere* in city limits, regardless of zone). Always pull the city's local amendments to the CBC and CRC.

### 1.4 Fire Hazard Severity Zones — what changes by zone

California uses three tiers (there is **no "Extreme" zone** in CAL FIRE's official terminology — that wording often appears in insurance ratings or media but not the regulation):

| Zone | Where it triggers Chapter 7A | Typical construction implications |
|---|---|---|
| **Moderate FHSZ** | SRA: Chapter 7A applies. LRA: usually does not. | Chapter 7A in SRA; commonly Class A roof; ember-resistant vents recommended. Insurance may still rate as wildfire-exposed. |
| **High FHSZ** | SRA: Chapter 7A applies. LRA: depends on local adoption. | Full Chapter 7A package required in SRA. Strong insurance pressure to fully harden. |
| **Very High FHSZ (VHFHSZ)** | **Always triggers Chapter 7A** in both SRA and LRA. Triggers **AB 38 disclosure** at sale. | Full Chapter 7A, plus higher likelihood of carrier non-renewal without IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home or equivalent. Defensible space PRC §4291 inspections. |

### 1.5 California's expanding WUI and the insurance crisis

- **Why the WUI is expanding:** longer fire seasons, drier fuels, expansion of housing into foothills and chaparral, more billion-dollar fires year over year. The 2025 LRA remap added thousands of California homes to High and Very High FHSZ designations.
- **Insurance crisis dynamics:**
  - **Non-renewals:** State Farm, Allstate, Farmers, USAA, Liberty Mutual have all pulled back from new business or non-renewed policies in WUI zip codes since 2020. State Farm announced major non-renewals across CA in 2023–2024.
  - **FAIR Plan exposure:** the state-mandated insurer-of-last-resort has ballooned. After January 2025 fires it sought a **36% rate increase** to remain solvent. Source: California Department of Insurance (https://www.insurance.ca.gov/).
  - **Policy response:** Commissioner Lara's "Sustainable Insurance Strategy" (2024–2025) — carriers can use catastrophe models in rate filings, *but* must write a percentage of new business in distressed (WUI) zip codes. The **Business Insurance Protection Act (SB 547)** extended moratoriums on non-renewals into commercial and HOA policies. The **FAIR Plan Stability Act (AB 226)** authorized catastrophe bonds and lines of credit through CalIBank.

### 1.6 Insurance implications for GCs — the conversation to have on day one

**Tell your client, in writing, before signing:** code-minimum construction is *not* insurable-minimum in many WUI zones. Building only to Chapter 7A may pass inspection and still result in a home that no admitted carrier will write at affordable rates. You should present three tiers in any WUI-area bid:

| Tier | Standard | Cost premium (typical, varies by design) | Insurance position |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Code minimum** | Chapter 7A as required by zone | Baseline | May fall to FAIR Plan + DIC wrap; expect 2–4× the premium of inland equivalents |
| **B — Code + Safer from Wildfires** | Chapter 7A + the 8–12 CDI hardening actions | +3–8% on shell | Qualifies for CDI-mandated discounts at admitted carriers (if they remain in the zip) |
| **C — IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home (Base or Plus)** | Independent certification, audit, recert every 3 years | +5–15% on shell | Some carriers offer significant discounts; emerging market acceptance; meaningful re-sale value |

The GC's role is **not** to sell insurance — it is to make the cost/benefit explicit so the homeowner makes an informed structural decision *before* framing.

## 2. California WUI Building Code Requirements — CBC Chapter 7A

> **2026 code reorganization note:** Under the 2025 Title 24 cycle (effective **January 1, 2026**), the Building Standards Commission **deleted Chapter 7A from CBC Part 2** and relocated its provisions into the new **California Wildland-Urban Interface Code (CWUIC), Title 24 Part 7**. Technical requirements carry forward substantially unchanged for the 2026 code cycle. References below use the long-standing "Chapter 7A / 7nnA" section numbering most contractors recognize; the substantive content is the same in CWUIC.

### 2.1 When Chapter 7A applies

- **New construction** in any FHSZ where the jurisdiction has adopted Chapter 7A (always in SRA Moderate/High/VHFHSZ; LRA VHFHSZ; locally, sometimes High FHSZ).
- **Additions** to existing structures in those zones.
- **Substantial repairs/replacements** of regulated assemblies (e.g., re-roof, residing, deck rebuild).
- **Rebuilds after fire** — this is the big one. **Pre-existing non-conforming construction does not get grandfathered when destroyed by fire** — the rebuild must meet current Chapter 7A.

### 2.2 The five ignition vectors Chapter 7A is designed to defeat

| # | Vector | What it is | Where it kills you |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Embers** | Burning brands carried by wind, sometimes miles ahead of the front | Vents, gutters, decks, gaps at eaves, debris on roof |
| 2 | **Radiant heat** | Heat radiating from a nearby burning fuel source or neighbor's structure | Glazing (single-pane glass cracks), siding, exposed combustibles |
| 3 | **Direct flame contact** | A flame touching the assembly | Decks under windows, fences attached to house, shrubs in Zone 0 |
| 4 | **Convective heat** | Hot gases moving up a slope or through an opening | Eaves on the down-slope side, attic spaces, soffits |
| 5 | **Spotting / brand showers** | Concentrated ember deposition on a single feature | Roof valleys, accumulated leaves in gutters, deck boards |

**Embers are the dominant cause of structure loss** — research consistently shows roughly two-thirds of structures lost in WUI fires ignite from embers rather than direct flame contact. This is why vent design and Zone 0 matter as much as siding choice.

### 2.3 Roofing — CBC 707A

**Required:** Class A fire-rated roof covering or **Class A roof assembly** (tested per ASTM E108 or UL 790). Class A is the highest of three classes (A, B, C) and describes how an assembly resists flame spread, intermittent flame, and burning brand exposure.

**Materials that qualify when correctly assembled:**

| Material | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Asphalt fiberglass composition shingles** | Many GAF Timberline, CertainTeed Landmark, and Owens Corning Duration products carry Class A *as an assembly* — only with the manufacturer's specified underlayment and (sometimes) a Type X gypsum or mineral-surfaced cap sheet. **Class A is a property of the assembly, not just the shingle.** |
| **Concrete tile / clay tile** | The most fire-resistant common roof. Birdstop foam at eaves and ridge required to block ember entry. |
| **Standing-seam or exposed-fastener metal panels** | 24–26 ga steel typical; verify the *listed* assembly includes the substrate (plywood + underlayment). |
| **Slate, brick, fired-clay shingle** | Naturally Class A. |
| **Fiber cement tile / shake** | Yes if listed; verify Cal Fire BML. |

**Materials that look Class A but often aren't:**

- **Wood shake / shingle** — even "treated" pressure-impregnated FRT wood shake **rarely retains Class A** over the life of the roof; almost no jurisdiction in WUI accepts it any more. **Do not propose wood shake in any WUI project.** Replace as part of any reroof scope.
- **TPO / PVC single-ply membranes** — Class A is achievable in the assembly but is not the membrane alone; confirm listing.
- **"Class A rated shingle" as a marketing line** — verify it is the assembly that is rated. Check the actual UL or FM test report number.

**Critical detailing:**
- **Valleys:** must be either closed-cut with Class A material or lined with 26 ga (min) noncombustible metal flashing; no exposed underlayment.
- **Eaves and rakes:** drip edge required; no exposed combustible underside.
- **Ridge vents:** must use a *listed ember-resistant ridge vent* (e.g., Brandguard, Vulcan, O'Hagin Fire & Ice). A standard plastic ridge vent in WUI is a fail point.
- **Open eave joints / between rafter tails:** see soffit/eave section below.

### 2.4 Gutters and Downspouts — CBC 708A

- **Noncombustible gutters and downspouts required** (aluminum, galvanized steel, copper, stainless). PVC vinyl gutters are out.
- If you use gutters at all, **you must install a gutter cover/screen** that prevents debris (pine needles, leaves) from accumulating. Mesh size and material must prevent ember-receptive fuel from collecting.
- **Acceptable products:** Gutterglove Pro, LeafFilter, K-Screen, and other noncombustible micromesh systems. Avoid foam inserts (they hold debris and can themselves combust).
- **Detail point:** verify the gutter cover does not create a debris shelf where embers can land *on top* of leaves. The point is to keep needles *out*, not to collect them on a cover.

### 2.5 Eaves and Soffits — CBC 709A

**Eaves are the #1 architectural fire failure point** because hot gases and embers travel up walls and into the soffit cavity.

**Compliant approaches** (in order of preference):

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Enclosed soffits with noncombustible material** | Fiber-cement soffit panel (James Hardie Soffit), aluminum soffit, or fire-rated gypsum on the underside of the rafter tails. Best practice. |
| **Boxed-in eave with 1-hour fire-resistive assembly** | Type X gypsum on the underside; fully enclosed. |
| **Ignition-resistant materials** (SFM 12-7A-5 listed) | Acceptable but rarely cost-competitive vs. fiber cement. |
| **Open-eave construction** | Permitted only if blocking between rafters and all exposed wood meets ignition-resistant requirements. **Strongly discouraged in High/VHFHSZ** — the small gain in attic ventilation is not worth the ember exposure. |

**Vented vs. unvented eaves:**
- **Best practice in WUI:** unvented attic with closed-cell spray foam at the roof deck (or a hot-roof assembly) eliminates soffit vents entirely. This is the cleanest ember solution and often a code-compliant alternative path (CRC R806.5).
- **If you must vent at the soffit:** install **listed ember- and flame-resistant vents** (Brandguard, Vulcan, O'Hagin Fire & Ice). 1/16"–1/8" corrosion-resistant mesh is the *legacy* compliance path; ASTM E2886-listed vents are the modern standard and are what most plan checkers expect now.

### 2.6 Exterior Walls — CBC 710A

Exterior wall covering shall be of one of the following:

| Acceptable | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Noncombustible material** | Stucco (3-coat, minimum 7/8" over WRB and wire lath — the gold standard), brick, stone veneer, concrete, structural masonry. |
| **Ignition-resistant material per SFM 12-7A-5** | Fiber cement (James Hardie Artisan, Cemplank, Cempanel, HardieShingle all OSFM-listed); some fire-retardant-treated lumber assemblies. |
| **Heavy timber** | Minimum nominal 4× thickness; rare in residential. |
| **Log wall** | Solid log minimum 6" thickness, narrow joints. |
| **5/8" thick exterior fire-retardant-treated wood** | Acceptable if SFM-listed; verify the specific product. |

**Not acceptable in WUI:**
- Untreated wood lap siding (cedar bevel, redwood lap, untreated T1-11)
- Vinyl siding — melts at relatively low temperatures, exposing sheathing
- Standard hardboard / OSB siding without ignition-resistant listing

**Combustible lap siding (wood) requirements:** if you use wood siding at all, it must be **fire-retardant-treated, SFM-listed product** with a specific listing for exterior WUI use. Even then, most plan checkers and insurers will push back. Default to fiber cement or stucco unless the architectural program demands wood.

**Vertical extension of wall covering:** wall covering must extend from the top of the foundation to the underside of the roof sheathing; no exposed sheathing or framing.

### 2.7 Decks, Porches, Balconies, and Stair Surfaces — CBC 710A.4

**The deck is often the ignition point of the house.** Embers land in the gaps and under-deck debris, the decking ignites, flame attacks the underside of the wall, and the house is lost.

**Required for any walking surface within 10 ft of the building** (deck, porch, balcony, exterior stair):

| Option | Description |
|---|---|
| **Noncombustible** | Concrete pavers, poured concrete, stone, steel grating. |
| **Ignition-resistant** | Must pass **SFM 12-7A-4** (under-deck flame exposure) and **SFM 12-7A-5** (flame spread). |
| **Listed exterior fire-retardant-treated wood** | Verify product on the CAL FIRE BML. |
| **Heavy timber** | 4× nominal or greater. |

**Under-deck enclosure:** the underside of the deck within 10 ft must be either:
- Enclosed with 1-hour fire-resistive construction (Type X gypsum + WRB or fiber cement skirting), **or**
- Constructed entirely of ignition-resistant / noncombustible material such that combustibles cannot accumulate beneath.

**At-grade surfaces within 10 ft of the building:** must be noncombustible (concrete, pavers, gravel) — **no bark mulch, no wood chips, no wood deck-on-grade.** This is also the Zone 0 requirement; see §4.

### 2.8 Definitions to memorize — CBC 701A.3

These are *test-defined categories*, not intuitive ones. Use them precisely in plans and submittals.

| Term | Means |
|---|---|
| **Noncombustible material** | Material that passes **ASTM E136** (no combustion in a 750 °C furnace) **or** assembly with structural base of noncombustible material and combustible surface < 1/8" thick with flame spread ≤ 50. |
| **Ignition-resistant material** | Material that passes **SFM Standard 12-7A-5** (extended ASTM E84 with 30-minute exposure, flame spread ≤ 25, no flaming beyond 10 ft, no progressive combustion). Fiber cement and some FRT wood products qualify. |
| **Heavy timber** | Minimum nominal dimensions per IBC Chapter 6 (e.g., columns ≥ 8×8, beams ≥ 6×10, decking ≥ 3×). |
| **Fire-retardant-treated wood** | Pressure-impregnated wood meeting AWPA U1 for fire retardancy; must be SFM-listed for exterior use to count in WUI. |

### 2.9 Exterior Windows and Skylights — CBC 712A

**Required:** Exterior windows, window walls, glazed doors, and skylights shall be one of the following:

1. Multi-pane glazing with **at least one tempered pane**, *or*
2. **20-minute fire-resistance-rated** glazing (tested per NFPA 257), *or*
3. **Glass-block** assemblies.

**Frame material:**
- **Aluminum** — acceptable; performs well in heat.
- **Fiberglass** — acceptable; lower thermal conductivity than aluminum.
- **Vinyl** — **acceptable per code if dual-pane tempered, but a known weak point**: vinyl frames distort/melt under sustained radiant heat, dropping the glass. Many fire-hardening specs and the IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home standard prefer metal or fiberglass.
- **Wood frames** — only acceptable if SFM-listed for WUI; most are not.

**Skylights:** must be tempered or fire-rated; flat skylights collect debris and embers — slope helps.

### 2.10 Vents — CBC 713A (the #1 ember entry point)

Every vent into a roof, attic, soffit, gable, or crawlspace is a potential ember intake. Chapter 7A allows two compliance paths:

| Path | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Prescriptive (legacy)** | Noncombustible, corrosion-resistant mesh, **1/16" to 1/8" opening size**, covering the full vent area. The mesh must be metal — fiberglass insect screen fails. |
| **Listed (modern, preferred)** | Use a vent listed to **ASTM E2886** (ember and flame intrusion test) on the OSFM Building Materials Listing (BML). Examples below. |

**Listed ember-resistant vent manufacturers (OSFM BML):**

| Manufacturer | Product line | Applications |
|---|---|---|
| **Brandguard** | Soffit, eave, dormer, gable, foundation, ridge | All vent types |
| **Vulcan Vents** | Soffit, eave, gable, foundation, dryer, ridge | Intumescent strip closes vent under flame |
| **O'Hagin** | **Fire & Ice** roof vent line; also gable and soffit | Mostly low-profile roof vents |
| Embers Out | Soffit and gable | Less common |
| StealthVent | Roof and soffit | Newer entrant |

**Detailing rules:**
- **Foundation/crawlspace vents:** retrofit market is huge — most pre-2008 houses have unscreened or 1/4" mesh foundation vents that fail Chapter 7A. Replace with listed ember-resistant foundation vents (Brandguard FVS, Vulcan VFV).
- **Attic vents:** if you have a vented attic, replace all gable and soffit vents with listed product. Better yet, **switch to an unvented (sealed) attic** with closed-cell spray foam at the roof deck — eliminating the vector entirely.
- **Dryer vents and bath fan terminations:** must have noncombustible cover and backdraft damper; if listed product available, use it. Per CRC, dryer vents shall not have screens (lint clogs), but listed ember-resistant dryer terminations now exist (Brandguard, DryerJack with damper).
- **Vents on the underside of eaves, in the soffit:** prohibited in some jurisdictions per local Chapter 7A amendments; check AHJ.

### 2.11 Exterior Doors — CBC 714A

- **Solid-core wood doors** (minimum 1-3/8") or
- **Noncombustible doors** (steel, fiberglass, aluminum) or
- **20-minute fire-rated assembly**.

Glazed doors follow the window glazing rules.

**Garage doors:**
- Must meet the same materials standard for solid surface area.
- **Weatherstripping and gaps**: a single-car garage door with a 1/2" gap at the bottom is an ember intake big enough to ignite a stack of boxes. Spec a **WUI-grade garage door with continuous bottom seal and side weatherstrip**. Wayne Dalton, Clopay, and Amarr have WUI-rated lines.
- **Pet doors and mail slots:** discouraged on exterior doors in WUI. If required, use a metal-flap, gasketed unit.

## 3. Insurance-Driven Standards — Beyond Code

### 3.1 California's "Safer from Wildfires" regulation (CDI, 2022)

The California Department of Insurance adopted "Safer from Wildfires" in fall 2022 — the first regulation in the nation **requiring insurers to give discounts** for wildfire mitigation. Insurers that use wildfire risk in pricing must also offer discounts when these measures are present:

**Home/parcel-level actions (10 items):**

1. **Class A fire-rated roof**
2. **5-ft ember-resistant Zone 0** (no combustibles within 5 ft of the home)
3. **Ember- and fire-resistant vents** (1/16–1/8" metal mesh or ASTM E2886 listed)
4. **Cleared yard and roof of debris** (annual maintenance)
5. **Defensible space compliance** (PRC 4291 / 100 ft)
6. **Multi-pane / tempered windows**
7. **Enclosed eaves** (ignition-resistant or noncombustible soffits)
8. **Upgraded exterior** (fiber cement, stucco, masonry, or other ignition-resistant)
9. **Upgraded deck** (ignition-resistant / noncombustible)
10. **Water supply** (community or on-site for firefighter use)

**Community-level actions (2 items):**

11. **Firewise USA** participation (community designation)
12. **Fire Risk Reduction Community** designation (CAL FIRE)

**Discount magnitude:** ranges from a few percent up to **~50%+** off the wildfire portion of premium with full stack, varies by carrier.

Source: California Department of Insurance, "Safer from Wildfires": https://www.insurance.ca.gov/01-consumers/200-wrr/Safer-from-Wildfires.cfm

### 3.2 California FAIR Plan hardening discounts (Nov 15, 2025 onwards)

The FAIR Plan filed and received approval for a **12-discount wildfire hardening discount matrix** effective **for policies dated November 15, 2025 or later**. Categories:

| Category | # of discounts | Approx max savings |
|---|---|---|
| Immediate Surroundings (Zone 0 / 0–5 ft) | 5 | — |
| Structure (vents, roof, siding, windows, deck) | 5 | — |
| Property-level Completion bonus | 1 | — |
| Community designation (Firewise / FRRC) | 1 | — |
| **Dwelling Fire policy total** | **12** | **up to 16.4%** |
| **Commercial policy total** | **12** | **up to 13.8%** |

This is on the *wildfire portion* of premium only (which is typically the bulk of FAIR Plan premium in a WUI zone).

### 3.3 IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home — Base and Plus

The Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety launched **Wildfire Prepared Home** in 2022; updated standard published 2024. This is the wildfire equivalent of the established **FORTIFIED Home** program (which targets hurricane/hail/wind).

**Base level (entry tier — focus: embers):**

- Class A roof, sealed and free of gaps
- 5-ft noncombustible Zone 0 (no plants, mulch, fences-to-house, wood piles, gas tanks, doormats, furniture)
- Ember-resistant vents (ASTM E2886 listed)
- Enclosed eaves (noncombustible or ignition-resistant soffit)
- Gutters cleared and protected (or gutter-less design)
- 30-ft defensible space well-maintained
- Specific deck and fence requirements (no combustible fence within 5 ft of home)

**Plus level (adds radiant heat / direct flame defense):**

- Everything in Base, plus
- Noncombustible siding (or fiber cement)
- Tempered/multi-pane windows
- Noncombustible deck or no deck within 10 ft
- Stricter material standards on outbuildings
- Annual maintenance audit; **full recertification every 3 years**

**Inspection:** by an IBHS-trained evaluator. Designation is a property-attached credential. A growing number of California carriers (Mercury, USAA, Farmers, others on case-by-case basis) recognize the designation for either rate filings or willingness to underwrite.

Source: https://wildfireprepared.org/

### 3.4 IBHS FORTIFIED — relevant comparison

FORTIFIED is the long-running IBHS program for hurricane / wind / hail / hailstorm — **not a wildfire program**. The three tiers (FORTIFIED Roof, Silver, Gold) target sealed roof deck, improved attachment, garage door bracing, etc. In California it has limited direct relevance except where the GC also works on the Gulf or East Coast — but the *methodology* (independent verification + maintenance recert) is essentially the model IBHS used to design Wildfire Prepared Home.

### 3.5 Carrier-by-carrier patterns (general — verify each policy)

| Carrier | Position in CA WUI (2025) | What helps |
|---|---|---|
| **State Farm** | Major non-renewals 2023–2024, restricted new business | Full Safer from Wildfires stack + IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home is the strongest path to retention/re-write |
| **Allstate** | Largely paused new business in CA | Same |
| **Farmers** | Limited new business; offers discounts for mitigation | Hardening + community designation matters |
| **USAA** | Continues to write members; underwrites carefully on WUI exposure | Specific concern with roof condition, defensible space, ember vents |
| **Mercury** | Continues writing in CA, including some WUI; meaningful hardening discounts | Documentation of mitigation actions, photos |
| **CSAA / AAA** | Selective | Mitigation matters |
| **FAIR Plan** | Insurer of last resort; growing book; rate-stressed | The 12-discount matrix; community designations |

**Tell your client:** carrier acceptance is moving toward **documentation-based underwriting**. Photos, an IBHS designation, a Firewise USA community membership, and an AB-38-style home hardening checklist will materially affect whether they get an admitted carrier or end up on FAIR Plan + DIC.

## 4. Ember-Resistant Construction — Going Beyond Code

### 4.1 The ember ignition model

The dominant ignition path for WUI structure loss is **wind-blown embers** — small burning brands carried by the fire's plume sometimes a mile or more ahead of the front. A home does not need to be *near* the fire to be lost. The chain is:

1. Ember storm arrives, sometimes 30–60 minutes before flame front (sometimes hours, sometimes never reaches flame).
2. Embers accumulate in gutters, on decks, against fences-to-house, in vent intakes, in cracked window seals, under doormats.
3. One ignition takes hold (usually deck, vent, or roof debris).
4. Fire enters the structure and burns from inside out.

**This means:** the most cost-effective fire hardening dollar is on **closing ember intake paths**, not on more expensive cladding. A house with cedar siding and listed ember-resistant vents + Zone 0 may survive an ember storm; a house with stucco walls and stock soffit vents may not.

### 4.2 The Three Zones — defensible space (PRC §4291)

> **GC scope caveat:** defensible space *vegetation management* is the property owner's responsibility, not the GC's. But you must understand it because **construction details and landscape choices together determine survival** — and Zone 0 in particular often falls in your scope (hardscape, fence, deck materials, ground cover) even when planting is the owner's.

| Zone | Distance | Standard | What it means |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Zone 0** | 0–5 ft from structure (and under decks/eaves) | **Ember-resistant zone** | No combustible mulch (wood chips, bark), no wood fences attached to house, no shrubs or plants, no firewood piles, no propane tanks. Hardscape only: concrete, pavers, gravel, decomposed granite. Doormats: rubber/synthetic. |
| **Zone 1** | 5–30 ft | **Lean, clean, green** | Plants permitted but spaced; trees limbed up 6 ft; dead wood removed; lawn mowed; mulch limited to noncombustible or composted (not bark). |
| **Zone 2** | 30–100 ft (or to property line) | **Reduced fuel zone** | Tree spacing per CAL FIRE; ladder fuels removed; grass mowed to 4". |

**Zone 0 regulatory status (CRITICAL — evolving):**

- **Statutory basis:** Public Resources Code §4291 amended in 2020 to require the Board of Forestry to develop a 0–5 ft ember-resistant zone regulation.
- **Executive Order N-18-25** (issued by Governor Newsom in early 2025) accelerated rulemaking; the Board of Forestry was directed to complete Zone 0 regulations by **December 31, 2025**.
- **Expected application:** new construction upon effect; existing homes phased in over **3 years**.
- **Local jurisdictions ahead of the state:** Berkeley (5-ft vegetation ban now in effect Jan 2026), Murrieta, and others have adopted local Zone 0 ordinances ahead of statewide rulemaking. Always check local code for stricter requirements.

**Practical Zone 0 design moves for the GC:**

- Concrete walk or pavers all the way around the house, minimum 3 ft wide (5 ft on uphill side).
- No wood gates or fences attached to the structure; if needed, the section within 5 ft of the house must be noncombustible (steel, masonry).
- Trash bins, recycle bins, firewood piles, propane tanks — **none of these go within 5 ft of any wall or under any deck**. Design a dedicated, accessible bin alcove at the curb-side or in a detached structure.
- Roof eaves drip line should not overlap planted landscape directly under the eave — even a rosemary bush under the eave is a Zone 0 violation in spirit and (soon) in law.

### 4.3 Construction details that matter most for ember defense

| Detail | Why it matters | Spec |
|---|---|---|
| **Gutter guards** | Pine needles in gutters are the most reliable ignition source on a Class A roof | Noncombustible micromesh, lifetime warranty; clean annually |
| **Enclosed eaves** | Hot gases and embers travel up the wall and into open eaves | Fiber cement soffit panels with listed vents |
| **Ember-resistant vents** | The biggest single point-of-entry | Brandguard / Vulcan / O'Hagin Fire & Ice |
| **Garage door seal** | A 1/2" floor gap admits embers; box contents are very combustible | WUI-rated garage door, continuous bottom seal, side & top weatherstrip |
| **Door sweeps** | Same vector at every exterior door | Adjustable metal sweep with neoprene gasket |
| **Window/door weather seals** | Heat distortion + ember intrusion | Inspect on existing homes; replace cracked seals |
| **Sub-floor / crawlspace vents** | Often overlooked; ground-level embers easy to ingest | Brandguard FVS or Vulcan VFV foundation vents |
| **Roof-to-wall flashing** | Embers collect at this transition | Continuous metal step flashing; no exposed underlayment |
| **Skylight curbs** | Debris collection point | Slope, keep clear; consider eliminating in High/VHFHSZ |
| **Fence-to-house attachment** | A burning wood fence becomes a wick into the wall | Either change last 5 ft of fence to noncombustible, or use a steel gate between fence and house |

## 5. Rebuild After Fire — Permitting and Process

### 5.1 California's disaster-response permitting toolkit

After the 2025 LA fires (Palisades, Eaton, Hurst), the Governor issued multiple executive orders to compress rebuild timelines. Key mechanisms:

| EO / Law | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Executive Order N-4-25** (Jan 12, 2025) | Suspended CEQA and Coastal Act review for like-for-like rebuilds in burn-scar areas |
| **Executive Order N-29-25** (July 2025) | Further streamlined state permitting; required state agencies to issue rebuild-relevant permits in compressed timeframes |
| **AB 38 (2019)** | Imposed home-hardening disclosure on sales in VHFHSZ; required state to develop joint hardening program |
| **Local "like-for-like" permitting** (LA, etc.) | Rebuilds at ≤110% of original footprint and rough dimensions get fast-track plan check (planning approvals in 3 days vs. months) |

**Per the Governor's office** (Jan 2026): in the LA burn scar, rebuild permits were issued roughly **3× faster than comparable single-family/ADU permits in the prior 5 years**.

### 5.2 Chapter 7A and pre-existing non-conforming

The big surprise for homeowners: **a house destroyed by fire does not get to rebuild to its old code.** Rebuild = new construction = current Chapter 7A.

- Old house had wood shake roof? **Rebuild gets Class A.**
- Old house had stock plastic ridge vent? **Rebuild gets ember-resistant vent.**
- Old house had wood deck wrapped around three sides? **Rebuild gets ignition-resistant deck or noncombustible at-grade hardscape within 10 ft.**

**This is where the insurance-rebuild-gap problem starts.** The insurance policy usually pays "replacement cost of like-kind and quality" — i.e., what was there. The actual cost to rebuild **to current code** (Chapter 7A + Title 24 energy + current ADA/CBC) is materially higher. Most policies have a **Building Code / Ordinance & Law endorsement** to cover that gap — typical limits 10–25% of dwelling coverage. **Verify and document the endorsement's limit with the adjuster early.** If the policy is short, the homeowner pays out of pocket — or downsizes.

### 5.3 Debris removal — the first decision after the fire

Two paths in declared-disaster fires:

| Path | Cost to owner | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **CalRecycle / state-managed program (Phase 1 + Phase 2)** | Free (Phase 1 covers HHW; Phase 2 covers structural debris; insurance proceeds for debris go to state) | Owner signs ROE (right-of-entry). Most owners take this path. Slower but no out-of-pocket cost. |
| **Owner-hired licensed contractor** | Owner pays; insurance reimburses up to debris-removal limit | Faster start; allows ash/foundation removal on owner's schedule. Requires CalRecycle / county Environmental Health certification of clean closure. |

**GC's role:** if the owner self-removes, you may not start any framing or even site prep until **clean closure documentation** is in hand from the county Environmental Health Department. Photograph the cleared site at closure.

### 5.4 Soils after fire

Severe wildfire creates **hydrophobic soils** — heat drives organic compounds down into the soil, forming a water-repellent layer 1–3" below the surface. Effects:

- Runoff and erosion in the **first rainy season after the fire** are dramatically elevated.
- Foundation excavations may encounter weakened, friable soil that doesn't behave like the prior geotech assumed.
- If the site is on a slope, **debris flow risk is the single largest post-fire hazard** — historically more deadly than the fire itself (e.g., Montecito 2018).

**Practical:**
- Get a **post-fire geotech evaluation** before designing new footings, even on a like-for-like rebuild.
- Install **erosion control (silt fence, fiber rolls, hydromulch)** as part of site work; many jurisdictions require it during the rainy season.
- Coordinate stormwater with the AHJ; some jurisdictions impose interim measures for the first 1–2 seasons.

### 5.5 Utility reconnection

- **PG&E / SCE / SDG&E**: meter and service panel typically destroyed; expect a new service drop. Coordinate with utility well ahead — schedules are bottlenecked after a major fire.
- **Service panel:** plan a full replacement; even if the panel looks salvageable, the buss bars and breakers will have experienced thermal stress and most AHJs require replacement.
- **Gas service:** if the home was gas-fed, the meter must be re-set and lines pressure-tested.
- **Water service:** check for PVC laterals that may have been damaged underground by heat; private wells require water-quality testing.
- **Sewer / septic:** septic systems near burned debris may need pumping/inspection; cleanouts may be heat-damaged.

### 5.6 Like-for-like vs. modified design

| Approach | Permit speed | When to choose |
|---|---|---|
| **Like-for-like rebuild** (same footprint, ±10% variance, similar height) | Fastest — fast-track plan check, possibly no CEQA, EO-streamlined | When the prior home worked and the insurance pays cleanly |
| **Modified rebuild** (different footprint, additional sq ft, ADU added, different elevation) | Standard plan check, possibly CEQA, possibly Coastal | When the prior layout was outdated or owner wants to densify |
| **Major redesign** | Full new-construction permit path | When the owner is essentially building a different house |

LA Mayor Bass's **Executive Directive 13** (2025) created a **pre-approved standard plan library** — a "virtual catalog" of code-compliant designs that fast-track rebuild. If your client's needs match a stock plan, the permit can be issued in days, not months.

### 5.7 Documentation for insurance rebuild

The single biggest source of friction post-fire is **insurance adjuster scope vs. actual rebuild cost**. The adjuster's estimate will rely on Xactimate or similar regional pricing, which is typically **20–35% behind current California rebuild reality** in any high-cost market, especially after a fire that has spiked local labor demand.

**Best practice scope-of-work documentation:**

- Itemized **CSI-format SOV** with current vendor quotes (roofing, framing, MEP).
- Photographs of pre-fire condition (Google Street View captures, owner's pre-fire photos) cross-referenced to each line item.
- Code-upgrade line items broken out separately (Chapter 7A vents, ember-resistant deck, Class A roof assembly delta over standard Class C) so the adjuster can apply the Ordinance & Law endorsement.
- A **change-order log** kept religiously — every adjuster scope dispute should resolve before work proceeds.

## 6. Specific WUI Construction Details

### 6.1 Roof assembly — the critical details

- **Full Class A *assembly***, not just a Class A shingle. Verify the listed assembly with the manufacturer; common path is shingle over 30-lb felt or synthetic underlayment over plywood, with mineral-surfaced cap sheet in some Class A assemblies (e.g., wood shake replacement assemblies — though wood shake itself is out).
- **Hip vs. gable**: **hip roofs perform measurably better** in wildfire — they have no gable triangle to admit wind-driven embers, reduce eave exposure, and have a more uniform thermal profile. Where architecturally workable, prefer hip in High/VHFHSZ.
- **Ridge vent**: a **listed ember-resistant ridge vent** (Brandguard, Vulcan, O'Hagin Fire & Ice) — not a stock plastic profile. Combine with baffled ridge cap.
- **No exposed underlayment** at any edge — drip edges, valleys, and rake all get noncombustible metal.
- **Fascia**: noncombustible (fiber cement or aluminum-clad) or fire-retardant-treated wood.

### 6.2 Wall-to-roof interface — the most critical gap

The intersection of the wall plane with the roof eave is where convective heat, embers, and accumulated debris all converge. Bad detailing here kills the house first.

- **Tight, gap-free transition** between wall covering (stucco, fiber cement) and roof underside. No exposed framing or sheathing.
- **Continuous metal flashing** turned up the wall behind the WRB.
- **Ember-resistant baffling** at any vent in this zone.
- For unvented (sealed) attics: no transition vents at all — easiest detail to keep tight.

### 6.3 Foundation vents — retrofit and new

For retrofits on existing homes, this is **the single highest-ROI fire-hardening action** for the dollar.

**Retrofit pattern:**
1. Survey foundation perimeter; count and measure existing vents.
2. Order matched-size **Brandguard FVS** or **Vulcan VFV** ember-resistant foundation vents.
3. Remove existing vent assemblies; install new ember-resistant units in the same RO.
4. Caulk perimeter with noncombustible sealant.

Typical cost: $40–80/vent material + 15–30 min installed labor each. For a 1,500 sq ft house with 10 foundation vents, the entire job is well under $2,500 — and it removes one of the top three ignition vectors.

**New construction:** spec listed ember-resistant foundation vents from day one.

### 6.4 Exterior windows — installation details

- **Glazing:** dual-pane minimum, with at least one tempered pane (typical: tempered outer + annealed inner; verify spec).
- **Frame material preference for WUI:** **fiberglass** (Pella Impervia, Marvin Ultimate Fiberglass) and **aluminum** (Western Window Systems, Fleetwood) outperform vinyl and wood. Fiberglass has the better thermal performance of the two.
- **Vinyl frames:** code-acceptable for the glazing requirement but documented to deform/melt under sustained radiant heat. **IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home Plus disfavors vinyl** in direct exposure conditions.
- **Putty glazing vs. structural / thermally broken**: thermally broken aluminum or fiberglass with proper structural glazing tape outperforms older putty-glazed wood-frame windows in radiant-heat tests.
- **Installation gaps**: spray-foam the rough opening; cover with metal flashing; trim with noncombustible material (no wood-trim window-buck details left exposed).

### 6.5 Deck design

**Material options:**

| Material | Class A WUI? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Trex Refuge** (PVC) | Yes — Class A flame spread; ASTM E2768 IWUIC compliant | Premium price; verify regional availability |
| **Trex Transcend** (composite) | Listed for WUI in many jurisdictions; check current BML | Mid-tier |
| **Trex Select** | Some listings; verify | Entry-level |
| **TimberTech AZEK** (PVC) | Many products Class A | Strong WUI product line |
| **Fiberon** | Several Class A products | Verify the specific product |
| **Concrete pavers / poured slab** | Noncombustible | Best for at-grade |
| **Aluminum decking** (Versadeck, AridDek) | Noncombustible | Premium, but bulletproof |
| **Hardie Artisan / fiber-cement decking** | Class A | Limited product availability |
| **Wood — even FRT** | Generally not | Almost never specified in WUI; treatments lose efficacy |

**Under-deck enclosure options:**
- 1-hour fire-rated soffit with Type X gypsum or fiber-cement sheathing.
- Fiber-cement skirting (Hardie Trim) over framing.
- For at-grade decks: **eliminate the deck**; use concrete pavers on sand. Cheaper, lower-maintenance, noncombustible, zero ember accumulation.

**Deck drainage:** any deck-over-living-space (or over an enclosed crawl) needs a tight WRB system that does not collect debris. Specify a slope, scuppers / drains, and an inspection routine.

### 6.6 Exterior paint — intumescent coatings

**Intumescent paints** swell when heated, forming an insulating char layer. They are widely used in steel-frame commercial construction (e.g., AESS columns) and have a niche role in residential WUI.

**Where they add value:**
- On exposed structural timber or heavy-timber posts/beams that would otherwise be ignition points.
- On accent siding that *must* be wood for architectural reasons.
- On under-deck framing as belt-and-suspenders alongside enclosure.

**Where they do not substitute for hardening:**
- They are **not a substitute** for changing siding from wood to fiber cement.
- They have limited durability outdoors (UV degradation); they need a topcoat and periodic maintenance.
- They do not satisfy Chapter 7A's "ignition-resistant material" definition unless the *assembly* is SFM-listed.

## 8. Retrofit and Fire-Hardening Existing Homes

### 8.1 AB 38 — disclosure and the new June 2025 update

**AB 38 (2019)** requires sellers of homes in **High or Very High FHSZ** to disclose home-hardening status to buyers and (for homes built before 2010) provide documentation of defensible space compliance.

**June 2025 update (CAR form revision):** the California Association of Realtors revised the **Fire Hardening and Defensible Space Disclosure and Addendum**. Sellers must now affirmatively answer disclosure on **12 specific home hardening conditions**, including:

1. Roofing (Class A?)
2. Vents (ember-resistant?)
3. Eaves and soffits (enclosed?)
4. Siding (ignition-resistant?)
5. Windows (tempered/dual-pane?)
6. Decks (compliant material?)
7. Rain gutters (covered/protected?)
8. Garage door (continuous bottom seal?)
9. Driveway access (apparatus?)
10. Defensible space (PRC 4291 compliant?)
11. Address visible from street?
12. Other locally required hardening items.

**Effective date for the new format:** July 1, 2025.

**GC angle:** when a homeowner gets a non-renewal notice or starts a sale, they often need an **inspection and remediation plan**. There's a growing market for **fire-hardening assessments** ($300–800 typical) plus phased retrofit packages. This is now a viable practice area.

### 8.2 High-ROI retrofit checklist (in order of $/risk-reduction)

| # | Retrofit | Approx cost (1,500 sq ft home) | Risk-reduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Clean gutters + install gutter guards** | $400–1,200 | Very high (removes #1 roof ignition source) |
| 2 | **Replace foundation/crawlspace vents** with ember-resistant | $1,000–2,500 | Very high |
| 3 | **Replace attic/gable/soffit vents** with ember-resistant | $1,500–4,000 | Very high |
| 4 | **Install Zone 0 hardscape** (pavers/gravel ring around house) | $2,000–8,000 | Very high |
| 5 | **Replace garage door** with WUI-rated continuous-seal model | $1,800–3,500 | High |
| 6 | **Replace deck** with Class A composite or pavers | $8,000–25,000 | High (decks are common ignition) |
| 7 | **Re-roof to Class A assembly** (if not already) | $15,000–35,000 | High |
| 8 | **Replace single-pane windows** with tempered dual-pane | $20,000–60,000 | Medium-high (radiant heat) |
| 9 | **Re-side to fiber cement** | $25,000–80,000 | Medium-high (radiant heat) |

Walk a homeowner from #1 toward #9. The first three items typically cost under $5K total and remove the dominant ember-ignition vectors — far cheaper than re-siding the whole house.

### 8.3 Permit requirements for fire-hardening retrofits

| Retrofit | Permit? |
|---|---|
| Gutter guards | No |
| Foundation/attic vent replacement (same opening) | **Often no** — but check local AHJ; some require a permit for changes to attic ventilation |
| Re-roof | **Yes** — and full Chapter 7A applies in WUI zones |
| Re-side (same material category) | Usually no |
| Re-side (change of material) | Sometimes — check local |
| Window replacement (in-kind, retrofit) | Generally no — but Title 24 fenestration U-factor applies |
| Deck rebuild | **Yes** — and full Chapter 7A applies |
| Garage door swap | Generally no |

**Recommend pulling a permit when in doubt**, especially in disaster-rebuild jurisdictions where uncerted work can complicate insurance later.

### 8.4 The GC's growing service line: fire-hardening assessment

A productized offering:

| Tier | Deliverable | Price (typical CA, 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| **Assessment** | Site walk + photo report + written checklist mapped to Safer from Wildfires / IBHS Base | $400–800 |
| **Phase 1 retrofit** | Items 1–5 from §8.2 above | $5,000–15,000 |
| **Phase 2 retrofit** | Adds deck and re-roof | $25,000–60,000 |
| **Full IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home Plus build-out** | Everything to designation standard, including coordinated inspection | $60,000–120,000+ |

**Marketing angle:** "Get insurable. Stay insurable." Tie deliverables to the FAIR Plan 12-discount matrix and the carrier-specific hardening checklists. Document with photos and invoices in a clean PDF the homeowner hands to their agent — this is the artifact that drives the actual premium reduction.

### C. The 10-minute pre-bid checklist (use on every WUI project)

1. Pull the CAL FIRE FHSZ for the parcel. Print and file.
2. Identify SRA vs. LRA. Identify the AHJ (city/county/district fire marshal).
3. Pull local CBC amendments (city building department website).
4. Confirm whether Chapter 7A / CWUIC applies; identify any local stricter requirements.
5. Confirm whether AB 38 disclosure applies (will if VHFHSZ).
6. Identify Zone 0 status — is the local jurisdiction enforcing a 5-ft noncombustible ring?
7. Check defensible space history (PRC 4291 — for SRA, CAL FIRE inspection records may be available).
8. Review the homeowner's current insurance: admitted carrier or FAIR Plan? When does it renew?
9. Walk the site for ember vulnerabilities — gutter debris, vent screens, deck condition, fence-to-house, mulch in Zone 0.
10. Produce a 3-tier bid (Code minimum / Safer from Wildfires / IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home) and let the client choose with eyes open.
