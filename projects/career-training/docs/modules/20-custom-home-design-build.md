# Custom Home Design-Build for the Residential General Contractor

> A reference for the California residential GC who wants to take a client from raw land or an existing property through design, permitting, and construction as a single point of responsibility. Design-build collapses two contracts into one — and collapses two risk pools into one. Done well, it is the highest-margin, most-defensible delivery model in custom residential. Done poorly, it exposes the GC to design liability they were not insured for and scope disputes they cannot win.

## 1. Design-Build Fundamentals for Residential

### 1.1 What Design-Build Actually Means

In a true design-build (DB) project, the **owner signs one contract with one entity — the design-builder — who is responsible for both design and construction**. The design-builder either (a) employs the architect/engineers directly, or (b) subcontracts to them. Either way, the owner has **one throat to choke** and the design-builder owns the gap between drawings and built reality.

This is materially different from how most residential GCs currently work. Three things change at the contract level:

| Element | Design-Bid-Build (DBB) | Design-Build (DB) |
|---|---|---|
| Number of owner contracts | 2 (architect + GC) | 1 (design-builder) |
| Who holds the architect's contract | Owner | Design-builder (GC) |
| Who carries design errors-and-omissions risk | Architect (against the owner) | Design-builder, who back-charges architect via the B141 |
| Who decides constructability questions | Architect designs, GC bids what's drawn | GC contributes from day one |
| Cost certainty | Hard bid at 100% CDs | Progressive — GMP locked when design is mature |
| Schedule | Sequential (design then bid then build) | Overlapping (build foundation while finalizing interior) |
| Change orders for design errors | Owner pays GC, sues architect | Design-builder absorbs |

### 1.2 How DB Differs from DBB in Practice

The clearest practical differences:

- **GC is involved from day one.** Site visits with the architect, sketch reviews, early budget feedback. The GC's first job is not estimating — it is *shaping the design toward something buildable for the budget*.
- **Constructability is a design input, not a punch-list complaint.** A DB GC who watches a 14-foot cantilever appear in SD says "show me the structural concept before we go further." A DBB GC has to bid the cantilever as drawn and price the headache into the number.
- **Speed.** Foundation permit is often pulled while interior CDs are being finalized. Lead-time items (windows, cabinets, steel) are released against DD-level information. Six to twelve weeks of calendar time disappears.
- **Owner trust requirement is higher.** In DBB the owner has an architect watching the GC. In DB the GC *is* the watchdog over the design. The owner has to either trust the GC or hire an independent third-party owner's representative to police the design-builder.

### 1.3 The Hybrid Most Residential GCs Actually Run

Most California residential GCs do not actually offer design-build. They offer **"GC with a preferred architect"** — a loose collaboration where:

- The architect is hired directly by the owner.
- The GC is referred by the architect (or vice versa) and gets a handshake first-look on the project.
- The GC provides early budgeting and constructability feedback during design.
- The GC bids the project against 90% CDs and converts to lump sum or cost-plus.

This is **not** design-build. It looks like it, but the contractual architecture is identical to DBB. The GC has zero design liability and the owner can fire either party without breaching the other contract. The benefits (early collaboration, faster decisions, fewer surprises) are largely captured without the legal exposure.

**Why this matters:** If you describe your service as "design-build" in marketing but contractually you are running the hybrid, you create an expectation that you cannot legally satisfy. If a design error causes a $40K mistake during construction, the owner thinks *you* own it — because that is what design-build means — but your contract says the architect (whom you do not pay) is responsible. This is the most common source of GC-owner litigation in pseudo-design-build projects.

**Pick a lane:**

| If you want... | Use this model | Key contract |
|---|---|---|
| To market as "design-build" with full liability | True DB | AIA A141 + B141 |
| Early collaboration, no design liability | Hybrid (GC + preferred architect) | AIA A102/A103 with owner; separate B101 owner-architect |
| Phased commitment, exit option | Progressive design-build | AIA A141-PDB |
| Stock plans + light customization | Design-build "lite" | Construction agreement with in-house designer scope |

### 1.4 When Design-Build Works Best in Residential

DB is not the right delivery for every residential project. It performs best when:

- **Custom homes with a firm budget.** The owner cannot afford to design an unbuildable dream and then value-engineer back. DB enforces budget discipline by design.
- **ADUs.** Tight site, tight budget, repeat-able systems, often state-approved pre-checked plans available. DB is overwhelmingly the most efficient delivery for ADUs and is how nearly all professional ADU builders operate.
- **Room additions.** Constructability of the tie-in to the existing structure is half the design problem. A DB GC catches the existing-roof-to-new-roof flashing detail in SD, not in the field.
- **Design-sensitive renovations.** Historic homes, structural reconfigurations, kitchens-with-everything. The interaction of existing conditions, design intent, and field reality is where DB shines.
- **Repeat clients and high-touch builders.** If you do four or five custom homes a year and your clients refer to each other, DB lets you build a process around your team rather than retrofit your team to each new architect.

DB performs poorly when:

- The owner has an architect they love and have hired already.
- The project is a design competition entry (a "statement" home where the architect is the lead artist).
- The owner is sophisticated about construction and wants to bid the work competitively.
- The GC does not have an architect they trust and have worked with at least twice.

### 1.5 The Risks the GC Inherits

When a GC moves from DBB to DB, three categories of risk transfer onto their balance sheet:

#### 1.5.1 Design Liability (Errors and Omissions)

In DBB, if the architect omits a beam pocket and the framer has to chip out concrete in the field, the owner pays the change order and sues the architect. In DB, the GC's contract with the owner says *they* are responsible for the design. The GC can in turn pursue the architect under the B141, but:

- The GC's contract is with the owner — the meter runs against the GC's pocket first.
- The architect's E&O policy typically excludes claims by parties they have a *contract* with for *construction* — many AIA-form architects' policies cover them for design liability to *owners*, not to a design-builder employer. **You must verify the architect's E&O policy covers a B141 design-build arrangement.** Many do not without an endorsement.
- The GC's general liability policy almost certainly excludes professional services. The GC needs to either: (a) buy contractor's professional liability (CPL) coverage, typically $1–3M, $5K–$15K/yr premium for a small residential book; or (b) require the architect to carry $1M+ E&O naming the GC as additional insured.

#### 1.5.2 Scope Definition Before Design Is Complete

In DBB the owner signs a construction contract against 100% CDs — scope is unambiguous. In DB the owner signs a design-build contract against an *Owner's Criteria* document that may be 5–20 pages of programmatic statements: "3,800–4,200 SF, four bedrooms, primary suite on main, separate office, three-car garage, modern-traditional aesthetic, $X budget." Everything between those bullets is up for interpretation.

The GC's defense is the **basis-of-design (BoD) document** — a snapshot of the assumptions baked into the GMP when it is set. If the BoD says "Marvin Signature Coastline windows, dark bronze, standard sizing" and the owner later wants Sierra Pacific multi-slide doors, that is a change order. If the BoD is vague ("high-quality windows"), the owner wins the dispute. Specificity is liability protection.

#### 1.5.3 Cost Certainty Challenges

A lump-sum DB contract signed at concept stage is a way to lose money. The math:

- At schematic design (SD, ~15% complete), the cost estimate has roughly ±25% range.
- At design development (DD, ~50% complete), the range tightens to ±15%.
- At construction documents 90%+, the range is ±5–8%.

A GC who signs a lump-sum DB at SD is taking the ±25% on their own balance sheet. The correct contract structure is **cost-plus with a GMP set at DD or CD milestone**, with progressive design-build the cleanest legal vehicle (see §4).

## 2. Assembling the Design Team

### 2.1 Finding and Vetting Residential Architects

The single most important business decision a DB residential GC makes is *who is on the architect's chair*. A great architect at a fair fee makes every project easier; the wrong architect makes every project a litigation risk.

#### Vetting checklist

| Criterion | What to look for | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| Portfolio | 5+ completed residential projects, ideally similar to your typical scope (custom home, addition, etc.) | All renderings, no built work |
| Project type fit | Modern, traditional, transitional — does it match your subs' skill set? | Aesthetic mismatch (e.g., glass-and-steel architect with stucco-tract subs) |
| Design-build experience | Has worked under a B141 or design-builder contract before | Hostility toward GC-led process; "I work *for* the owner" |
| Communication style | Responds to RFIs in <48 hrs, attends OAC meetings, gives clear directives | Treats GC as adversary; goes silent during construction |
| Production capability | In-house team or trusted draftspeople; not solo principal who disappears at CDs | One person doing everything (means CDs will be late or thin) |
| Licensure | Active California architect license (verify at [cab.ca.gov](https://www.cab.ca.gov/)) | Lapsed license, out-of-state-only, or "designer" calling themselves architect |
| E&O insurance | Minimum $1M per claim, $2M aggregate, with sub-consultants covered | No E&O, or refuses to share certificate |
| Fee transparency | Clear fee letter — % of construction cost, phased deliverables, what is included | Vague monthly retainer with no scope |
| References | 3+ recent owners willing to take a call; 1+ GC who has worked with them | "Confidentiality clauses" preventing references |
| Cultural fit | Open to value engineering, accepts cost feedback without ego | Refuses to redesign over budget; "the design is the design" |

#### How to source

1. **Reverse-engineer Houzz and 1stDibs.** Filter for completed work in your county, then look at the architects credited on projects you admire.
2. **AIA California chapter directories** — [aiacalifornia.org](https://aiacalifornia.org/find-an-architect/) lists chapter members.
3. **Permit office plan-check staff** — they know which architects produce clean drawings and which produce four rounds of corrections.
4. **Other GCs (non-competitors).** A North Bay GC will gladly recommend a Marin architect to a Peninsula GC.
5. **Build the relationship before you need it.** Take three architects to lunch per year. The right partnership is a 5–10 year asset.

### 2.2 Subcontracting the Architect vs. Referring to the Owner

This is the **single most legally consequential decision** in residential design-build. The question is: who signs the architect's contract — the GC (you) or the owner?

#### Option A — GC subcontracts the architect (true design-build)

**Contract chain:** Owner → GC (A141) → Architect (B141)

| Implication | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design liability | GC is responsible to owner for design errors; GC pursues architect via B141 |
| Insurance | GC needs Contractor's Professional Liability (CPL); architect's E&O must respond to design-builder claims |
| Architect's loyalty | Architect's client is the GC, not the owner — creates ethical tension during owner-GC disputes |
| Cost transparency | GC marks up architect's fee (typically 10–15% management fee); must be disclosed |
| Speed of decisions | GC can direct architect immediately (no owner-architect bottleneck) |
| Owner perception | Some owners feel "the architect should work for me" — must educate |
| Profit | Architect markup is GC margin (a 10% mark-up on $120K design fee = $12K) |
| Best for | Production builders, ADUs, repeat custom homes, packaged offerings |

#### Option B — Owner contracts architect directly (hybrid; not true DB)

**Contract chain:** Owner → GC (A102/103); Owner → Architect (B101) — two parallel contracts

| Implication | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design liability | Architect is liable directly to owner; GC has no design risk |
| Insurance | GC's standard GL + worker's comp; no CPL needed |
| Architect's loyalty | Clear — architect represents owner |
| Cost transparency | Owner pays architect directly; no GC markup |
| Speed of decisions | Owner is in the loop for every design directive — slower |
| Owner perception | Familiar to most owners; matches DBB expectations |
| Profit | No architect markup; rely on construction margin only |
| Best for | High-end custom homes where owner wants the architect relationship; risk-averse GCs |

#### Option C — Progressive / hybrid (most defensible)

The cleanest setup for most residential DB practices:

1. **Pre-construction services agreement** (PCSA) — GC charges hourly or fixed fee to manage architect, run early estimating, coordinate selections. Architect is hired by *owner* under a B101.
2. **At ~90% CDs**, owner and GC convert to construction agreement (A101 or A102/103) with GMP.
3. GC's design oversight is a *coordination* role, not a design-of-record role. Architect remains the design professional of record.

This captures most of the DB benefits (early input, faster schedule, integrated team) without the design liability exposure.

### 2.3 Residential Designer vs. Licensed Architect — California Rules

California Business & Professions Code §5537 exempts certain residential work from the requirement that plans be prepared by a licensed architect. The key carve-out for residential GCs:

> Unlicensed persons may prepare plans, drawings, specifications, instruments of service, or other design documents for **single-family dwellings of woodframe construction not more than two stories and basement in height; multiple dwellings containing no more than four dwelling units of woodframe construction not more than two stories and basement in height**; and **garages or other structures appurtenant** to such buildings.

What this means in practice:

| Project type | Architect required? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Single-family home, 2-story + basement, conventional wood frame | No — designer OK | Most California custom homes |
| Single-family home, 3+ stories | Yes — architect required | Includes a "floor" above the roof per code definition |
| Custom home with steel moment frame, cantilever, or non-conventional structure | Architect or engineer required | Triggered by §5537(c) — any deviation from CRC prescriptive provisions |
| Duplex / triplex / fourplex, 2 stories, wood frame | No — designer OK | The "no more than four dwelling units" exemption |
| Apartment building (5+ units) | Yes — architect required | Outside §5537 exemption |
| Commercial (any) | Yes — architect required | Always |
| ADU, detached, 1 story | No — designer OK | Most common ADU configuration |
| ADU, 2 stories | No — designer OK if appurtenant to SFD | Subject to local variations |
| Renovation/addition — non-structural | No — designer OK | But check local jurisdiction |
| Renovation/addition — structural | Engineer stamp required for calcs | Designer can prepare arch plans; engineer signs structural |

**Critical caveat — engineer stamp is a separate question.** Even when an architect is not required, **structural calculations and structural sheets must be signed by a licensed engineer** (California PE — civil or structural) whenever the structure deviates from the California Residential Code (CRC) prescriptive provisions. In a custom home, that is essentially always — large openings, tall walls, cantilevers, irregular plan, steel beams, hold-down schedules. Plan to budget for a structural engineer regardless of architect-vs-designer question.

**Why use a designer instead of an architect?**

- **Fee.** A residential designer charges $1.50–$5/SF for full-service plans, vs. $8–$15/SF (or 8–15% of construction cost) for an architect. On a 3,500 SF custom home at $600/SF ($2.1M), that is a $30K–$80K difference.
- **Speed.** Designers tend to produce permit-ready CDs faster (fewer iterations, less design exploration).
- **Cooperativeness.** Designers are often more willing to work under a GC-led process. Many were former contractors or in-house production staff at production builders.

**Why use an architect instead of a designer?**

- **Design quality and resale value.** A well-designed architect-driven custom home appraises higher and sells faster.
- **Liability.** Architects carry E&O. Most designers do not (or carry very thin policies). If a design error becomes litigation, designer assets may be worthless.
- **Plan check.** Some jurisdictions favor architect-stamped drawings — fewer corrections, faster turnaround.
- **Owner expectation.** High-end clients expect an architect.
- **Required by code.** As above — once you cross 2 stories + basement, or deviate from CRC prescriptive provisions in a material way, you have no choice.

[Sources: California Architects Board — Building Official Information Guide; CAB Stamp Requirements]

### 2.4 Interior Designers in the DB Workflow

Interior designers (ID) sit awkwardly in residential DB. Their scope overlaps the architect's (cabinetry, plumbing fixtures, lighting), but they typically lack the engineering and code background. Best practice:

- **Define ID scope explicitly in the design-builder's contract.** Interior architecture vs. furniture-fixtures-and-equipment (FF&E) only? Cabinetry specification vs. design? Lighting plan vs. fixture selection?
- **Designate a "specification authority"** for each category. Plumbing fixtures: ID. Plumbing rough valves: architect/MEP. Lighting plan: architect. Decorative fixtures: ID. Without this matrix, two professionals will spec contradictory things.
- **Get the ID involved at SD or early DD**, not at finishes selection. The reason: the ID drives cabinetry, which drives plumbing rough, which drives framing. Late-stage IDs trigger costly redesigns.
- **Coordinate with the GC's project manager, not the architect.** The architect manages design coordination; the GC manages execution coordination. ID specs flow into the GC's submittal log.

Interior designer fees in California typically run $150–$300/hr or $5–$15/SF for full-service. On a 3,500 SF custom home, expect $25K–$55K of ID fee. This is often paid by the owner directly even on DB projects.

### 2.5 Engineers

The engineering team for a California custom home almost always includes:

| Discipline | Always required? | Typical fee | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Structural | Yes for custom homes | $4K–$25K, scale to size | PE stamp required for any non-CRC-prescriptive elements |
| Civil | When grading >50 CY, retaining walls >4', or stormwater management required | $5K–$30K | Required for hillside, larger lots, complex drainage |
| Geotechnical (soils report) | Yes for hillside, expansive soils, retaining walls; usually required in California | $4K–$10K | Local jurisdiction will require — get early |
| Title 24 energy compliance | Yes, mandatory | $1K–$3K | Required for permit; certified energy consultant or CEPE |
| Mechanical (HVAC) engineer | Optional — most often design-build with sub | Built into MEP sub bid | Required for radiant, geothermal, complex systems |
| Electrical engineer | Optional for residential | Built into sub | Required if solar/battery design beyond standard scope |
| Plumbing engineer | Rare for residential | Built into sub | Custom water features, complex hydronic |
| Surveyor | Yes for new construction | $2K–$8K | Boundary survey + topo |

**Design-assist MEP** vs. **engineered MEP**:

- **Engineered MEP** (architect's MEP consultant produces drawings, sub bids and installs): cleaner CDs, more designer control, higher fee, common in $3M+ custom homes.
- **Design-assist MEP** (MEP subcontractor is brought in early during DD, helps engineer the system, then installs): cheaper, faster, more practical, common in $500K–$3M projects. The sub takes engineering responsibility under their C-20 / C-10 / C-36 license.

For most residential DB projects in California, design-assist is the right answer. See §6.

### 2.6 Design Fees — Typical Structures

| Fee model | Typical range | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| % of construction cost | 8–15% for full-service architect | New custom homes, complex projects |
| Per square foot | $2–$15 per SF (designer to high-end architect) | Predictable for SFD with known size |
| Hourly | $100–$350/hr depending on seniority | Renovations, feasibility studies, limited scope |
| Fixed fee | $20K–$200K depending on scope | Repeat plans, ADUs, defined-scope renovations |
| Phased | Separate fees per phase (SD/DD/CD/CA) | Most defensible — owner can exit between phases |

#### Typical % breakdown across phases

| Phase | % of total architect fee | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|
| Schematic Design (SD) | 15% | Site plan, floor plans, elevations at concept |
| Design Development (DD) | 20% | Plans + sections, structural concept, MEP layouts |
| Construction Documents (CD) | 40% | Permit set + bid set |
| Bidding/Negotiation | 5% | Sub bid review (collapses in DB — GC owns) |
| Construction Administration (CA) | 20% | RFIs, submittals, site visits, punch list |

In DB, the bidding phase fee typically transfers to the GC (no separate bid). The CA fee remains with the architect and is critical — do not let the owner cut CA to save money; an architect with no CA scope is an unprotected GC during construction.

## 3. The Design Process — GC's Role at Each Phase

The single biggest value-add of a DB GC is being present, useful, and constructive at every design milestone. The architect leads design; the GC leads constructability, cost, and schedule.

### 3.1 Pre-Design / Programming (1–4 weeks)

**Architect's role:** Lead owner interviews, document program (room list, sizes, relationships), site analysis.

**GC's role — and where you create the most value:**

| Activity | Output |
|---|---|
| Site walk with architect and owner | Photo log, access notes, neighbor proximity, tree assessment, slope/drainage observations |
| Utility investigation | Locations of existing sewer/water/gas/power; SFPUC/PG&E/local water district contacts |
| Budget reality check | "Owner wants 4,500 SF for $1.8M — that is $400/SF, well below current Bay Area mid-range. Either reduce SF or expand budget by $1M." This single conversation, held in week 1, prevents 80% of all DB blowups. |
| Schedule reality check | "From signed contract to occupancy on a project this size in your jurisdiction: 18–28 months realistic. Owner is hoping for 14 — manage expectations now." |
| Permit pre-research | Jurisdiction quirks, height limits, FAR, setback, design review requirements (especially Marin, Berkeley, Palo Alto, coastal zones) |
| Existing-conditions documentation (renovations) | Hidden conditions — knob-and-tube, asbestos likelihood, foundation issues, structural quirks |

**Deliverable:** A 2–3 page **basis of design (BoD)** document signed by owner before SD begins. Includes program, budget, schedule, site constraints, jurisdiction issues, sustainability targets, and key assumptions. This is the GC's defense against later scope creep claims.

### 3.2 Schematic Design (SD) — 4–10 weeks

**Architect's role:** Massing, site placement, rough floor plan, exterior character, two or three design options for owner review.

**GC's role — review for:**

| Check | What you're looking for | Action if wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Site access | Can a concrete truck, lumber delivery, crane reach the building site? | Flag access mitigation costs ($5K–$50K) |
| Equipment access | Where will the boom truck for steel/trusses position? | Adjust building location or driveway routing |
| Neighbor impacts | Setbacks, sun/shade impact, construction noise/dust paths | Address in design review submittal |
| Fire department access | 150-ft hose pull, turnaround radius (CRC R313, local amendments) | Required for permit — catch early |
| Utility connections | Where do new lines tie in? Cost of trenching and tap fees | Add to budget ($15K–$60K typical) |
| Building shape and structure | Cantilevers, long spans, irregular plan = $$ | Show alternative massing |
| Roof complexity | Hip/valley count, pitch, eave detail | Push for simpler roof if budget tight |
| Glazing percentage | Title 24 compliance, structural implications of large openings | Coordinate with energy consultant early |
| Existing structure (renovations) | What's keeping vs. demolishing? Bearing walls, plumbing chases | Save owner from demolishing more than needed |
| Rough order-of-magnitude estimate (ROM) | ±25% range at this stage | Reconcile against owner budget — adjust scope before DD |

**Deliverable:** GC produces an **SD-level estimate** broken down by CSI division (16 divisions, residential-adjusted), with stated assumptions, allowances clearly called out, and a recommended contingency of 15–20%. Present to architect and owner together.

### 3.3 Design Development (DD) — 6–12 weeks

**Architect's role:** Develop the chosen SD scheme into a coordinated set: floor plans + reflected ceiling plans + sections + key elevations + structural concept + MEP intent drawings + exterior material call-outs.

**GC's role — pricing major systems and flagging substitutions:**

| Activity | What you do | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Get budgetary numbers from key subs | Foundation, framing, MEP, roofing, windows, cabinets | Validate architect's assumptions; flag scope expansion |
| Review structural concept with framer/SE | "This 30-ft clear span — moment frame or steel beam? What does framing cost look like?" | Cheaper structural decisions made now |
| Flag substitution opportunities | "European tilt-turn windows are $200K — Marvin Modern delivers similar aesthetic at $90K" | Owner choice, but show them the trade |
| Review MEP approach | Forced-air vs. radiant vs. heat pump; tankless vs. tank water; PV array sizing | Long-term operating cost vs. first cost trade-offs |
| Constructability red-flag list | Wet-on-wet stucco over rigid foam? Glass guardrail with continuous handrail? | Catch the details that will become RFIs |
| DD-level estimate | ±15% range | Reconcile, adjust |
| Allowance schedule | Establish realistic allowances for items not yet selected | Prevents "low-ball placeholder" syndrome |

**Critical DD-phase activity: the value engineering session.** When the DD estimate comes back over budget (it usually does, by 10–25%), the GC convenes a structured VE meeting with architect and owner. Present 20–40 specific line-item reductions, categorized as:

- **No design impact** (mechanical, structural, hidden) — first priority, no aesthetic compromise
- **Modest design impact** (slightly different materials, simpler details) — owner choice
- **Material design impact** (changed massing, different windows, omitted features) — owner choice with full understanding

Document every choice; the architect updates DD set; re-estimate. Goal: enter CD phase with budget reconciled to within ±10%.

### 3.4 Construction Documents (CD) — 8–16 weeks

**Architect's role:** Permit-ready drawings. Typically:
- Cover sheet, code data, vicinity map
- Site plan, civil drawings, demolition plan
- Floor plans (architectural), reflected ceiling plans
- Building sections, wall sections
- Exterior elevations + window/door schedule
- Interior elevations (kitchens, baths, built-ins)
- Structural drawings (engineer-sealed) with calcs
- Title 24 documentation
- MEP plans (intent or coordinated)
- Finish schedule, specifications

**GC's role:**

| Activity | Output |
|---|---|
| Detailed quantity takeoff | Cubic yards of concrete, board-feet of lumber, SF of drywall, etc. |
| Identify likely RFIs before permitting | "Wall section A doesn't show how rim joist meets foundation — get architect to clarify before plan check, not in the field" |
| Hard sub pricing | Issue scope letters to your bid list; get firm written prices |
| Permit prep | Application, fees calculation, Title 24, energy calcs, school fees, impact fees |
| Trade coordination meetings | Bring framer + MEP + waterproofing into one room to walk the set — they will find each other's conflicts |
| Final GMP development | This is where you set the construction GMP (see §4) |
| Allowance reconciliation | All allowances either replaced with firm pricing or set at defensible levels |
| Schedule | Detailed CPM schedule with sub-contractor input |

**CD-level estimate is ±5–8% range.** If you cannot underwrite a GMP at this point with confidence in your contingency, you do not have a complete CD set. Push the architect to finish.

### 3.5 Construction Administration (CA)

CA is the architect's role during construction. It is the single most underrated phase in residential DB, and the most commonly cut by cost-conscious owners. Do not let them cut it.

| Activity | Who does what |
|---|---|
| Site observation visits | Architect, 1–4x per month depending on complexity |
| RFI response | Architect within 48–72 hrs (per contract) |
| Submittal review | Architect for design-significant items; GC reviews everything else |
| Shop drawing review | Architect for casework, glazing, steel; GC for everything else |
| Mock-up review | Architect approves mock-ups before production |
| Field changes | Architect signs off on substitutions/deviations from CDs |
| Punch list | Architect prepares; GC executes; architect verifies |
| Final inspection (architectural) | Architect's certificate of substantial completion |

**GC-architect division of responsibility (residential DB):**

| Issue | Owner expects | Architect handles | GC handles |
|---|---|---|---|
| "The tile pattern looks off" | Architect | Approves layout pre-install | Installs per approved plan |
| "There's a crack in the slab" | GC | (Reviews if structural) | Owns warranty repair |
| "I want a different paint color" | Either | Approves substitution | Coordinates with painter; change order if cost impact |
| "The framer didn't follow the plan" | GC | Verifies via observation | Corrects at GC cost |
| "The structural engineer wants a change" | Architect/Engineer | Issues field directive | Pricing and execution |
| "I want to add a wine cellar" | Either | Designs new scope | Estimates, change order |

## 4. Owner Contracts for Design-Build

### 4.1 AIA A141-2024 — The Standard Form for True Design-Build

The **AIA A141-2024 (Agreement Between Owner and Design-Builder for a Traditional Design-Build Project)** is the standard form for true design-build in residential and commercial work. It is structured very differently from A101 (DBB lump sum):

**Document composition:**
- **The Agreement** — main terms
- **Exhibit A** — Insurance and Bonds
- **Exhibit B** — Design-Build Amendment (executed once Owner and Design-Builder agree on the Contract Sum — this is the "GMP set" milestone)
- **Exhibit C** — Sustainable Projects (optional)

**Two-phase compensation structure** — this is the most important feature:

| Phase | When | Compensation |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | Pre-Amendment (design + preconstruction) | Hourly, fixed fee, or % of construction cost — paid as design progresses |
| Phase 2 | Post-Amendment (construction) | The "Contract Sum" — typically lump sum or cost-plus-fee with GMP |

**Process flow:**
1. Owner provides Owner's Criteria (program, budget, site, design intent)
2. Design-Builder reviews and develops Preliminary Design
3. Design-Builder provides Contract Sum proposal
4. Mutual agreement → Design-Build Amendment is executed
5. Construction proceeds under the Contract Sum

**2024 changes worth knowing:**
- Expanded warranty — design-builder warrants both the design and the construction
- Clearer treatment of betterments and substitutions
- Updated insurance requirements (Exhibit A)
- Sustainable-project framework integrated (Exhibit C)

**Companion document — B141-2024 (Agreement Between Design-Builder and Architect):** This is the contract between you (the GC/design-builder) and your architect. It mirrors A141 — defines architect's services, fee, deliverables, and assigns design liability between you and the architect.

[Sources: AIA A141-2024 Summary; AIA B141-2024 Summary; ABA Construction Lawyer 2024 forms review]

### 4.2 Progressive Design-Build (A141-PDB)

The AIA's **A141-PDB-2024 (Progressive Design-Build)** is functionally the best contract for most residential DB:

- **Phase 1: Design services** — Design-Builder is paid on a cost-reimbursable or fixed-fee basis through design completion. Owner can terminate at the end of design without obligation to proceed.
- **Phase 2: Construction** — At the end of design (or earlier if both parties agree), Owner and Design-Builder negotiate a GMP. If they cannot agree, Owner can take the design and bid it elsewhere (with appropriate compensation to the Design-Builder for design work performed).

**Why progressive is the residential sweet spot:**

- Owner does not commit to construction price before design is mature.
- GC does not have to underwrite a GMP at concept stage.
- Off-ramp at end of design protects both sides.
- Matches how residential clients actually want to buy — "let's design it and then talk price."

The trade-off: the owner has the option to walk away with your design and bid it competitively. Most clients do not, but it is a risk that the contract should price into the design fee (i.e., charge full freight for design services — do not subsidize design hoping to recover in construction).

### 4.3 Cost-Plus with GMP for Custom Homes

The most common construction-phase contract structure in California residential DB:

**Cost of the Work + Fee, with Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)**

- **Cost of the Work** — actual cost of labor, materials, equipment, subs, general conditions (defined in contract)
- **Fee** — GC's overhead and profit, typically 12–18% on residential custom (split: ~5–8% OH, 7–10% profit)
- **GMP** — total cap above which GC absorbs overruns
- **Savings split** — if final cost is under GMP, savings are typically split 50/50 to 75/25 (owner/GC)

**Why lump sum is hard before design is complete:**

A lump sum requires you to price the entire scope at signing. In design-build, scope is incomplete at signing — finishes, fixtures, and details are still being designed. To convert a DB to lump sum, you either:
- Set the lump sum at 100% CDs (defeats the speed advantage of DB)
- Set allowances for everything not designed (effectively a cost-plus disguised as lump sum)
- Underwrite massive contingency in your number (loses competitively)

GMP solves this by capping the upside risk while leaving room for design completion.

**How to set a realistic GMP:**

| CD completion | GMP confidence | Action |
|---|---|---|
| <50% | Do not set GMP | Continue under PDB / design-only |
| 70–80% | Set GMP with 8–12% GC contingency | Acceptable for trusted clients |
| 90%+ | Set GMP with 4–7% GC contingency | Industry standard residential |
| 100% | Set GMP with 3–5% GC contingency | Tightest possible — only if all subs locked |

**GMP setting checklist:**
1. CDs are at 90%+ with no major open design questions
2. Structural calcs and Title 24 are complete
3. All major sub categories have written proposals (not estimates)
4. Long-lead items (windows, cabinets) have firm vendor quotes
5. Allowance schedule reflects realistic owner selection levels
6. Schedule is agreed (DB construction durations vary by ±20%)
7. Contingency is sized to remaining design uncertainty
8. Permit conditions are known (jurisdiction has reviewed plans at minimum)

### 4.4 Owner's Contingency vs. GC's Contingency

This is one of the most-fought-over concepts in residential DB. Be explicit and document it in the contract.

| Type | Whose money | What it covers | Who controls |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GC's contingency** (in GMP) | Inside the GMP — GC's risk | Means and methods, sub price escalation, sub default, minor design errors, weather impacts within tolerance | GC, with disclosure |
| **Owner's contingency** (outside GMP) | Owner's separate reserve | Scope changes the owner requests, design upgrades, unforeseen conditions outside GC's scope, force majeure | Owner, drawn down with mutual agreement |
| **Allowances** (inside GMP, line-item) | Inside the GMP — owner pays the actual | Selections not yet finalized (tile, fixtures, etc.) | Owner picks within allowance; over/under reconciled |

**How to communicate the difference to the owner:**

> "We are giving you a GMP of $X. Inside that number is our contingency of 5% — that protects you against our risk: sub problems, errors in our pricing, normal stuff that comes up. Outside that number, we recommend you also hold an owner's contingency of 5–10% in your bank account — that protects you against your own risk: changes you decide to make, conditions we cannot see (like dry-rot found in a wall demo), and force majeure. Together, you should plan a total project cost of GMP × 1.05 to GMP × 1.10."

A common mistake: GCs do not disclose their internal contingency. The owner thinks the GMP is "the price" — and then when the GC's contingency is consumed, the owner sees a change order request and thinks they are being upcharged. **Disclose, line-item, and explain.**

### 4.5 Allowances — Setting Them Honestly

Allowances are the GC's biggest reputational risk in DB. Set them low to win the contract → owner buys real product → blow through allowance → owner thinks GC sandbagged. Set them realistically → owner thinks GC is expensive vs. competitors.

**Best practice: tier the allowances.**

| Tier | What it represents |
|---|---|
| "Bronze" | Builder-grade — what a tract builder would put in |
| "Silver" | Mid-range — Pottery Barn, Restoration Hardware non-flagship |
| "Gold" | High-end — Waterworks, Visual Comfort, Wolf/Sub-Zero |
| "Platinum" | Luxury — Vola, designer-only, custom |

For each finish category (tile, plumbing fixtures, lighting, appliances, hardware, etc.), specify which tier the allowance is set for. Then the owner knows: "the tile allowance is set at Silver — if I want Gold, that is an add."

**Typical allowance line items in a California custom home:**

| Category | Bronze | Silver | Gold | Platinum |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plumbing fixtures (per fixture) | $200 | $500 | $1,200 | $3,500+ |
| Tile (per SF, material only) | $4 | $10 | $25 | $60+ |
| Cabinetry (per LF, base + upper) | $400 | $900 | $1,800 | $3,500+ |
| Appliance package (kitchen, total) | $8K | $18K | $40K | $80K+ |
| Lighting fixtures (per house) | $4K | $12K | $30K | $75K+ |
| Hardware (per opening) | $25 | $90 | $250 | $600+ |
| Flooring (per SF, material) | $4 | $9 | $20 | $50+ |
| Stone slab (per SF, material) | $50 | $90 | $180 | $400+ |
| Countertops, fabricated and installed (per SF) | $70 | $120 | $250 | $500+ |
| Window/door package (% of construction cost) | 4% | 6% | 10% | 15%+ |
| Landscape, hardscape and irrigation (per SF site) | $15 | $35 | $80 | $200+ |

*These ranges are 2025 California estimates — verify with your suppliers.*

**The "honest mid-tier" approach:** for each item, set the allowance at the *middle of the tier you and the owner have discussed*. Document the tier in the contract. When the owner picks above tier, change order. When they pick below, refund (or apply to other categories).

### 4.6 Change Order Authority

In DB, the GC has design authority and construction authority. The owner must understand who can authorize what.

| Phase | Decision type | Who authorizes | Documentation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Design phase | Minor design refinement (no cost/schedule impact) | Architect | Design log entry |
| Design phase | Material substitution (no cost impact) | Architect or GC | Substitution log |
| Design phase | Scope change (cost or schedule impact) | Owner | Design change order (DCO) |
| Construction phase | Field condition / minor RFI response | GC | RFI log + field directive |
| Construction phase | Design clarification (no cost impact) | Architect | RFI response |
| Construction phase | Substitution due to availability | GC, owner notified | Substitution request |
| Construction phase | Owner-requested change | Owner | Change order proposal → executed change order |
| Construction phase | Unforeseen condition | GC documents; owner authorizes | Time + materials authorization or change order |

**Practical rule for residential DB:** No work proceeds on any change >$1,500 or >1 day schedule impact without a written, signed change order. Track everything in a numbered log. Reconcile monthly. **The single largest source of disputes in residential DB is informal verbal change requests that go undocumented.**

## 5. Budget Management Throughout Design

### 5.1 The Critical Mistake

The single most common failure mode in residential DB is **letting design run without cost checkpoints**. Architect designs through SD → DD → CD with the owner's enthusiasm building → CDs are 90% done → GC delivers a number → owner is $400K over budget → painful 3-month VE exercise → relationship damaged.

**The fix is structural:** *mandate a cost-budget reconciliation at the end of every design phase, with sign-off required before advancing to the next phase.*

| Phase | Cost confidence | Action at end of phase |
|---|---|---|
| Programming | Order of magnitude ($/SF benchmark) | Confirm budget aligns with program |
| SD | ±25% ROM estimate | Reconcile; if >10% over, VE before DD |
| DD | ±15% estimate | Reconcile; if >10% over, VE before CD |
| CD 70% | ±10% estimate | Reconcile; final VE round |
| CD 90% | ±5–8% GMP-ready estimate | Set GMP |

### 5.2 Cost-per-SF Benchmarks — California 2025

These ranges reflect mid-2025 California pricing for new construction *exclusive of land*, *exclusive of design fees*, *inclusive of soft costs typical to permitting and engineering*. Highly location-dependent.

| Tier | Range ($/SF) | Profile | Typical California regions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low-spec | $300–$450 | Production-grade finishes, conventional construction, simple plan | Sacramento, Central Valley, parts of Inland Empire |
| Mid-spec | $450–$650 | Quality stock finishes, mid-tier fixtures, some custom millwork | San Diego, Sacramento metro, North Bay rural |
| High-spec | $650–$1,000 | Designer-driven, high-end appliances, custom millwork, complex roof | Bay Area, LA Westside, Marin, Peninsula, OC coast |
| Luxury / Ultra | $1,000–$2,000+ | Show-home level, imported materials, complex steel/glass, AV/home automation | Atherton, Beverly Hills, Pacific Heights, Malibu, Pebble Beach |

**Adjustments to apply:**

| Factor | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Hillside / sloped site | +10% to +30% |
| Coastal zone (CCC review) | +5% to +15% |
| Historic district review | +10% to +20% |
| Demo of existing structure | +$15K–$80K (above the per-SF) |
| Significant site work / retaining | +$50K–$300K (above the per-SF) |
| Detached ADU on same property | +$200K–$450K (separate per-SF math; ~$400–$700/SF for ADU alone) |
| Bay Area labor premium vs. Central Valley | +20% to +35% |
| Solar + battery + EV charging | +$30K–$80K |
| Geothermal HVAC | +$40K–$100K |
| Wine cellar / home theater / gym | +$50K–$250K |
| Pool | +$80K–$250K |
| ICF, SIP, or steel-frame construction | +10% to +25% over conventional |

[Sources: Golden State D&E 2025 California Custom Home Cost; Marin Builders Association; JDJ Consulting LA Construction 2025]

### 5.3 Open-Book Estimating

In DB, the most defensible business model is **fully open-book** — sharing your cost breakdown with the owner during design and construction. The argument that "open book makes the owner haggle your margin" is overrated; in practice, owners who get open-book access settle into trust quickly.

**What open book actually means:**
- You share sub bids (or summaries with names redacted if subs require)
- You share your GMP build-up (line by line, with fee and contingency clearly marked)
- You share your monthly cost-to-complete reports during construction
- You share the change order math (cost + markup, not just a lump number)

**What it does not mean:**
- You waive your fee
- You let the owner pick subs you do not work with
- You disclose your overhead allocation methodology in full forensic detail

**Tools:** A monthly cost-to-complete (CTC) report tracks:
- GMP
- Executed change orders (additions)
- Committed cost (subs + materials under contract)
- Cost to date
- Cost to complete (estimated)
- Variance (CTC + cost to date vs. GMP)
- Contingency consumption
- Allowance reconciliation

### 5.4 Value Engineering Sessions

A structured VE session is a 2–4 hour working meeting with architect, owner, GC, and (when relevant) key subs. The agenda:

1. **Review current estimate vs. budget gap** (the number, in dollars, that must come out)
2. **GC presents a categorized list of VE candidates** — typically 30–60 items
3. **Walk each item:** what is the change, what is the savings, what is the design impact?
4. **Owner decides each:** keep, modify, omit
5. **Architect documents** the design changes; GC updates the estimate
6. **Sign-off** — owner signs the VE log

**Categorization framework:**

| Category | Definition | Owner reluctance | GC recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| A — Invisible | No design or function impact (e.g., 2x6 vs. 2x4 framing where structurally equivalent) | Low | Always take |
| B — Equivalent quality | Different brand/material, same look and function (e.g., Schluter vs. on-site mud bed under same tile) | Low | Almost always take |
| C — Modest design change | Minor visible change (e.g., 8" vs. 10" baseboards) | Medium | Take if any meaningful savings |
| D — Material design change | Visible aesthetic change (e.g., quartz vs. quartzite countertops) | High | Owner choice |
| E — Functional change | Reduces scope (e.g., omit wine cellar) | Very high | Owner choice |
| F — Specification change | Lower tier (e.g., Wolf vs. Bosch range) | Highest | Last resort |

**Goal of a VE session:** typically a 10–20% cost reduction without going past Category C. Going into D/E/F means the owner over-programmed the project; address through scope reduction.

### 5.5 Budget-to-Design Reconciliation at Each Phase

Set the expectation at contract signing:

> "At the end of SD, DD, and 70% CDs, we will deliver an updated estimate. If we are within 5% of budget, we proceed. If we are 5–10% over, we will recommend specific reductions; you decide. If we are 10%+ over, we pause and have a structured VE session before continuing. If we cannot close the gap to within 5% after VE, we may need to discuss reducing program or expanding budget."

This sets the deck for "this is normal, we have a process for it" rather than "oh no, the GC failed us."

### 5.6 Contingency Drawdown

Contingency should reduce as design uncertainty reduces.

| Stage | Contingency % | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Programming | 20% | Everything is uncertain |
| End of SD | 15% | Massing locked; major systems still uncertain |
| End of DD | 10–12% | Systems chosen; details uncertain |
| 70% CDs | 8% | Mostly designed; sub pricing emerging |
| 90% CDs (GMP set) | 5–7% | Design largely complete; some allowances and field unknowns |
| 50% complete (construction) | 3–4% | Past most demo/foundation surprises |
| 80% complete | 1–2% | Punch list and final selections |

Tell the owner up front: "Your project budget at signing assumes 15% contingency on top of the design estimate. As design resolves, we expect to release 5–10% of that contingency back to scope (upgrades) or back to the owner (savings)."

## 6. Selecting and Managing Subs on Design-Build

### 6.1 Early Sub Involvement (Design Assist)

In DBB, subs bid at 100% CDs. In DB, the best subs are involved during DD — particularly MEP and complex trades.

**Subs to bring in at DD:**

| Trade | Why early | What they contribute |
|---|---|---|
| Foundation / concrete | Sets foundation system before structural is finalized | Mat vs. stem-wall vs. piers based on geotech |
| Framer | Reviews structural with SE | Identifies costly framing situations |
| HVAC (C-20) | Design-assist on system selection | Equipment selection, duct routing, refrigerant lineset paths |
| Plumber (C-36) | Design-assist on water heating, drain layout | Manifold vs. trunk-and-branch, tankless siting |
| Electrician (C-10) | Design-assist on service size, panel location, EV/solar | Service upsizing needed? Sub-panel locations? |
| Solar (C-46) | Roof layout, conduit chases | Coordinates with roof and electrical |
| Cabinet maker (C-6) | Reviews kitchen and bath cabinetry plans | Identifies dimension issues, fabrication efficiency |
| Steel/iron (C-51) | Reviews moment frames, exposed steel | Detail and connection design |
| Glazier (C-17) | Reviews windows, sliding doors | Specifies hardware, structural sizing |
| Stucco/EIFS (C-35) | Reviews drainage plane, sheathing | Coordination with windows and flashing |

**Design assist deliverables (per sub):**
- Material/system recommendations
- Estimated cost (firm or budgetary)
- Critical-path lead times
- Coordination notes (impacts on other trades)
- Constructability red flags

**Compensating design-assist subs:** Most design-assist work is unpaid — the sub gets it back as their share of the construction work. For specialty trades doing significant pre-construction engineering (e.g., a custom glazier producing shop drawings during DD), a separate paid pre-construction agreement is appropriate ($2K–$10K typical).

[Sources: IMEG Design-Assist Engineering; ConsensusDocs 410 Cost+GMP DB]

### 6.2 Pre-Pricing and Locking Sub Pricing

A common DB question: when do you lock sub prices?

| Sub category | When to lock | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Long-lead specialty (windows, cabinets, custom steel) | At DD or earlier with deposit | 6–16 week lead times; need firm price to release |
| Major trades (framing, MEP, roofing) | At 80–90% CDs | Pricing meaningful only when scope defined |
| Finish trades (paint, tile, flooring) | At 90%+ CDs | Final selections drive pricing |
| Site/utilities | At permit-ready | Field conditions known |

**Sub pre-pricing strategies:**
- **Cost-not-to-exceed (CNTE)** — sub commits to a maximum, with reconciliation against actual cost
- **Guaranteed sub price with allowances** — sub commits to fixed price for known scope; allowances for un-finalized items
- **Time and materials (T&M) for early work** — sub bills hourly + cost during design phase

### 6.3 Sub Contract Structure in Design-Build

Your sub agreements must flow down the DB risks intelligently. Key clauses to include:

| Clause | What it does |
|---|---|
| Flow-down of design responsibility | Sub is responsible only for work shown in CDs they bid against; design changes are GC's risk, not sub's |
| Schedule | Sub commits to milestone dates, not just substantial completion |
| Lead time guarantee | Critical for long-lead — sub warrants delivery date or pays delay damages |
| Substitution rights | Sub cannot substitute without GC approval; if owner-spec'd item unavailable, GC handles |
| Coordination obligation | Sub must coordinate with other trades, attend coordination meetings |
| Change order process | Same as GC's process with owner — written, signed, before work proceeds |
| Insurance | Per California requirements: workers' comp, GL ≥ $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate, auto ≥ $1M |
| License compliance | Verify CSLB at time of contract and annually |
| Indemnification | Sub indemnifies GC for sub's negligence; mutual where appropriate |

**Design-assist sub protection:** If you bring a sub in to help design a system, then bid the work to a different sub, you have created an enemy and possibly an enforceable claim. Either:
- Pay the design-assist sub for their pre-construction time, and reserve the right to bid the work; or
- Bring them in design-assist with a commitment to construction work, contingent on price reconciliation

### 6.4 Coordination Drawings

In residential DB, MEP coordination drawings are often skipped — and this is where costly field conflicts emerge in custom homes with complex kitchens, wine cellars, home theaters, and tight mechanical chases.

**When MEP coordination drawings are essential:**

| Project condition | Coordination required? |
|---|---|
| Standard single-family, 8-ft ceilings, conventional plan | Often not — fieldcoordinate |
| Custom home with high ceilings, exposed beams, mechanical-in-ceiling | Yes — produce coordination drawings |
| Tight mechanical room with multiple trades | Yes |
| Hydronic radiant + forced air + ERV + spray-foam roof assembly | Absolutely yes |
| Wine cellar (separate climate zone, condensate, drain) | Yes |
| Home theater (acoustic isolation, HVAC, AV cable trays) | Yes |
| Complex kitchen (multiple appliances with gas/water/electrical/exhaust) | Yes — at minimum a kitchen MEP sheet |

**Who produces them:**
- **In a simple project:** the GC's PM, marking up the MEP plans with conflict callouts
- **In a complex project:** the MEP design-assist subs collaborate on a coordinated 3D model or 2D overlay; the architect or GC manages clash resolution
- **Scale:** 1/2" = 1'-0" for typical rooms; 1" = 1'-0" for tight spaces (mechanical rooms, soffit details)

The cost of producing coordination drawings is $2K–$15K depending on complexity. The cost of *not* producing them, in a complex custom home, is $20K–$100K in field rework.

## 7. Selection and Finishes Management

### 7.1 The Selections Process

Selections are the place where DB projects most often go sideways. The owner did not expect to make 200 decisions in 6 months. The architect did not expect to play interior designer. The GC did not expect to chase decisions every Tuesday.

**Structured selections workflow:**

| Step | Owner role | Architect role | GC role | ID role (if engaged) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Selections matrix established | Reviews | Drafts | Validates lead times | Suggests options |
| 2. Selection deadlines aligned to CPM | Acknowledges | — | Owns | — |
| 3. Owner presented with options | Decides | Curates | Confirms feasibility | Curates |
| 4. Decision documented | Signs | — | Logs | — |
| 5. Specification issued to sub | — | Updates spec | Issues PO | — |
| 6. Submittal returned | Reviews | Approves | Manages | Reviews |
| 7. Installation | — | Site verification | Executes | Site verification |

**Selection categories — typical custom home has 80–150 line items:**

- **Exterior:** Roofing, siding, stone/brick veneer, windows, exterior doors, garage doors, exterior lighting, address numbers, mailbox, gutters/downspouts, exterior railings
- **Interior architecture:** Door styles, door hardware, baseboard/casing, crown moulding, wainscoting, ceiling treatments
- **Kitchen:** Cabinets, counter material, backsplash, sink, faucet, appliances (range, ovens, hood, refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave, beverage center, ice maker, etc.), pendant lights, under-cabinet lighting
- **Bathrooms (per bathroom):** Vanity cabinet, vanity top, vanity faucet, mirror, lighting, toilet, shower/tub fixtures, valves, drains, tile (floor + wall + shower floor), accessories (towel bars, hooks, tissue holder)
- **Living spaces:** Flooring, paint colors (per room), fireplace surround, built-ins, lighting fixtures
- **MEP:** HVAC equipment selection, thermostat, water heater, water softener/filter, electrical panel, switches/outlets (style and color), smart home controls
- **Outdoor:** Hardscape, landscape plants, irrigation, outdoor lighting, pool/spa, outdoor kitchen, fencing

### 7.2 Selection Schedule — Working Backward from Construction Milestones

For each item, the selection deadline is determined by:

**Lead time + fabrication time + on-site coordination buffer + construction milestone**

| Item | Lead time (typical) | Selection deadline before installation milestone |
|---|---|---|
| Windows / exterior doors | 6–16 weeks (custom up to 20+) | 16–20 weeks before rough opening |
| Cabinetry (custom) | 8–16 weeks | 12–16 weeks before install |
| Appliances (high-end Wolf/SubZero) | 6–12 weeks (some up to 6 mos) | 12–16 weeks before install (need for kitchen MEP rough) |
| Stone slabs (countertops) | 4–10 weeks (selection + slab pull + fab) | 8–10 weeks before install |
| Tile (stock) | 2–6 weeks | 4–6 weeks before install |
| Tile (custom/import) | 8–16 weeks | 12–16 weeks before install |
| Plumbing fixtures (in-stock) | 1–3 weeks | 3–4 weeks before rough |
| Plumbing fixtures (special order, e.g., Vola, Dornbracht) | 8–16 weeks | 12–16 weeks before rough |
| Light fixtures (stock) | 1–3 weeks | 3–4 weeks before rough |
| Light fixtures (custom/import) | 6–14 weeks | 10–14 weeks before rough |
| Hardware (doors, cabinets) | 1–6 weeks | 4–8 weeks before installation |
| HVAC equipment | 2–8 weeks (heat pumps, ERVs longer) | 6–10 weeks before rough |
| Garage doors | 6–12 weeks | 10–14 weeks before installation |
| Roofing material (clay tile, custom standing seam) | 6–14 weeks | 10–14 weeks before installation |
| Specialty steel (railings, custom items) | 8–20 weeks | 12–20 weeks before installation |

**The master selections calendar:** Build a Gantt-style chart with all selection deadlines plotted against the construction schedule. Review weekly with owner. The most common cause of delay in custom homes is late selections, *not* construction problems.

[Sources: SoCo Wholesale Windows lead times; cabinet industry standards]

### 7.3 Owner Decision Fatigue

The owner does not want to choose between 50 tile patterns. They want to choose between 3.

**Curation strategy:**

| Stage | Number of options | Who curates |
|---|---|---|
| Initial concept | 1 (architect's recommendation) | Architect |
| Owner review | 3 options at most | Architect + ID + GC |
| Final selection | 1 | Owner |

**Tactics to reduce fatigue:**
- **Group selections by room and visit showrooms together** — make 30 decisions in one focused 4-hour session, not 30 emails over 6 weeks
- **Use a "selections book"** — physical or digital binder with samples and specs, organized by room
- **Default-decisions framework** — for low-stakes items (e.g., outlet color), the architect picks and owner approves with a single click
- **"Family of 3" curation** — for each item, present a "value", "recommended", and "premium" option, all from the same designer's aesthetic
- **Make-by dates with consequences** — "If we don't have your window selection by July 15, we have to substitute a 4-week-lead window or accept a 6-week schedule slip"

### 7.4 Interior Designer Coordination

When an ID is on the team, the GC's job is interface management:

| ID deliverable | GC's action |
|---|---|
| Spec sheet for a fixture | Log in submittal tracker; issue PO; manage delivery |
| Tile layout drawing | Issue to tile sub with the tile order |
| Cabinetry design | Coordinate with cabinet sub; verify dimensions against framing |
| Lighting schedule | Issue to electrician with rough-in plan |
| Paint schedule | Issue to painter; order samples; manage approval |
| FF&E (furniture) | Typically owner-purchase; GC coordinates only delivery and protection |

**Common ID-sub conflicts and how to resolve:**

| Conflict | Resolution |
|---|---|
| ID specs a fixture the plumber says won't work | GC mediates; either ID re-specs or plumber accommodates |
| ID changes tile layout after install starts | Change order; ID's request, owner authorizes |
| ID's spec sheet is incomplete (model # missing) | GC sends RFI to ID; do not order until complete |
| Sub installs per spec but ID doesn't like result | Document spec was followed; if change desired, change order |

### 7.5 Mock-ups and Samples

Mock-ups cost money but prevent expensive mistakes.

**When to mock up:**

| Item | Mock-up worth it? | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Stucco color/texture on a 4'×4' panel | Yes — always | $200–$500 |
| Stone veneer pattern on a 8'×8' panel | Yes for complex stonework | $500–$2,000 |
| Tile pattern (especially mosaic, Moroccan, herringbone) | Yes for any pattern owner is uncertain about | $500–$3,000 |
| Paint colors on an actual wall (multiple samples, viewed at multiple times of day) | Always | $100–$300 |
| Wood floor finish on a 4'×4' panel | Yes for custom stains | $300–$1,000 |
| Cabinet door sample with full finish | Yes — always | $200–$800 |
| Window head/jamb detail mockup (especially for exposed metal or custom trim) | Yes for any non-stock condition | $500–$2,500 |
| Plaster (Venetian, lime wash, etc.) on a sample panel | Yes — always | $500–$2,000 |
| Stone slab — view the actual slab before fabrication | Yes — always | $0–$1,500 (delivery) |

A $500 mock-up that prevents a $20,000 rework is the highest ROI activity in custom home construction. Budget $5K–$15K for mock-ups in any high-end custom home.

### 7.6 Substitution Management

The owner has selected a particular tile. The supplier reports it has been discontinued. Now what?

**The substitution protocol:**

1. **Document the original spec** — model, color, size, finish, supplier
2. **Confirm unavailability** — written from supplier
3. **Propose 2–3 substitutions** — same or better quality and aesthetic
4. **Present to owner** with samples
5. **Owner selects** or requests a different option
6. **Document the substitution** in the selection log
7. **Pricing adjustment** — if equivalent, no change; if upgrade, change order at differential

**When the substitution is over allowance:**

- "Your selection is $3K over the tile allowance. We can either: (a) issue a change order for the $3K, (b) we can find an equivalent within allowance, or (c) we can move $3K from another allowance you're under, if you'd like." Present options without pressure.

**Never substitute without owner approval.** Even for "equivalent" items. The owner picked what they picked; their judgment is not yours to override.

## 8. Permit Strategy for Design-Build

### 8.1 Pre-Application Meetings

Most California jurisdictions offer pre-application or "pre-submittal" meetings with planning and building staff. For DB projects, **bring both GC and architect**.

**Benefits:**
- Identifies major code or zoning issues before design proceeds
- Establishes a working relationship with plan check staff
- Gets jurisdiction-specific quirks early (e.g., Berkeley's "Living Building" suggestions, Marin's fire-zone requirements, Palo Alto's design review thresholds)
- Documents the building official's interpretation of ambiguous code provisions
- Often discounts plan-check time when issues are pre-resolved

**Meeting agenda:**
1. Site overview — show site plan, vicinity
2. Project description — size, height, scope
3. Zoning compliance — setback, FAR, height, lot coverage
4. Design review triggers — does this require formal DR?
5. Major code questions — fire access, energy, structural triggers
6. Required studies — soils, hydrology, arborist
7. Permit path — single permit or phased
8. Estimated review time — when can we expect plan check completion
9. Contacts — who handles each issue

### 8.2 Phased Permitting

For large or schedule-sensitive projects, phased permitting can save 6–12 weeks of calendar time.

**Common phasing options:**

| Phase 1 permit | Phase 2 permit |
|---|---|
| Demolition only | Full new construction |
| Grading and site work | Foundation through final |
| Foundation and underground utilities | Vertical construction through final |
| Shell (structure, exterior, MEP rough) | Interior finishes (less common) |

**When phased permitting works:**
- Jurisdiction allows it (some do not — verify early)
- Construction documents for the phase being permitted are complete
- Civil/structural for foundation is fully designed even if interiors are not
- Geotech and soils complete
- The remaining design has no impact on what's being built (i.e., interior layout cannot change foundation locations)

**Risks of phased permitting:**
- Plan check for Phase 2 finds a conflict with Phase 1 already built — must be resolved at GC cost
- Owner change in scope after Phase 1 in ground may require demo-and-redo
- Some jurisdictions charge full plan-check fees per phase (cost increase)

### 8.3 Deferred Submittals

Deferred submittals are items the engineer of record approves *after* the main permit is issued, with the building official's prior approval (per CBC §107.3.4.1).

**Commonly deferred items in residential:**

| Item | Why deferred |
|---|---|
| Truss engineering | Truss manufacturer engineers the package to fit; truss layout shown on roof framing plan |
| Hold-down hardware (shear wall schedule) | Fabricator-specific calcs |
| Stair stringers (manufactured) | Stair sub provides shop drawings |
| Pre-engineered metal canopies, awnings | Manufacturer-engineered |
| Fire sprinkler shop drawings (NFPA 13R/13D) | Fire sprinkler sub designs to fit; submitted to fire dept |
| Photovoltaic system | Solar contractor designs and permits separately or as deferred |
| Solar hot water | Same as PV |
| Cabinet shop drawings | Cabinet maker's responsibility |
| Stone veneer attachment | Engineered separately for some installations |

**Tracking deferred submittals:**
- Log every deferred submittal item in a tracker at permit issuance
- Set deadlines for each tied to the construction schedule
- Verify each item is approved *before* the related work is installed and *before* the related inspection
- Maintain the deferred submittal log as part of the permit record

**2024–2025 trend:** Some California jurisdictions (notably Sonoma County effective October 2024) are tightening or eliminating deferred submittals for trusses, fire sprinklers, and PV. **Verify your jurisdiction's current rules at the pre-application meeting** — do not assume what was deferrable last year still is.

[Sources: Permit Sonoma 9-4-20 Deferred Truss Submittals; Permit Sonoma October 2024 update; CBC §107.3.4.1 Redding interpretation]

### 8.4 Permit-Ready vs. Design Development Drawings — The Hidden Risk

Some clients (and some GCs) try to permit under thin drawings to save design time. This usually costs more than it saves.

**Symptoms of under-detailed permit drawings:**
- Plan check returns 30+ comments
- Multiple rounds of corrections
- Field conditions not anticipated (e.g., framer asks "how does this beam land?")
- Conflicts emerge during construction (MEP doesn't fit where assumed)
- RFIs spike during early construction (>10 per week)

**Cost of under-detailed permit drawings:**
- 4–12 weeks additional plan-check time
- $5K–$30K in plan-check correction architect/engineer fees
- $20K–$100K in field RFI rework costs
- Schedule overrun of 2–8 weeks

**Better path:** Push the architect to 90%+ CDs before permit submittal. Yes, it adds 4–8 weeks of pre-permit design. Yes, the owner is impatient. But every CD week saves 2–3 weeks of permit + construction time.

## 9. Communication and Client Management in Design-Build

### 9.1 The Single Point of Contact Advantage

In DB the owner has one number to call. This is sold as a feature; it is also a responsibility.

**Establishing the SPOC role:**
- One person on the GC team is the owner's primary contact — usually the project manager or principal
- Architect communications go through the GC's PM (or are CC'd)
- Sub questions to owner are filtered through the PM
- Owner does not call subs directly; subs do not call owner directly
- Weekly status reports come from one source

**When the SPOC role breaks down:**
- Owner emails architect directly with a design change → architect drafts a sketch → no one tells the GC → field finds the change → cost overrun → finger pointing
- Sub calls owner with a question → owner answers → sub does the wrong thing → no documentation

**Enforce the protocol from week 1.** If the owner calls a sub directly, the PM follows up: "I see you spoke with [sub]. Let's make sure I'm in the loop so we don't have miscommunications. From now on, please copy me on anything to do with the project."

### 9.2 Weekly OAC Meetings

The **Owner-Architect-Contractor (OAC) meeting** is the heartbeat of any well-run residential DB project.

**Cadence:** Weekly during construction; bi-weekly during design (or after major milestones).

**Standard agenda (60–90 min):**

1. Safety / incidents (5 min)
2. Schedule — last week's progress, this week's plan, two-week look-ahead (10 min)
3. Budget — cost-to-complete, change orders, contingency status (10 min)
4. RFIs — open list, status (10 min)
5. Submittals — open list, status (5 min)
6. Selections — items due this week and next two weeks (10 min)
7. Design decisions / open items (10–20 min)
8. Change orders — proposed, pending, executed (5–10 min)
9. Quality / inspections — recent and upcoming (5 min)
10. New business (5 min)
11. Action items — review and confirm (5 min)

**Documentation:**
- Agenda issued 24 hrs in advance
- Minutes issued within 48 hrs after
- **Decision log** — every binding decision recorded with date, decision, decider
- **Action item log** — every commitment with owner, due date, status

Use a single shared system (Procore, Buildertrend, CoConstruct, or even Notion + a Google Sheet decision log) — not buried in email threads.

### 9.3 Managing the Owner-Architect Relationship

In DB the architect is your sub (or your B141 partner), but the architect has a deep personal relationship with the owner. When tensions arise, you must navigate between:

**When the GC steps in:**
- The architect is non-responsive to owner emails for more than 48 hrs
- Owner is making design decisions that don't match what the architect is drawing
- Architect has overpromised on a deadline they cannot meet
- Owner is asking architect for things outside the contract scope

**When the GC steps back:**
- Owner-architect are working through a design choice that's clearly aesthetic
- Architect is helping owner with FF&E selections (let it happen, but watch scope)
- Architect is doing site visits and reporting back to owner — that's their job

**The "joint front" rule:** Never disagree with the architect in front of the owner. Pull the architect aside, work it out, return with a unified position. Public disagreement erodes owner confidence in the whole team.

### 9.4 Scope Creep in Design-Build

The "can we just add..." dynamic is the most insidious cost driver in DB. Every "small" addition compounds.

**Examples of scope creep:**
- "Can we just add a little nook for a built-in desk?" → $4K added to millwork
- "Can we just add a sink in the laundry?" → $1,500 plumbing, $800 cabinet, $400 counter
- "Can we just upsize the dining room by 2 feet?" → $15K structural and finishes
- "Can we just add a wine fridge?" → $2K appliance, $400 electrical, $300 cabinet mod
- "Can we just paint the garage interior?" → $1,800 paint
- "Can we just add some recessed lighting?" → $200/can installed, 8 cans = $1,600

After 10 of these "small" requests, you are $25K–$50K over budget with no change orders signed.

**The discipline:**
- **Every** change, no matter how small, is captured as a change order proposal
- A simple form: scope addition, cost impact, schedule impact, owner signature
- Even $200 changes get a CO — the discipline trains the owner to think before asking
- "I'll write this up — I'll have a CO for you tomorrow" rather than "sure, no problem, I'll just add it"

**Setting the expectation at contract:**

> "Any change to scope, no matter how small, will be captured in a written change order. This protects both of us — you'll always know exactly what something costs, and we'll never have a surprise at the end of the project. Even if the change is $100, we'll write it up. You may think this is overkill, but it's how we maintain trust and clarity."

### 9.5 Transparency on Cost

Two philosophies on cost transparency in residential DB:

**Lump sum / closed-book:** GC delivers a single number. Owner has no visibility into sub costs, GC's overhead, or fee. Pros: simpler for owner; protects GC from second-guessing. Cons: feels opaque to sophisticated owners; reduces trust; difficult to manage allowances and substitutions.

**Open-book / cost-plus with GMP:** Full visibility into sub bids, GC overhead, fee, contingency. Pros: builds trust; enables informed VE; aligns owner and GC interests. Cons: owner sometimes haggles individual line items; requires more administrative work.

**Recommended hybrid for residential custom DB:**
- Fixed fee for GC (disclosed) — typically 5–8% overhead + 7–10% profit on cost
- Open-book on subs (bids shown; sub names redacted if subs request)
- GC's contingency disclosed but managed by GC
- Owner's contingency held by owner, drawn down with mutual agreement
- Monthly CTC reports with full transparency
- Final reconciliation at project close — savings split per contract

This builds long-term relationships and produces the referrals that make residential GC businesses profitable. Owners who feel they were treated with transparency become your best marketing.

## 10. Profitability of Design-Build

### 10.1 Fee Structure

The GC's revenue in DB comes from several layered fees:

| Revenue stream | Typical rate | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-construction services agreement (PCSA) | $5K–$50K fixed, or 1.5–3% of estimated construction cost | Paid monthly during design phase |
| Design management fee | 5–10% of design contract value | When architect is subbed to GC |
| Construction overhead | 5–8% of cost | Standard residential markup |
| Construction profit | 7–10% of cost | Standard residential markup |
| Change order markup | 15–20% on subcontracted change costs, 10–15% on self-performed | Per contract |
| Savings split (under GMP) | 25–50% of savings | Per contract |
| Allowance markup | 0–10% — varies by contract | Per contract |

**For a $2M custom home in California, GC revenue might look like:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| PCSA (6 months @ $5K/mo) | $30,000 |
| Design management fee (10% of $180K architect fee) | $18,000 |
| Construction OH (6% of $1.7M cost of work) | $102,000 |
| Construction profit (9% of $1.7M cost of work) | $153,000 |
| Change orders (avg 5% of contract @ 15% mark-up) | $15,000 |
| Savings share (3% under GMP, 33% split) | $20,000 |
| **Total GC revenue** | **$338,000** |
| **Total project cost to owner** | **$2,000,000** |
| **GC revenue as % of total** | **16.9%** |

The same project in DBB might produce $190K–$240K in GC revenue (8–10% margin on construction only). The 6–8% increment is the value of pre-construction and design management — provided the GC delivers actual value (not just markup).

### 10.2 Pre-Construction Services Agreement

The PCSA is how you get paid for the design-phase work before the construction contract is executed.

**What's in scope:**
- Attending design meetings
- Preliminary estimating at SD, DD, CD milestones
- Constructability review
- Subcontractor pre-pricing and design-assist coordination
- Permit research and pre-application coordination
- Schedule development
- VE session facilitation
- Selections coordination support

**How to price:**

| Project size | Typical PCSA fee |
|---|---|
| ADU ($350K–$600K) | $5K–$15K fixed |
| Renovation/addition ($500K–$1.5M) | $10K–$30K fixed |
| Custom home ($1.5M–$3M) | $25K–$75K fixed, or $5K–$10K/mo through CDs |
| Custom home ($3M–$8M) | $50K–$150K fixed, or 1.5–2.5% of estimated cost |
| Luxury custom home ($8M+) | 2–4% of estimated cost, or hourly with cap |

**How to sell it:**

> "Our pre-construction services are how we earn the right to be your construction partner. During design, we'll provide three estimates at SD, DD, and 70% CDs — each with detailed assumptions and trade-offs. We'll coordinate with the architect on constructability, get pre-pricing from our long-term subs, run VE sessions when needed, and have you to a permit-ready set with a real GMP — not a guess. Without this work, you'd either go to bid at 100% CDs and discover the design is over budget (then waste 3 months on VE), or you'd get a contractor's "estimate" that has no real underwriting behind it. Our PCSA is a real-money investment in confidence at GMP signing."

**Crediting the PCSA toward construction:** Some GCs credit 50–100% of the PCSA against the construction contract if the project proceeds. This is good practice — it aligns incentives and gives the owner a clean off-ramp.

### 10.3 When Design-Build Is More Profitable

DB outperforms DBB on margin when the GC actually captures the operational advantages:

| Advantage | Margin impact |
|---|---|
| Faster schedule | Less general conditions cost (each month of GC's on-site overhead = 0.5–1% of project) |
| Fewer change orders | Less administrative overhead; less owner-GC friction |
| Higher allowance attachment | Markups on additional selections owners make |
| Pre-construction fees | New revenue stream not available in DBB |
| Better-vetted subs | Repeat subs at known performance = less risk premium |
| Repeat clients and referrals | Lower marketing cost; higher close rate |

**The compound effect:** A GC running 3 DB projects/year at 16% net margin produces ~$960K revenue on $6M of work. The same GC running DBB at 8% margin produces $480K on the same volume. To match DB revenue in DBB, the GC needs to double volume — which means doubling the team, the overhead, and the operational complexity.

### 10.4 When Design-Build Goes Wrong

**Design liability exposure (the biggest single risk):**
- GC holds the architect's contract; architect makes an error; client demands the GC fix it
- GC's general liability does not cover design negligence
- GC pursues architect under B141 — but the architect's E&O has exclusions, or the policy limit is exhausted, or the architect is insolvent
- GC absorbs the cost

**Mitigation:**
- Require architects to carry $1M+ E&O with the GC named as additional insured for the project
- Carry Contractor's Professional Liability (CPL) — $1M minimum, ideally $2M, premium ~$5K–$15K/yr for a small residential book
- Use B141 form (not custom architect agreements) — it allocates risk in your favor
- Structure as PDB or hybrid where possible to limit your design-of-record exposure

**Scope disputes between design intent and constructed reality:**
- Owner says "this is not what we wanted" — even though it matches CDs
- "Design intent" claims absent specification detail
- Aesthetic complaints (color, texture, scale) that are subjective

**Mitigation:**
- Mock-ups, mock-ups, mock-ups
- Photographic documentation of all milestones
- Architect's site observation reports as documentation
- Selections logs signed by owner
- Pre-installation inspections by owner with sign-off

**Owner changes midstream:**
- Owner changes their mind about a major design element after construction is underway
- Owner asks "can we just" 50 times
- Owner blames GC for the cost of changes

**Mitigation:**
- Discipline on change orders (everything documented)
- Clear contract language on owner-initiated changes
- Architect involved in change pricing (validates that change is the owner's, not a design omission)
- Schedule communication — every change extends the schedule; show the owner what it costs in time as well as money
