# Outdoor Living and Specialty Residential Scopes — California GC Reference

> **Scope of this module.** You are a residential GC. You hold a B (General Building) license. You will not self-perform pool construction (requires C-53), and probably will not self-perform landscape installation (C-27), masonry retaining walls (C-29), or ornamental gate fabrication (C-61/D-03). But clients hand you the outdoor scope as part of a whole-house remodel or new build, and the only way to manage it is to **scope it correctly, price it within reason, coordinate it with the other trades, and inspect the quality**. This document is the operating manual for doing that.

> **California overlay.** Everything here is filtered through California: CRC 2022 (effective Jan 1 2023, in effect for most of 2026 until the next cycle), CBC for any commercial overlap, Title 24 Part 6 for energy, CalGreen Part 11 for stormwater/MWELO, CSLB classification rules, and AQMD restrictions for wood-burning. Frost depth is essentially a non-issue in coastal/valley California; seismic, wind, and fire-risk zones dominate instead.

**The single biggest licensing trap for GCs:** thinking you can self-perform pool work under your B license. **You cannot.** CSLB Code §7026.1 and B&P §7058.5 require a C-53 for any swimming pool work. Even pool-related plumbing/electrical inside the pool envelope rolls up under the C-53. The pool decking (apron) and equipment-pad concrete are not pool work per se — those can be C-8.

## 1. Decks and Elevated Platforms

### 1.1 Code basis (California 2022)

| Code | Applies to | Key sections |
|---|---|---|
| CRC R301–R301.5 | Loads — 40 psf live for residential decks, 60 psf for guards | Adopted from IRC unchanged in most jurisdictions |
| CRC R311.7 | Stairs | Riser/tread, headroom, handrail |
| CRC R312 | Guards | Height, opening limits |
| **CRC R507** | **Exterior decks — the workhorse section** | Footings, posts, beams, joists, ledger, lateral |
| AWC DCA 6-15 | Prescriptive Residential Wood Deck Construction Guide | Span tables, referenced by CRC R507.5 footnotes |
| **CRC R337 / Chapter 7A** | **Wildland Urban Interface (WUI) — ignition-resistant construction** | Decking material requirements in fire zones |
| CRC R105.2 | Exemption thresholds | Decks under 200 sf, 30" above grade, not attached, not serving exit door — exempt from permit but still must meet code |

**The 30-inch rule (and its three flavors).** Three different "30-inch" thresholds get confused constantly:
1. **Guardrail required** at 30" or more above grade or floor below (R312.1.1). Measured to the walking surface.
2. **Permit threshold** in many jurisdictions: under 30" + under 200 sf + not attached → no permit (but still code-compliant).
3. **Lateral load connection** required for any deck attached to dwelling regardless of height (R507.9.2).

### 1.2 Loads (CRC Table R301.5)

| Element | Live load |
|---|---|
| Deck walking surface | 40 psf |
| Guard top rail | 200 lb point load any direction |
| Guard infill (balusters) | 50 lb on 1 sf area |
| Stair tread | 40 psf or 300 lb point load |
| Handrail | 200 lb point load |

Deck dead load is typically 10 psf for wood, 8 psf for composite over PT framing. Total design ~50 psf.

### 1.3 Span tables — the practical numbers

The full tables live in **AWC DCA 6-15**. The most-used numbers, for **#2 Southern Pine or DF-L, 40 psf LL + 10 psf DL**:

**Joist spans (joist to joist span, edge of beam to edge of beam or to ledger), 16" o.c.:**

| Joist | Span (no cantilever) | Max cantilever |
|---|---|---|
| 2x6 | 9'-11" | 1'-3" |
| 2x8 | 13'-1" | 2'-2" |
| 2x10 | 16'-2" | 2'-8" |
| 2x12 | 18'-0" | 3'-0" |

**Beam spans (post-to-post), built-up beam, supporting 8 ft joist span:**

| Beam | Span |
|---|---|
| 2-2x8 | 6'-2" |
| 2-2x10 | 7'-1" |
| 3-2x10 | 8'-10" |
| 3-2x12 | 10'-7" |

**Cantilever max** is ¼ of backspan (or table limit, whichever less).

**Post sizing**: 4x4 max 8 ft tall and 48 sf tributary; 4x6 to 8 ft and 75 sf; 6x6 required if over 8 ft tall, or for any deck per many jurisdictions because of beam bearing area.

### 1.4 Ledger attachment — the single most failure-prone detail

> **The ledger is what kills people.** Every fatal deck collapse you'll see in the news traces back to one of three failures: (1) ledger nailed (not bolted), (2) lag bolts into the rim joist sole plate or sheathing only, (3) water rot behind unflashed ledger.

**CRC R507.9 prescriptive ledger:**

- **Bolt type**: ½" lag screws (with washers) OR ½" through-bolts. Never nails alone, never construction screws (unless ICC-ES rated for ledger — Simpson SDWS or FastenMaster ThruLOK are the common rated alternatives, and they have their own spacing tables).
- **Embedment**: lag screws must penetrate at least ½" past the back side of a 2x rim joist (so ½" lag in 1½" rim + ½" sheathing + min penetration). Typical: 5" lag.
- **Spacing** (Table R507.9.1.3(1)) for joist span up to 12 ft, deck LL 40 psf:

| Spacing | Lag screw / through-bolt |
|---|---|
| Deck joist span 6 ft | 30" o.c. |
| 8 ft | 23" o.c. |
| 10 ft | 18" o.c. |
| 12 ft | 15" o.c. |
| 14 ft | 13" o.c. |
| 16 ft | 11" o.c. |

- **Stagger** rows 2" min, 5" max from top and bottom of ledger. Two rows offset.
- **Edge distance**: 2" min from end, ¾" from top/bottom edge of ledger, 2" from top of rim.
- **No lag into sole plate or top plate only** — must hit rim joist or solid blocking.

**Lateral load connection (R507.9.2)** — separate from gravity ledger. Two options:

1. **1500 lb option**: Four DTT2Z (or equivalent) hold-downs, two pairs, each pair within 24" of deck end, bolted through ledger into floor framing inside the house. Most jurisdictions require this even when the prescriptive lag pattern is followed.
2. **750 lb option (R507.9.2 alt)**: more hold-downs spread along the ledger.

**Flashing — non-negotiable:**

```
Wall sheathing (existing)
   |
   |  ┌─── building paper / WRB lapped DOWN over flashing
   |  │
   |  │   ┌── Z-flashing (galvanized or copper or PVC), 4" up wall, 3" out over ledger top
   |  │   │
   |  │   └────────────────────────┐
   |  │                            │
   |  ├────────────────────────────┤ <-- ledger
   |  │   2x ledger, PT, bolted    │
   |  │                            │
   |  └────────────────────────────┘
   |       ↑
   |  Cap flashing turns down over face of ledger
```

- WRB **behind** ledger (kickout to flashing).
- Z-flashing or cap flashing **over** ledger top, **under** WRB above.
- Drip edge turn-down min ½" past face of ledger.
- Use **stainless or hot-dip galvanized** flashing only — ACQ-treated lumber will eat aluminum and electro-galv in 3 years.
- Spacer (¼") behind ledger (Simpson DTT2Z + 5/16" plastic washers, or specifically rated standoff system) **dramatically** improves life by letting water drain.

**Ledger to concrete/masonry (R507.9.1.5):** ½" expansion anchors or ⅝" epoxy anchors, 2½" min embedment in 2500 psi concrete, 12" max spacing. CMU requires fully grouted cells at anchor locations.

**Ledger to brick veneer**: **Not allowed.** Brick veneer is not structural. Must penetrate to structural backup and treat veneer as a layer to flash through.

### 1.5 Footings and foundations

**California-specific:**
- No frost line in the typical CA project — but R403.1.4.1 still requires footing bottom **min 12" below undisturbed ground surface** OR to bear on undisturbed soil capable of 1500 psf.
- Soils report may require deeper — expansive clay (San Joaquin, parts of Bay Area) often needs 24–36" or post-tension/grade beam.
- Seismic Design Category D0–E for most populated CA — affects lateral connection and post embedment.

**Footing options:**

| Type | Use case | Typical size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cast-in-place concrete pier (Sonotube) | Standard | 12" Ø × 36" deep, or 18" Ø for heavier | Bottom bell at 24" Ø is common; check soils |
| Spread footing (square pad) | Heavy decks, multi-story | 24" × 24" × 12" thick min | Often needed for tall posts |
| Helical pier | Hillside, poor soils, no excavation access | 2⅞" or 3½" shaft, double helix | Engineered; req. torque log; ICC-ES report |
| Surface-mount post base (no footing) | Only for low ground-level decks, not allowed when deck > 30" or attached lateral | n/a | Most jurisdictions disallow |
| Driven steel pile | Soft soils, near water | Engineered | Rare residential |

**Post-to-footing connection:** Always a code-listed post base (Simpson ABU, MPB, or CBSQ) with **1" min standoff** from concrete (R317.1.4) to keep PT post end out of standing water. Anchor bolt min ⅝" with 7" embedment.

### 1.6 Decking materials

| Material | Cost/sf (2026, CA, installed) | Lifespan | Hardware needed | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pressure-treated SYP / DF-L | $20–$30 | 15–25 yr | **Stainless or HDG** (ACQ corrodes electro-galv) | Cheapest, splinters, needs annual seal |
| Western Red Cedar | $25–$35 | 15–20 yr | Stainless or HDG | Soft; pretty when new; greys |
| Redwood (Construction Heart) | $30–$45 | 20–30 yr | Stainless preferred | Sustainable supply tightening; CA native |
| Composite — capped (Trex Transcend, Fiberon Concordia, TimberTech) | $35–$55 | 25–30 yr (warranty 25–50) | Hidden fastener clips | Won't rot; fades; hot in sun |
| PVC / Cellular PVC (Azek, Wolf) | $45–$65 | 30+ yr | Hidden fastener | Coolest in sun, most stain-resistant, light |
| Tropical hardwood (Ipe, Cumaru) | $50–$75 | 30–50 yr | Stainless + pre-drilled | Dense, heavy, FSC sourcing critical |
| Aluminum decking | $40–$60 | 50+ yr | Proprietary clip | Cool, ADA-compliant slip, expensive |

**ACQ + hardware**: Modern PT lumber uses ACQ (alkaline copper quaternary) or CA (copper azole). Both are far more corrosive than the old CCA. Required hardware: **Stainless (SS304 or SS316 for coastal) or hot-dip galvanized (G185 coating, ASTM A153)**. Electroplated zinc fasteners will fail in 2–5 years.

**Hidden fastener systems** (Cortex, Camo, Tiger Claw, Eb-Ty) — required by some composite manufacturers to maintain warranty. Spec carefully: gap, clip spacing, end-of-board face screws still required.

### 1.7 Guardrails and stairs

**Guards (CRC R312):**
- Required when surface > 30" above grade measured within 36" horizontally.
- **Height: 36" min residential**, 42" if deck serves accessible route or per local. (Many CA hillside jurisdictions amend to 42".)
- **4-inch sphere rule**: balusters/openings cannot pass a 4" sphere. Note: at stairs, a **6" sphere** is allowed in the triangle formed by the riser, tread, and bottom rail.
- Top rail must resist 200 lb in any direction at any point (load test for design).
- Cable rail: requires intermediate vertical pickets every 36" or tensioned to meet 4" sphere (most fail at 3" spacing).
- Glass rail: tempered, laminated where loss of one ply would create danger of falling.

**Handrails (R311.7.8):**
- Required on at least one side of stair with 4+ risers.
- Height 34"–38" from tread nosing.
- **Graspability**: Type I (1¼"–2" circular) or Type II (perimeter 4"–6¼" with finger recess). A 2x4 on edge is **not** code-compliant.
- Returns to wall or post at ends (no projecting hazard).

**Stairs (R311.7):**
- **Max riser 7¾", min tread 10"** (residential). 7-11 is the common spec; 6-12 reads as luxurious.
- Variation max ⅜" between any two risers or treads in a flight.
- Nosing ¾"–1¼" if treads less than 11".
- Headroom 6'-8" min.
- Width 36" min (clear).
- Landings required top and bottom; min depth equals stair width; max 7¾" drop from threshold (R311.3).
- **Stringer**: 2x12 cut stringer with min 3½" throat remaining; or solid carriage with applied treads.

### 1.8 Wildland Urban Interface (WUI) — CRC Chapter 7A / R337

This is **major** for any CA project outside dense urban areas. CAL FIRE maps SRA (State Responsibility Area) and LRA-VHFHSZ (Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone). Most foothill, mountain, and hillside lots are in.

**Deck requirements in WUI (R337.9):**
- Decking within **10 ft** of an exterior wall: must be one of:
  - Noncombustible (concrete, steel, aluminum, mineral composite)
  - Ignition-resistant (passes SFM 12-7A-4 Part A)
  - Heavy timber (4x min, certain species)
  - Exterior fire-retardant-treated wood
- Decking 10 ft to property line / 30 ft from structure: same options, or fire-rated composite (Trex Enhance, TimberTech AZEK — verify SFM listing).
- **Underside** of deck within 10 ft: enclosed or sheathed with ignition-resistant material; vents 1/16" mesh.

**AB 38 / Zone 0 (effective 2025–2026 rollout):** New CA law requires a **5-ft ember-resistant zone (Zone 0)** around homes in VHFHSZ. Means **no combustible material within 5 ft of the structure** — including wood decking, wood fencing attached to house, mulch, vegetation. This is being enforced as of 2025-2026. **Talk to your client early** — they may have to rip out a year-old redwood deck within 5 ft of the house, or move it to a noncombustible zone (concrete patio).

### 1.9 Permitting workflow

1. **Pre-design check**: jurisdiction permit thresholds, setbacks, max coverage, WUI zone.
2. **Plans**: site plan, framing plan, sections, connection details (ledger, lateral, post-to-beam, post-to-footing), guardrail detail with infill calc or listing.
3. **Submittal**: usually goes through over-the-counter or expedited if under engineering threshold.
4. **Inspections**: footing (post-rebar, pre-pour), framing (post-decking, before fasteners-covered), final.

### 1.10 Pricing benchmarks (2026, coastal/Bay Area / LA basin)

| Deck type | $/sf installed (deck only) |
|---|---|
| Ground-level PT, simple rectangle | $35–$55 |
| Elevated PT with stairs, simple guard | $75–$110 |
| Elevated composite, picture-frame, cable rail | $110–$180 |
| Ipe with stainless cable rail, multi-level | $180–$280 |

## 2. Swimming Pools and Spas

### 2.1 The license reality

**You as B-classified GC cannot pull the pool permit or build the pool.** California B&P §7058.5 and CSLB Code §7026.1 reserve pool work for **C-53**. You can:

- Sign a separate contract with a C-53 (most common — the homeowner signs directly with the pool company, you coordinate as construction manager).
- Subcontract the C-53 to you and bond them in (less common, more liability).
- Self-perform **decking around the pool** (C-8 work, falls under your B), the equipment-pad concrete, and **non-pool electrical/plumbing routing** that doesn't enter the pool envelope.

What rolls up under C-53: excavation of the pool shell, steel placement, plumbing inside the bond beam, gunite/shotcrete, plaster, tile/coping, equipment pad pluming and primary electrical, anti-entrapment, deck drains tied to pool system, pool barrier when included in pool contract.

### 2.2 Pool types

| Type | Cost (2026, CA, 14×28 + spa) | Build time | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gunite / Shotcrete** | $90k–$200k+ | 10–16 weeks | Any shape/size, durable, customizable | Longest, most labor, plaster every 10–15 yr |
| **Fiberglass shell (Leisure, San Juan)** | $70k–$120k | 4–8 weeks | Fast, smooth, low maintenance | Size/shape limited, transport constraints, gel coat eventually crazes |
| **Vinyl liner** | $50k–$80k | 4–6 weeks | Cheap, smooth, custom shape | Liner replacement every 7–12 yr, punctures, less premium |

**Gunite vs. shotcrete (the terminology):** Both are pneumatically-applied concrete. Gunite = dry-mix (water added at nozzle). Shotcrete = wet-mix (premixed at batch plant). California is shifting to **wet-mix shotcrete** for quality control reasons (ACI 506, PCA recommendations). Either is acceptable; spec the **ACI 506 compliance** in your sub contract.

### 2.3 Construction sequence (gunite/shotcrete pool — the dominant CA build)

```
Week 1–2:   Layout, soils review, plan submittal, permit, locating utilities (DigAlert/811)
Week 3:     Excavation (rough dig to dimensions)
Week 4:     Steel placement (#3 rebar typical, 12" o.c. each way, bond beam #4)
            >>> PRE-GUNITE INSPECTION — verify steel, plumbing, bonding grid
Week 4:     Plumbing rough (skimmer, returns, main drain, spa lines, autofill)
            Electrical rough (bonding grid #8 copper, light niches, deck pots)
Week 5:     Gunite/shotcrete shoot (1 day for the shell)
            Cure 7–14 days (water cure or sealed)
Week 6–8:   Tile (waterline), coping (cantilever concrete, pavers, or stone)
Week 8–9:   Decking — concrete pour or pavers, expansion joint at coping
Week 9–10:  Equipment pad — pad slab, plumbing, electrical, equipment set
Week 10:    Plaster / pebble / Pebble Tec / quartz finish (1 day shoot + cure)
            >>> FILL IMMEDIATELY after plaster (within 4–6 hours)
Week 11:    Startup — brush daily 1st week, chemistry, train owner
            >>> BARRIER FINAL must pass before water + use
```

**Critical inspections (C-53 schedules, you should verify completion):**
1. **Pre-gunite**: steel pattern, plumbing pressure test (40 psi 24 hr hold), bonding inspection, depths.
2. **Pre-deck**: plumbing again, bonding around deck (5 ft of pool — re-bond), electrical conduit.
3. **Plaster**: dry shell condition, finish color sample approval before shoot.
4. **Barrier final**: gate hardware, height, climbability — see §2.5.
5. **Final**: water clarity, equipment operation, GFCI, anti-entrapment in place.

### 2.4 Permits

A pool requires **three permits** typically pulled by the C-53 (sometimes combined into one packet):

1. **Building/pool permit** — structural (shell), depths, drainage, encroachment on setbacks (typically 5 ft from PL, 5 ft from structure foundation; check local).
2. **Plumbing/gas permit** — gas line to heater (often a separate C-36 / plumbing sub), water service.
3. **Electrical permit** — equipment circuits, bonding grid, lighting, deck receptacles.

**Plan-check time**: 4–10 weeks in most CA jurisdictions; some (LADBS, San Diego) offer over-the-counter for simple pools.

**Soils**: Geotech may be required on hillside lots, or where pool is within 1.5× height of any slope. Often the bond beam needs to be tied into a structural slab or anchored. **This is where pools blow budgets** — a hillside pool can run $100k more than a flat-lot pool of the same size for the structural steel and shotcrete thickness alone.

### 2.5 Pool barriers — California Pool Safety Act (Health & Safety Code §115920–115929)

> **The GC's nightmare.** Final occupancy of a pool will not close unless the barrier is in. If you're remodeling and the project triggers re-inspection of an existing pool, the existing barrier must be brought to current code. Read this section twice.

The Act (often called the **Swimming Pool Safety Act / Drowning Prevention Act**) requires **at least two** of the following seven safety features for any new pool or pool undergoing remodel exceeding $5,000 in construction cost (raised periodically; verify current threshold):

1. Enclosure (fence) per CRC Appendix V / Appendix G:
   - 60" min height (5 ft) on outside
   - Max 4" between vertical members
   - Max 2" gap at bottom (or 4" over solid surface)
   - **No horizontal members on outside** if vertical spacing > 1¾" (climb-prevention)
   - Solid wall with handholds: not allowed
2. Removable mesh fence (per ASTM F2286) + self-closing gate
3. Approved safety pool cover (ASTM F1346 — must support a child + adult)
4. Exit alarms on doors providing direct access to pool (87 dB at 10 ft, auto-reset)
5. Self-closing, self-latching device on doors (latch min 54" off floor)
6. Pool alarm (ASTM F2208) — surface or submerged
7. Other means equal or greater in protection (rare, requires AHJ approval)

**Gates (CRC AV103 / Appendix V):**
- Open **outward** from pool
- Self-closing, self-latching
- Latch on pool side, min **60" above ground** (some local amendments require 54" — verify)
- Lock mechanism

**Common scope gap:** the C-53 quotes "pool barrier" thinking they're including the gate hardware. The B GC is doing the fence as part of site work. **Coordinate this explicitly** — who's responsible for the latch height on the side gate of the house that opens into the pool yard? Usually nobody until inspection fails. **Put it in writing**: gate hardware compliant with §115923 is by [GC / fence sub / pool sub].

### 2.6 Electrical — NEC Article 680 (CEC adopts with amendments)

**Equipotential bonding (680.26)** — this is the most-violated requirement. Required bond:
- **#8 solid copper** connecting:
  - All metal within 5 ft of inside pool wall and within 12 ft above max water level
  - Pool reinforcing steel (welded to bonding grid — done at steel stage)
  - Metal forming shell of pool
  - Metal coping, ladder anchors, diving board base
  - Pool water (intentional bond, 9 sq in min metal surface in contact — most CA pools satisfy this through metal ladder or pool light niche)
  - All pool equipment metallic parts
  - Metal piping (gas, water, structural) within 5 ft
  - Light niche
  - Skimmer, drain frames
- **Deck rebar grid bonding (680.26(B)(2))**: any concrete deck within 3 ft of pool perimeter must have steel grid bonded to equipotential system. For paver decks, an **equipotential copper grid** (typ. 12"×12" #8 solid copper grid 4–6" below pavers) is installed.

**GFCI required (680.22, 680.23, 680.32):**
- All 15A/20A 125V receptacles within **20 ft** of inside pool wall — must be GFCI.
- Within 6'-10' from pool: receptacle required for filter/pump area, GFCI.
- Pool pump motor circuits: GFCI required (single phase 240V, max 60A, since 2017 NEC).
- Underwater lighting: GFCI required regardless of voltage; **low-voltage (12V)** strongly preferred — niche-mount with secondary side bonded.

**Clearances (680.9):**
- Overhead conductors: 22.5 ft min clearance over water (most rooflines force trenching).
- Subpanels and equipment: 5 ft horizontal min from pool inside wall unless separated by permanent barrier 5 ft tall.

### 2.7 Plumbing and hydraulics

**System components:**
- **Main drain** (or two main drains 3 ft apart, or single ASME A112.19.8b certified anti-entrapment cover + SVRS)
- **Skimmer(s)** — one per ~400 sf surface area
- **Returns** — 2–4 typical, distributed
- **Pump** — variable-speed required (Title 20 since 2010 for pumps over 1 hp)
- **Filter** — cartridge, DE, or sand; size by flow rate
- **Heater** — gas, heat pump, solar, or combo
- **Sanitation** — chlorine (tabs/liquid), salt chlorine generator (SCG), UV, ozone, or combo

**VGB Act (Virginia Graeme Baker Pool & Spa Safety Act, federal, 2008):**
- All pools and spas must have **anti-entrapment drain covers** (ASME A112.19.8 / ANSI A112.19.8) certified for the application.
- Single-drain pools must have a **Safety Vacuum Release System (SVRS)**, gravity drain, or other approved system.
- Replace covers per stamped expiration date (typically 5–10 years).

**Hydraulic design** is something the C-53's engineer or designer handles, but you should know:
- Suction pipe sizing: keep velocity under 6 ft/s (FPS). 2" PVC = ~73 gpm max.
- Return pipe: under 8 ft/s. 2" = ~98 gpm.
- Turnover time: 6–8 hours for residential pool (= total volume / flow rate × 60).
- Friction loss matters — long runs to remote equipment pad will demand bigger pump.

### 2.8 Heating

| Type | Cost (equip + install) | Heat-up time / efficiency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gas (NG or propane) — 400k BTU | $5k–$8k | Fast (1–2°F/hr), 84–95% AFUE | **Low-NOx model required statewide (SCAQMD Rule 1146.2, BAAQMD Reg 9-6) — Raypak Avia, Pentair MasterTemp 125; old Atmospheric models banned for new install** |
| Electric heat pump — 110k–140k BTU | $5k–$9k | Slow (¼–½°F/hr), COP 4–6, needs 50°F+ ambient | Best for spas and warm-month use; needs 50A 240V |
| Solar (panels on roof) | $4k–$8k | Variable, free fuel | 100% of pool surface area in panels for sole heat |
| Combo (gas + solar, or HP + solar) | $10k–$16k | Best of both | Most common high-end install |

**Title 24 Part 6** restricts gas pool heaters — only **listed low-NOx heaters** can be installed in air districts that have adopted Rule 1146 (which is essentially all populated CA).

### 2.9 Equipment pad

- **Location**: minimum 5 ft from PL, 5 ft from any opening (window/door) of dwelling, ideally **15 ft from bedroom windows** for noise. Downwind of prevailing breeze when possible.
- **Setback to combustibles**: gas heater needs 4 ft min from any combustible siding (mfr spec; some require 8 ft).
- **Pad size**: 6 ft × 8 ft minimum for pump + filter + heater + chemical feeder. 8 ft × 12 ft if spa equipment + automation + salt cell.
- **Construction**: 4" concrete slab, 6×6 W2.9 WWM, on 4" compacted base. Slope ¼" per ft away from house. Edge bevel for hose drag.
- **Drainage**: backwash discharge — to sanitary or per local (LADBS allows to landscape if filtered; most jurisdictions require sanitary connection or french drain to keep chlorinated water out of storm).
- **Sound enclosure**: variable speed pumps are now quiet, but the heater fan is the loudest piece. Acoustic enclosures (Pentair Sound Shield, custom CMU) reduce 8–12 dB.

### 2.10 Spas and hot tubs

| Type | Install effort | Permit | Power |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Portable spa, plug-in 120V (110/15A)** | Set on pad, plug in | None typical | Cord-and-plug; GFCI receptacle within sight, min 6 ft from spa wall |
| **Portable spa, 240V hardwired** | Set on pad, electrical permit | Electrical | 50A or 60A circuit, GFCI breaker, disconnect within sight 5–10 ft |
| **In-ground spa attached to pool** | Built as part of pool, shared equipment with auto-valves | Yes (in pool permit) | Bonded to pool grid |
| **In-ground freestanding spa** | Own shell, own equipment | Yes (mini-pool permit) | Same as pool |

**Portable spa pad**: 4" concrete slab, level within ¼" in 10 ft. Full spa weight = 4,000–7,000 lb with water and people — check soil bearing, especially on deck-supported installs (deck must be engineered for spa, not just deck LL).

**Spa on deck**: requires structural engineering. Spa adds 100+ psf concentrated, possibly 150 psf with people. Most stock decks fail.

**Common scope gaps that bite the GC:**
- Auto-fill water line tie-in (who runs the ½" copper from house cold-water main to pool?). Usually pool sub stubs it out, GC's plumber connects.
- Gas line BTU sizing — 400k BTU heater needs ⅞" CSST or ¾" black iron on a typical run; existing house service may not have spare capacity.
- 240V circuit for heat pump or salt cell — verify panel capacity early.
- Deck **expansion joint** between concrete apron and coping — typically ½" foam, sealed; pool sub provides coping, GC's concrete sub does the slab — agree on joint detail.
- Drainage — equipment pad drain, deck drains, planter drains all need somewhere to go. Pool sub typically does pool drains only.

### 2.12 Quality checkpoints for the GC (you're not the pool guy, but you sign off)

1. **Steel inspection**: photos before gunite. #3 rebar at 12" o.c. (or per plan), bond beam #4, light niches and skimmer steel reinforcement, bonding lugs visible.
2. **Plumbing pressure test**: 40 psi held 24 hr; verify gauge reading at start and end with C-53 present.
3. **Plaster color**: get a 12"×12" test panel cured for 7 days outside before approving the shoot. Pebble finishes especially vary lot to lot.
4. **Startup chemistry**: pH 7.2–7.6, total alkalinity 80–120 ppm, calcium hardness 200–400 ppm, free chlorine 1–3 ppm. C-53 should provide a startup log.
5. **Equipment commissioning**: pump variable-speed schedule programmed, heater pilot/ignition verified, light fired, all returns flowing.

## 3. Outdoor Kitchens and BBQ Areas

### 3.1 Construction types

| Type | Cost ($ for ~10-ft straight run, mid-grade appliances) | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CMU block + stucco or stone veneer** | $18k–$35k | Permanent, fire-rated, weather-proof, customizable | Slow build, heavy footing needed, expensive |
| **Metal stud + Hardiebacker + veneer** | $12k–$22k | Faster, lighter footing, design flexibility | Less massive feel, harder to flash perfectly |
| **Prefab modular (Alfresco, Lynx, RTA Outdoor, BBQ Coach)** | $8k–$20k base + appliances | Fastest, factory-built, predictable | Limited customization, finish quality varies |
| **Cabinet — marine-grade polymer or stainless (NewAge, Werever)** | $10k–$25k | Quick, cabinet-style functionality | Less "built-in" feel, polymer fades |

### 3.2 Foundation

Outdoor kitchens are heavier than they look. A 10-ft CMU run with granite top = ~3,500 lb dead. Required:

- **Thickened slab edge**: 4" slab body, 12" wide × 12" deep thickened footing under wall locations. #4 rebar in footing (2 bars).
- **Independent strip footing** if attached to existing patio (don't tie new heavy wall to thin existing slab — it will crack).
- For grills/burners: noncombustible base (CMU or steel framing with Hardiebacker — no wood within 1 ft of grill body).

### 3.3 Gas rough-in (this is where permits get pulled)

**Sizing example — typical outdoor kitchen demand:**

| Appliance | BTU/hr |
|---|---|
| 36" gas grill | 60,000–90,000 |
| Side burner | 15,000–20,000 |
| Pizza oven | 35,000–60,000 |
| Outdoor refrigerator (gas — rare) | n/a (electric) |
| Outdoor fire pit | 65,000–100,000 |
| **Typical total** | **150,000–250,000 BTU/hr** |

That's a substantial gas load. House gas meter on a typical CA residence is sized 250–400 cfh (about 250k–400k BTU/hr) — adding 200k of outdoor demand can require **meter upgrade** with the gas utility. **Verify gas capacity before committing.** PG&E / SoCalGas timelines for meter upgrade: 4–12 weeks.

**Piping:**
- **Black iron pipe** (Sch 40) — traditional, code-friendly, robust. ¾" or 1" typical for outdoor kitchen branch.
- **CSST** (Corrugated Stainless Steel Tubing — Gastite, TracPipe) — faster, must be **bonded** (CSST bonding requirement, NEC 250.104 + mfr instructions: #6 copper from CSST to grounding electrode).
- Each appliance gets a dedicated **shutoff valve** in an accessible location.
- **Stub-up at appliance** with quick-disconnect on flex line for grill mobility.
- **Pressure test**: 10 psi for 15 min (residential standard) — required by inspector.

### 3.4 Electrical

- All outdoor receptacles **GFCI** (NEC 210.8(A)(3)). All outdoor outlets, including those under cover.
- **WR (Weather Resistant)** rated receptacles required outdoors.
- **Bubble-cover (in-use)** weatherproof box covers — required for any in-use receptacle in damp/wet location.
- **Dedicated circuits**:
  - Outdoor refrigerator: 20A dedicated
  - Lighting: 15A or 20A; consider switch-leg from house
  - Any 240V (icemaker, kegerator electric) on dedicated circuit
- **Lighting** in covered area: damp-location rated. Open to sky: wet-location rated. Fan over kitchen: outdoor-rated wet location.

### 3.5 Plumbing

- **Outdoor sink** allowed in most jurisdictions:
  - Drain to sanitary sewer (preferred, often required)
  - Some jurisdictions allow to landscape/dry well if "non-greasy" use only — but if grill cleaning happens at sink, treat as kitchen drain (sanitary).
- Hot/cold supply: insulate cold against condensation; **freeze protection** — in inland CA where 28°F nights occur, install ball valve inside garage to shut off and drain outdoor line in winter. (Most coastal CA doesn't need this.)
- **Backflow**: hose bibb / outdoor sink must have backflow prevention (vacuum breaker on hose bibb, integrated atmospheric vacuum breaker on outdoor sink supply).

### 3.6 Ventilation

For **covered outdoor kitchen** (under solid-roof patio cover, pergola w/ solid roof, etc.):
- **Vent hood required** above grill: UL 762 listed for outdoor use, stainless construction.
- Min CFM: 100 CFM per 10,000 BTU; so a 90k BTU grill needs 900 CFM hood (round up to 1,200 typical).
- Min hood width = grill width + 6" each side; hood depth = grill depth; hood height 30"–36" above grill grates.
- Ducting: **straight up through roof** is best; horizontal runs require makeup air consideration.
- Clearance to combustibles: per mfr (typ. 6"–8" from sides of hood to combustible wall).

For **open-air outdoor kitchen** (no roof above): no hood required, but provide clearances to any overhead structure (min 3 ft from grill top to any combustible).

### 3.7 Countertops

| Material | Outdoor performance | $/sf | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Concrete (cast in place or precast)** | Excellent, seasonal sealing | $80–$200 | Custom shape, polishable, can crack |
| **Granite** | Excellent | $70–$150 | UV-stable, durable, seal annually |
| **Quartzite** | Excellent (natural stone) | $100–$200 | Confirm true quartzite (not quartz — see below) |
| **Quartz (engineered, resin-bound)** | **POOR — yellows in UV, manufacturer voids warranty outdoors** | n/a outdoor | Many clients want it; **do not use outdoor** |
| **Porcelain tile (large format slab, 12mm+)** | Excellent | $60–$120 | Dekton, Neolith — UV stable; thin so support critical |
| **Soapstone** | Good | $80–$140 | Patinas, naturally non-porous |
| **Tile (small format)** | OK with proper grout, frequent regrout | $30–$70 | Many grout lines, mildew risk |

### 3.8 Fire pits and outdoor fireplaces — California AQMD restrictions

**Wood-burning fire features**: heavy restrictions in California's two largest air districts:

- **South Coast AQMD (LA, Orange, Riverside, San Bernardino counties)** — Rule 445: outdoor wood-burning fire features in residential settings allowed, but **no-burn days** prohibit any use (Check Before You Burn). New permanent wood-burning fireplaces in many cities require natural-gas-only OR EPA Phase II certified.
- **Bay Area AQMD** — Reg 6 Rule 3: 25–30 winter no-burn days/year. New wood-burning hearths in new construction **banned** (gas-only required) since 2008.
- **Sacramento Metro AQMD**: similar restrictions.

**Gas fire features** (the practical choice for new construction in most CA):
- **Gas line sized 65k–150k BTU** typical.
- **Manual shutoff** within 6 ft, key-valve at fire feature.
- **Ignition**: standing pilot, electronic (battery), or fully automated (24V with remote).
- **Lava rock / fire glass / ceramic logs**: media must be rated for outdoor use; max fill depth varies.
- **Burner**: stainless or brass; sized to media tray.
- **Air mixer**: required for natural gas to prevent soot.

**Setbacks from combustibles:**
- Fire pit body to combustible: 10 ft typical (mfr spec)
- Fire pit body to any overhead: 10 ft from flame, no overhang
- Fire pit to property line: per local zoning (often 5 ft min)

**WUI / VHFHSZ**: AB 38 / Zone 0 requires no combustible vegetation within 5 ft of structure — a wood-burning fire pit within that zone is generally prohibited.

| Trigger | Permits required |
|---|---|
| Gas appliance added | **Plumbing/Gas permit** |
| Electrical circuit added | **Electrical permit** |
| Plumbing (sink) added | **Plumbing permit** |
| Structure attached to dwelling | **Building permit** |
| Fully prefab island, only plug-in, no gas | Usually exempt (verify locally) |

Combo permits common — your local will likely issue one "MEP" permit covering all three.

## 4. Pergolas, Patio Covers, and Shade Structures

### 4.1 Types and permit thresholds

| Type | Description | Permit? |
|---|---|---|
| **Open lattice freestanding** under 120 sf, under 12 ft tall | Open 2x or 1x lattice top, no solid roof | Often exempt (CRC R105.2 §10) |
| **Open lattice attached** | Attached to dwelling | **Permit always** (lateral) |
| **Solid roof attached patio cover** | Insulated panel, sloped to drain | **Permit always** |
| **Solid roof freestanding patio cover** | Pavilion-style | Permit (varies); over 120 sf almost always |
| **Aluma-Wood / aluminum prefab patio cover (Alumawood, Duralum)** | ICC-ES listed system | Permit; engineering is in the ESR report |
| **Louvered pergola (Equinox, Azenco, StruXure, Renson)** | Motorized adjustable aluminum louvers | Permit; ICC-ES report from mfr |
| **Pergola covered with cloth / shade sail** | Tensioned fabric | Permit usually exempt for ≤200 sf; over that, structural |
| **Solid roof patio cover with skylight / electrical / heat** | Becomes "habitable adjacent space" | Permit, possibly Title 24 implications |

### 4.2 Structural design

Loads in CA (CRC):
- Roof live: 20 psf for occupiable patio cover (or 12 psf if open lattice not designed for occupancy)
- Wind: governed by Vult per ASCE 7 → most coastal CA = 110 mph; some inland = 95 mph; Bay area gusty zones up to 115 mph
- Seismic: not usually controlling for low pergolas, but post anchorage details still required

**Typical sizing — wood, freestanding pergola, 12 ft x 16 ft:**

| Member | Size | Spacing |
|---|---|---|
| Posts | 6×6 DF-L #1 | 4 corners + 1 mid if span > 12 ft |
| Beam (header) | 6×12 DF-L #1, or 4×12 doubled | Spans 12–14 ft post-to-post |
| Rafters | 2×8 or 2×10 | 24" o.c., spanning beam-to-beam |
| Lattice top | 2×2 on edge or 1×2 flat | 12"–18" o.c. |

For **attached** patio cover: ledger detail and lateral the same as deck (§1.4 above). The cover must not become a moment frame on the house unless engineered.

**Post anchorage:**
- Embedded post in concrete: 12" Ø × 36" deep min footing, anchor bolt + Simpson ABU66 (6×6) or PB (post base) with 1" standoff.
- For tall posts (over 10 ft) or wide spans: spread footing 24×24×12 with engineered hardware.

**Prefab aluminum patio cover** (Alumawood, Duralum, Four Seasons):
- Comes with **ICC-ES report** with span tables, load tables, anchorage requirements.
- Plan submittal = site plan + manufacturer cut sheet + ESR number.
- Installation 1–3 days typical.
- Post-mount details: surface-mount post base anchored to concrete deck with epoxy or expansion anchors per ESR.

### 4.3 Louvered pergola systems (motorized)

The premium product (Equinox, StruXure, Renson, Azenco). 2026 cost: **$130–$200 per square foot installed** for the structure alone, plus electrical and any integrations.

**GC scope-management points:**
- **Footing**: structure weighs ~12–18 psf — typical post-mount on 12" × 36" pier acceptable; verify mfr-specific.
- **Electrical**: 120V to controller (low-amp), low-voltage to motors. Pre-plan a switch leg and 120V outlet near the structure for the controller box.
- **Drainage**: louver pergolas drain through hollow posts into a buried pipe — coordinate sleeve and tie-in to drain system **before** concrete pour. If you miss this, you're chasing it later.
- **Integrations**: most have rain sensor (closes automatically), wind sensor (opens to vent), home automation API (Lutron, Control4, Crestron). Smart-home sub should be looped in.
- **Sealing at louver pivots**: not perfect — water can drip during heavy rain even when "closed." Set client expectations.

### 4.4 Attached cover — flashing

This is just like the deck ledger problem — except now you're adding a roof that sheds water onto a wall:

```
Existing roof shingles cut back
   ↓
   ┌─── Step flashing or counter-flashing under existing roof
   │
   │   ┌── New patio cover panel ledger / beam
   │   │
   ↓   ↓
─────────── existing wall
     │ │ ← WRB lapped over ledger flashing
     │ │
     └─┴── ledger bolted to wall framing through WRB
```

- WRB integration: cut WRB above ledger location, install ledger, install metal Z-flashing or proprietary flashing kit, re-lap WRB **down** over flashing.
- If attaching below existing roof: counter-flash up under siding, kickout at lower edge.
- **Never** depend on caulk-only at attached cover ledger.

### 4.5 Electrical in pergola / patio cover

Damp vs. wet location is determined by water exposure:

| Location | Rating |
|---|---|
| Under solid roof, no water blown in | **Damp** location |
| Open lattice or open pergola | **Wet** location |
| Edge of solid cover (within 3 ft of open edge) | **Wet** location |

**Ceiling fans**: must be rated "**outdoor**" — most explicitly state "wet" or "damp." Standard interior fans **will rust**, and the warranty voids.

**Recessed lighting**: shallow LED disks rated for damp/wet, in IC-rated housings if insulation contacts.

**String lights / festoon**: use **outdoor-rated** wire and connections; receptacles outdoors must be GFCI even when feeding string lights through a switch.

### 5.2 Permits and zoning

**Permit (CRC R105.2 + local)**: fences **over 7 ft** require permit. Pool barriers (any height) need to comply with §115920 (see §2.5).

**Setback/height typical CA zoning** (verify locally):
- Front yard: 3'-6" max height in front setback (some allow 4 ft solid + open above to 6 ft)
- Side / rear: 6 ft max without permit; 7 ft+ requires permit + structural
- Corner lot: front-setback rules apply to both street frontages
- Sight triangle at intersections: max 30"–36" height within 25 ft of driveway/corner

**Property line**: **zero setback typical** but verify — some HOAs and a few cities require 6" setback from PL to allow maintenance access. **Recommend a survey** before installing along disputed lines — a wrongly-placed fence is a real-property dispute.

### 5.3 Wood fence construction details

```
                ┌── cap board (2x6) — protects post tops + top rail
                │
   ──────────────────────── top rail (2x4)
   │                      │
   │  pickets (1x6 dog-eared
   │  or square-top), 6" wide,
   │  butted or with ~⅛" gap
   │                      │
   ──────────────────────── mid rail (2x4)
   │                      │
   │                      │
   ──────────────────────── bottom rail (2x4, kicker board optional 1x6)
   ║                      ║
   ║ 4x4 PT post          ║
   ═══════════ grade ═════════
   ║                      ║
   ║ Concrete collar,     ║
   ║ 10" Ø, 24"–30" deep  ║
   ║ (1/3 post length)    ║
   ║                      ║
   ╚══════════════════════╝
```

- **Posts**: 4x4 PT (.40 retention min, .60 better for ground contact) for fence ≤ 6 ft. 6x6 for 7-8 ft fence or windy sites.
- **Embedment**: 1/3 of total post length above grade, minimum 24". A 6 ft fence with 8 ft post = 32" buried.
- **Concrete collar**: 10" diameter min, full depth of hole. Crown top of concrete to shed water away from post.
- **Post base alternatives**: Postmaster steel posts (Master Halco), Simpson FPBM strong-tie post bases on slab.
- **Rails**: 3 rails on a 6 ft fence (top, mid, bottom). Rail bracket (Simpson 2x4 fence hangers) or toe-screwed.
- **Pickets**: 1x6 redwood/cedar/PT; corrosion-resistant 1¼" ring-shank nails or 1⅝" SS deck screws. **Two fasteners per rail per picket.**
- **Cap**: 2x6 across post tops sheds water and protects end-grain — adds significantly to fence life.

**Post longevity**: rot starts at the **soil line, not at the bottom of the hole**. Best practice: pour concrete with a crown above grade, allow water to shed away. Avoid filling the hole only with dirt and gravel — moisture wicks into post.

### 5.4 Gates

**Pedestrian gates:**
- Sag prevention: diagonal brace (compression brace from hinge-side bottom to latch-side top) or cable + turnbuckle (tension diagonal).
- Hinges: 3 heavy-duty strap or barrel hinges for a 4 ft wide gate.
- Latch: thumb latch or gravity drop latch standard; pool gates need self-closing self-latching per §2.5.

**Vehicle / driveway gates:**

| Type | Cost (gate + operator installed) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Slide gate (V-track or cantilever) | $8k–$20k | Best for narrow lots; cantilever avoids ground track |
| Swing gate, single | $5k–$15k | Simple; needs swing clearance |
| Swing gate, dual | $8k–$22k | Symmetric; both leaves powered |
| Bi-fold | $12k–$30k | Compact swing arc; complex |

**UL 325 (2018+) — gate operator safety standards. Critical for any motorized gate.**
- **Class I/II/III/IV** classification: residential = Class I or II, commercial = III/IV.
- **Entrapment protection**: every motorized gate must have **two independent means** of entrapment protection from each of two categories:
  - **Inherent** (built-in motor sensing — operator detects resistance)
  - **External** (photo eye / photo beam, edge sensors)
- **Photo eyes**: typically required at both ends of travel + along travel path.
- **Edge sensors**: pneumatic or electric edges on leading edges of gate.
- **Manual release**: required for power-failure egress.
- **Warning signs**: must be posted on both sides of gate.
- **Knox box / Click2Enter / fire dept access**: required by most CA fire marshals — coordinate with fire department on access protocol.

**Electric service to gate operator**: 120V dedicated circuit, GFCI breaker. Most operators are 24V DC, fed by 120V transformer in the operator box. Battery backup recommended (almost standard).

**Loop detectors / exit wand** (free-exit driveway): in-ground inductive loop saw-cut in concrete (or pre-installed in conduit before pour), exit-wand wireless transmitter, or keypad / intercom. Coordinate with low-voltage sub.

### 5.5 Retaining wall with fence on top — engineering trigger

A retaining wall over **3 ft** of retained height (most jurisdictions; some allow up to 4 ft) requires engineering. **Add a fence on top** and you've effectively raised the design height:

- The fence adds wind load to the wall above (lever arm).
- The retained height for engineering purposes is still measured to the top of retained soil, but post embedment, surcharge calculations, and overall wall design must account for the fence.
- Often the cleanest solution: **separate** the fence footings from the wall (offset 12"+ horizontally), so the fence isn't a load on the wall.
- Or: design the wall with post pockets / cores that anchor the fence into the wall.

## 6. Hardscape — Concrete Flatwork, Pavers, and Retaining Walls

### 6.1 Concrete flatwork

**Subgrade prep:**
- Strip topsoil to firm subgrade
- Compact subgrade to 90% relative compaction (95% for driveway / heavy use)
- Base rock: 4"–6" compacted Class 2 aggregate base (CAB), wet and compacted in lifts

**Reinforcement:**

| Slab use | Thickness | Reinforcement |
|---|---|---|
| Patio walk (light foot only) | 4" | 6×6 W2.9 WWM or #3 bar at 18" o.c. |
| Patio under furniture / heavy use | 4" | #3 at 16" o.c. each way |
| Driveway (auto) | 4"–5" | #3 at 16" o.c. each way, or #4 at 24" o.c. |
| Driveway (heavy truck / RV) | 5"–6" | #4 at 18" o.c. each way |
| Equipment pad | 4" | 6×6 W2.9 WWM, edge thickened |

**Concrete mix:**
- 3,000 psi (Class A) for flatwork
- 4,000 psi for driveway or freeze-thaw exposure (rare in CA, but mountain areas)
- Air-entrainment 5–7% for freeze-thaw exposure
- Slump 4"–5" for flatwork (pump and finish-friendly)
- Fiber mesh (synthetic) often added in lieu of or in addition to mesh — does not replace structural rebar

**Control joints (the most-violated detail):**
- **Spacing**: 24× slab thickness max, in feet. 4" slab = 8 ft, 5" slab = 10 ft.
- Aspect ratio: panels should be roughly square (max 1.5:1 length:width).
- **Depth**: 1/4 of slab thickness (1" deep for 4" slab) — saw-cut within 12 hours of pour, or tool-jointed in fresh concrete.
- Pop-outs at all reentrant corners (around posts, at columns).

**Isolation joints (expansion joints):**
- Slab to building foundation: full-depth ½" foam, sealed.
- Slab to coping at pool: ½" foam, sealed with polyurethane sealant.
- Slab to slab when poured separately: foam.

**Finish options:**

| Finish | Description | Cost premium | Slip rating |
|---|---|---|---|
| Broom (medium) | Light texture across slab | baseline | Good (CoF >0.6 wet) |
| Smooth trowel | Glassy | baseline | Poor wet (slick) |
| Exposed aggregate | Top wash exposes rocks | +$3–$8/sf | Excellent |
| Salt finish | Rock salt pressed, washed | +$2–$4/sf | Good |
| Stamped | Patterned with mat tools | +$8–$18/sf | Variable — sealer dependent |
| Acid stained | Color etched in | +$4–$10/sf | Good if textured |
| Polished (rare outdoor) | Mechanically polished | +$15–$30/sf | Poor wet |

**Sealing**: cure-and-seal or penetrating siloxane sealer 30 days after pour. Re-seal every 2–5 years.

### 6.2 Stamped concrete

The premium concrete finish — looks like stone/slate/cobble for 50–70% of the cost.

**Process:**
1. Pour standard 4"–5" slab with integral color (more permanent) or no integral.
2. Apply color hardener (broadcast onto surface).
3. Apply release agent (powder or liquid, contrasting color).
4. Stamp with mats while still plastic (a 2-3 hour window — labor intensive).
5. Wash off excess release after 24+ hours.
6. Cure and seal — multiple coats over time.

**Quality factors:**
- **Contractor experience matters enormously.** Stamped concrete is a craft skill. Get 3+ references with site visits to 2-year-old work.
- **Pattern fade** is real: top 1/16" of the surface holds the color and detail. Heavy wear (under furniture, pool deck) wears it through.
- **Resealing every 2–3 years** is required to maintain appearance.
- **Slip resistance**: anti-slip additives (Crystalfine, ShakeAway) must be added to the sealer — without them stamped is slippery.

### 6.3 Permeable / pervious paving

Increasingly required by California stormwater rules — **CalGreen / SUSMP / MS4 permit** requires reducing impervious surface for sites adding > 500 sf hardscape in many jurisdictions.

| Option | Description | Maintenance |
|---|---|---|
| **Pervious concrete** | No-fines concrete, water flows through | Annual vacuum to clear voids |
| **Permeable pavers** (Belgard Aqua-Roc, etc.) | Solid pavers with permeable joints | Annual blow-out / occasional sweep with new joint stone |
| **Open-joint pavers + gravel joints** | Standard pavers, wider gravel joints | Sweep joint material |
| **Gravel / decomposed granite** | Loose stone | Re-grade and refresh top dressing |
| **Grass pavers / turf block** | Concrete grid with grass | Mow, irrigate |

For **MS4 stormwater compliance** in most California jurisdictions, redevelopment over 2,500–10,000 sf triggers SUSMP / WQMP requirements: roughly **50% of new/replaced impervious area** must be treated by BMP (bio-retention, permeable surface, infiltration trench, etc.). **Verify with city stormwater engineer at design phase.**

### 6.4 Concrete pavers (interlocking pavers)

**Standard installation (ICPI guidelines):**

```
Pavers (60–80mm thick for vehicular)
  ────────────────  paver
  ░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░  1" bedding sand (ASTM C33 coarse sand)
  ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓  6" compacted Class 2 base (1.5" minus)
  ░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░  geotextile separator (over soft subgrade)
  ////////////////  compacted subgrade
```

- **Base**: 4" for pedestrian, 6" for vehicular, 8" for heavy/truck
- **Bedding**: exactly 1" of clean coarse sand, screeded smooth, **not compacted**
- **Pavers**: 60mm (2⅜") for patios, 80mm (3⅛") for driveways/heavy use
- **Joint width**: ⅛"–⅜" with **polymeric sand** (Techniseal, Gator, Alliance) brushed and water-activated
- **Edge restraint**: PVC or steel edging (Snap Edge, Permaloc) spiked every 12"; or concrete bond beam at perimeter (toe-cast 4" wide × slab depth)
- **Compaction**: plate compactor over completed pavers with rubber pad — twice

**Pattern matters for traffic:**
- **Herringbone (45°)** — best interlock, required for driveway / vehicular
- **Running bond / stretcher** — pedestrian only; locks poorly under torque
- **Basketweave** — pedestrian only
- **Random ashlar** — pedestrian; some movement possible

**Edge restraint is the #1 failure point** — paver fields without restraint spread, joints widen, weeds invade.

### 6.5 Natural stone (flagstone / slate / bluestone)

| Installation | Use case | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry-set on sand base** | Casual patios, walkways | Same as paver base; weed-prone joints; movement OK |
| **Mortar bed on concrete slab** | Permanent patios, around pool, formal | 1½" mud-bed mortar; cement joints; needs slab subbase |
| **Pedestal-set (rooftop / deck)** | Roof decks, terraces | Adjustable plastic pedestals; level paver tops |

**Sealing**: most natural stones benefit from penetrating sealer (impregnator). Bluestone and slate often spec'd un-sealed for natural appearance — accept staining. Limestone and travertine **must** be sealed (acid-sensitive).

**Slip resistance**: most natural stones are good wet, but polished slate / honed stone outdoors will be slippery. **Thermal / flamed / cleft finishes** are the safe choice for wet areas (around pools).

### 6.6 Retaining walls (landscape scale)

**Decision matrix:**

| Retained height | Common solution | Engineering? |
|---|---|---|
| Under 3 ft | Stack-stone, boulder, dry-stack block, segmental block | No engineering typically |
| 3–4 ft | Segmental block w/ geogrid, low CMU wall | Often required (CRC R404) |
| 4 ft + | Engineered: CMU/concrete w/ reinforcing, MSE w/ geogrid, soldier pile | **Always engineered** |
| Any retained height + surcharge (vehicle, structure, slope above) | Engineer regardless | **Always engineered** |

**Segmental block walls (Allan Block, Versa-Lok, Keystone, Anchor Diamond):**
- Block + geogrid into backfill creates a Mechanically Stabilized Earth (MSE) wall.
- Manufacturer provides design tables for height, geogrid spacing, embedment length, drainage.
- Typical construction:
  - Excavate to footing depth (~6" below grade for first course)
  - 6" leveling base of compacted ¾" minus
  - First course leveled to ⅛" tolerance — this sets the whole wall
  - Each subsequent course set back per block design (1° batter typical)
  - Geogrid every 2–3 courses, extending 60–80% of wall height back into compacted backfill
  - **Drainage**: 12" gravel drain zone behind wall, perforated pipe at base, daylight or tie to storm
  - Cap units glued to top course

**CMU retaining walls:**
- Footing per engineer (typ 16"–24" wide × 12" deep with #4 bars top and bottom)
- Vertical wall steel: #4 at 16"–24" o.c., grouted cells
- Horizontal steel: #4 at 16" o.c. or in bond beams
- Drainage: same as segmental — gravel + pipe + weep holes every 6 ft (3" Ø) or behind-wall French drain
- Waterproof back side if any habitable space behind

**Timber walls (railroad tie / pressure-treated timber):**
- **Generally not recommended** for permanent walls.
- Even .60 retention PT will rot in 15–25 years.
- Dead-men into hillside required for stability.
- Code-acceptable but consider as temporary or for low (≤24") landscape edging only.

**Drainage behind any retaining wall — the rule that prevents 90% of failures:**

```
       ↓ surface drainage away from wall top
                     │
       ════════════════════ wall cap
   ┃                  ┃ │  filter fabric wrap (prevent fines)
   ┃                  ┃ │
   ┃   retained       ┃ ▓ 12" gravel drain zone
   ┃   soil           ┃ ▓
   ┃                  ┃ ▓
   ┃                  ┃ ░░░ 4" perf pipe at base, daylighting or tied to storm
   ═══════════════════════════════ footing
```

Without this drainage, **hydrostatic pressure builds** behind the wall, especially in CA winter rain events. A 6 ft wall with saturated backfill can see lateral pressures double or triple the design value. Failure modes: wall rotation (top tips out), wall sliding (entire wall translates outward), wall bulging (mid-height bowing). All preventable with proper drainage.

## 7. Landscaping Coordination (the GC's role with the C-27)

### 7.1 Hardscape vs. softscape sequencing

**Golden rule: all hardscape complete before softscape installation.** Heavy equipment, mixers, deliveries, and saw-cutting will damage finished planting.

Sequence:
1. Rough grading and drainage (under-slab plumbing, drains)
2. Hardscape (patios, walks, walls, pools, decks)
3. Irrigation rough-in and sleeves (under all hardscape)
4. Hardscape complete
5. Fine grading (1"–2" topsoil add, soil prep)
6. Irrigation main installation, valve manifolds, heads
7. Planting (trees first, then shrubs, then groundcover, then sod / hydroseed)
8. Mulch top dressing
9. Landscape lighting commissioning

### 7.2 Irrigation sleeving (the #1 missed item)

**Before any concrete or paver installation**, lay **PVC sleeves** under every hardscape feature you'll need to cross with irrigation or low-voltage later:

- **Sleeve size**: 2× the diameter of the pipe being sleeved. ¾" main = 1½" sleeve; 1" main = 2" sleeve.
- **Routing**: every isolated lawn or planter zone needs a sleeve from the irrigation valve zone to it.
- **Quantity rule of thumb**: 2 spare sleeves (1.5" or 2") at every driveway crossing, 1 at every walk crossing. Cap and flag with surveyor pin + GPS coordinate.
- **Low voltage**: separate dedicated 1" sleeve for landscape lighting wire.
- **Gas to fire features / outdoor kitchen**: sleeve dedicated to gas line, even if line is installed later.

**Missed sleeves = saw-cutting concrete or pulling pavers later.** A 30-second decision in pre-pour saves $3,000 in retrofit.

### 7.3 MWELO — Model Water Efficient Landscape Ordinance

California's **MWELO (CCR Title 23, §§490–495)** applies to new and rehabilitated landscape projects:

| Project | MWELO compliance |
|---|---|
| New construction, project landscape ≥ 500 sf | **Full MWELO compliance** + landscape documentation package |
| Existing landscape rehabilitation ≥ 2,500 sf | **Full MWELO compliance** |
| Under 500 sf or DIY homeowner | Generally exempt (verify local) |

**Compliance requires:**
- **Maximum Applied Water Allowance (MAWA)** calculation (limits irrigation budget based on local ET₀)
- **Estimated Total Water Use (ETWU)** must be ≤ MAWA
- **Hydrozones** (high/medium/low water plants grouped by zone)
- **Smart irrigation controller** (WaterSense-labeled, weather-based)
- **Pressure regulation** at valves (recommended 30–40 psi at heads)
- **Master shutoff + flow sensor** with controller integration
- **Mulch 3" deep min** in all planting areas (except turf and from species that don't tolerate)
- **Soil management report** for plantings (organic amendment, infiltration)
- **Audit + certification** at completion by certified landscape auditor

**Documentation submittal**: typically at building permit application; some jurisdictions accept at final inspection. **C-27 prepares**, GC reviews and signs as project lead.

### 7.4 Drainage coordination

**Grading away from structure (CRC R401.3 / R403.1.7.3)**:
- **6" fall in first 10 ft** from foundation in all directions.
- If 10 ft can't be achieved, swale or area drain to redirect.
- Hard-piped roof drains tied into site drainage system.

**Area drains and catch basins:**
- 9"–12" Ø atrium grates or NDS area drains at every low point
- 4" SDR-35 pipe at 1% min slope (½" per 4 ft) to daylight or to dry well
- French drain (perforated pipe in gravel) along hillside cuts or behind retaining walls
- Sump pump where gravity drainage impossible (basement walkouts)
- **Roof leaders**: never discharge at foundation — extend 5–10 ft away or tie to storm

**Permeable design (CalGreen / MS4):** for SUSMP-applicable sites, on-site infiltration BMPs may be required:
- **Bio-retention basins** (vegetated swale with engineered soil)
- **Infiltration trenches** (gravel-filled with overflow)
- **Permeable pavers + open-graded base** as treatment volume
- **Dry wells** in CA's clay-heavy soils often **don't work** — verify infiltration testing first

### 7.5 Planting near structures

| Element | Setback recommendation |
|---|---|
| Trees, mature canopy 30 ft+ | 20–25 ft from house, 10 ft from pool, 10 ft from septic |
| Trees, mature canopy 15–20 ft | 10–15 ft from house |
| Shrubs (mature 6 ft+) | 3–5 ft from foundation |
| Groundcover / perennials | 12"–18" from foundation |
| Bamboo (running types) | **NEVER plant runners without root barrier — use clumping bamboo or steel root barrier 30" deep** |
| Aggressive roots (willows, ficus, eucalyptus) | Avoid within 30 ft of sewer/water; never near pool |

**Foundation waterproofing** can be punctured by tree roots within 10 years if planted too close. **No woody-stem plants within 18" of basement walls.**

### 7.6 Landscape lighting

| Type | Voltage | Permit | Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-voltage** | 12V or 24V via 120V transformer | None | Path lights, accent, uplights — most landscape |
| **Line voltage** | 120V | Electrical permit | Larger fixtures, longer runs (some commercial) |
| **Solar** | None | None | Path / accent, light output limited |

**Low-voltage design:**
- **Transformer**: 300W, 600W, 900W, 1500W common; located in garage / equipment area / weather enclosure
- **Wire run**: 12 AWG or 10 AWG common; voltage drop becomes issue past 100 ft on 12 AWG
- **Fixtures**: brass, copper, or stainless for longevity; aluminum (powder-coated) for budget; LED retrofits standard
- **Photo cell + timer** or smart controller (Halo, Kichler, FX Luminaire)
- **Sleeving**: 1" PVC under all hardscape (see §7.2)

### 8.5 Warranty handoff to owner

**Provide owner at closeout (binder or digital):**

2. **Deck**: composite manufacturer warranty (registration online by owner — remind them), framing connector hardware spec, sealer schedule (if PT/cedar)
3. **Outdoor kitchen**: appliance warranties, gas pressure test report, electrical permit closure, ventilation maintenance instructions
4. **Pergola/Cover**: ICC-ES report (for prefab), mfr warranty, motor / louver warranty (for louvered systems), seasonal maintenance instructions
5. **Fence**: stain/sealer schedule (wood), hardware spec, gate operator manuals + UL 325 compliance certificate

### 8.6 The owner conversation — setting expectations on outdoor scope

Three conversations the GC should have at preconstruction:

1. **"Pool is its own project."** Pools require a C-53. We coordinate. The owner often has a direct contract with the C-53 (separates liability and lets them choose the pool company). Decisions on tile, coping, plaster color, equipment level, and barrier solution affect timeline and cost — schedule decisions early.

2. **"WUI changes everything in fire zones."** Zone 0 (5 ft from house, no combustibles), Chapter 7A decking material, vegetation management — these aren't optional. If the house is in VHFHSZ, the deck/pergola/fence materials are constrained.

3. **"Final grade is sacred."** The biggest source of post-completion drainage failures is fine grade getting wrong at landscaping. Coordinate **with the landscape sub** about exact finish grade elevations at every transition — patio to lawn, walk to planter, foundation to soil.

## 9. Appendix — Quick-Reference Numbers and Formulas

### 9.1 Deck calculations

- Joist tributary area for beam sizing: ½ × joist span × beam span = sf supported
- Footing tributary: ½ × beam span × ½ × joist span (each footing supports a quarter of two beam spans)
- Cantilever: max ¼ backspan or table limit
- Ledger bolt count: linear feet of ledger ÷ spacing per Table R507.9.1.3(1)

### 9.2 Pool quick numbers

- Volume (gallons): L × W × avg depth × 7.5
- Turnover: gallons ÷ pump flow (gpm) ÷ 60 = hours
- Heater sizing: 65k BTU per 10k gallons for slow heat; 200k BTU for spa quick heat
- Filter sizing: 1 sf cartridge per 1,500 gallons capacity (minimum)
- Suction velocity max: 6 ft/s (2" PVC ≈ 73 gpm max)

### 9.3 Concrete quick numbers

- CY needed: L × W × thickness (in inches) ÷ 12 ÷ 27 (then add 5–10% waste)
- 4" slab control joint spacing: 8–10 ft max
- 4" slab control joint depth: 1"
- 3,000 psi mix: ~6 sack mix typical

### 9.4 Footing depths (CA, no frost)

- Decks and light structures: 12" below grade, undisturbed soil
- Pools / spas: per geotech (typically 18"–36" + bond beam keying)
- Retaining walls: 1/8 of wall height below grade (3 ft wall = 6" embed; verify engineer)

### 9.5 Common pool barrier numerics

- Fence height min: 60" (5 ft)
- Vertical opening max: 4"
- Gap at ground max: 2"
- Latch height: 60" (verify local; some 54")
- Distance pool wall to fence min: typically 4 ft (anti-climb)
