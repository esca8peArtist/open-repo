# Residential Renovation and Remodeling for the General Contractor

> **Reader assumption**: You've built new houses. You can read plans, manage subs, run a schedule, and close out a project. This guide is about everything that breaks when the building already exists.

The fundamental shift: in new construction, **you control the building**. In renovation, **the building controls you**. Existing conditions, occupants, prior trades' mistakes, hidden hazards, and code triggers dictate sequencing, scope, and price in ways that have no analogue in new work. The GC who treats a remodel as "a smaller new build" will lose money on the first three jobs and possibly their license on the fourth.

---

## 1. Why Renovation is Different from New Construction

### 1.1 The Discovery Problem

In new construction, the unknowns are external (weather, supply chain, labor). In renovation, the unknowns are **internal to the building** and you cannot see them until you open walls.

| What you expected | What you find | Cost impact |
|---|---|---|
| 2x6 stud wall | 2x4 wall with sistered 2x2 furring | Re-frame or accept thinner cavity |
| Copper supply lines | Galvanized iron with 1/4" effective ID from scale | Whole-house re-pipe |
| Standard 16" o.c. framing | 24" o.c. balloon-framed with fire-stops missing | Engineering review, fire-blocking retrofit |
| 200A panel | 100A Federal Pacific Stab-Lok (uninsurable) | Service upgrade $4–8k |
| Plywood subfloor | 1x6 diagonal sheathing with gaps | Overlay or replace |
| Solid bearing wall | Notched studs for old DWV, no header at 6' opening | Structural repair |
| Vented attic | Knob-and-tube wiring buried in blown cellulose | Re-wire + insulation remediation |
| Slab on grade | Crawlspace converted to slab with no vapor barrier | Moisture mitigation |

The discovery problem is not a risk to manage — it is **the central reality** of renovation work. Every estimate, every schedule, every conversation with the owner must assume discoveries will happen.

### 1.2 Existing Structure Constraints

You cannot:
- Move a bearing element without engineering and (usually) a foundation point load solution.
- Ignore an out-of-plumb wall — new cabinets, tile, doors all reveal it.
- Pretend the existing floor is flat — countertops, appliances, and tile layouts all assume flatness within tolerance.
- Add load to a foundation without verifying capacity (second-story additions almost always require foundation work).
- Cut a joist or beam without a structural engineer's sign-off, even for plumbing or HVAC.

**Tolerance reality**: existing residential framing is typically out of plumb 1/2" to 1" over 8 feet, floors out of level 1/4" to 1" across a room. Modern cabinet and tile work assumes 1/8" in 10' tolerances. **The gap between existing tolerance and finish tolerance is where your labor budget dies.**

### 1.3 Code Upgrade Triggers

The act of touching an existing system often triggers compliance for the **whole system**, not just the portion you're changing. This is the single biggest hidden-cost trap in renovation estimating.

| Trigger | What gets upgraded | California reference |
|---|---|---|
| Reroof >50% of roof area | Cool roof, radiant barrier in some climate zones | Title 24 Part 6 §150.2(b) |
| HVAC change-out | Duct sealing, refrigerant, sometimes whole-house ventilation | Title 24 §150.2(b) |
| Adding conditioned floor area | Whole-addition Title 24 compliance, often QII inspection | Title 24 §150.2(a) |
| Panel upgrade or service change | AFCI on bedroom circuits, GFCI per current code, tamper-resistant receptacles | CEC Art. 210, 406 |
| Opening >50% of wall area | Insulation to current code in opened cavities | Title 24 §150.2(b) |
| Window replacement | U-factor and SHGC compliance per climate zone | Title 24 §150.2(b) |
| Water heater replacement | Strapping, expansion tank if check valve, drip pan, T&P discharge | CPC 504, 507 |
| Adding bathroom or kitchen | Mechanical ventilation per ASHRAE 62.2 | Title 24 §150.0(o) |
| Bedroom remodel | EERO (egress) compliance for window or door | CRC R310 |

The phrase "while we're in there" cuts both ways. Owners want to add scope. **Code requires you to add scope.** Both must be priced.

### 1.4 Occupied or Adjacent Spaces

A new build site is yours. A renovation site is **shared with people who live in it, or with adjacent units, neighbors, or tenants**. This drives:

- **Work hours**: typically 7 AM – 7 PM weekdays in most California jurisdictions; weekends restricted or prohibited; check local noise ordinance.
- **Dust control**: not a courtesy — a contractual obligation and a health hazard.
- **Sequencing**: noisy/dusty work must be batched and scheduled to minimize disruption.
- **Bathroom/kitchen continuity**: never leave occupants without a bathroom; address kitchen alternatives in pre-construction.
- **Pets, children, elderly**: presence affects access, hazardous material protocols (esp. lead RRP).
- **Neighbor relations**: in attached or zero-lot-line homes, your job affects their walls, their parking, their sleep.

### 1.5 Lead and Asbestos — The Pre-1978 Reality

**Lead**: any home built before 1978 is presumed to contain lead-based paint until proven otherwise. Federal RRP rule (EPA 40 CFR 745) is mandatory for any disturbance >6 sq ft interior or >20 sq ft exterior of painted surfaces in target housing.

**Asbestos**: any home built before approximately 1980 may contain asbestos in popcorn ceilings, vinyl tile, mastics, pipe insulation, duct wrap, roofing felt, joint compound, plaster, and exterior siding (transite, asbestos-cement shingles). California requires a survey before disturbance.

These are not optional. They are **federal and state legal obligations** with personal criminal exposure for the GC who ignores them.

### 1.6 Demolition Revealing Conditions

What demo reveals — and the order in which it reveals it — drives the entire renovation:

- **Mold**: behind tubs, in bath wet walls, under kitchen sinks, in crawlspaces. Triggers IICRC S520 remediation protocol.
- **Pest damage**: termite galleries in sill plates, carpenter ant damage in damp wood, rodent nesting in insulation with contaminated material.
- **Improper prior work**: cut joists, notched studs, removed fire-blocking, splices in walls (open or in non-metallic boxes), unvented gas appliances, illegal flex duct runs.
- **Structural compromises**: rot at sill plates, post bases sitting in standing water, sagging beams, undersized headers, missing collar ties, unbraced rafters.
- **Unpermitted prior renovations**: additions without permits, finished basements without egress, illegal ADUs.

**The Demolition Stop-Work Protocol**:
1. Stop work immediately at the affected area.
2. Document with photos before disturbance.
3. Call the appropriate consultant (structural engineer, industrial hygienist, pest inspector).
4. Notify the owner in writing — email with photos and a hold-pending-decision note.
5. Do not resume work until the path forward is documented and signed.

### 1.7 Scope Creep as a Business Reality

Remodel scopes grow. Average growth: 15–30% of original contract value. This is not a failure of estimating — it is the nature of the work. Plan for it:

- **Build it into pricing**: contingency line items, allowances, and explicit unit-price schedules for likely additions.
- **Capture it in change orders**: every CO is documented, priced, signed before work proceeds.
- **Track CO velocity**: a remodel with no COs is a remodel that's being eaten by the GC.
- **Train the owner**: in your pre-construction meeting, explain that COs are normal, expected, and how you'll manage them.

## 2. Pre-Construction Assessment

The pre-bid walk-through is the single highest-leverage activity in renovation work. Twenty minutes of careful observation saves five figures in change orders.

### 2.1 Walk-Through Protocol (Top to Bottom, Outside to Inside)

Bring: flashlight, level (4 ft and 8 ft), marble or ball, moisture meter, outlet tester, voltage tester, awl or screwdriver (probe wood), small mirror, phone with camera, notepad. Optional but valuable: borescope, thermal camera, laser distance meter.

#### Structural Checklist

| Observation | What it suggests | Investigation needed |
|---|---|---|
| Floors sagging toward center of room | Undersized joists, removed bearing wall, settled pier | Crawlspace inspection, possible engineer |
| Floors sagging away from exterior wall | Sill plate rot, foundation settlement | Sill probe, foundation inspection |
| Doors that don't latch, diagonal cracks above doors | Foundation movement or framing deflection | Crack monitoring, engineer |
| Out-of-plumb walls (>1" in 8') | Settlement, original construction, or framing deflection | Determine if active or static |
| Notched joists (especially mid-span) for plumbing/HVAC | Reduced capacity, possibly already overloaded | Engineer for repair |
| Cut beams (header for openings without proper support) | Improper prior work, unpermitted | Engineer required |
| Undersized headers (2x4 over 6' opening) | Original construction or unpermitted opening | Replace with proper header |
| Missing collar ties or rafter ties | Ridge sag, wall spread | Engineer, retrofit ties |
| Cracking in slab radiating from corners | Settlement | Soils/structural review |
| Bowed basement walls | Lateral earth pressure | Engineer, possible carbon fiber straps or piers |

#### Moisture Checklist

| Observation | What it suggests | Investigation |
|---|---|---|
| Brown rings on ceiling | Active or past roof/plumbing leak | Locate source, moisture meter |
| Efflorescence (white powder) on masonry | Water moving through masonry | Drainage, waterproofing |
| Soft drywall (probe with awl) | Active or recent moisture | Find source, scope of damage |
| Musty smell, especially basement/crawl | Mold or chronic moisture | Air sampling, source identification |
| Wood rot at sill plate, deck ledger, window sills | Long-term water exposure | Probe, scope, structural review |
| Peeling paint exterior | Vapor drive from interior or exterior water | Diagnose direction |
| Standing water in crawlspace | Drainage failure or groundwater | Drainage solution required |
| White or gray growth at baseboards | Mold (likely) | Test, S520 remediation |

#### Electrical Checklist

| Observation | Problem | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Federal Pacific Stab-Lok panel | Breakers fail to trip; fire hazard; uninsurable in many cases | Service upgrade in scope |
| Zinsco / Sylvania panel | Bus bar overheating, breaker failure | Service upgrade in scope |
| Pushmatic / Bulldog panel | Obsolete, parts unavailable, often degraded | Service upgrade in scope |
| Aluminum branch wiring (pre-1972, typically 1965–1973) | Oxidation, loose connections, fire risk | COPALUM or AlumiConn at every connection, or rewire |
| Two-prong outlets throughout | Ungrounded system; cannot install grounded receptacles without GFCI or grounding | GFCI strategy or rewire |
| Knob-and-tube wiring | Not damaged by age, but cannot be buried in insulation; insurance issues | Re-wire affected areas |
| Open splices in attic/crawlspace | Code violation, fire risk | Repair into junction boxes |
| Mixed grounded/ungrounded receptacles | Inconsistent grounding | Trace, map, fix |
| Double-tapped breakers | Overloaded circuit, code violation | Repair |
| Panel inside clothes closet or bathroom | Code violation (current code) | Relocate on upgrade |
| Service entrance conductors frayed/melted | Service-side problem, utility coordination | Utility coordination |

#### Plumbing Checklist

| Observation | Problem | Expected action |
|---|---|---|
| Galvanized iron supply | Scale build-up, reduced flow, leak risk; typical 40–70 year life | Plan for re-pipe |
| Polybutylene (gray plastic, 1978–1995) | Failure-prone, often subject of class actions | Re-pipe |
| Lead service line | Health hazard | Coordinate with water utility for replacement |
| Cast iron DWV with visible rust and pitting | End of life (typical 50–75 years), root intrusion risk | Plan for replacement |
| Mixed materials with dielectric failures | Galvanic corrosion at fittings | Replace at junctions |
| Low water pressure (<40 psi) | PRV failure, supply line restriction, utility issue | Pressure test, diagnose |
| High water pressure (>80 psi) | PRV needed (code), fixture/appliance damage | Install PRV |
| Water heater >10 years old | End of life approaching | Plan for replacement, possible code upgrades (strap, expansion tank, drip pan) |
| Water softener with brine line | Sodium discharge questions, drainage requirements | Disclose, evaluate |
| Slow drains throughout | Main line root intrusion or belly | Camera sewer line |
| S-traps under sinks | Code violation (siphoning) | Replace with P-trap and venting |
| No vacuum breaker on hose bibs | Code violation (current) | Add |

#### HVAC Checklist

| Observation | Problem | Action |
|---|---|---|
| System >15 years old | Approaching end of life, low efficiency | Plan replacement, plan ducts and Title 24 |
| White or gray fibrous wrap on ducts | Possible asbestos (Zonolite or asbestos-cement insulation) | Test before disturbing |
| Vermiculite insulation in attic | Likely Zonolite — presumed asbestos-contaminated | Test, possible abatement |
| Flex duct kinks, disconnections, crushed runs | Air flow failure | Repair or replace |
| Return air through wall stud bays (panned returns) | Code issue, leakage | Hard-duct the returns |
| No combustion air for gas appliances in closet | Code violation, safety | Add combustion air |
| Furnace in unconditioned space (attic, crawl) without proper insulation | Energy code issue | Address per Title 24 |
| Mismatched evaporator and condenser | Sub-optimal performance, warranty issue | Match on replacement |

#### Foundation Checklist

| Observation | Likely cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Vertical cracks <1/8" | Shrinkage (typical) | Monitor |
| Stair-step cracks in CMU | Settlement | Engineer |
| Horizontal cracks in foundation wall | Lateral pressure (serious) | Engineer urgent |
| Diagonal cracks at corners of openings | Settlement | Engineer |
| Patched cracks (epoxy or hydraulic cement) | Prior repair — may indicate ongoing issue | Investigate history |
| Efflorescence at base of foundation wall | Water intrusion | Drainage |
| Rusty bolts at sill | Long-term moisture | Probe sill |
| Concrete spalling exposing rebar | Carbonation, moisture, deicing salts | Engineer for repair |
| Foundation under-sized for what's above | Common in additions and converted spaces | Engineer |
| No anchor bolts visible | Pre-code construction | Seismic retrofit opportunity |
| Cripple wall without bracing | Earthquake vulnerability | Retrofit recommended |

### 2.2 Destructive Investigation

For high-uncertainty projects, **propose a paid destructive investigation before fixed-price bid**. Typical scope:

- 2–4 exploratory openings in suspect locations (above suspended ceilings, at wall corners, behind kick plates, in closets).
- Open cavities, photograph, measure framing, identify wiring, probe wood, sample for hazmat.
- Re-close openings to a basic state (poly, primer, basic patching — not finished).
- Deliverable: written report with photos and recommendations.
- Fee: $1,500–$5,000 typical, credited against contract if you get the job (sometimes).

How to sell it: "I can give you a number now based on assumptions, with a 25% contingency. Or I can spend a day opening four areas and give you a number with 10% contingency. The difference between those two numbers, for a $200k remodel, is $30,000."

### 2.3 Building Contingency Into a Remodel Bid

Three layers of protection:

1. **Visible contingency line**: 10–20% explicit line item, owner-acknowledged. "Owner Contingency Reserve — for unforeseen conditions; unused balance returned to owner."
2. **Allowance line items**: for known-unknowns (finishes not yet selected, hazmat abatement quantity).
3. **Hidden margin in unit prices**: for line items where the GC bears performance risk.

**Critical**: don't hide all contingency. Owners need to **see** the contingency line, understand why it exists, and be educated that it may or may not be used. Hidden contingency that gets spent becomes "going over budget" in the owner's mind.

## 3. Hazardous Materials — Legal Obligations

This section is the one most likely to end a contractor's career through ignorance. **Read it twice.**

### 3.1 Lead Paint — EPA RRP Rule (40 CFR 745, Subpart E)

**Applies to**: target housing (pre-1978 dwellings) and child-occupied facilities, when renovation disturbs **more than 6 sq ft of interior painted surface per room** or **more than 20 sq ft of exterior painted surface** or window replacements (any size).

**Required**:
- EPA-certified Renovator on site (8-hour training course, certificate valid 5 years).
- Certified Firm (firm registration with EPA, $300, valid 5 years).
- Pre-renovation lead pamphlet ("Renovate Right") delivered and signed by owner before work begins.
- Lead-safe work practices throughout.
- Records retained for 3 years.

**Lead-safe work practices**:
- Containment: 6-mil poly on floor extending 6 ft from work area, walls in some cases, doorways sealed with poly.
- Prohibited practices: open-flame burning, high-temperature heat gun (>1100°F), power sanding without HEPA shroud, abrasive blasting, dry scraping >1 sq ft.
- Mist surfaces before disturbance (wet methods).
- HEPA vacuum during and after.
- Waste: bag in 6-mil poly, double-bag, label, dispose per state/local rules (in California, often as construction debris if testing supports it, but document).
- Cleaning verification: white-glove wipe test at conclusion of work.

**Disclosure**: separate from RRP — the Title X disclosure rule (24 CFR 35) requires sellers and landlords of pre-1978 housing to disclose known lead, but **this is not the GC's obligation** unless you're also acting as a property manager.

**Testing**:
- **XRF (X-ray fluorescence)**: definitive, non-destructive, requires licensed inspector with calibrated device. Cost: $400–800 typical.
- **Paint chip sampling**: lab analysis, destructive, also definitive.
- **EPA-recognized test kits (LeadCheck, D-Lead)**: only valid for *negative* results — a positive may be confirmed by lab, but kit-positive surfaces must be treated as lead.
- **Presumption**: you may simply presume lead is present and follow lead-safe practices, avoiding testing cost entirely. Often the cheapest path on small jobs.

**California OSHA Lead Standard — Title 8 §1532.1**:
- Applies to *employee exposure*, not the homeowner exposure (RRP covers that).
- Initial determination: assess if any employee could be exposed at or above the Action Level (30 µg/m³ TWA).
- Construction trigger tasks (manual demolition of painted surfaces, manual sanding, abrasive blasting, etc.) presume exposure above PEL unless monitoring shows otherwise.
- Blood lead monitoring required when exposed >Action Level for >30 days/year. Medical removal at 50 µg/dL (more stringent than federal).
- Training, respiratory protection, change rooms, hand-washing, no-eating-in-work-area, etc.
- **Personal liability for the GC who does not implement this for crews on lead jobs.**

### 3.2 Asbestos — California Requirements

**Survey requirement**: California Health & Safety Code §25915 (NESHAP-equivalent), Cal/OSHA §1529, and local air districts require an asbestos survey by a certified inspector **before any demolition or renovation** in pre-1980 structures.

**Materials commonly containing asbestos in residential** (presume asbestos until tested):

| Material | Years installed | Typical % asbestos |
|---|---|---|
| Popcorn / acoustic ceiling spray | Pre-1977 (banned 1977 but stock used into 1980s) | 1–10% chrysotile |
| 9x9 vinyl floor tile | Pre-1980 | 5–20% chrysotile |
| 12x12 vinyl floor tile | Pre-1985 | Variable, often present |
| Vinyl tile mastic (black, "cutback") | Pre-1985 | Often asbestos |
| Sheet vinyl backing | Pre-1985 | Often asbestos in backing |
| Joint compound, textured drywall finishes | Pre-1980 | 1–5% chrysotile |
| Plaster (especially fireproof) | Pre-1980 | Variable |
| Pipe insulation (white, chalky wrap) | Pre-1980 | Often 50%+ amosite/chrysotile |
| Duct insulation (corrugated paper wrap or rigid block) | Pre-1980 | Variable |
| Boiler/furnace cement | Pre-1980 | Often asbestos |
| Zonolite / vermiculite attic insulation | All years if from Libby, MT (most US supply) | Tremolite contamination |
| Asbestos cement (transite) siding, pipe, panels | Pre-1985 | 12–50% |
| Roofing felt and asphalt shingles | Pre-1985 | Variable |
| Window glazing compound | Pre-1980 | Variable |
| HVAC gaskets, electrical wire insulation | Pre-1985 | Variable |

**Survey deliverable**: written report identifying each suspect material, sample locations, lab results, quantities, locations, recommended response (leave in place, encapsulate, abate).

**Abatement**:
- **Licensed asbestos contractor**: California DOSH registration required for any contractor working with asbestos containing material (ACM) — Cal/OSHA §341.9 registration plus contractor's state license (asbestos C-22 or qualifying classification).
- **De minimis exception**: less than 100 sq ft / 100 lin ft per job — homeowner may abate own residence without licensure. **GCs do not have this exception** when working for an owner.
- **Notification**: Cal/OSHA notification 24 hours before work involving >100 sq ft / 100 lin ft.
- **Air district notification**: BAAQMD Regulation 11 Rule 2 (Bay Area), SCAQMD Rule 1403 (SoCal), other district equivalents require advance notification of demolition (any amount of asbestos, or any demo of pre-1980 structures regardless), plus pre-removal survey copies, plus waste tracking.
- **Air monitoring**: required during abatement (background, work area, clearance). Independent third-party for clearance.
- **Waste disposal**: Class I landfill (in California, specific facilities accept asbestos), manifest tracking, double-bagged in 6-mil poly with asbestos warning labels.
- **Worker protection**: full Tyvek, fit-tested respirator (P100 minimum, often PAPR), decon chamber with shower, no eating/drinking, training, medical surveillance.

**The GC's path**: do NOT attempt asbestos work without licensure. **Sub it out** to a C-22 abatement contractor. Build the survey-then-abate sequence into your schedule and budget. On a typical pre-1978 kitchen remodel, expect $2,500–8,000 for popcorn ceiling and floor tile abatement; on whole-house jobs, $15,000–50,000+ is common.

### 3.3 Mold

Not federally regulated like asbestos and lead. But:

- **Disclosure**: in California, sellers must disclose known mold (Civil Code §1102.6); landlords have Health & Safety Code obligations. As GC, your disclosure obligation is contractual — if you find significant mold, **inform owner in writing immediately**.
- **Remediation protocol**: industry standard is IICRC S520 — establish containment, HEPA filtration, source remediation (removal of porous materials, cleaning of semi-porous), drying, verification.
- **Conditions categorization (per S520)**:
  - Condition 1: normal fungal ecology.
  - Condition 2: settled spores/fragments from elevated indoor.
  - Condition 3: actual mold growth.
- **Threshold**: >10 sq ft of Condition 3 typically triggers professional remediation per most state guidelines (no federal threshold). California Department of Public Health does not have a numeric mold standard but maintains advisory.
- **GC liability**: significant. Mold lawsuits are common in California. If you encounter mold and continue without remediation, you may be liable for occupant health claims for years. **Stop work, document, recommend a mold consultant or IEP (Indoor Environmental Professional).**
- **Insurance**: most GL policies exclude mold. Specific endorsement or separate policy required for any GC doing remediation.

## 4. Demolition Scope

### 4.1 Selective Demolition vs. Gut Renovation

| Selective demo | Gut renovation |
|---|---|
| Some finishes/walls retained | All finishes removed to studs |
| Higher protection cost | Lower protection cost (mostly empty) |
| Sequencing more complex | Sequencing simpler |
| Surprise risk lower | Surprise risk higher (more exposed) |
| Better for occupied homes | Usually requires vacancy |
| Tile-over-tile, paint-over-paint possible | Substrates renewed |

**Decision driver**: cost of protection vs. cost of replacement, plus owner tolerance.

### 4.2 Structural Stop-Work Triggers During Demo

Stop work and call engineer when you find:
- Notched, cut, or drilled structural members beyond CRC/CBC limits.
- Sagging, rotted, or termite-damaged structural elements.
- Missing or absent headers, hangers, ties, or connections.
- Improper prior alteration of structural members (e.g., a "soffit" that's actually a header that was cut).
- Bearing walls where you expected non-bearing (or vice versa).
- Foundation cracking patterns suggesting active movement.

**Document protocol**: photograph before, during, and after exposure; sketch on plan; written email to owner; quoted engineer cost added to scope.

### 4.3 Utility Disconnection Sequence

| Step | Utility | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Electric | First. Call utility for service-side disconnect for service changes; for branch circuit demo, isolate at panel and lockout-tagout. Verify dead before cutting. |
| 2 | Gas | Pressure-test isolation. Cap with full-port valve, not just a plug. Coordinate with utility for meter pull on full demo. |
| 3 | Water | Shut off at main, drain system, disconnect. Cap properly — soldered or threaded, not just a stuck-on cap. |
| 4 | Sewer | Plug to prevent gas migration during work, especially when removing fixtures for extended period. |
| 5 | HVAC | Disconnect and remove or protect ducts; cap supply/return openings during demo. |

**Permits**: most jurisdictions require permits for service-side disconnects and reconnects. Schedule with utility well in advance — PG&E may take 4–8 weeks for service disconnect/reconnect.

### 4.4 Demo Debris Management — CalGreen 50% Diversion

CalGreen (CCR Title 24 Part 11) §4.408 requires at least **65%** diversion of non-hazardous construction and demolition waste (the standard was raised from 50%). Many local jurisdictions require higher (San Francisco: 65%+; some require 75%).

- Choose licensed C&D recycling facility with weight receipts.
- Track concrete, metal, wood, drywall, asphalt separately if possible.
- Keep manifests/receipts; submit to building department at close-out.
- Hazardous materials (asbestos, lead-paint debris, treated lumber, paint, solvents) are **not** counted toward diversion and must be disposed at appropriate facilities.

### 4.5 Soft Demolition (Abatement First) vs. Hard Demolition

**Sequence** for any pre-1980 home:
1. Hazmat survey complete, results reviewed.
2. Abatement (asbestos, lead, mold) by licensed sub.
3. Clearance testing/sign-off.
4. Hard demolition by GC's crew.

Skipping abatement = exposure liability + criminal penalty + ruined career. **Do not let owners pressure you to "just rip it out — it's fine."**

### 4.6 Temporary Shoring During Demo

When removing bearing walls, beams, or floor systems:
- Engineer specifies shore design (post spacing, capacity, footing on competent surface).
- Adjustable steel shores or built-up wood posts; double headers above, sole plates below.
- Distribute load to capable bearing surface — slab on grade is usually fine; subflooring is not unless engineer specifies.
- Maintain shoring until permanent structure is installed and capable (not just framed — fully connected).
- Inspect shoring daily; verify still tight (wood shrinks).
- Mark shore with date installed and load it carries.

### 4.7 Dust Control in Occupied or Adjacent Spaces

Dust is the #1 complaint on occupied renovations. Aggressive dust control is non-negotiable.

**Containment**:
- Zip-walls or 6-mil poly on 1x3 or 2x4 framing, ceiling to floor, full-perimeter taped.
- Zip-door for access.
- Floor protection: ram board, brown paper, plastic, or hardboard, taped seams, extending past the work zone.
- HVAC: shut off the air handler that serves the work zone, cover returns and supplies with poly, or use temporary filter media.
- For high-dust work (drywall sanding, masonry cutting): negative air pressure inside containment using HEPA air scrubber exhausted outside or through filtration.

**Site practices**:
- HEPA vacuum, not shop vac (shop vacs blow fines into the air).
- Wet-sweep with sweeping compound, not dry sweep.
- Damp-wipe surfaces at end of day.
- Walk-off mats at entry to containment.
- Crew changes shoes or wears booties leaving the zone.

**Equipment recommendations**:
- HEPA air scrubber: 500–2000 CFM rated (BuildClean, AlorAir, Dri-Eaz).
- HEPA vacuum: Festool, Nilfisk, Mirka.
- Negative air machines with filtration ducted outside.

## 5. Code Compliance Triggers in Renovation

### 5.1 California Valuation-Based Triggers

The "alteration" definitions in CBC §3403 (commercial) and CRC §R102.7.1 (residential) differentiate ordinary repair from substantial alteration. Many local jurisdictions impose **valuation thresholds** for triggers — commonly 50% of building replacement cost triggers "substantial improvement" which may trigger full code compliance, including flood-plain elevations.

**Title 24 Part 6 (Energy Code)** distinguishes:
- **Additions**: must meet new-construction prescriptive or performance compliance for the addition.
- **Alterations**: trigger compliance for the altered components only (with exceptions).
- **Existing + addition combined**: when total is >700 sq ft addition or addition exceeds certain ratios, may require whole-building approach.

### 5.2 Electrical Triggers

| Renovation action | Triggered |
|---|---|
| Panel upgrade | Bring panel and all visible/accessible circuits to current code; AFCI on bedroom + most living areas; tamper-resistant receptacles throughout when replaced; GFCI for kitchen, bath, garage, exterior, basement, laundry, dishwasher |
| Service upgrade (e.g., 100A→200A) | New grounding electrode system per current code; new meter base; possible utility coordination; verify clearances |
| Kitchen remodel | GFCI all kitchen receptacles; AFCI; dedicated 20A small appliance circuits (min 2); dedicated circuits for dishwasher, disposal, microwave; range circuit appropriately sized |
| Bath remodel | GFCI; dedicated 20A bathroom branch circuit; fan ducted exterior; lighting controls |
| Adding circuits | AFCI on new circuits in living spaces; GFCI per location |
| Replacing receptacles | If grounded receptacle on ungrounded circuit, must be GFCI-protected with "No Equipment Ground" label |

### 5.3 Accessibility Triggers (CBC Chapter 11A and 11B)

**Residential (single-family)**: not subject to ADA. **However**:
- Multi-family (3+ units) is subject to CBC 11A and FHA (Fair Housing Act).
- Mixed-use buildings: commercial portions trigger CBC 11B/ADA.
- ADUs and JADUs: depending on size and configuration, may have accessibility implications, though most are exempt.
- **Rental units**: FHA accessibility provisions apply to ground-floor units in buildings with 4+ units built after 1991.

**Commercial alteration** (relevant if your GC also does light commercial work):
- 20% of project cost must be applied to path of travel (entrance, parking, restroom, telephone, drinking fountain) to achieve accessibility, up to disproportionate cost cap.
- This is a hard requirement and a major source of unpriced work in commercial alteration.

### 5.4 Energy (Title 24 Part 6) Triggers in Residential

| Action | Energy compliance triggered |
|---|---|
| Re-roof >50% area | Cool roof in climate zones 1, 3–16; radiant barrier in some |
| Re-roof with rigid above-deck insulation opportunity | Insulation upgrade in some scenarios |
| Window replacement | U-factor and SHGC per climate zone |
| HVAC change-out | Duct testing/sealing, refrigerant charge verification, possible whole-house fan or ventilation upgrade |
| Adding conditioned space (addition) | New-construction compliance for the addition |
| Wall cavity insulation when wall is opened >50% of single wall area | Cavity insulation to current code |
| Water heater replacement | Strap, drip pan, expansion tank where applicable; tankless transition triggers gas line sizing review |
| EV charger installation | Load calculation, dedicated circuit, sometimes service upgrade |

Document with **CF1R / CF2R / CF3R forms** through CHEERS or CalCERTS, signed by HERS Rater where required.

### 5.5 Non-Conforming Structures

A "non-conforming" structure was legal when built but does not conform to current zoning/code. The general rule: **you may maintain and repair a non-conforming structure, but expansion or substantial alteration may extinguish the non-conforming right.**

Examples:
- Setback non-conformity: existing house 3 ft from side property line (current code: 5 ft). Repair: fine. Addition extending the non-conforming wall: usually not permitted.
- Density non-conformity: 4-unit building in current 2-unit zone. Repair: fine. Major reconstruction (>50% damage): may require conformance.
- Height non-conformity: addition on top usually not permitted if existing already at or above current height limit.

**Always check zoning before bidding additions.** The owner expects you to know.

### 5.6 Unpermitted Prior Work

What you find when you open the wall:
- Subpanel without permit.
- Bedroom in basement without egress.
- ADU built without permits.
- Plumbing in addition without permit.
- Removed bearing wall without engineer.

**Your obligation**:
1. You **cannot ignore** it once found — if you complete work that incorporates or covers unpermitted construction, you take on liability.
2. **Notify owner in writing**. Provide options:
   - Permit the prior work through retroactive permit (some jurisdictions have an amnesty path; most require bringing to current code, which can be expensive).
   - Remove the unpermitted work.
   - Stop the project.
3. **Get a written direction from owner** before proceeding.

**The conversation**: "When we opened the wall in the basement, we found that the previous owner finished a bedroom down here without a permit. The window doesn't meet current egress requirements and the wiring isn't in conduit where it crosses the joist. I can't legally cover that work back up without it being permitted. Your options are: we can apply for a retroactive permit and bring it up to code, which will cost approximately $X for the egress window enlargement and $Y for the electrical work, plus permit fees. Or we can remove the basement bedroom and restore it to unfinished basement. Or we stop the entire project until we have a path forward. What's your preference? I'll need a written direction before I can continue."

## 6. Kitchen Remodels — Special Considerations

### 6.1 Full Gut vs. Cosmetic

| Cosmetic | Layout-preserving with new finishes | Full layout change |
|---|---|---|
| Paint, hardware, possibly counters | Cabinets in same location, new cabinets and counters | Walls moved, plumbing/electrical relocated |
| 2–4 weeks | 4–8 weeks | 8–16 weeks |
| Minimal subs | Counters, possibly plumber, electrician | All subs |
| Owner can stay | Owner can stay with constraint | Owner kitchen out of service entire job |
| Low surprise risk | Medium | High — opens up everything |

### 6.2 Critical Workflow

Approximate sequence (compress where possible):

1. Site protection (floors, adjacent rooms, HVAC).
2. Demo (full or selective).
3. Hazmat abatement if required (don't skip).
4. Structural changes (walls, beams, headers).
5. Rough plumbing (supply, drain, vent — relocate as needed).
6. Rough electrical (circuits, boxes, can lights, switches; verify code triggers).
7. HVAC modifications (relocate registers, add ventilation).
8. Insulation (if walls opened to required extent).
9. Drywall (hang, tape, texture).
10. Prime and first coat paint.
11. Flooring (if uniform across the room; alternatively after cabinets but with care).
12. **Cabinet installation** (must be installed level — shim as needed; floor must be ready).
13. Counter template (after cabinets installed and verified level).
14. Backsplash demo if not done; rough for any backsplash plumbing.
15. Counter fabrication (1–2 weeks for quartz/granite typically; check fabricator schedule).
16. Counter installation (single day usually, but allow drying for any seam/adhesive).
17. Plumbing trim (faucet, disposal, dishwasher hook-up).
18. Electrical trim (devices, switches, fixtures).
19. Backsplash tile.
20. Appliance installation.
21. Caulking, paint touch-up.
22. Final clean and walk.

### 6.3 Cabinet Coordination

| Cabinet type | Lead time | Customization |
|---|---|---|
| Stock (IKEA, big-box) | 1–3 weeks (in-stock) | Standard sizes only |
| Semi-custom (KraftMaid, Diamond, Shenandoah) | 4–8 weeks | Modify size and finish within parameters |
| Custom (local cabinet shop) | 8–16 weeks (or longer) | Anything |
| Full bespoke | 12–24 weeks | Anything |

**Order timing**: place order **before** demo on long-lead orders; place at demo start on stock. **Verify dimensions** after demo (when walls are exposed and measureable) before finalizing — many GCs have eaten cabinet re-orders because of a 1/2" wall thickness assumption.

**Receiving**:
- Inspect at delivery; refuse damage.
- Storage: owner's garage is not ideal (humidity); a climate-controlled space is best.
- In occupied homes, stage delivery to coincide with install — don't have cabinets sitting in the home for weeks.
- Verify count: doors, drawers, hardware, fillers, panels, crowns, toe kicks.

### 6.4 Countertop Templating

- **Template after** cabinets are installed, leveled, and verified. Do **not** template based on plan dimensions.
- Templater (fabricator's measurer) needs full access; clear all clutter.
- Lead time after template: typically 1–2 weeks for quartz/granite; longer for some natural stones or specialty.
- Schedule a sink and faucet (must be on site for templating to locate cutouts).
- Cooktop dimensions confirmed.
- Edge profile, seam locations, overhang dimensions, support brackets for unsupported overhangs >8–10".

### 6.5 Appliance Rough-In Specs

| Appliance | Electrical | Plumbing | Gas | Vent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gas range | 120V receptacle for ignition (typical 15A) | None | 1/2" gas line with shutoff valve in cabinet, flexible connector | Range hood |
| Electric range | 240V dedicated, typically 40A (8/3) | None | None | Range hood |
| Induction range | 240V dedicated, often 40–50A | None | None | Range hood |
| Cooktop | 240V dedicated or 120V for gas | None | 1/2" gas if gas | Hood/downdraft |
| Wall oven (single) | 240V dedicated, 30A (10/3) typical | None | None | None typically |
| Wall oven (double) | 240V dedicated, often 40–50A | None | None | None typically |
| Dishwasher | Dedicated 20A 120V (or hardwire per mfg) | 3/8" or 1/2" supply, hot only; drain to disposal port (with high loop or air gap per code) | None | None |
| Refrigerator | Dedicated 20A 120V (some 15A) | 1/4" water line for ice/water (consider shut-off valve in cabinet) | None | None |
| Microwave | Dedicated 20A 120V (over-range often shared with hood) | None | None | If OTR, vent through hood |
| Garbage disposal | 15A or 20A; switched | Drains to sink | None | None |

**Makeup air (California)**: when range hood capacity exceeds **400 CFM**, you must provide makeup air per CMC §504.3 and CEC §150.0(o). Makeup air system: dampered intake with interlock to hood operation, sized to match exhaust. Adds $1,500–4,000 to the job and is frequently missed.

**Combustion appliance interaction**: a high-CFM hood can backdraft a gas water heater or furnace in the same zone. Address through makeup air and/or sealed-combustion appliances.

## 7. Bathroom Remodels — Special Considerations

### 7.1 Wet-Area Waterproofing — Where Bathrooms Fail

Waterproofing failure is the #1 source of bathroom rebuilds, deck-below damage, and contractor liability. **Take it seriously, document it photographically, and follow the manufacturer's instructions to the letter.**

| System | Method | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional mud bed | Pre-slope mortar bed, PVC/CPE pan liner up walls 4" min, second mortar bed | Time-tested, labor-intensive, 2-day cure between courses; many fail at penetrations |
| Schluter-KERDI | Cement board or drywall substrate, KERDI sheet membrane bonded with thinset, KERDI-DRAIN | Bonded waterproof barrier; well-documented system; flood test before tile |
| Schluter KERDI-BOARD | Foam board substrate that is also waterproof | Lighter, faster; verify load capacity for stone |
| Wedi | Foam board substrate with sealed joints | Similar to KERDI-BOARD; full system warranty when used as system |
| Hydroban / RedGard liquid membrane | Roll-on or trowel-on liquid waterproofing over cement board | Owner-friendly cost; mil-thickness verification critical; flood test |
| Sheet membrane (Noble, Composeal) | Sheet membrane between substrate and tile | Various detailing requirements |

**Universal rules**:
- Substrate must be sound, flat, screwed (not nailed) to fully supported framing.
- **Slope the floor to drain at minimum 1/4" per foot.**
- Pre-slope under any pan liner (don't rely on later slope).
- Flood test floor pan: dam drain, fill with 2" water, leave for 24 hours, mark level, verify no loss. Photograph.
- Membrane up walls minimum 4" (often 6" or full-height in showers); around tubs, behind tile to splash height.
- Inside corners: detail with reinforcement strip or pre-molded corner.
- Penetrations (drain, mixing valve, niche): manufacturer-specified seals and gaskets.
- Niches: pre-fab or built-in-place, fully waterproofed inside the niche, sloped sill.

### 7.2 Tile Over Tile

| Acceptable when | Not acceptable |
|---|---|
| Existing tile sound, bonded, no hollow areas | Loose, hollow-sounding tile |
| Substrate flat (1/8" in 10') | Substrate >1/8" out of flat |
| Existing tile mechanically scored or chemically prepped | Glossy, unprepared surface |
| Weight load acceptable | Cumulative weight exceeds substrate capacity |
| Floor heights work (door clearance, transitions) | Door bottom would not clear |
| Wall thickness works | Plumbing fixtures would not extend through |

Better practice in most cases: remove existing tile. The labor saved by tiling over is often spent dealing with the additional thickness at fixtures, transitions, and trim.

### 7.3 Plumbing Change Triggers

- **Moving drain**: requires opening floor, possibly reconfiguring branch and vent, often re-piping vent if relocation exceeds developed length limits. Triggers floor patch and possibly waterproofing rebuild.
- **Adding fixture**: branch and vent sizing per CPC §703, §905. Calculate fixture units carefully.
- **Vent inadequacy**: many older homes have venting issues — S-traps, no air admittance valves where allowed, undersized stacks. Renovation is the chance to fix; if you leave them, you may own them.
- **Pressure-balance or thermostatic shower valve**: required for all new shower installations (CPC §417.5, anti-scald protection).
- **Hot water access**: where supply lines must be re-run, consider a hot-water recirculation pump if owner desires.

### 7.4 ADA-Accessible / Universal Design Bathroom

| Feature | Spec (per ADA / ANSI A117.1) |
|---|---|
| Door | 32" clear opening minimum (36" door typical) |
| Turning radius | 60" diameter or T-turn |
| Toilet | Centerline 16–18" from wall; seat 17–19" high |
| Grab bars | 33–36" above floor; 1.25–1.5" diameter; 1.5" clearance from wall; 250-lb load |
| Grab bar blocking | 2x10 horizontal blocking behind walls during framing — even if not installing now |
| Shower (roll-in) | 60"x30" minimum; threshold flush (max 1/2" beveled); folding seat optional |
| Shower (transfer) | 36"x36" with folding seat |
| Lavatory | Clear floor space 30"x48"; bottom of apron 27" above floor (knee clearance) |
| Mirror | Bottom edge ≤40" above floor |
| Switches and outlets | 15"–48" reach range; lever handles |

**Future-proofing**: even when owners don't want a fully accessible bathroom, install **plywood blocking 33–48" above floor** in shower and toilet walls. Costs $40–80 in materials. Saves $2,000+ in later remodel for grab bars.

### 7.5 Exhaust Fan and Ventilation

- **Minimum 50 CFM intermittent or 20 CFM continuous** (CMC §403.7 / ASHRAE 62.2).
- **Ducted to exterior** — never to attic or soffit (despite what you may have seen).
- **Rigid metal duct** preferred; flex acceptable in some jurisdictions if short and properly supported.
- **Insulated duct** in unconditioned space (to prevent condensation).
- **Backdraft damper** at exterior termination.
- **Controlled** by humidity sensor or timer (required in California per Title 24 §150.0).
- **Sound rating**: 1.5 sones or less for living spaces (a 4-sone fan in a small bathroom is loud and won't get used).

### 7.6 Bathroom Sequence

1. Demo (with hazmat as needed).
2. Rough plumbing.
3. Rough electrical.
4. Framing changes (blocking for grab bars, niches, vanities).
5. Insulation.
6. Cement board / waterproof substrate.
7. **Waterproofing membrane** (Schluter, etc.) — full system per manufacturer.
8. **Flood test** (24 hours, document).
9. Tile.
10. Grout (after appropriate cure).
11. Drywall and paint adjacent.
12. Vanity, toilet, accessories.
13. Glass enclosure (after templating to installed substrate).
14. Caulk and final.

## 8. Structural Changes in Renovation

### 8.1 Opening Bearing Walls

The most common high-impact renovation: removing a wall between kitchen and living room. Steps:

1. **Identify bearing**: walls perpendicular to joists, walls under load-bearing walls above, walls at edge of long spans. When in doubt, assume bearing.
2. **Engineer**: header size, post sizes at ends, load path to foundation. Calculations stamped and submitted with permit.
3. **Verify foundation**: posts must land on capable bearing — slab is usually OK, but verify thickness; framed floor requires post-down to a girder, post, or new pier.
4. **Temporary shoring**: install before any wall demo; engineer specifies. Both sides if floor system spans (typical) or one side if just removing a non-load-bearing connecting wall.
5. **Demolish wall**: studs, top plates carefully (preserve adjacent ceiling/wall connections if possible).
6. **Install header**: glulam, LVL, PSL, or built-up dimensional, per engineer.
7. **Install posts**: solid-sawn or engineered, with appropriate post bases and caps; load path continuous to foundation.
8. **Inspect**: rough framing inspection.
9. **Remove shoring** after inspection sign-off and full structural completion.

**Common errors**:
- Header bears on insufficient post or wall stud.
- Post lands on subfloor, not a girder or post below.
- Hardware not installed (hold-downs, post caps, hangers).
- Built-up beam not properly fastened (LVL or dim lumber sandwich must be nailed/screwed per spec).

### 8.2 Identifying Bearing Walls

| Indicator | Probably bearing |
|---|---|
| Wall runs perpendicular to joists/trusses above | Likely |
| Wall is centered on a beam or girder below | Yes |
| Wall is in line with a wall above | Yes |
| Wall has a post in the basement directly below | Yes |
| Wall is at the edge of a long floor span | Often (helps reduce span) |
| Wall runs parallel to joists, no wall above | Usually not |
| Wall is in the middle of a short joist span, nothing above | Usually not |
| Wall has plumbing/HVAC running through it | Bearing or not — could be either |

**Engineer determines**, not the GC's guess. Inspect from above (attic) and below (basement/crawl) before deciding.

### 8.3 Dormers and Bump-Outs

- **Triggers Title 24** for the new conditioned space.
- **Foundation**: cantilevered bump-outs <2 ft typically don't need new foundation; larger requires foundation extension.
- **Roof framing**: cricket, dormer cheeks, valley flashing — water management is critical.
- **Re-roof intersection**: tie-in flashing, integration with existing.
- **Structural**: ridge support, rafter ties, wall-to-floor connection, possibly LVL header at ridge.
- **HVAC**: extend system into new space, possibly upsize equipment.

### 8.4 Second-Floor Additions

The most engineering-intensive residential renovation. Always requires:

- **Foundation capacity verification**: existing footing dimensions, soil bearing capacity, new load calculation. Often requires foundation augmentation.
- **First-floor wall capacity**: existing walls were likely 2x4 at 16" o.c. — may need to be 2x6 or doubled to handle vertical load + lateral load from new second floor.
- **Lateral system**: new shear walls, possibly hold-downs added retrofit, anchor bolt upgrades.
- **Stairs**: code-compliant rise/run, headroom, landing dimensions; stair location and structural support.
- **Egress**: each new bedroom requires EERO (window or door meeting CRC R310 dimensions).
- **Title 24**: full Title 24 compliance for new conditioned area.
- **Utilities**: extend electrical (often service upgrade), plumbing (drain and vent stack capacity), HVAC (often new zoning or system).
- **Owner**: usually has to move out for much of the job.

### 8.5 Seismic Upgrade Opportunities

When walls are open, take advantage:
- **Cripple wall bracing**: plywood sheathing on interior face, nailed per ESR or current CRC R602.10.
- **Anchor bolts**: where missing, retrofit with epoxy or expansion anchors per engineer.
- **Hold-downs**: at shear wall ends, especially at re-entrant corners or new openings.
- **Sill plate ties**: where sill bolted but inadequate.
- **Continuous load path**: rafter-to-stud, stud-to-stud, stud-to-sill, sill-to-foundation. Hurricane ties, strap ties, etc.
- **Soft-story retrofit** (Bay Area): for residences over garages, often a separate program (e.g., SF SSRP) with prescriptive designs.

**Disclose to owner**: when you have an open wall and see a seismic deficiency, **inform them**. It's both ethical and a billable upgrade opportunity. Document either acceptance or refusal.

## 9. Phasing Work in Occupied Homes

### 9.1 Work Hours

Verify each jurisdiction. Common patterns:

| Jurisdiction | Weekday | Saturday | Sunday/holidays |
|---|---|---|---|
| Los Angeles (city) | 7 AM – 9 PM | 8 AM – 6 PM | Prohibited (most) |
| San Francisco | 7 AM – 8 PM | 9 AM – 8 PM | Prohibited |
| Berkeley | 8 AM – 8 PM | 9 AM – 8 PM | Prohibited |
| Oakland | 7 AM – 7 PM | 9 AM – 8 PM | 10 AM – 6 PM (some) |
| San Jose | 7 AM – 7 PM | 9 AM – 7 PM | Prohibited |
| San Diego | 7 AM – 7 PM | 7 AM – 7 PM | 10 AM – 4 PM in some zones |
| Unincorporated county | Varies; check |

HOA rules may be more restrictive than municipal.

### 9.2 Dust and Noise Sequencing

- Demo and noisy work: first hour of the workday (before owners need to be on calls, while they're still in routine).
- Cutting masonry/tile: outside if possible; with HEPA cutter and water if inside.
- Painting/finish: last in the day; ventilate overnight.
- Communicate the day's plan with owner each morning: "Today we'll be running the saw from 8 to 10, then doing quiet trim work after that."

### 9.3 Kitchen Out of Service

In pre-construction meeting, address:
- **Duration**: typical kitchen remodel: 6–12 weeks fully out.
- **Temporary kitchen options**:
  - Microwave + hot plate + small fridge in garage, basement, or dining room.
  - Sink: utility sink in basement/garage, or a counter-top sink with portable supply if available.
  - Refrigerator: pull existing fridge into adjacent room before demo.
- **Eating budget**: owners eat out and order in more — typical incremental cost $500–1500/month per couple. This is the owner's expense, but you should mention it.
- **Owner buy-in**: confirm owner is mentally prepared. Many remodels turn sour because owners didn't realistically estimate the disruption.

### 9.4 Bathroom Phasing

**Critical rule**: never leave an occupied home with zero functional bathrooms (toilet + sink minimum) for any overnight period.

For homes with multiple bathrooms:
- Phase the work: complete remodeled bathroom before starting next.
- Always have at least one functional bathroom for occupants.

For homes with single bathroom:
- Pre-construction: arrange portable toilet on site + handwashing station, OR arrange for owners to relocate (Airbnb, family, hotel), OR phase work intensively to minimize down-days.
- "Out of bathroom" days: minimize to fixture install days (1–2 days max), pre-arranged.

### 9.5 Staging and Material Storage

Owners' garages and yards become construction zones. Pre-construction agreement should address:
- Specific designated areas for material staging.
- Dumpster location, dumpster permit (if in street, most jurisdictions require), dumpster duration.
- Vehicle parking — crew vehicles, where?
- Portable toilet (recommended even on small jobs for crew of 3+).
- Site fence or security if extended exterior work.
- Lockable shed or container for tools if owners are uncomfortable with crew accessing through home.

### 9.6 Security

- **Keys**: track every key issued; collect at end of job; recommend re-keying after completion (offer it).
- **Lockbox**: better than circulating keys to subs; rotate code monthly.
- **Daily lock-up**: last person out is responsible, signs out, photographs locked condition.
- **Belongings**: pre-construction, owner removes valuables from work zones. Walk-through document existing condition of adjacent items.
- **Cameras**: many owners have Ring/Nest cameras. Disclose in writing that crew is aware and they record.
- **Crew background**: for occupied jobs, prefer crew known to you or to a trusted sub. Subcontractors who bring random day laborers into a family home are a liability.

## 10. Managing Owner Expectations on Remodels

### 10.1 The "While You're At It" Problem

Mid-job additions are inevitable. The right framework:

- **Welcome reasonable additions** (they're billable work).
- **Always document and price before performing** — never do an add on a verbal "go ahead."
- **Distinguish "while you're at it" (owner-initiated additions) from "discovered conditions" (necessary additions)** — they should be priced and presented differently.
- **Maintain CO log** visible to owner, ideally in a shared system, with running total.
- **Schedule impact**: every CO has time impact; quote both cost and schedule.

**Template language**: "Yes, we can add that. Let me get you a number by end of day tomorrow. If you'd like to proceed, we'll prepare a change order for your signature. The change will add approximately 3 days to the schedule, and we'll update the completion date accordingly."

### 10.2 Owner-Supplied Materials

Owners increasingly want to supply fixtures, lights, tile, hardware. Address in contract:

- **GC not responsible** for: material quality, fit, completeness, timing of delivery, warranty.
- **GC responsible** only for: installation per industry standard.
- **If material is missing/wrong/damaged**: owner is responsible to remedy; meanwhile, work proceeds elsewhere; if no work can proceed, owner pays standby/delay.
- **Receiving**: owner is responsible for receiving and storing on site, OR owner pays GC to receive and inspect (recommended at $50–150 per delivery).
- **Returns**: owner's responsibility entirely.

**Get this in writing.** The "I bought my own faucet, you install it, you fix the warranty" expectation is rampant and ruinous.

### 10.3 Decision Fatigue

A typical kitchen + 2 bath remodel requires owners to make 200–400 individual selections. Most owners under-estimate this.

- **Selection schedule**: deliver in pre-construction, organized by trade, with decision deadlines.
- **Decision deadlines tied to procurement timeline**: e.g., "cabinet finish color due by week 2 of design; tile selection due by start of demo; counter selection due by week 4 of construction."
- **Designer or design consultant**: for jobs over a certain scale, strongly recommend (or require) an interior designer or selection coordinator. Reduces decision fatigue and decision-driven delays.
- **Sample boards**: physical samples assembled together — countertop + cabinet + tile + paint — under the actual lighting of the room. Catches lighting issues before purchase.
- **Decision lockout**: after a decision is signed, changing it triggers a CO. Communicate this up front, not after the fact.

### 10.4 Living Through Construction

Owners on remodels are experiencing:
- Loss of privacy (strangers in their home daily).
- Sensory overload (noise, dust, smells, disruption).
- Schedule uncertainty (when will it end?).
- Financial pressure (cash out the door, plus parallel rent if relocated).
- Decision fatigue.
- Spousal stress (different visions, different tolerances).

**Communication cadence** to manage this:
- **Daily**: morning brief from your lead with the owner (or text), what's happening today.
- **Weekly**: written update — completed last week, planned this week, decisions needed, CO log status, schedule status.
- **Phase milestones**: walk-through after major milestones (rough complete, drywall complete, etc.) — review and confirm before moving forward.
- **Owner office**: a designated chair/table where the owner can talk to you, not in the middle of work.

### 10.5 Handling Discoveries — "It's Going to Cost More"

The most stressful conversation in the business. Framework:

1. **Lead with the facts**: "When we opened the wall behind the tub, we found extensive rot in the wall studs and subfloor. Here's what it looks like." (Show photos.)
2. **Explain why this is a problem**: "We can't install new tile over a compromised structure — it will fail again in 2 years."
3. **Present the option(s)**: "We need to replace the studs and a portion of the subfloor, and treat the framing for moisture. Here's the CO." (Hand them a written CO with detail.)
4. **Acknowledge the impact**: "I know this is hard news — we'd been hoping not to find this. The CO comes from the contingency we set aside for exactly this kind of thing."
5. **Provide timing**: "If you can sign this by tomorrow, we won't lose schedule. If you'd like to think about it, we'll continue working in the kitchen and circle back."

**Do not**:
- Apologize for the building being old (you didn't build it).
- Promise it was the only surprise (it usually isn't).
- Discount your work because the owner is upset (you didn't cause it).
- Continue working on the discovery without a signed CO.

### 10.6 Warranty on Remodels

Standard residential GC warranty is typically 1 year (workmanship). California statute of limitations: 4 years for patent defects, 10 years for latent defects (CCP §337.1 and §337.15).

**On a remodel, scope your warranty carefully**:

| Covered | Not covered |
|---|---|
| Workmanship on new work installed by GC | Existing conditions retained |
| Materials installed by GC if defective | Owner-supplied materials |
| New systems installed by GC | Pre-existing systems retained, even if connected to new |
| Code compliance of new work | Pre-existing code non-compliance not in scope |
| Warranties pass-through from manufacturers | Owner's misuse, deferred maintenance |

**Document existing conditions** at the start — photos and written description of what is retained — so warranty disputes don't try to drag pre-existing issues into your responsibility.

## 11. Estimating Renovation Work

### 11.1 Contingency

| Project type | Contingency |
|---|---|
| New construction (well-documented plans) | 3–5% |
| New construction (preliminary design) | 5–10% |
| Renovation — cosmetic only | 5–10% |
| Renovation — moderate (one or two rooms gut) | 10–15% |
| Renovation — whole house gut | 15–25% |
| Renovation — addition with major existing modifications | 15–25% |
| Historic renovation | 20–30%+ |
| Pre-1978 home with hazmat unknowns | 20–30%+ until survey |

**How to explain to the owner**: "On new construction we work with 3–5% contingency because we control all the variables. On your project, we're working with a 90-year-old building. Every wall I open could reveal something we couldn't see. I'm pricing this with a 15% contingency, which means the contract price reflects that buffer. If we don't use it, you get it back. If we use part of it, the cost reflects only the work performed. If we exceed it, we'll have a written CO before continuing. This is more honest than pricing with no contingency and hitting you with $30k of unexpected COs at month four."

### 11.2 Exploratory Work as a Pre-Bid Service

For projects over a certain scale or with substantial unknowns:
- Propose a paid exploratory phase: 1–3 days of careful destructive investigation.
- Deliverable: written assessment + photos + revised scope + tighter price.
- Fee: $1,500–$5,000 typical, sometimes credited toward contract.
- This is **not** free pre-construction; it's billable consulting.

### 11.3 Allowances

What goes in allowance vs. hard-priced:

| Allowance (owner picks later) | Hard price (defined now) |
|---|---|
| Tile (any) | Demo, framing, drywall, electrical, plumbing |
| Plumbing fixtures (faucet, toilet, etc.) | Pipes, valves, drains |
| Light fixtures | Wire, boxes, switches, can lights with standard trim |
| Cabinet hardware | Cabinets if model selected |
| Paint color (not paint quality grade) | Paint quantity and labor |
| Appliances | Appliance rough-in |
| Countertop material/edge | Counter labor (template, fab, install) |
| Door hardware | Doors, frames, hinges |

**Allowance pitfalls**:
- Owner picks something 3x the allowance: CO for the difference, plus markup. Document allowance reconciliation in writing.
- Allowance too low: causes mid-job CO pain. Set allowances at realistic mid-range, not lowball.
- Owner picks below allowance: refund the difference (don't pocket it — destroys trust if discovered).

### 11.4 Change Order Velocity

Remodel CO frequency: **typically 30–50% more change orders than comparable new construction**. Price your administrative time:

- **CO production**: 0.5–2 hours per CO (estimating, writing, presenting). At your labor rate, that's $75–300 per CO. Sometimes recoverable as line-item markup; sometimes built into general conditions.
- **Owner meeting time**: more frequent than new construction; budget weekly 1-hour meeting + emails.
- **Tracking system**: a real system (Procore, Buildertrend, Co-Construct, BuilderTREND, or a clean spreadsheet) — not text messages. Audit trail for everything.

### 11.5 T&M Portions and Hybrid Contracts

For high-uncertainty scopes, hybrid contracts protect both parties:

| Approach | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Fixed-price entire job** | Plans complete, existing conditions known, low uncertainty | 
| **Fixed-price with allowances** | Most remodel projects; covers defined scope with allowances for items not yet selected |
| **Fixed-price for defined scope + T&M for discovered conditions** | When pre-bid investigation reveals concerning indicators; T&M with not-to-exceed for the discovery line item |
| **Cost-plus with GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price)** | Very large or very uncertain projects; owner sees true cost; GC limited to fee |
| **Full T&M (time and materials)** | Emergency repairs, very small scope, or projects where owner accepts full risk; many states require detailed billing |

**Always**: written contract per state law (California: written required for >$500 home improvement contracts; specific disclosures and 3-day right of cancellation per Business & Professions Code §7159).

## Appendix A — Pre-Construction Owner Conversation Script

Before signing a contract on a renovation, walk the owner through:

1. **The discovery reality**: "This home is 70 years old. We've inspected what we can see and we've priced what's visible. There will almost certainly be things we discover when we open walls. We've set aside contingency for that. I want you to know now: there will be change orders. That's not failure — that's renovation."

2. **The decision pace**: "You'll be making decisions on tile, paint, fixtures, hardware, light fixtures, cabinet pulls — hundreds of decisions over the next four months. Here's the selection schedule. I need decisions by these dates or our schedule slips."

3. **The disruption reality**: "Your kitchen will be out of service for 8–10 weeks. Your laundry room will have construction debris staged in it. There will be a dumpster in your driveway. The dust will be aggressive but we'll do everything to control it. You will probably not love living here for the next 4 months."

4. **The communication plan**: "Every morning my lead will text you the day's plan. Every Friday I'll send you a written update with the schedule, CO log, and decisions needed. We'll walk through together at each major milestone."

5. **The hazmat reality** (if pre-1980): "Your home was built in [year]. We are required to survey for asbestos and follow lead-safe work practices. Here are the documents. Here is the survey schedule and cost."

6. **The relationship**: "I will be in your home for a long time. I want you to know that if something is bothering you, tell me directly — don't wait until it becomes a problem. And if I tell you I've found something, please trust that I'm telling you because I think you need to know, not because I'm looking for more billing."

## Appendix C — Common Renovation Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

| Mistake | Cost | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Bidding pre-1978 home without hazmat survey | $5–50k mid-project surprise | Always include survey line; or survey before bid |
| Skipping destructive investigation on high-uncertainty job | 25%+ cost overrun | Propose paid pre-bid investigation |
| Assuming bearing wall is non-bearing | $10–30k unplanned structural | Engineer review for any wall removal |
| Verbal CO authorization | Owner refuses to pay | Written, signed CO before work begins |
| Owner-supplied materials without contract language | GC eats warranty costs | Explicit clause on owner-supplied items |
| No contingency line | "Going over budget" stress | Visible contingency line, owner-acknowledged |
| Inadequate dust containment in occupied home | Owner complaints, possible HVAC contamination | Zip walls, HEPA negative air, daily cleaning |
| No documentation of existing conditions | Disputes over what was pre-existing | Photo survey at start, with date stamps |
| Tile installed without flood-tested waterproofing | Callback in year 2–3 | Always flood test, photograph, keep records |
| Cabinet measurements from plans, not opened walls | Cabinet re-order, weeks of delay | Verify after demo, before final order |
| Demo without utility disconnect verification | Injury, fire, water damage | Verify dead/closed before cutting |
| Range hood install without makeup air over 400 CFM | Code failure, inspection rejection | Engineer makeup air solution from start |
| No flood test on shower pan | Leak to floor below, year-2 disaster | Mandatory flood test, document |
| Demolishing without hazmat survey on pre-1980 | Federal/state violations, fines, possible criminal | Survey first, always |
| Bidding remodel with new-construction contingency (3–5%) | GC eats overruns | 10–20% on remodels |
| Not addressing existing code violations found during work | Inspector flags, callback | Disclose immediately, CO to address |
| Working past local hour limits | Police complaint, neighbor relations destroyed | Know the ordinance, stick to it |
| Single bathroom home with no occupancy plan | Owner crisis, hotel costs disputed | Address in pre-construction |

## Closing Note

Renovation is harder than new construction. The GCs who succeed in it are the ones who:
1. **Assume discoveries** and price for them transparently.
2. **Take hazmat seriously** because the legal exposure is career-ending.
3. **Communicate intensively** with owners — daily, written, photographed.
4. **Document everything** because memory is unreliable and disputes are common.
5. **Build relationships** with hazmat subs, structural engineers, and abatement contractors before they need them.
6. **Charge appropriately** — contingency, allowance, hard price, CO admin time, exploratory work.

The first three remodels will be hard. The discipline of pre-construction assessment, owner communication, and disciplined CO management compounds. By the tenth remodel, you'll see a project at the walk-through and know within $5,000 what it will actually cost — including the things you haven't found yet.
