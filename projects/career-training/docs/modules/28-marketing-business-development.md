# Marketing and Business Development for the Residential General Contractor

> Most residential GCs are excellent builders and mediocre marketers. They win work because someone they built for liked them enough to tell a friend. That's a great start — and a terrible business plan. A GC who builds a marketing and sales system gets to choose their projects, charge premium rates, work with better clients, and stop riding the feast-or-famine cycle. This guide is the operating manual for that system.

> **The bias to overcome**: builders trust craft and distrust "marketing." That's understandable — most contractor marketing is bad. But marketing is not about manipulation; it's about being findable, being trusted, and being chosen by the right clients. Done well, it's an extension of the same craft and integrity you bring to a build.

## 1. The Residential GC's Marketing Fundamentals

### 1.1 Why Most GC Marketing Fails

Most residential GC marketing fails for predictable, fixable reasons. If you recognize yourself in any of these, that's where to start.

| Failure mode | What it looks like | What it costs |
|---|---|---|
| **Chasing low-price clients** | Bidding against three other GCs on Angi/HomeAdvisor leads, racing to the bottom on price | Thin margins, demanding clients, no repeat work |
| **No follow-up system** | Sending a proposal and waiting for the phone to ring | 50–70% of deals lost simply because no one followed up |
| **No differentiation** | "We're a full-service GC, family-owned, with quality craftsmanship and great communication" — same line every other GC uses | Forced to compete on price |
| **Inconsistent presence** | Active marketing when slow, invisible when busy; relationships go cold | Boom-bust cycle, never builds momentum |
| **Building without measuring** | No idea where leads come from, no idea what's working | Spending money on the wrong channels |
| **No qualification** | Driving across town for any homeowner who calls | 60% of "estimating" hours produce zero revenue |
| **Generic positioning** | "We do everything from bathroom remodels to additions to new construction" | No one remembers you for anything; no referral hook |
| **Treating marketing as event, not system** | A Yelp ad here, a Facebook post there, hoping something sticks | No compounding effect |

### 1.2 The Three Ways to Grow

Every revenue increase in a contracting business comes from one of three levers. Most GCs only think about the first.

| Lever | What it means | How to pull it | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **More clients (volume)** | More projects per year at current revenue/project | Wider top-of-funnel, more leads, more bids | Medium — requires marketing investment |
| **Larger projects (revenue per client)** | Higher ticket sizes per project | Move from bathrooms to kitchens to whole-home; from remodels to additions; from $50K to $500K+ | Hard — requires capability, portfolio, and confidence to charge |
| **Better clients (margin)** | Same project size, better margin per dollar | Niche positioning, value-based pricing, qualified leads who won't haggle | Hardest — but most profitable |

**The compounding truth**: a GC who triples revenue by chasing more $30K bathrooms is exhausted and broke. A GC who triples revenue by moving to $300K kitchens with 25% margins (vs. 10% on bathrooms) works less, earns more, and runs a better company.

### 1.3 Positioning — What You Stand For

Positioning is the answer to four questions, written down, that you and your team can recite without thinking.

1. **Who do you serve?** (geographic area, project type, client profile, budget range)
2. **What do you do for them?** (specific outcome — not "remodel houses")
3. **Why are you different?** (the genuine, defensible reason)
4. **What's the result?** (the transformation, not the deliverable)

**Example — generic GC** (forced to compete on price):
> "We're a licensed general contractor serving the Bay Area with quality remodels and additions."

**Example — positioned GC** (charges 25% more, books out 9 months):
> "We rebuild fire-damaged homes in Marin and Sonoma. We work with insurance, navigate WUI codes, and deliver hardened, code-compliant homes in 12 months — so families can come home. We've done 47 fire rebuilds since 2017."

The second one has a niche, a credibility marker, a specific outcome, and a number that proves it. A homeowner who just lost their house calls the second GC; they get five quotes from the first.

### 1.4 Niche Strategy — Specialization Beats Generalization

The single highest-leverage marketing decision a residential GC can make is to pick a niche.

**Why specialization works**:

- **Referral specificity**: an architect refers "the ADU guy," not "a GC I know who might do ADUs"
- **Search dominance**: ranking #1 for "ADU contractor Berkeley" beats #15 for "general contractor Berkeley"
- **Margin defense**: clients pay a premium for the specialist; generalists are commodity
- **Operational efficiency**: same details, same subs, same suppliers, same problems — you get faster and better
- **Marketing efficiency**: one portfolio focus, one message, repeated until it lands
- **Better subs**: specialists attract specialist subs who do better work
- **Authority compounds**: every project deepens your expertise and your credibility

**Viable California residential GC niches** (each has real demand and real margin):

| Niche | Why it works in California | Typical project size |
|---|---|---|
| **ADUs / JADUs** | State-level mandates, every city/county encouraging; homeowners need code expertise | $150K–$450K |
| **Fire rebuilds** | WUI rebuilds in Sonoma, Napa, Marin, LA, Butte — insurance-funded, specialized code | $500K–$2M |
| **Hillside / foundation-difficult homes** | Bay Area, LA hills — most GCs avoid these; specialists charge a premium | $300K–$3M |
| **Historic / vintage remodels** | Bay Area Victorians, Craftsman bungalows, mid-century — preservation expertise | $200K–$1.5M |
| **High-end kitchen / bath** | High-margin scope, design-forward clients, repeat referrals | $80K–$400K |
| **Whole-home remodels in target ZIPs** | Geographic dominance in one or two affluent ZIPs | $400K–$2M |
| **Net-zero / Passive House / Title 24+** | Sustainability-focused buyers, premium pricing | $400K–$2M+ |
| **Accessibility / aging-in-place (CAPS)** | Demographic wave, specialized credential | $50K–$300K |
| **Seismic retrofits + remodels** | Mandatory in many CA jurisdictions; pairs well with kitchen/bath | $30K + remodel |
| **Multi-unit small infill (2–8 unit)** | SB 9, SB 10 enabled; few residential GCs are good at it | $1M–$5M |

**How to pick**: choose the intersection of (a) what you're already good at, (b) what has real demand in your geography, (c) what you can charge a premium for, and (d) what you actually enjoy. The last one matters — you'll talk about this for 10 years.

### 1.5 Brand vs. Reputation

For a GC, your brand IS your reputation. You don't have a Nike brand built by Super Bowl ads — you have what 47 past clients say about you to their friends.

**Brand components for a GC**:

| Component | What it means | How to manage |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual identity** | Logo, truck wraps, jobsite signage, business cards, website look | Hire a designer once; use consistently for 10 years |
| **Verbal identity** | How you describe what you do, the words on your website, the way your team talks | Write it once, train the team, repeat |
| **Reputation** | What clients, subs, architects, inspectors, and suppliers say about you | Earned daily on the jobsite |
| **Reviews** | Public, written reputation — the only one strangers can read | Systematically request and respond |
| **Portfolio** | Documented evidence of your work | Photograph every project |

**Reputation is managed systematically**, not by hoping:

1. **On every project**: jobsite cleanliness, weekly client updates, accurate change orders, on-time progress
2. **At closeout**: punch list completed, warranty package delivered, owner orientation done
3. **Within 30 days post-close**: review request, photo permission, referral ask
4. **Annually**: holiday card / anniversary check-in / one-year warranty walk
5. **Continuously**: respond to every review, monitor Google/Yelp/Houzz mentions

## 2. Referral Network — The Core Channel

For a residential GC, referrals are not one marketing channel among many — they are **the** channel. Everything else is support.

### 2.1 Why Referrals Dominate Residential Construction

| Factor | Cold lead | Referred lead |
|---|---|---|
| **Trust at first contact** | Zero — defensive client | High — trust transferred from referrer |
| **Close rate** | 10–20% | 50–80% |
| **Price sensitivity** | High — comparison shopping | Low — referrer set expectations |
| **Project size** | Often smaller; testing you | Larger; came expecting to hire you |
| **Time-to-close** | Weeks to months | Days to weeks |
| **Lifetime value** | One project | Multiple projects + their referrals |
| **Cost to acquire** | $500–$3,000 in marketing | $0–$200 (relationship maintenance) |

A residential GC with a strong referral network spends 1–2% of revenue on marketing and operates at 90%+ utilization. A GC without one spends 5–8% chasing cold leads and operates at 60% utilization. The math is decisive.

### 2.2 Primary Referral Sources

#### A. Architects

The single highest-value referral source for a residential GC. An architect refers you **before the client has talked to any other contractor** — you walk in as the recommended choice, often with no other GC competing.

**Why architects refer**:
- They need a GC they trust to execute their drawings (their reputation is on the line)
- They need a GC who communicates professionally with their client
- They need a GC who values the design intent and doesn't value-engineer it to death
- They need a GC who pays subs on time and doesn't create drama
- They are reciprocity-driven: if you make them look good, they bring you more work

**What architects want from a GC**:

| What they want | What it looks like in practice |
|---|---|
| Constructibility input early | Read drawings, flag issues during DD/CD, not during construction |
| Realistic pricing | Honest cost feedback, not lowballed to win then padded with COs |
| Field problem-solving | Solve issues collaboratively, don't run to the owner with every problem |
| Clean documentation | RFI logs, submittals, organized closeout binder |
| Respect for design intent | Don't substitute materials or details without consultation |
| Communication discipline | Weekly OAC meetings, written change orders, no surprises |
| Loyalty | If they refer you, you don't poach the client for direct work later |

**How to build the relationship** (a 3-year game, not a 3-month one):

1. **Identify the 20–40 architects** in your target geography doing your type of work. AIA local chapter directories, Houzz, recent permit pulls.
2. **Initial outreach**: a thoughtful email or LinkedIn note — not a generic blast. Reference a specific project of theirs you admired and why.
3. **Get on a project together** — even a small one. Execute flawlessly.
4. **Maintain contact**: quarterly coffee/lunch, jobsite visits on shared projects, share interesting trade info, refer them clients who need architects.
5. **After every shared project**: written thank-you to the architect, ask for feedback, ask what could be better.
6. **Stay top-of-mind**: holiday gift (book, not branded swag), invite to project completion events, copy them on owner orientation.

#### B. Interior Designers

Interior designers are often engaged **before an architect** on a renovation — they're frequently the first professional the client talks to. Cultivate them separately and aggressively.

**Differences from architects**:

- More focused on finish, less on structure — but on a remodel that drives 60% of the budget
- Often more design-forward clients who want a specific aesthetic
- Tend to be relationship-loyal — once they trust a GC, they refer repeatedly
- Many work with the same 1–2 GCs exclusively
- High overlap with the high-end kitchen/bath niche

**How to engage**: NKBA (National Kitchen and Bath Association), ASID (American Society of Interior Designers), local design showrooms, Instagram (designers live there), Houzz pro network. Lunch-and-learns at their office. Showroom partnerships.

#### C. Real Estate Agents

Agents know who is buying a fixer-upper, who is selling a house that needs work to sell, and who is moving and planning a remodel. Both sides of the transaction are referral opportunities.

**Two types of agent referrals**:

| Type | When | What client wants |
|---|---|---|
| **Buyer's agent referral** | New homeowner moving in | Renovation before move-in, or staged improvements |
| **Listing agent referral** | Seller prepping a home for market | Fast cosmetic work to boost sale price |

**How to build the relationship**: target the top 5–10 agents in your geographic niche. Most production agents work with 30–60 clients/year and have 5–15 who will renovate. Be the GC they trust to (a) respond fast, (b) give honest scope/budget, (c) not embarrass them with the client. Pre-purchase walkthrough is a high-value service — agents love a GC who'll spend 30 minutes at a listing with their client.

#### D. Structural Engineers

Underrated source. Engineers see every job that needs a foundation, a retrofit, a hillside fix, or an addition with significant structural work. They refer GCs because:

- The work requires structural literacy most GCs lack
- Clients often come to them first ("I think my foundation is cracking — who can fix it?")
- They want a GC who reads structural drawings correctly and executes details

**How to engage**: identify the structural engineers in your target geography (ASCE chapter, recent permit pulls, architect referrals). Get one in the field with you on a job. Pay them on time when they're consulting. Be the GC they bring their hardest clients to.

#### E. Past Clients

The ultimate referral source. Every past client should be a marketing asset for 10+ years.

**How to systematically activate past clients**:

| Touchpoint | Timing | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **Closeout walkthrough** | Final day | Deliver warranty package, ask for review, mention referrals |
| **30-day check-in** | 1 month post-completion | Phone call: "Everything working well?" + review reminder if not done |
| **6-month check-in** | 6 months | Email or call: any issues? + photo permission ask |
| **1-year warranty walk** | 12 months | In-person walkthrough, fix anything still under warranty |
| **Annual touch** | Years 2–10 | Holiday card, occasional email with relevant content |

**The direct referral ask** (most GCs are too shy):

> "We grow our business almost entirely through referrals from clients we've enjoyed working with — like you. If you know anyone planning a [kitchen remodel / ADU / addition], we'd be grateful if you mentioned us. Feel free to give them my cell."

Most clients want to refer but don't know how or when. Make it explicit, easy, and natural.

#### F. Other GCs

A counterintuitive but powerful source. When you're booked out 9 months and a homeowner calls with a 3-month timeline, you have a referral to give. Other GCs in your area do the same.

**How it works**: build relationships with 5–10 GCs at your tier (size, quality, geography). When you can't take a job — too small, too far, too tight a timeline, outside your niche — refer it to a peer. They reciprocate. CLCA (California Landscape Contractors Association), NARI (National Association of the Remodeling Industry), local builder associations are good places to meet peers.

#### G. Material Suppliers and Showrooms

Lumber yards, tile showrooms, plumbing fixture showrooms, appliance dealers, cabinet showrooms — they talk to homeowners every day, and homeowners frequently ask, "Do you know a good GC?"

**How to engage**:

- Be a frequent, profitable customer at the showrooms in your niche
- Know the salespeople by name
- Drop off business cards and a one-pager about your firm
- Refer your clients to them ("Go see Sarah at Studio Bath, tell her I sent you")
- Showroom co-marketing: client appreciation events, lunch-and-learns

**Highest-leverage suppliers for residential GCs**: high-end kitchen/bath showrooms, tile showrooms, custom cabinet shops, architectural lighting showrooms, hardwood flooring specialists, window/door dealers.

### 2.3 Building Referral Relationships — What It Actually Takes

Referrals are earned, not asked for. The work is consistent, low-key, and compounds over years.

**The monthly referral discipline**:

| Activity | Frequency | Time investment |
|---|---|---|
| Lunch with a referral source | 2x/month | 4 hours/month |
| Coffee meeting / drop-in | 2x/month | 2 hours/month |
| Jobsite visit invitation | 1–2x/month | 2 hours/month |
| Handwritten thank-you notes | 5x/month | 30 min/month |
| Email check-in with cold contacts | 5x/month | 30 min/month |
| Holiday/year-end gifts | Annually | 1 day in December |
| Referral source event (BBQ, project tour) | 1–2x/year | 1 day each |

**Total**: roughly 8–10 hours/month. If that produces 2–3 referred projects/year worth $150K–$1M each, it's the highest-ROI marketing time you'll ever spend.

### 2.4 Referral Tracking

Even a Google Sheet works. Track for every referral source:

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| Name, role, firm, contact info | Basic CRM |
| Source category (architect, designer, agent, etc.) | Segment by channel ROI |
| Date of last contact | Trigger follow-up cadence |
| Projects referred (count, dollar value) | See your top referrers |
| Projects you referred to them | Reciprocity tracking |
| Personal notes (spouse name, kids, interests) | Real relationship maintenance |
| Next planned touch | Action item |

**Pareto rule**: 80% of your referrals will come from 20% of your sources. Identify the top 10–15 and over-invest in them.

## 3. Digital Presence — The Credibility Layer

Digital presence is not where most of your leads come from — it's where they verify you before saying yes. A referred client will Google you. A Houzz lead will check your reviews. Your digital presence either confirms the trust referrals create, or undermines it.

### 3.1 Google Business Profile (GBP) — The Single Most Important Free Tool

For a local service business, GBP is more important than your website. It's what appears in Google Maps, the "local pack" at the top of search results, and Google's knowledge panel. It's free.

**Optimization checklist**:

| Field | Best practice |
|---|---|
| **Business name** | Exact legal name + DBA — don't keyword-stuff (violates Google policy) |
| **Categories** | Primary: "General Contractor" — secondary: "Kitchen Remodeler," "Bathroom Remodeler," "Home Builder," etc. |
| **Service area** | List specific cities you serve (not just a radius) |
| **Hours** | Accurate, including holiday closures |
| **Phone** | Direct local number, not 800 number |
| **Website** | Your own domain |
| **Description** | 750 chars max, lead with niche + geography + experience |
| **Services** | List every service with descriptions (e.g., "Kitchen remodel — $80K–$300K, design-build, average 14 weeks") |
| **Photos** | 50+ high-quality photos; refresh monthly; include team, jobsites, finished projects |
| **Posts** | Weekly updates — new project, finished project, tip, photo |
| **Q&A** | Seed common questions yourself; answer all user questions |
| **Reviews** | Goal: 50+ reviews with 4.8+ average; respond to every review |
| **License #** | Display CSLB license number; toggle license verification |

**Why this matters**: when someone searches "kitchen remodel contractor [your city]," Google shows a "local pack" of three businesses with map pins. Getting into that pack is the difference between 50 calls/month and 5. Optimization is the largest factor in ranking, after proximity and review count.

### 3.2 Website — What a GC Site Must Have

A GC website does not need to be fancy. It needs to be **fast, mobile, and trustworthy**.

**Mandatory pages**:

| Page | Purpose | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Home** | First impression | Hero image of best project, one-line positioning, 3 service tiles, recent work, client logos, CTA |
| **Portfolio** | Prove you can build | 8–20 projects, before/after photos, project descriptions, location (general), budget range |
| **Services** | Show scope | One page per primary service (kitchen, bath, ADU, etc.) with FAQ and pricing range |
| **About** | Build trust | Owner story, team photos, why you started, what you stand for, license/insurance |
| **Process** | Reduce anxiety | Step-by-step what working with you looks like — from inquiry to closeout |
| **Reviews / Testimonials** | Social proof | Pull from Google/Houzz/Yelp; include client photos if possible |
| **Contact** | Easy to act | Phone, email, contact form (5 fields max), service area map |
| **Blog (optional)** | SEO + authority | One post/month — project case studies, FAQs, code updates |

**Technical requirements**:

- **Mobile-first**: 70%+ of GC traffic is mobile. Test on a phone, not just a desktop browser.
- **Loading speed**: under 3 seconds. Use Google PageSpeed Insights. Compress images.
- **SSL (https)**: required — Google penalizes non-HTTPS sites.
- **Clear CTA on every page**: "Call (xxx) xxx-xxxx" or "Request a consultation" button.
- **License number displayed**: builds trust, required for some advertising platforms.
- **Address**: a real one, even if just an office. Google checks NAP (Name/Address/Phone) consistency.

**What not to do**:

- No music or autoplay video
- No stock photos of generic homes you didn't build
- No "Click here" or "Submit" buttons — use action language
- No 10-page essays — clients scan, don't read
- No flash, no obscure plugins, no auto-popups within 5 seconds
- No fake reviews — Google's algorithm detects them and de-ranks you

**Platform options**: Squarespace ($200–$400/year, easy), Wix (similar), WordPress (more flexibility, more maintenance), Webflow (designer-friendly), or a custom build if budget allows ($3K–$15K one-time).

### 3.3 Houzz — Built for Your Industry

Houzz is the residential design/build vertical Pinterest + Yelp + LinkedIn. Homeowners use it specifically to find contractors and designers. If you do high-end kitchen, bath, addition, or whole-home work, you should be on Houzz.

**Houzz Pro subscription** ($100–$500/month depending on tier):

| Feature | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Enhanced profile** | More photos, more visibility in search |
| **Project pages** | Each project gets its own page with photos, scope, and team credits |
| **Reviews** | Houzz reviews — separate from Google, important for high-end |
| **Best of Houzz badge** | Earned through reviews — clients filter for it |
| **Ideabooks** | Save and share with clients — also visible to other Houzz users |
| **Houzz Pro CRM and leads** | Lead management tools (mileage varies) |

**How clients use Houzz**:

1. Search "kitchen remodel [city]" or browse photos by style
2. Click into photos they love → see who designed and built it
3. Read the GC's profile, reviews, other projects
4. Save to ideabook
5. Eventually message the GC or call

**Optimization**: 50+ photos across 5+ projects, complete profile, request reviews from happy clients, tag collaborators (architect, designer) so you appear in their networks.

### 3.4 Instagram — Where Renovation Lives

Instagram is the dominant social platform for residential construction visual content. Designers and design-forward clients spend hours there.

**Content types that work for GCs**:

| Type | Performance | Effort |
|---|---|---|
| **Before/after carousel** | Highest engagement | Low |
| **Reels (15–60 sec video)** | Highest reach (Instagram pushes Reels) | Medium |
| **Time-lapse of construction** | High engagement | Low (set up a phone) |
| **Detail close-ups** | Saves and shares | Low |
| **Behind-the-scenes / team** | Builds connection | Low |
| **Designer collaboration tag** | Cross-posting to their audience | Low |

**Cadence**: 2–4 posts/week. 30 minutes/week if batched. Use Later, Planoly, or Meta Business Suite to schedule.

**What not to do**: text-heavy posts, blurry phone photos, posting one project for two weeks straight, ignoring DMs, buying followers.

**Hashtags**: 5–15 per post. Mix of broad (#kitchenremodel), local (#bayareacontractor), and niche (#adubuilder, #passivehouseca). Don't use the same 30 every time — Instagram flags it.

### 3.5 LinkedIn

For residential GCs, LinkedIn is **not** for finding clients. It's for connecting with referral sources: architects, designers, structural engineers, real estate agents, developers.

**Use it for**:
- Adding every referral source you meet
- Sharing 1–2 project posts/month so you stay visible
- Tagging your collaborators (huge — drives engagement from their network)
- Following up with people you met at events

### 3.6 Yelp

Yelp matters for residential construction whether you like it or not. Clients searching "remodeling contractor [city]" see Yelp results, and Yelp reviews carry weight.

**Manage your Yelp presence**:

- Claim and complete your business page
- Add 30+ photos
- Respond to every review — especially negative ones (professionally, never defensively)
- Do **not** ask clients for Yelp reviews — Yelp's filter aggressively hides solicited reviews
- Encourage clients to "find you on Yelp" — organic reviews are kept

**Yelp Ads (paid)**: typically poor ROI for construction. Leads tend to be price-shopping and tire-kicking. Most experienced GCs report disappointing results. Skippable for most.

### 3.7 NextDoor

NextDoor is the neighbor-to-neighbor network. Homeowners regularly post "Looking for a contractor recommendation in [neighborhood]" and get 10–20 responses from neighbors.

**How to engage** (without being spammy, which is critical):

- Claim a business page for your GC firm
- Respond to recommendation threads — but **only if a past client tags you**. Don't self-promote into threads.
- Post occasional value-add content: "We just got a permit for an ADU in [neighborhood] — happy to share what we learned about your local planning process."
- NextDoor Ads exist but are expensive for the leads they produce.

**The leverage**: ask past clients in each neighborhood to tag you in NextDoor recommendation threads. One mention from a real neighbor outweighs 50 ads.

### 3.8 SEO Basics for Local Contractors

You don't need to become an SEO expert. You need to do the few things that matter and ignore everything else.

**The 80/20 of local contractor SEO**:

| Factor | Impact | Effort |
|---|---|---|
| **Google Business Profile optimization** | Highest | Low |
| **Reviews (volume + recency + response rate)** | Very high | Medium |
| **Local citations** (NAP consistency across Yelp, Houzz, BBB, Angi, etc.) | High | Low (one-time cleanup) |
| **Page titles with city + service** | High | Low (one-time) |
| **Mobile-friendly, fast website** | High | Medium (or hire) |
| **Backlinks from local sources** (newspapers, neighborhood blogs, association directories) | Medium | Medium |
| **Blog content** | Low–medium | High |
| **Schema markup** (LocalBusiness) | Low | Low (or hire) |

**What to ignore** (or pay an agency for if you really care):

- Detailed keyword research beyond "[service] + [city]"
- Link-building campaigns
- Technical SEO audits
- Content marketing at scale

**When to hire an SEO agency**: if you're spending $1K+/month on Google Ads, you probably want $500–$1.5K/month on SEO. Look for agencies that specialize in contractors (not generalists). Demand monthly reports on rankings, traffic, and form fills.

## 4. Reputation Management — Reviews

Reviews are the single most leveraged marketing asset a residential GC can build. They appear in Google search, in the local pack, on Yelp, on Houzz, in NextDoor recommendations, and in every prospect's pre-call due diligence.

### 4.1 Why Reviews Drive Residential GC Leads

The journey of most residential GC leads:

1. Homeowner Googles "kitchen remodel contractor [city]"
2. Sees local pack (3 GCs) with star ratings + review counts
3. Clicks the top one with most/highest reviews
4. Reads 5–10 reviews — especially the 4-star and 3-star ones for honesty signal
5. Calls or fills out form

**A GC with 80 reviews at 4.9 stars** is the default first choice. A GC with 12 reviews at 4.6 stars is the third choice. The work to bridge that gap is months of systematic asking.

### 4.2 When to Ask

| Moment | Why it works |
|---|---|
| **Owner orientation walkthrough at closeout** | Client is thrilled, the project is fresh, you're in front of them in person |
| **Within 7 days of move-back-in** | Excitement is at its peak |
| **30 days post-closeout** | Last chance before they forget — also catches anyone who needed time to "live in" the space |

**Never ask** during a problem, before completion, or when you're chasing a final payment. Reviews must feel earned and freely given.

### 4.3 How to Ask

Direct, specific, easy. Three components:

1. **Frame it personally**: "Working with you and [spouse] was one of our favorite projects this year."
2. **Make the ask explicit**: "Would you be willing to leave us a Google review? It helps us find more great clients like you."
3. **Make it easy**: send a text or email immediately after with the direct link to your Google review form.

**Template text/email**:

> Hi [Name],
> Thanks again for trusting us with the [project type]. It was a pleasure working with you both.
> If you'd be willing, a quick Google review would mean a lot — it's how most of our future clients find us. Here's the link: [bit.ly/yourGoogleReviewLink]
> No pressure at all, and thanks again.
> [Your name]

**Direct link generation**: in your Google Business Profile dashboard, there's a "Get more reviews" → "Share review form" button that gives you a short URL. Use that, never a generic Google search link.

### 4.4 Responding to Reviews

| Review type | Response approach | Time |
|---|---|---|
| **5-star** | Brief, personal, thank them by name, mention the project specifically | Within 48 hours |
| **4-star** | Thank them, acknowledge anything they noted, mention you're working to address | Within 48 hours |
| **3-star or below** | Thoughtful, non-defensive, public-facing — see below | Within 24 hours |

**Negative review response framework**:

1. **Acknowledge** their experience without admitting fault or arguing facts publicly
2. **Apologize** for the experience (not necessarily for the action)
3. **Action**: state what you're doing or have offered
4. **Offline**: invite them to continue the conversation directly

**Example response to a 2-star review**:

> [Name], thank you for the feedback. I'm sorry your experience didn't meet the standard we hold ourselves to. I've tried to reach you by phone this week to walk through your concerns and make it right — please call me directly at [number] or email me at [email]. I'd appreciate the chance to resolve this. — [Your name], Owner

Prospective clients read negative reviews looking for **how the contractor responded**. A measured, professional response can turn a negative review into a positive trust signal. A defensive, accusatory response makes the GC look unhinged and costs more leads than the bad review itself.

### 4.5 The One Bad Review

Every GC will get one. It's not a question of if. What matters:

- **Volume neutralizes**: 1 bad review among 50 good ones reads as "they're real, not perfect." 1 bad review among 8 total reads as "high-risk GC."
- **Recency matters**: a 1-star review from 4 years ago with 30 positive reviews since is heavily discounted.
- **Response quality matters**: see above.
- **Pattern matters**: if you have three 1-star reviews all citing the same issue (communication, change orders, timeline), that's a real signal. Fix the underlying business issue, not just the reviews.

**Do not** try to get reviews removed unless they violate Google's policy (defamatory, off-topic, fake, posted by a competitor). Most "remove this review" attempts fail and waste time.

| Platform | Priority | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Google** | Tier 1 | Most leads start here; dominates local search |
| **Houzz** | Tier 1 for high-end | Buyer demographic skews affluent and design-forward |
| **Yelp** | Tier 2 | Significant for renovation searches; manage but don't solicit |
| **Facebook** | Tier 2 | Helpful but secondary |
| **BBB** | Tier 3 | Older clients check it; basic membership ($500/year) signals legitimacy |

## 5. Lead Qualification — Protecting Your Time

A residential GC's most precious resource is their time. Every hour spent on a prospect who was never going to hire you is an hour not spent on a real client. Qualification is the discipline of saying no — politely, quickly, and systematically.

### 5.1 The Cost of Bad Leads

| Activity | Time cost |
|---|---|
| Phone call | 30 minutes |
| Site visit | 2–4 hours (drive + walk + notes) |
| Estimate prep | 8–20 hours for a real bid |
| Proposal write-up | 2–4 hours |
| Follow-up calls | 1–2 hours |
| **Total per bid** | **15–30 hours** |

At a $150/hour effective rate (owner-operator GC), that's $2,250–$4,500 per bid. A 25% close rate means real cost-per-client is $9K–$18K. If your average margin is $30K/project, you spent a third of your margin getting the work. **Bidding fewer, better-qualified jobs at a 60–80% close rate is the only way to a profitable business.**

### 5.2 Qualification Criteria

The five filters every lead must pass:

| Filter | Question to ask | Disqualify if |
|---|---|---|
| **Project size** | "What's your approximate budget for this project?" | Below your minimum (e.g., $75K for a kitchen specialist) |
| **Project type** | "Tell me about what you're planning." | Outside your niche or capability |
| **Timeline** | "When are you hoping to start, and is there any hard deadline?" | Unrealistic (e.g., "starting next month" with no plans drawn) |
| **Decision authority** | "Who else is involved in the decision?" | Spouse hasn't bought in; getting bids "just to compare" |
| **Project readiness** | "Do you have plans drawn? Permits? Designer engaged?" | No plans, no designer, and no urgency to get them |

**Set your minimums and stick to them**. A GC who'll take any project under $30K to keep busy will spend their life on small projects with low margin and high client demand per dollar.

### 5.3 The Qualification Conversation

The first phone call is qualification, not sales. Goal: in 15–20 minutes, decide whether to spend any more time.

**Script structure**:

| Phase | Time | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **Open** | 1 min | Introduce yourself, confirm you have the right person |
| **Their story** | 5 min | "Tell me about the project. What are you hoping to accomplish?" Listen. |
| **Qualifying questions** | 5 min | Budget range, timeline, plans/permits, decision-makers, other contractors involved |
| **Your fit** | 3 min | Briefly describe your firm, your process, your typical project |
| **Next step** | 2 min | If qualified: schedule site visit. If not: refer or politely decline. |

**The budget question** is where most GCs flinch. Don't.

> "I want to make sure I'm not wasting your time with a process that doesn't fit your budget, and I want to be honest about what's possible. Kitchens in this market usually fall between $80K for a refresh and $250K+ for a full reconfiguration. Where in that range are you imagining?"

If they say "I don't have a budget, I just want to hear what it'll cost" — they're either inexperienced or evasive. Inexperienced clients can be educated. Evasive clients are red-flag clients.

### 5.4 Red Flags

Hard pass on any of these:

| Red flag | Why |
|---|---|
| **Extreme price sensitivity** ("I just want the cheapest bid") | Will fight every change order, sue at the end |
| **History of contractor disputes** | Pattern, not coincidence |
| **No design, no permits, but starts in 4 weeks** | Unrealistic; misalignment of expectations from day one |
| **Owner can't articulate scope** | Endless scope creep coming |
| **Owner argues with their spouse in front of you** | Decision-making will be chaos |
| **Multiple GCs fired previously on this project** | They are the problem, not the GCs |
| **"I'll pay you cash to skip the permit"** | Liability, illegal, end of conversation |
| **Demands a fixed price on a poorly-defined scope** | Inevitable conflict |
| **Asks you to start before signed contract** | Will not pay |
| **Resists references or licensing questions** | They have something to hide |

Trust your instinct. If something feels off in the first call, it will be much worse in month six.

### 5.5 Paid Discovery vs. Free Estimates

A trend in higher-end residential is **charging for pre-construction services** — design assistance, detailed estimating, value engineering, scheduling. This filters out tire-kickers and compensates you for real work.

**When to charge**:

- Project is $250K+
- Scope is unclear and requires significant pre-construction work
- Client has no architect/designer and wants you to help define scope
- You're providing real value (detailed estimate, allowances, schedule, sub bids)

**Typical fee structure**:

| Service | Fee |
|---|---|
| Initial consultation / site visit | Free (90 min cap) |
| Pre-construction services agreement | $2,500–$15,000+ depending on scope |
| Design-build phase | 5–10% of estimated construction cost, credited against contract if signed |

**How to position the fee**:

> "Our pre-construction process takes 60–90 hours of senior team time — detailed estimating, sub bidding, scheduling, and a clear scope document. We charge $5,000 for this, which gets credited against the construction contract if we move forward. If we don't, you keep all the work product and you've gotten honest, detailed pricing instead of a back-of-napkin bid. Most clients appreciate the rigor."

Clients who balk at $5K of pre-construction are clients who will balk at every dollar in construction. The fee filters for fit.

## 6. The Sales Process — From Lead to Contract

A repeatable sales process is the difference between feast-and-famine and steady backlog. Each step is a checkpoint where leads either advance or are filtered out.

### 6.1 Step 1 — Initial Contact (Response Speed)

**Studies (HBR, MIT Sloan, Inside Sales) consistently show**:

- Responding within 5 minutes increases conversion 8–10x vs. responding after 1 hour
- Conversion drops dramatically after 24 hours
- Responding after 3 days: client has already hired someone else 70% of the time

**The implication for a residential GC**: your first response, not your final proposal, often determines who wins the job.

**Practical implementation**:

- Forward every web/phone inquiry to your cell + email
- Aim for **acknowledgment within 1 hour** during business hours, 2 hours after-hours
- Acknowledgment ≠ proposal — a short "Got your inquiry, will call tomorrow at 10am" is fine
- If you can't respond fast yourself, hire a part-time admin or use an answering service

### 6.2 Step 2 — Discovery Call

15–30 minutes, by phone. (See Section 5.3 for the qualification script.) Goals:

1. Qualify the lead (budget, timeline, scope, authority)
2. Build initial rapport
3. Educate them about your process
4. Decide whether to schedule a site visit

**End every discovery call by setting the next step**: either a site visit date, or a referral to another GC, or a polite decline. Never leave it ambiguous.

### 6.3 Step 3 — Site Visit

The single most important sales moment in the process. Most GCs treat the site visit as a free estimate. Reframe it: this is your chance to demonstrate expertise, build trust, and learn enough to write a compelling proposal.

**Site visit checklist**:

| Before | During | After |
|---|---|---|
| Confirm 24 hrs in advance | Show up 5 minutes early | Send recap email within 24 hrs |
| Review any plans/photos they sent | Park considerately, ID yourself | Confirm next step + timeline |
| Bring: notepad, tape measure, laser, camera, business card, sample portfolio | Walk the entire space slowly | Add to CRM with notes |
| Plan for 60–90 minutes | Ask questions; listen 70%, talk 30% | |
| Dress: clean work clothes, branded shirt | Take 30+ photos | |
|  | Take notes on scope, conditions, constraints | |
|  | Talk through process — what working with you looks like | |
|  | Discuss budget ranges based on what you're seeing | |
|  | Set expectation for proposal turnaround | |

**Key behaviors that close deals at site visits**:

- Treat the homeowner like an adult and the home with respect (shoe covers, clean hands, don't move things)
- Ask their goals before talking solutions
- Be honest about constraints and trade-offs — clients trust honest GCs
- Identify one or two issues the previous GCs missed; you've now proven expertise
- Don't price in the meeting — but give a credible range

### 6.4 Step 4 — Proposal

A good proposal is a sales document, not just a price quote. It communicates your scope, your process, your team, and your value — and it makes the buying decision easy.

**Proposal structure** (recommended):

| Section | Length | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Cover page** | 1 page | Project name, client name, your firm, date, photo |
| **Executive summary** | 1 page | Scope summary, schedule summary, total price, next steps |
| **About us** | 1 page | Brief firm intro, team, similar projects, licenses |
| **Scope of work** | 3–10 pages | Detailed scope, room by room or trade by trade |
| **Inclusions / exclusions** | 1–2 pages | What's in, what's not — see below |
| **Schedule** | 1 page | Phases, durations, milestones, target completion |
| **Allowances** | 1 page | Itemized list of allowances (fixtures, finishes, etc.) |
| **Payment terms** | 1 page | Deposit, progress payments, retainage, final |
| **Why us** | 1 page | Differentiators, references, warranty |
| **Next steps** | 1 page | Sign here, payment instructions, kickoff timeline |

**Total**: 10–20 pages. Long enough to demonstrate rigor, short enough to be read.

**Presentation: in-person vs. email**:

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Project >$200K | **In-person presentation** — walk them through the proposal page by page, answer questions, close on the spot if possible |
| Project $75K–$200K | Either in-person or video call — depends on rapport |
| Project <$75K | Email PDF, follow up with call within 48 hours |

In-person presentations close at 2–3x the rate of email-only. The client gets to ask questions, you get to handle objections in real time, and you can read body language.

### 6.5 Step 5 — Follow-Up (The Most Neglected Step)

Most lost deals are not lost because of price — they're lost because no one followed up. The follow-up cadence:

| Day | Action |
|---|---|
| **Day 0** (proposal delivered) | "Sent the proposal — any initial questions?" text |
| **Day 2** | Phone call: "Wanted to make sure it came through and answer any questions" |
| **Day 5** | Email: "Anything I can clarify? Happy to walk through it again." |
| **Day 10** | Phone call: "Where are you in your decision-making process?" |
| **Day 14** | Email: "Wanted to check in once more. Are you still considering this project?" |
| **Day 21** | Final email: "I'll close out our file unless you'd like to keep discussing. Either way, thanks for the opportunity." |
| **Day 60** | Light check-in: "Wanted to circle back — how are things progressing on the [project]?" |
| **Day 180** | Final check-in: many projects re-emerge 6+ months later |

**Most GCs follow up once or twice and give up. The client interprets this as the GC not really wanting the work.** Five professional follow-ups is normal; clients expect it and respect it.

### 6.6 Step 6 — Closing

Closing is asking for the business and handling the objections that come up.

**Asking for the business**:

> "Based on our conversations, I think this is a good fit and we'd love to do the project for you. Are you ready to move forward, or what's still on your mind?"

Direct, calm, not pushy. Then listen.

**Common objections and responses**:

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "We need to think about it." | "Of course. What specifically do you need to think through? I might be able to help." |
| "We need to talk to our spouse / partner." | "Makes sense. When would be a good time for me to follow up with both of you?" |
| "We got a lower bid." | (See Section 7.4) |
| "We need to wait for [event] before deciding." | "Understood. Let's keep the conversation warm — can I check in [date]?" |
| "We're not sure about the timeline." | "Let's talk about it. What timeline would work for you?" |
| "The budget is too high." | "Tell me where it's coming in too high — let's see if there are scope adjustments that get us to where you need to be." |

**Never lower your price without changing scope.** Dropping price without scope change tells the client you padded the price to begin with — you're now negotiable on everything.

### 6.7 Win Rate Tracking

Track every lead through every stage. This is the data that tells you where to improve.

| Stage | Industry benchmark | Your number |
|---|---|---|
| Lead → Qualified | 50% | Track yours |
| Qualified → Site visit | 80% | Track yours |
| Site visit → Proposal sent | 70% | Track yours |
| Proposal sent → Contract | 30–50% (cold), 60–80% (referral) | Track yours |

**Diagnostic**:

- **Low Lead → Qualified**: too many bad leads (improve channel mix or qualification rigor)
- **Low Qualified → Site visit**: scheduling friction or you sound unconvincing on the phone
- **Low Site visit → Proposal**: spending site-visit time on people who weren't serious
- **Low Proposal → Contract**: proposals not compelling, follow-up weak, or pricing off

Your weakest stage is your highest-ROI improvement target.

## 7. Pricing Strategy — Winning Without Being the Cheapest

The single biggest mistake residential GCs make is competing on price. The price-competing GC attracts the worst clients, runs the thinnest margins, gets the worst subs (who are also price-competing), and burns out within five years. The premium GC charges more, attracts better clients, gets the best subs, and builds a sustainable business.

### 7.1 Why Competing on Price Loses

| Effect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Client quality** | Price-shoppers are the most demanding and least loyal clients |
| **Margin** | Razor-thin margins leave no room for problems, change orders, or weather |
| **Sub quality** | Best subs charge real rates; you can't afford them |
| **Reputation** | Price-shoppers leave the worst reviews when anything goes wrong |
| **Cash flow** | Tight margins mean any delay is catastrophic |
| **Burnout** | High volume / low margin = working twice as hard for the same income |
| **Exit value** | A business built on low margins has no enterprise value |

### 7.2 Value-Based Pricing

Value-based pricing means pricing to what the work is worth to the client, not just cost-plus-margin.

**What clients actually pay for** (besides materials and labor):

| Value | Dollar value to client |
|---|---|
| **Peace of mind** | Avoiding the disasters they've heard about |
| **Quality / craftsmanship** | Work that lasts 30+ years vs. 10 |
| **Reliability / schedule** | Project finishes when promised |
| **Communication** | Knowing what's happening, no surprises |
| **Code compliance** | Avoiding inspection failures and rework |
| **Design intent** | Their architect's vision actually executed |
| **Warranty** | 1+ year warranty with real responsiveness |
| **References** | Trust built on others' positive experiences |
| **Cleanliness** | Daily site cleanup, considerate of neighbors |
| **Trade management** | Subs who show up, on time, sober, qualified |

The cheapest bid may have ignored or skimped on every one of these. Your job in the proposal and the conversation is to make these visible.

### 7.3 Communicating Value in the Proposal

A good proposal makes the comparison to a cheaper bid look like comparing apples to a half-eaten apple.

**Where to communicate value**:

| Where | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Cover letter** | Lead with the outcome and the experience, not the price |
| **Scope of work** | Detail every assembly, every detail, every allowance — show the comprehensiveness |
| **Inclusions** | Make explicit everything you're providing (dumpsters, permits, supervision, etc.) |
| **Exclusions** | (See 7.5) |
| **Process** | Show your weekly OAC meetings, daily updates, change-order process |
| **Team** | Show who works on the project — superintendent, project manager, ownership |
| **References** | 3–5 names with phone numbers (with permission) |
| **Warranty** | Explicit, written warranty terms — not just "we stand behind our work" |
| **Insurance + license** | Display CSLB license, GL insurance amount, workers' comp |

### 7.4 Handling "I Got a Lower Bid"

This conversation comes up on roughly 40% of proposals. The response is not to drop your price — it's to reframe the comparison.

**Script**:

> "Thanks for telling me. I'd actually love to look at the other bid with you, because in my experience, big price differences usually come from real scope differences — not the same scope priced differently.
>
> Without seeing the other bid, I'd guess the difference might be in [common gaps: scope of demo, allowances for finishes, structural contingency, electrical/plumbing rework, permits, supervision, cleanup]. The other GC may also be planning to come back with change orders for what's not in the scope.
>
> Here's what I'd suggest: let's sit down for 30 minutes, lay the two bids side by side, and identify the actual differences. Then you can make an informed decision. Sound fair?"

**Why this works**:

- Doesn't disparage the competitor (which makes you look insecure)
- Reframes the conversation from price to scope
- Invites them to engage further (which closes more deals)
- Reveals that lower bids often have padding-via-change-order built in
- Shows confidence in your own pricing

**When you do this exercise**, 80% of the time you find the other bid:

- Excludes demo, dumpsters, or permits
- Has lower allowances (which client will exceed)
- Excludes electrical/plumbing rework that the project clearly needs
- Doesn't include supervision/PM time
- Has lower-quality material specs
- Has unrealistic timeline that will trigger penalty clauses or rushed work

### 7.5 Exclusions as a Positioning Tool

Most GCs hide their exclusions in fine print. Premium GCs use clear, detailed exclusions to show how comprehensive their scope is **and** how vague the competition's may be.

**Example exclusion section** (positioned, not buried):

> **Exclusions to this proposal — what's NOT included**
> The following items are not included in our pricing. We've called them out specifically so there are no surprises:
>
> - Asbestos or lead abatement (subject to testing; we will provide separate proposal if found)
> - Hazardous soil remediation
> - Discovery of structural damage hidden behind existing finishes (handled via change order with itemized cost)
> - Owner-supplied appliances, fixtures, or finishes not specified in our allowances
> - Landscape repair beyond construction footprint
> - HOA or design review fees (handled separately)
> - Utility upgrades beyond 200A service
> - Permit fees (paid by Owner at cost — typically $X–$Y for this scope)

This serves three purposes:
1. Protects you legally
2. Sets expectations honestly
3. Implicitly highlights that the other bid probably didn't address any of this

### 7.6 Pricing Models — Fixed Price vs. Cost-Plus vs. GMP

| Model | Best for | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed price (lump sum)** | Well-defined scope, complete drawings | Predictable for client, premium for risk transfer | All scope risk on you; pad accordingly |
| **Cost-plus (T&M with fee)** | Loosely defined or evolving scope, design-build | Aligns incentives, no surprises | Client anxiety about open-ended cost |
| **Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)** | Larger projects, sophisticated clients | Cap protects client, transparency wins trust | Complex accounting; you absorb overruns above GMP |

**Most California residential GCs**: use fixed price for small/well-defined projects ($30K–$300K), cost-plus with a not-to-exceed ceiling for larger or design-build projects, and GMP for high-end whole-home or custom builds.

**Markup vs. margin** — know the difference:

- **Markup** = (price - cost) / cost (e.g., 25% markup on $100K cost = $125K price)
- **Margin** = (price - cost) / price (e.g., 25% markup = 20% margin)

To net a 20% gross margin, you need a 25% markup. Many GCs mistakenly apply 15% markup and wonder why they have no profit — that's a 13% margin, which after overhead leaves nothing.

**Healthy California residential GC margins** (gross, before overhead and owner's salary):

| Project type | Healthy gross margin |
|---|---|
| Small remodel ($30K–$80K) | 25–35% |
| Mid-sized remodel ($80K–$300K) | 20–28% |
| Large remodel / addition ($300K–$1M) | 15–22% |
| Whole-home / custom build ($1M+) | 12–18% |
| Niche / specialty (fire rebuild, hillside) | +3–5% premium |

### 8.4 When to Hire a Marketing Agency

- You want to scale beyond $3M revenue
- You can afford $1.5K–$5K/month minimum

- **Specialize in residential contractors** (not generalists)

- Offer monthly reporting on leads, calls, traffic, rankings
- Don't lock you into 12+ month contracts
- Will manage Google Ads, Local SEO, GBP, and possibly Houzz/Instagram

**Red flags**: agencies promising #1 rankings in 30 days, asking for full payment upfront, no transparency on what they're doing.

## 9. Content and Portfolio Strategy

For a residential GC, your portfolio is your résumé, your sales tool, and your marketing engine. Every project is an asset — but only if it's documented.

### 9.1 Before/After Photos — The Highest-Leverage Content

A great before/after photo is the single most effective marketing asset a residential GC can produce.

**How to take a great before/after**:

| Element | Best practice |
|---|---|
| **Same angle, same lens** | Before and after from the identical position |
| **Time of day** | Soft natural light (morning or late afternoon); avoid harsh midday shadows |
| **Cleanup** | Site spotless — no tools, no debris, no leftover packaging |
| **Staging** | Light staging is fine — a vase, a fruit bowl, towels in a bath |
| **Wide angle** | 16–24mm equivalent shows the full space; a smartphone with wide-angle lens works |
| **Composition** | Straighten verticals, level horizontals; use grid lines |
| **Multiple angles** | 5–10 angles per room minimum |
| **Detail shots** | Close-ups of joinery, hardware, tile transitions |

**Phone vs. professional photographer**:

| Source | Cost | Quality | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Smartphone (iPhone 13+ or Pixel 6+) | $0 | Good | Daily progress, social media, time-lapse |
| Mirrorless camera (Sony A7, Fuji X-T) | $1,500 owned | Very good | Project photography by capable owner-operator |
| Professional architectural photographer | $1,000–$3,000 per shoot | Excellent | 2–4 hero projects/year |

**ROI of a professional shoot**: $1,500 for a 4-hour shoot producing 30–50 hero images of one project. Those images get used on:
- Website portfolio (5–10 years)
- Houzz project page
- Instagram (10–15 posts spread over a year)
- Print marketing materials
- Proposals (the project as a reference)

Cost per image: $30–$50. Cost per use across all channels: pennies.

### 9.2 Case Studies

A written case study turns a project into a story prospects can connect with.

**Case study structure**:

| Section | Length |
|---|---|
| **Headline** | 1 line — "Hillside Mid-Century Addition in Mill Valley" |
| **Client situation** | 100 words — who they were, what they wanted, what was hard |
| **Challenge** | 150 words — the specific obstacles (site, codes, schedule, design) |
| **Solution** | 200 words — what you did, how you collaborated with architect/designer |
| **Result** | 100 words — the outcome, including client quote if possible |
| **Project details** | Bullet list — size, duration, scope, team |
| **Photos** | 8–15 images including before/after |

**Where case studies live**: website (one per project, organized by category), shared as PDF in proposals, posted to Houzz, broken into Instagram carousel posts, sent in monthly newsletter.

### 9.3 Video

Video has become essential for residential construction marketing. Reels and YouTube Shorts have eaten Instagram's static photo reach.

**Video formats that work**:

| Format | Length | Effort | Performance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time-lapse construction** | 30–60 sec | Low (set up phone, time-lapse mode) | High |
| **Before/after reveal** | 15–30 sec | Low | Very high |
| **Walkthrough of completed project** | 60–120 sec | Medium | High |
| **Owner testimonial** | 60–90 sec | Medium (need to coach the client) | Very high |
| **Process explainer** ("How we frame a wall") | 30–90 sec | Medium | Medium (good for SEO/YouTube) |
| **Behind-the-scenes / day in the life** | 30–60 sec | Low | High for personal brand |

**Tools**: smartphone, $30 tripod, CapCut or InShot for editing (free), trending audio on Instagram/TikTok.

### 9.4 Building a Portfolio From Scratch

If you're starting fresh with no documented portfolio:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Photograph every current and recent project — even with a phone, get the shots |
| **2** | Reach back to past clients: "We're updating our portfolio — would you mind if we came by for 30 min to photograph?" 80% say yes |
| **3** | Get a written permission form (use Houzz / website / marketing materials) |
| **4** | Hire a photographer for 2–3 of your best recent projects |
| **5** | Build a simple website portfolio page (Squarespace template, 6 hours of work) |
| **6** | Cross-post to Houzz, Instagram, Google Business Profile |
| **7** | From now on: every project gets photographed at completion. Block it on the schedule. |

A portfolio of 12 well-photographed projects beats a portfolio of 60 phone snapshots. Quality over quantity.

## 10. Business Development Pipeline Management

A pipeline is the structured view of every lead at every stage. Even a basic one transforms a GC's business — from guessing whether you'll have work in three months, to knowing.

### 10.1 CRM — What You Need

A "CRM" can be:

| Tool | Cost | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Google Sheet** | $0 | Solo GC, <30 active leads |
| **Trello / Notion** | $0–$10/mo | Solo GC who likes visual boards |
| **HubSpot Free** | $0 | Growing GC, multi-user, decent automation |
| **Buildertrend / CoConstruct** | $100–$400/mo | GC who wants project mgmt + CRM unified |
| **Jobber / Housecall Pro** | $50–$200/mo | Smaller projects, more transactional |
| **Salesforce / Zoho** | $25+/user/mo | Large GC with sales team |

**The bar to clear**: any system you'll actually update weekly. A perfect CRM you don't use is worse than a Google Sheet you do.

**Minimum data per lead**:

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| Name, phone, email | Basic contact |
| Project address | Site reference |
| Project type | Categorize for analytics |
| Estimated budget | Pipeline value |
| Estimated start date | Cash flow forecasting |
| Stage (see below) | Where they are in the process |
| Referral source | Track channel ROI |
| Last contact + next action + due date | Drives follow-up discipline |
| Notes | Anything important from conversations |

### 10.2 Pipeline Stages

A standard residential GC pipeline:

| Stage | Definition | Typical % conversion to next |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Inquiry** | New lead just contacted | 50% |
| **2. Qualified** | Passed phone qualification | 80% |
| **3. Site Visit Scheduled** | On the calendar | 90% |
| **4. Site Visit Complete** | Met onsite | 75% |
| **5. Proposal Sent** | Quote delivered | 40% (cold), 70% (referral) |
| **6. Negotiation / Refinement** | Engaged, working on scope/price | 60% |
| **7. Verbal Yes** | Committed verbally | 90% |
| **8. Contract Signed** | Closed-won | 100% |
| **9. In Construction** | Active project | — |
| **10. Closed Out** | Project complete | — |
| **Lost** | Closed-lost (track reason) | — |

### 10.3 Tracking Conversion Rates

Run the math monthly. Spot the bottleneck.

**Example scenario**:

> A GC has 100 inquiries/year. After phone qualification, 50 are qualified. 40 get site visits. 30 proposals go out. 12 close. Total: 12 projects per year, average $200K = $2.4M revenue. Conversion: 12%.

Diagnostic questions:
- **50 of 100 disqualified** — Where are these leads coming from? Bad channel mix.
- **30 of 40 to proposal** — Strong site-visit-to-proposal rate.
- **12 of 30 close** — 40% close rate — middle of the pack. Improve proposal quality and follow-up.

Bottleneck is **lead quality** (50% disqualified) and **proposal close rate** (40%). Improving each by 10 points adds 4–5 projects/year — $800K–$1M revenue.

### 10.4 Pipeline Value and Revenue Forecasting

Multiply each lead's estimated project value by its stage's probability of closing:

| Stage | Probability |
|---|---|
| Inquiry | 10% |
| Qualified | 25% |
| Site Visit | 35% |
| Proposal | 50% |
| Negotiation | 70% |
| Verbal Yes | 90% |
| Contract | 100% |

**Example pipeline at end of Q1**:

| Lead | Value | Stage | Probability | Weighted |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | $300K | Proposal | 50% | $150K |
| B | $180K | Site Visit | 35% | $63K |
| C | $450K | Negotiation | 70% | $315K |
| D | $220K | Qualified | 25% | $55K |
| E | $600K | Verbal Yes | 90% | $540K |
| F | $150K | Inquiry | 10% | $15K |
| **Total weighted pipeline** | | | | **$1.14M** |

If your annual revenue target is $2.5M and you're tracking $1.14M weighted at end of Q1, you're on pace. If you're tracking $400K, you have a pipeline-building problem to solve **now**, not in three months when you have no work.

### 10.5 Annual Planning — Working Backwards From Revenue

The math residential GCs rarely do — but should:

1. **Revenue target**: e.g., $3M in contract value
2. **Average project size**: e.g., $250K → 12 projects/year
3. **Close rate**: e.g., 40% → need 30 proposals
4. **Proposal rate from site visit**: e.g., 75% → need 40 site visits
5. **Site visit rate from qualified**: e.g., 80% → need 50 qualified leads
6. **Qualified rate from inquiry**: e.g., 50% → need **100 inquiries**

**Now**: where do 100 inquiries come from?

| Channel | Target inquiries/year | How |
|---|---|---|
| Past client referrals | 25 | Systematic 30-day, 6-month, 1-year touches |
| Architect / designer referrals | 30 | Cultivate 15 active relationships |
| Real estate agent referrals | 10 | Target 5 top agents |
| Google Business Profile (organic) | 15 | Optimization + reviews |
| Houzz | 8 | Pro subscription, photo updates |
| Google Ads | 8 | $1K/month managed |
| Other (Instagram, NextDoor, etc.) | 4 | Maintenance |
| **Total** | **100** | |

This is your plan. Without numbers, "marketing more" is wishful thinking.

| Day | Activity | Time |
|---|---|---|

### 11.3 Quarterly Reviews

Every quarter, review:

- Leads by channel (which channels are producing?)
- Win rate by stage (where am I losing?)
- Project profitability (which projects had healthy margins, which didn't?)
- Referral source ROI (who is sending the most/best work?)
- Marketing spend vs. revenue produced
- Pipeline value vs. annual target

Adjust budget allocation accordingly. Double down on what's working; cut what isn't.

### 11.4 Annual Review — Strategic Questions

Each year, ask:

1. Did I hit my revenue target? Why or why not?
2. What were my best three projects (margin, client experience, learning)? What did they have in common?
3. What were my worst three projects? Same question.
4. Should I narrow my niche further?
5. Which referral sources are over-indexing? Which under-indexing?
6. Where am I leaving money on the table — better clients, larger projects, higher margins?
7. What do I need to stop doing?
8. What's my plan for the next year?

## 12. Final Synthesis — The 10 Principles

If you remember nothing else from this guide:

1. **Niche beats generalist.** Pick a lane and own it.
2. **Referrals are the channel.** Everything else is support.
3. **Respond in under an hour.** Speed wins more deals than price.
4. **Reviews are the marketing.** Ask every client, respond to every review.
5. **Qualify ruthlessly.** Your time is the asset.
6. **Sell value, not price.** The cheapest GC has the worst life.
7. **Follow up five times.** Most lost deals are lost in silence.
8. **Document every project.** Today's photo is a decade of marketing.
9. **Track everything.** A pipeline you can see is a pipeline you can grow.
10. **Show up consistently.** Marketing compounds — relationships, content, reputation, all of it. Three years in, you'll have a business that prints clients while you build.
