# Change Order Management and Value Engineering

> **Purpose.** Change orders are the single largest variable between the margin you estimated and the margin you make. A GC who prices and documents changes well consistently outperforms the market. A GC who allows scope to drift without written change orders consistently underperforms, regardless of how well they build. This module covers both sides of the change order equation: capturing legitimate additional cost from owner-directed changes, and the companion discipline of value engineering — helping owners find cost savings that protect your relationship without sacrificing scope.

## 0. Mental Model — Changes Are Normal; Disputes Are Not

Every project of substance has change orders. Changes arise from incomplete design, owner decisions made in the field, unforeseen conditions, and the simple reality that what looks good on paper doesn't always look good in three dimensions. A baseline assumption: a typical residential project will generate 10–25% in additional change orders above the original contract value. Industrial projects often run 5–15%.

The GC's job is not to prevent changes — changes are legitimate responses to real conditions. The GC's job is to:
1. Identify changes as soon as they arise.
2. Price them accurately and promptly.
3. Obtain written authorization before performing the changed work (or immediately after, if emergency conditions require acting first).
4. Collect the revenue and update the project schedule accordingly.

GCs who routinely fail to recover change order revenue make the same errors repeatedly:
- Performing work without written authorization.
- Not tracking the cost of changed work separately.
- Failing to account for schedule impact in their pricing.
- Being conflict-averse and eating the cost to preserve the relationship — which is counterproductive because uncompensated changes breed resentment, quality shortcuts, and eventual disputes far worse than the original conversation.

## 1. Change Order Triggers and Procedures

### 1.1 What Triggers a Change Order

A change order is required whenever the work departs from the original contract scope, schedule, or contract sum. The following events always trigger a change order:

**Owner-Initiated Changes:**
- Owner adds, deletes, or modifies scope in writing or verbally.
- Owner selects a material or finish different from the specification baseline.
- Owner requests acceleration of the schedule.
- Owner's architect or designer issues a revised drawing or specification.

**Design/Engineering-Initiated Changes:**
- Architect issues an Architect's Supplemental Instruction (ASI) or Construction Change Directive (CCD) that affects cost or schedule.
- Engineer requires structural modifications not on the original drawings.
- RFI response reveals a design error, omission, or conflict requiring additional work.

**Condition-Initiated Changes:**
- Differing site conditions (hidden conditions materially different from those indicated in the contract — see Module 29, Section 4.3).
- Hazardous materials discovered (asbestos, lead paint, soil contamination).
- Existing conditions worse than represented (rotten framing, inadequate foundation, undersized electrical service).

**Code and Regulatory Changes:**
- Inspector requires work not shown on the approved plans.
- Code interpretation differs from the project specification.
- AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction) requires a change during inspection.

**Schedule-Disruption Changes:**
- Owner-caused delay requiring acceleration.
- Extended general conditions from an owner-caused delay.
- Phasing requirements changed by owner.

### 1.2 The Correct Procedure When a Change Arises

**Do not guess; do not assume; do not absorb.** When any of the above events occur, follow this sequence:

1. **Identify the change.** In the field, the superintendent recognizes that work is required outside the original scope or that a condition has been discovered that changes the cost.

2. **Stop or flag the work.** If the work can be safely deferred, do not proceed until the change is documented and authorized. If emergency conditions require immediate action, proceed but document immediately.

3. **Notify the owner immediately.** Same day, in writing:
   > "We've encountered [description of change/condition] at [location]. We believe this is outside our original contract scope because [reason]. We will provide a cost estimate within [2–5] business days. Please advise if you want us to proceed while pricing is being developed. This notice preserves our right to a change order under [contract section]."

4. **Track costs separately.** Open a new cost code immediately. Every hour of labor, every material purchase, and every equipment hour related to this change should flow to that code, not to general project costs.

5. **Prepare the change order request (COR).** Within the timeframe stated in your notice, submit a formal cost and schedule analysis.

6. **Obtain written authorization before (or immediately after) proceeding.** The written authorization is the change order — signed by owner's authorized representative.

### 1.3 The "Proceed" Order Problem

The most common change order dispute pattern: owner says "Just go ahead and we'll sort out the price later." This is comfortable for the owner and terrible for the GC.

**Your response to a verbal "just proceed" should always include:**

A same-day written confirmation of the direction:
> "This confirms your direction to proceed with [scope description] as discussed today at [time]. We estimate the additional cost at approximately $[X], with approximately [Y] days of schedule impact. We will submit a formal change order request for your signature within [5] business days. If you believe this scope is within our original contract, please advise in writing so we can discuss. We are proceeding to maintain the project schedule."

This letter does three things:
1. Documents the direction so it cannot be later denied.
2. Creates a paper trail of the owner's authorization.
3. Puts the owner on notice that a formal CO is coming — avoiding the "surprise" dynamic when the CO arrives.

## 2. Cost Impact Analysis and Pricing Strategies

### 2.1 Components of a Change Order Price

A complete change order price captures all cost impacts, not just the direct labor and material:

| Cost Component | Description | Common Error |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct labor** | Trade workers for the changed work, at prevailing or market rates | Under-estimating hours; failing to include supervision labor |
| **Materials** | Materials required for the changed work, at current market pricing | Using original bid pricing when materials have escalated |
| **Sub costs** | Subcontractor pricing for any portion of changed work | Not getting firm pricing from subs before submitting CO |
| **Equipment** | Owned equipment (daily rate) or rented equipment for the change | Forgetting crane or equipment costs on structural changes |
| **Escalation** | Price premium for materials needed on short notice | Forgetting expediting premium on long-lead items |
| **Overhead** | GC's percentage overhead on direct costs — typically 10–15% | Omitting this entirely, or using too low a rate |
| **Profit** | GC's markup on total costs — typically 10–15% for COs | Using a lower profit rate than on the original work; COs should carry equal or higher margin |
| **Schedule impact cost** | Extended general conditions if the change delays completion | Almost always forgotten; often the largest cost component on complex changes |
| **Bond premium** | Additional bonding cost on added value — typically 1–2% of added contract value | Omitted on almost every CO; check your bond agreement |
| **Insurance premium** | GL and builders risk adjustments for scope additions | Omitted routinely; include a note if you'll need to adjust |

**The overhead and profit rates in the contract:** Many construction contracts (especially public works) specify maximum overhead and profit rates the contractor may charge on change orders. Read your prime contract for this language before pricing. AIA A201-2017 §7.3.8 provides for overhead and profit unless the parties have agreed on percentages in the Special Conditions. Standard market rates are 10% overhead + 10% profit on direct costs, or 15% combined.

### 2.2 Time-and-Material (T&M) Change Order Pricing

When the scope of a change is too uncertain to give a fixed price, use **time-and-material pricing**:
- All labor tracked by worker, classification, and hours, at the agreed burdened rate.
- All materials at invoice cost plus a markup (typically 10–15%).
- All equipment at the agreed rate (daily or hourly).

**The T&M ticket:** A paper or digital form signed by the owner's representative at the end of each shift documenting the day's labor, materials used, and equipment. Getting this signature is non-negotiable — unsigned T&M tickets are worth nothing in a dispute.

**T&M cap:** Whenever possible, establish a "not-to-exceed" cap on a T&M change order. This gives the owner protection from runaway costs and gives you the flexibility to proceed quickly. Example: "T&M not to exceed $15,000; will convert to lump sum once scope is defined."

### 2.3 Pricing the Schedule Impact

Extended general conditions cost is the most frequently omitted and most consequential element of a change order price on large projects.

**General conditions costs that accrue with extended schedule:**
- Superintendent salary
- Project manager time allocated to this project
- Site trailer rent
- Temporary utilities (power, water, phone)
- Job-site security
- Equipment rental and standby costs
- Bonding and insurance premium continuation

**Example calculation — 3-week schedule extension:**
```
Superintendent salary:  $2,500/week × 3 = $7,500
PM time (25% allocation): $1,500/week × 3 = $4,500
Site trailer/office:    $500/week × 3 = $1,500
Temp utilities:         $300/week × 3 = $900
Equipment standby:      $1,200/week × 3 = $3,600
TOTAL extended GC:      $18,000
```

This $18,000 is a legitimate and substantial element of the change order cost — and is almost never captured if the GC doesn't think to calculate it.

## 3. Negotiation Tactics and Owner Relationship Management

### 3.1 Framing the Conversation

The biggest mistake GCs make in change order negotiations is presenting the change as an adversarial demand. The most effective approach presents the change order as **delivering transparency** to the owner:

**Less effective:** "You changed the scope and now you owe me more money."

**More effective:** "We want to make sure there are no surprises at the end of the project. Here's the cost impact of the changes you've made so far — let's review it together so you can decide how you want to proceed."

This reframe positions you as a partner in budget management, not as an adversary extracting money. It also works: owners who feel ambushed by a large CO at the end of a project are defensive. Owners who have been walked through the cost implications of their decisions along the way feel informed and respected.

### 3.2 The Concurrent Change Order Presentation

Issue change orders promptly and individually — not in a batch at the end of the project. A single $3,000 change order is easy to approve; a batch of ten change orders totaling $30,000 at month six is a shock that triggers owner resistance.

**Target:** Submit a change order request within 5 business days of the event triggering the change. Have it signed within 10–14 days. Never let unsigned change orders accumulate for more than 30 days.

### 3.3 Handling Owner Pushback

The most common owner objections to change orders, and how to address them:

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "That should have been included in your original price." | Pull up the original scope document and specification; walk through the specific exclusion or what was "assumed." Documentation created at bid time is your best response. |
| "Your price is too high." | Provide the cost breakdown — material invoices, labor tickets, sub quotes. A transparent itemized breakdown is more persuasive than a lump-sum defense. |
| "We didn't authorize that work." | Produce the daily log entry for the date, the email or text directing the work, the meeting minutes if applicable. You authorized it verbally on [date]; here's our documentation. |
| "The architect says this is in your scope." | Request the architect's basis for that opinion in writing. If the contract is ambiguous, a professional interpretation is evidence — but not necessarily dispositive. Offer to meet with owner and architect together. |
| "We'll deal with it at the end." | Politely decline: "Our practice is to document changes as they arise so there are no surprises. We'd rather have this resolved now than have an unresolved balance that creates complications at closeout." |

### 3.4 When to Stand Firm vs. When to Concede

Not every change order requires a fight. Evaluate each based on:

- **Documentation strength:** If you have a signed directive and time-stamped photos, stand firm. If it's ambiguous, negotiate.
- **Relationship value:** A long-term client relationship has real financial value. On a one-off project, recover every dollar. On a relationship that generates $1M per year in referrals, occasional concessions are a reasonable cost of relationship maintenance — but make the concession explicitly ("We're absorbing this $2,500 as a good-faith gesture; here's our documentation if you want to see the underlying cost").
- **Amount:** Fight for large items ($5,000+) every time. Don't spend $500 in PM time arguing over a $300 change order.

## 4. Change Order Documentation and Administration

### 4.1 The Change Order Log

Maintain a running change order log on every project, updated after every change event:

| CO # | Description | Date Submitted | Amount | Schedule Impact | Status | Date Approved | Approved Amount |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CO-001 | Owner-requested window type change, Rm 104-107 | Mar 14 | $4,200 | 0 days | Approved | Mar 19 | $4,200 |
| CO-002 | Unforeseen rock excavation, SW corner | Mar 22 | $18,500 | 3 days | Under review | — | — |
| CO-003 | Add gas stub-out, outdoor kitchen | Apr 5 | $2,100 | 0 days | Approved | Apr 7 | $2,100 |
| CO-004 | Electrical panel upgrade per inspector | Apr 12 | $3,800 | 2 days | Submitted | — | — |
| **TOTALS** | | | **$28,600** | **5 days** | | | **$6,300** |

The log tells you at a glance: what you've submitted, what you've been paid, and the gap between them. Review it in every OAC meeting. Owners who see you maintaining a transparent log are more likely to trust the process.

### 4.2 Constructive Change Orders — Documenting Informal Changes

A **constructive change** is work outside the original scope that was directed verbally or implicitly — without a formal change order. These are the most common source of uncompensated work.

**Documentation protocol for constructive changes:**

1. The moment you believe you're being directed to do something outside scope, send a same-day confirmation email (see Section 1.3).
2. Log it in the daily report with a "CO flag" notation.
3. Add it to the change order log as "CO-XXX — Pending."
4. Track costs to a separate cost code.
5. Submit a formal COR within 5 business days.

If the owner disputes that the work was directed (they say it was in scope), you have the confirmation email, the daily log entry, and the separate cost code as your evidence. This three-layer documentation system makes constructive change disputes winnable.

### 4.3 Change Order Numbering and Version Control

Use a clear numbering system from the start:
- **COR** (Change Order Request) — GC's request to owner; still pending.
- **CO** — Executed, signed change order.
- **PCO** — Potential Change Order — flagged before pricing is complete.

Never reuse numbers. Never overwrite a submitted COR with revised pricing — issue a revision with a suffix: CO-007-R1. Keep every version of every CO in the project file.

## 5. Value Engineering Methodology

### 5.1 What Value Engineering Is (and Is Not)

**Value engineering (VE)** is a systematic approach to finding cost reductions that maintain function and quality — often called "cost reduction without scope loss." It is NOT:
- Reducing quality to reduce cost (this is a scope reduction, not VE).
- Substituting inferior materials without the owner's knowledge.
- Finding ways to shortchange the specification.

VE is a collaborative process between GC, owner, and often the design team, where alternative methods, materials, and sequences are proposed and analyzed against the original design.

**When VE is most valuable:**
- During pre-construction, when changes are cheap (no installed work to demolish).
- When the project is over budget and the owner needs to find savings.
- When you have industry knowledge the design team lacks — a GC who knows a more cost-effective structural system or a better-performing material at lower cost is adding real value.

### 5.2 The VE Methodology — Four Steps

**Step 1 — Function Analysis.** For each system or element being analyzed, define its primary function: "This structural steel transfer beam functions to span the 30-foot opening and carry the floor above." Only then can you evaluate whether there are alternative ways to perform that function at lower cost.

**Step 2 — Creative Phase.** Generate alternative approaches without evaluating them. The goal is quantity. For each function, what are all the possible ways to achieve it?

| Element | Original Design | VE Alternatives |
|---|---|---|
| Structural transfer beam | W18×86 wide-flange steel at $45,000 installed | (A) Engineered LVL beam at $28,000; (B) Pre-stressed concrete beam at $31,000; (C) Redirected structural framing to eliminate long span |
| HVAC system | Traditional ducted forced-air | (A) Mini-split ductless; (B) In-floor radiant; (C) ERV with high-efficiency mini-splits |
| Exterior cladding | Cedar bevel siding at $62,000 | (A) James Hardie fiber cement at $38,000; (B) LP SmartSide at $42,000 |

**Step 3 — Evaluation Phase.** For each alternative, analyze:
- Does it perform the same function at the same level?
- What is the cost differential?
- What is the schedule impact?
- What is the long-term maintenance difference?
- Does it affect any other building systems?

**Step 4 — Recommendation.** Present alternatives with a clear comparison matrix. Let the owner decide. Never implement a VE alternative without owner consent — even if the alternative is objectively better.

### 5.3 VE Communication with the Owner

Present VE recommendations as a menu of options, not a single recommendation:

```
VALUE ENGINEERING OPTIONS — [Project] — [Date]

SYSTEM: Structural Transfer Beam (Grid Line B, Level 2)

ORIGINAL DESIGN: W18×86 wide-flange steel beam
Original estimated cost: $45,000 (fabrication, delivery, crane, erection)

OPTION A: Engineered LVL Composite Beam
Estimated cost: $28,000
Savings: $17,000 (38%)
Performance: Equivalent structural capacity per engineer's review
Schedule impact: None
Trade-offs: Requires structural engineer approval (2-week review period); LVL 
beam dimensions slightly larger but within the architectural profile

OPTION B: Redirect framing to eliminate long span
Estimated cost: $12,000 (framing reconfiguration; trades coordination)
Savings: $33,000 (73%)
Performance: Equivalent structural performance; eliminates transfer element risk
Schedule impact: 3-day framing adjustment
Trade-offs: Requires architect and engineer approval; affects floor plan layout 
slightly (see attached sketch)

RECOMMENDATION: Option A offers significant savings with no schedule or architectural impact.
Option B maximizes savings but requires design team approval and minor plan revision.

Owner decision required by: [Date] to maintain current schedule.
```

## 6. Design Alternatives and Trade-Off Analysis

### 6.1 Systems-Level VE — Where the Big Savings Are

The largest VE savings typically come from systems-level decisions, not from individual item substitutions. The table below identifies high-value VE opportunities on residential and commercial projects:

| System | Common Over-Design Pattern | VE Opportunity | Typical Savings |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Structural** | Over-specified steel or concrete for conservative engineering | Review with structural engineer for right-sizing; LVL alternatives to steel | 15–35% of structural budget |
| **HVAC** | Oversized ducted system; too many zones | Right-size through Manual J; consider ductless mini-splits for zones | 10–25% of HVAC budget |
| **Electrical** | Oversized main panel; excessive can lights; home automation roughed for all rooms | Right-size panel; simplify lighting; rough only where needed | 5–15% of electrical budget |
| **Exterior cladding** | Cedar, ipe, or exotic wood siding | Fiber cement, engineered wood, or synthetic alternatives | 20–45% of siding budget |
| **Roofing** | High-profile concrete or clay tile** | Premium composition shingles or metal panel | 20–40% of roofing budget |
| **Windows** | Premium European windows on budget-constrained project | Right-size to Milgard or equivalent domestic window at similar energy performance | 25–50% of window budget |
| **Flatwork and concrete** | Over-specified slab thickness | Review with structural or geotechnical engineer; rebar spacing optimization | 8–15% of concrete budget |
| **Cabinetry** | Custom shop cabinetry through architect | Semi-custom or stock cabinet brands with custom details | 30–60% of cabinet budget |

### 6.2 The Trade-Off Analysis Framework

Not every VE option is obviously better. Use a trade-off matrix when the decision involves conflicting factors:

| Factor | Weight | Option A (Original) | Option B (VE) | Option C (VE) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| First cost | 40% | $45,000 | $28,000 | $12,000 |
| Long-term maintenance | 20% | Low | Low | Low |
| Performance/function | 25% | ✓ Full | ✓ Full | ✓ Full |
| Schedule risk | 10% | Low | Low | Medium |
| Aesthetic impact | 5% | None | None | Minor |
| **Weighted score** | | 3.2 | 4.1 | 3.8 |

Present the matrix to the owner; let them adjust the weights based on their priorities. The GC's role is to provide the data; the owner makes the value judgment.

## 7. Scope Creep Prevention Strategies

### 7.1 What Scope Creep Costs

Scope creep is the accumulation of small, uncompensated additions to the project — each one too small to fight over, but collectively significant. A $500,000 residential project that experiences 15% scope creep without corresponding change orders has delivered $75,000 in free work. At a 15% net margin, that's half a year's profit on one job.

Scope creep patterns on residential projects:
- "Can you just ..." additions (outlet here, switch there, door closer, shelf in closet).
- Material upgrades selected during construction at no additional cost (verbally agreed).
- Rework ordered by owner that wasn't a defect but a preference change.
- Additional site visits, meetings, and coordination beyond the contracted scope.

### 7.2 The Written Scope Baseline — Your Primary Defense

The most effective scope creep prevention tool is a detailed, written scope of work prepared at contract signing. The more specific your baseline, the easier it is to identify and charge for deviations.

**Scope document structure:**

1. **Inclusions** — explicitly what the contract includes (with references to specific drawing sheets and specification sections).
2. **Exclusions** — explicitly what the contract does NOT include ("This contract excludes: exterior site lighting, landscaping, window treatments, appliances not specifically listed in Section [X], and low-voltage systems.").
3. **Allowances** — items where the owner has not yet made a selection; include an allowance amount that substitutes for the actual selection (e.g., "Tile allowance: $8.00/SF installed. Selections above this allowance require a change order.").
4. **Assumptions** — conditions you've assumed to be true; if they're not, a CO is warranted (e.g., "Existing electrical service assumed adequate for new load additions; upgrade not included.").

Walk the owner through this document line by line at contract signing. Have them initial each page. This single practice eliminates the majority of scope disputes.

### 7.3 The Change Order Culture

Establish a change order culture through consistent behavior:

- **Every time** a change arises, no matter how small, issue a CO. A $200 CO trains the owner that changes have costs. If you skip the $200 CO, you're training the owner that changes are free — and then the $5,000 CO feels like a shock.
- **Never perform work and ask for money later.** This is the GC's version of "just proceed and we'll sort it out" — it never ends well.
- **Positive framing:** "Here's the CO for the outlet you added yesterday — $185. Ready for your signature." Routine, professional, frictionless.

### 7.4 Design Changes vs. Defect Corrections

A critical distinction that protects your margin:

**Design change:** The owner changes their mind after the original work was correctly performed. Always a change order.

**Defect correction:** The work was not performed correctly to the specification. Never a change order — it's a warranty or quality obligation.

The trap: owners sometimes describe design changes as defects ("I don't like how that turned out; it's not what I expected"). The GC must compare the installed work to the contract specification, not to the owner's mental image. If the work matches the spec, the owner's dissatisfaction is a design change — and a CO is appropriate.

### 8.2 Change Order Pricing Quick Reference

**Typical overhead and profit rates for COs:**

| Project Type | Overhead | Profit | Combined |
|---|---|---|---|
| Residential (custom homes, remodel) | 10–15% | 10–15% | 20–30% |
| Light commercial | 10% | 10% | 20% |
| Public works (per contract) | Per contract spec; often 10% + 5% | — | — |
| Industrial | 10–15% | 10–12% | 20–27% |

**Note:** Many public works prime contracts specify maximum OH&P rates on change orders (often 10% + 5% = 15% total). Know your contract limits before pricing.

**Subcontractor markup on sub-to-GC billing on changes:**
- Sub's own CO should include sub's OH&P (10–15%).
- GC then adds 5–10% markup on sub's cost for managing the sub's change work.

### 8.3 CO Admin Quick Reference — Timelines

| Action | Target Timeline |
|---|---|
| Issue PCO (flag the event) | Same day as triggering event |
| Send written notice to owner | Within contract notice period (7 days if not specified) |
| Submit COR with pricing | Within 5 business days of notice |
| Owner response/approval | Per contract (typically 7–14 days) |
| Perform changed work | Only after signed CO in hand (or confirm proceed per §1.3) |
| Submit payment application including approved CO | Next regular pay application cycle |
| Maximum unsigned CO backlog | Zero — do not let unsigned COs age over 30 days |
