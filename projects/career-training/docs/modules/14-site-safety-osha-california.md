# Site Safety and Cal/OSHA Compliance for the Residential General Contractor

> **Why this matters.** Construction is roughly 6% of the U.S. workforce but accounts for ~20% of worker fatalities. The "Focus Four" — falls, electrocutions, struck-by, and caught-in/between — kill the overwhelming majority. On California residential sites you add heat illness, silica, lead, and asbestos to that list. Every fatality on a residential job site is preventable with a real safety program. This module gives you the program.

> **California vs. Federal.** California is a state-plan OSHA state. Cal/OSHA (the Division of Occupational Safety and Health, DOSH) enforces Title 8 of the California Code of Regulations (T8 CCR). California's standards are often *stricter* than federal 29 CFR 1926 — never less strict. When in doubt, follow the California rule.

> **2025 change you must know.** Effective **July 1, 2025**, California reduced its residential fall-protection trigger height from 15 feet (framing) and 7.5 feet (general) down to **6 feet**, matching federal OSHA. T8 §§1671.1, 1716.2, 1730, and 1731 of the Construction Safety Orders were amended. If your toolbox talks still reference "15 feet for framing," they are out of date. ([CalChamber](https://calchamberalert.com/2025/05/09/residential-construction-fall-protection-trigger-height-drops-to-6-feet/))

## 1. Safety Program Fundamentals — The IIPP

### 1.1 The Single Most Important Rule: §3203

Title 8 §3203 requires **every California employer** — including one-person LLCs with a single hire — to establish, implement, and maintain a written **Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP)**. There is no size exemption. ([DIR §3203](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/3203.html))

If Cal/OSHA shows up tomorrow and asks "Where is your IIPP?" and you cannot produce a written program, that is a **serious** citation by itself, separate from any underlying hazard. It is also the violation Cal/OSHA finds most often.

### 1.2 The Eight Required IIPP Elements

| # | Element | What it means in practice |
|---|---------|---------------------------|
| 1 | **Responsibility** | Name the person (by name or title) with authority and accountability for the program. For a small GC, this is usually the owner. Put it in writing. |
| 2 | **Compliance** | A system to ensure employees actually follow safe work rules — discipline progression, recognition, conditions of continued employment. |
| 3 | **Communication** | Two-way: you communicate hazards/training to workers; workers can report hazards without fear of reprisal. Non-English workers must get info in their language. |
| 4 | **Hazard Identification** | Scheduled, periodic inspections; new-process inspections; whenever a new hazard appears. Document what you found and when. |
| 5 | **Accident/Exposure Investigation** | Investigate injuries, illnesses, *and near-misses*. Document cause, contributing factors, corrective action. |
| 6 | **Hazard Correction** | Imminent hazards → stop work immediately. Lesser hazards → documented timeline, severity-driven. |
| 7 | **Training** | At hire, at new assignment, when a new hazard is introduced, when you learn an employee doesn't understand. Document who, when, what. |
| 8 | **Recordkeeping** | Inspections and training kept for at least 1 year (3 years recommended). Employees and their reps get IIPP records within **5 business days** of a written request (2017 amendment). |

A working IIPP is roughly 15–40 pages for a small residential GC, plus appendices (forms, training rosters, inspection checklists). It is not a generic binder you bought online — it must reflect *your* sites, *your* hazards, *your* people, *your* names.

### 1.3 Where to Get a Compliant Template

- **Cal/OSHA Model IIPP** — free, the gold standard. Pamphlet "Developing Your Workplace Injury & Illness Prevention Program" with model written program. ([DIR PDF](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dosh/dosh_publications/iipp.pdf))
- **eTools and "S-Series" model programs** at dir.ca.gov/dosh.
- **Industry templates** from the California Building Industry Association, NAHB, or your workers' comp carrier (the carrier's safety consultant is free — use them).

Customize it. Do not adopt verbatim. Inspectors recognize the model program; if every detail is generic, they assume you don't run the program.

### 1.4 Hazard Communication (HazCom) — T8 §5194

GHS-aligned. You need:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Written HazCom program** | Often folded into your IIPP. Lists chemicals on-site, where SDSs live, who maintains. |
| **Chemical inventory** | Every product with a hazardous chemical — paint, solvent, adhesive, gasoline, propane, sealer, treated lumber preservative residues. |
| **Safety Data Sheets (SDS)** | 16-section GHS format. Must be accessible to every worker every shift — on site (binder in the trailer) or via phone/tablet. |
| **Container labeling** | Primary containers keep manufacturer labels. Secondary containers (a job-site sprayer filled from a 5-gal pail) must be labeled with product identifier and hazard pictograms. |
| **Training** | Before exposure; whenever a new chemical is introduced; topic-specific (not generic). Document. |
| **Right-to-Know** | Workers, their physician, and union/representative have right to SDSs. Refusal to provide is a separate violation. |

### 1.5 Cal/OSHA 300 Log — Recordkeeping (T8 §14300)

Most construction employers with **more than 10 employees** at any time in the previous calendar year must maintain:

- **Cal/OSHA Form 300** — log of work-related injuries and illnesses (running).
- **Cal/OSHA Form 301** (or equivalent) — incident report for each entry.
- **Cal/OSHA Form 300A** — annual summary.

**Recordable** means an injury that resulted in any of: death, days away from work, restricted duty or job transfer, medical treatment beyond first aid, loss of consciousness, or significant diagnosis. ([T8 §14300.7](https://www.dir.ca.gov/t8/14300_7.html))

**First aid does not require recording.** Cleaning a cut, applying a bandage, removing splinters, eye flushing — all first aid. Stitches, prescription meds (more than a single dose), and physical therapy are *not* first aid and are recordable.

**Posting:** Form 300A must be posted in a visible location at each establishment from **February 1 through April 30** each year, even if you had zero injuries.

**Electronic submission:** Construction employers with **20–249 employees** must electronically submit Form 300A annually by **March 2** through the federal ITA portal. Employers with **250 or more** must also submit Forms 300 and 301. Most small residential GCs are exempt — but if you have growth years that cross 20 employees, get on the portal.

Keep all three forms for **5 years** after the calendar year they cover.

### 1.6 Serious-Injury / Fatality Reporting — T8 §342

You must **immediately** (and not more than **8 hours** after you knew or should have known) report any of the following to Cal/OSHA: ([DIR §342](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/342.html))

- **Fatality** (work-connected)
- **Serious injury or illness**, meaning:
  - **Inpatient hospitalization** for other than medical observation or diagnostic testing (regardless of duration), OR
  - **Amputation** (any), OR
  - **Loss of an eye**, OR
  - Any **serious degree of permanent disfigurement**

How to report:
1. Call the **nearest Cal/OSHA district office** (find list at dir.ca.gov/dosh/districtoffices.htm), or
2. Use the **online reporting form** at dir.ca.gov/dosh/report-accident-or-injury.html, or
3. Call **1-800-963-9424** (24/7 reporting line)

**Do not move equipment, alter the scene, or clean up** unless necessary to prevent further injury or to render aid. Cal/OSHA almost always inspects after a serious-injury report. Preserve the scene.

Note: As of 2020, AB 1804 expanded the definition (added "any inpatient hospitalization" regardless of duration; replaced "loss of a member" with "amputation"; added "loss of an eye"). The old 24-hour window is gone — it's 8 hours, no exceptions for nights/weekends. ([CDF Labor Law](https://www.cdflaborlaw.com/blog/governor-signs-new-law-expanding-definition-of-serious-injury-and-changing-reporting-requirements-to-cal-osha-labor-code-section-6302))

## 2. Fall Protection — The #1 Killer

Falls kill more construction workers than any other hazard. On residential sites the typical scenarios are: roofers sliding off eaves; framers stepping off second-floor edges; carpenters falling through floor or skylight openings; ladder users overreaching; and unsecured scaffolds collapsing.

### 2.1 Trigger Heights — Federal vs. California

| Standard | Construction trigger | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Federal OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501 | **6 ft** | Above a lower level. |
| Cal/OSHA T8 §1670 (general) | **7.5 ft** | "Unprotected sides, edges, walking/working surfaces." Still in force for non-residential. |
| Cal/OSHA T8 §1716.2 (residential, pre-7/1/2025) | 15 ft framing / 20 ft roofing | **Superseded.** |
| **Cal/OSHA residential (effective 7/1/2025)** | **6 ft** | Aligns with federal. Applies to framing, roofing, sheathing, and most residential leading-edge work. |

The 7.5-ft general rule still appears in many older Cal/OSHA references. For residential work today, **assume 6 ft as your trigger** and you will not be wrong. ([CalChamber](https://calchamberalert.com/2025/05/09/residential-construction-fall-protection-trigger-height-drops-to-6-feet/))

### 2.2 Acceptable Fall-Protection Methods

In rough priority (preference): elimination > guardrails > nets > PFAS > controlled access zones.

| Method | When to use | Key specs |
|---|---|---|
| **Guardrails** | Edges, openings, scaffolds, elevated platforms | Top rail **42 inches ± 3"** (39"–45"); mid-rail roughly mid-height; toeboard 3.5" min if material can fall. Top rail withstands **200 lbf** downward/outward; mid-rail **150 lbf**. |
| **Personal Fall Arrest System (PFAS)** | Roof, leading edge, where guardrails are infeasible | Full-body harness (no body belts), shock-absorbing lanyard or self-retracting lifeline (SRL), anchor rated **5,000 lbf** per person *or* 2× max arresting force engineered. |
| **Safety nets** | Bridges, large structures (rare residential) | Within 30 ft below work; drop-tested. |
| **Hole covers** | Floor openings, skylights | Marked "HOLE" or "COVER"; secured against displacement; support **2× max load**. |
| **Warning lines + safety monitor** | Low-slope roof work only (≤ 4:12) under federal rule; Cal/OSHA more restrictive | Generally avoid — easy to cite. |
| **Controlled Access Zone (CAZ)** | Limited use, e.g. leading edge with monitor | Documentation-heavy; not a default solution. |

### 2.3 PFAS — Components and Math

**The four elements (ABCD):** **A**nchor → **B**ody wear (harness) → **C**onnector (lanyard/SRL) → **D**eceleration device (shock absorber, integrated).

**Free-fall distance limits:** 6 ft maximum with shock-absorbing lanyard. SRL can reduce to ~2 ft.

**Fall clearance calculation** (the math that saves the worker from hitting the deck):

> Anchor height – (lanyard length + deceleration distance + worker height below D-ring + safety factor) = available clearance

A 6-ft lanyard + 3.5-ft deceleration + 5-ft worker + 3-ft safety factor = **~17.5 ft** required below anchor. That's why anchoring at your feet on a low roof can still result in striking the ground. SRLs solve this on lower elevations.

**Anchor strength:**
- **5,000 lbf** per attached worker (default), OR
- Engineered system maintaining **safety factor of 2** over max arresting force.

**Inspect before each use.** Heat, UV, acids (battery, masonry cleaner), abrasion, cuts, or any deployed shock pack → tag out and destroy. A harness involved in a fall is *retired forever*, even if it looks fine.

### 2.4 Roof Work — Residential Specifics

| Roof condition | Required protection |
|---|---|
| Pitch ≤ 4:12 (low slope) | Guardrail at eave, PFAS, or warning line + safety monitor (narrowly applied). |
| 4:12 < Pitch ≤ 8:12 | PFAS or slide guards (slide guards alone are increasingly disfavored; PFAS is the safe answer). |
| Pitch > 8:12 (steep) | PFAS with roof anchors, properly installed before work begins. |
| Leading edge / no edge | PFAS mandatory. Pre-plan anchorage. |
| Skylights / openings | Cover or guardrail. Skylights are routinely fatal — covers must be rated, marked, and screwed down. |

**Common solutions for residential roofers:**
- Ridge anchors installed at the peak (screwed into rafters per manufacturer instructions — not into sheathing alone).
- SRLs run from ridge to provide low fall distance.
- Pre-installed reusable roof brackets and toe-boards on steep work.
- Pre-job hazard plan in writing identifying anchor points and rescue plan (mandatory: you must be able to retrieve a fallen worker within ~6 minutes to prevent suspension trauma).

### 2.5 Framing Stage — Walking/Working Surfaces

The deadliest stage of residential construction. Specific Cal/OSHA expectations:

- **Sheath the floor first**, then frame walls. Walking on joists is fine *legally* (if under 6 ft over the next lower level), but unsafe and unproductive — sheath.
- **Cover floor openings immediately** as they are created — stair openings, plumbing chases, HVAC chases.
- **Stair access** to upper floors as soon as practicable; ladders ok temporarily but become a bottleneck.
- **Wall opening protection** at 6 ft — temporary 2x4 guard rails or PFAS.
- **Erect wall guardrails** before sheathing exterior — once decked, rails are easier.

### 2.6 Ladders (T8 §1675–1676)

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Angle** | **4:1 rule** — 1 ft horizontal for every 4 ft of working length. Roughly 75°. Too steep = tip back; too shallow = base kick. |
| **Extension above landing** | **3 ft minimum** above the landing/eave when used to access a roof or platform. |
| **Secure the top** | Tie off, hold, or use ladder hooks/stand-offs. Most ladder falls happen at top or bottom during transition. |
| **Single point of contact** | At least one hand or appropriate body contact when climbing. Don't carry tools/material up in your hand — use a hoist or tool belt. |
| **Inspect daily** | Bent rails, cracked rungs, missing feet, paint (hides defects — never paint a ladder). |
| **Rating** | Type IA (300 lb) or Type IAA (375 lb) for construction. Type I (250 lb) household ladders are not job-site rated. |
| **Metal ladders + electricity** | Fiberglass within 10 ft of energized lines. |
| **Stepladders** | Never stand on top step or top cap. Lock spreaders fully. |

### 2.7 Scaffolding (T8 §1635 et seq.)

| Topic | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Erection by competent person** | Required. The "competent person" is one who can identify hazards and has authority to correct them. |
| **Platform width** | **18 inches minimum**; full planking edge-to-edge; no more than 1" gap. |
| **Toe boards** | 3.5" minimum where workers below or material could fall. |
| **Guardrails** | At **10 ft** and above for supported scaffolds. Top rail, mid-rail. |
| **Access** | Built-in ladder, attached ladder, integral end-frame steps. Climbing cross-braces prohibited. |
| **Daily inspection** | By competent person before each shift. Document. |
| **Capacity** | 4× intended load. |
| **Tagging** | Green = safe; Yellow = caution (e.g. partial use); Red = do not use. |
| **Mobile scaffolds** | Wheels locked when occupied. Don't ride a scaffold being moved unless engineered for it. |

## 3. Excavation and Trenching

Cal/OSHA: **T8 §1539–1541** (general), **§1541.1** (protective systems). Federal counterpart: 29 CFR 1926 Subpart P. ([DIR §1541](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/1541.html))

Trench collapse is uniquely lethal: a cubic yard of soil weighs ~2,700 lb. There is no surviving a 5-ft-deep collapse without protective systems.

### 3.1 Competent Person — Required

Before any worker enters an excavation **4 ft or deeper**, a **competent person** must:

- Classify soil (see below).
- Select a protective system.
- Inspect the excavation daily before each shift, after rain, and after any event that could increase hazard (vehicle near edge, vibration, water intrusion).
- Have authority to remove workers and suspend work.

The competent person must be trained — there's no minimum hours required by regulation, but a 24-hour OSHA Excavation Competent Person course is the de facto standard.

### 3.2 Permits — California Health & Safety Code §6500

A **Cal/OSHA permit** is required *before* work begins for any excavation **5 ft or deeper into which a person is required to descend**. Permit obtained from the local Cal/OSHA district office. This is separate from your building/grading permit and from USA underground-utility notification. Trench-cave fatalities still happen on jobs that lacked this permit — and after the fact, prosecutors notice.

### 3.3 Soil Classification

| Type | Description | Field tests |
|---|---|---|
| **Stable Rock** | Solid mineral, vertical sides stable | Rare in residential. |
| **Type A** | Cohesive, unconfined compressive strength ≥ 1.5 tsf (clay, silty clay, sandy clay). | Thumb penetration with great difficulty; rolls into 1/8" ribbon without crumbling; plastic. |
| **Type B** | Cohesive but lower strength (0.5–1.5 tsf), or angular gravel, or previously disturbed Type A. | Thumb penetrates with effort. Crumbly when rolled. |
| **Type C** | Granular (sand, gravel, loamy sand), submerged soil, soil with water seeping. *Default class on most California jobs* until proven otherwise. | Thumb penetrates easily; runs through fingers; will not hold a ribbon. |

**Cardinal rule:** If conditions change (rain, water table, vibration, freezing/thawing, prior disturbance), **reclassify**. Soil from a previously open trench, regardless of original classification, is **Type C** when re-excavated.

Field test methods are spelled out in T8 §1541.1 **Appendix A**. ([Appendix A](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/1541_1a.html))

### 3.4 Sloping — Maximum Allowable Slopes (< 20 ft deep)

| Soil | Slope (H:V) | Angle |
|---|---|---|
| Stable Rock | Vertical | 90° |
| Type A | 3/4 : 1 | 53° |
| Type B | 1 : 1 | 45° |
| Type C | **1.5 : 1** | 34° |

For excavations **deeper than 20 ft**, a **registered professional engineer** must design the protective system. Don't eyeball it.

### 3.5 Shoring and Shielding

If sloping isn't practical (space, utilities, structures nearby), use:

- **Hydraulic shoring** — aluminum cylinders + waler/post plywood. Tabulated data manufacturer-supplied for soil type. Lightweight, fast, single-worker installable.
- **Trench shields ("trench boxes")** — protect workers inside the box even in a collapse. Box must extend ≥ 18" above the trench top (or sloped above the top). Workers must not be outside the box at trench bottom level.
- **Timber shoring** — slow, rarely used residentially.

Critical: **never enter an unprotected trench to "just grab one thing."** Most fatalities are the worker who went in for 30 seconds.

### 3.6 Atmospheric Testing

Required when excavation is **deeper than 4 ft** *and* an oxygen-deficient (≤19.5%) or toxic atmosphere could reasonably exist (sewer line, near a landfill, near gasoline tank, near manure — common on residential remodels of older properties). Test before entry; ventilate or use respiratory protection as needed.

### 3.7 Access/Egress

In excavations **4 ft or deeper**, a stairway, ladder, ramp, or other safe means must be provided so no worker is more than **25 lateral feet** from a means of egress.

### 3.8 Spoil Pile, Vehicles, and Surcharge Loads

- Spoil pile / equipment / material: **minimum 2 ft from the edge** of the excavation (T8 §1541(j)).
- Vehicles operating near the edge: stop blocks, awareness training, separation distance.
- Surcharge load reduces slope safety dramatically — pile up high or close, and you collapse the wall.

### 3.9 Underground Utilities — Call Before You Dig

**California Underground Service Alert (USA North 811 / Dig Alert)** must be called at least **2 working days** before excavation. Hand-dig the "tolerance zone" — 24 inches each side of marked utility — before mechanical excavation in that zone. Striking an unmarked gas line is criminal exposure in California (Penal Code §369i, plus civil).

## 4. Struck-By and Caught-In/Between Hazards

Second and third in the Focus Four. On residential sites: vehicle traffic on the curb, falling tools, swinging loads, equipment rollovers, framing snags, table-saw and circular-saw kickbacks.

### 4.1 Vehicle and Equipment Traffic

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Internal traffic control plan** | For each site: where deliveries enter, where parked, where pedestrians walk, blind spots. |
| **High-vis apparel** | **ANSI/ISEA 107 Class 2** minimum near vehicle traffic; Class 3 when speeds > 50 mph or low-light. |
| **Flaggers** | When traffic control on a public road, flagger must be certified (typically ATSSA or equivalent). Flagger PPE: hard hat, Class 2 (Class 3 in low light), STOP/SLOW paddle. |
| **Spotter for backing** | Required whenever operator's view is obstructed; positive communication; spotter never directly behind. |
| **Equipment audible alarms** | Backup alarm working before each shift. |
| **Pedestrian separation** | Physical barrier where practical. |

### 4.2 Overhead Power Lines — T8 §2946

Most common electrocution mechanism on residential sites: framing crew lifting a wall under an overhead line, or a delivery truck raising a bed into a service drop.

| Line voltage | Minimum approach distance (any equipment or person/conductor) |
|---|---|
| ≤ 50 kV | **10 ft** |
| 50–200 kV | 15 ft |
| 200–350 kV | 20 ft |
| 350–500 kV | 25 ft |
| > 500 kV | 35 ft |

**Service drops to a single-family house are commonly 240V** — still requires 10-ft clearance for tools, ladders, and materials. **De-energize, insulate (line covers from utility), or move the line** before work that cannot maintain clearance. PG&E and SCE provide temporary line covers free or for nominal cost — call them.

Aluminum ladders, scaffolding, rebar, gutters, and even garden hoses can carry fatal current. Use fiberglass ladders within 10 ft of any line.

### 4.3 Excavation Collapse — Already covered §3.

### 4.4 Power Tool and Equipment Hazards

- **Guards in place** — never remove blade guards, riving knives, anti-kickback pawls. Plunge cuts on a circular saw are the legitimate exception, but the guard must spring back the instant the cut completes.
- **Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)** — T8 §3314. For service, maintenance, or guard removal, isolate the energy source (electrical disconnect, pneumatic supply, hydraulic), apply a lock, tag it, and verify zero energy. Each worker affixes their own lock.
- **Pre-shift inspection** — handles, cords, GFCI test, guards, switches, sharp blades.

### 4.5 Nail Gun Triggers

Two trigger types:

| Trigger | Mechanism | Risk profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Contact trigger** ("bump-fire") | Tip and trigger both must be depressed; either order. Bump-firing is fast and dangerous. | Highest injury rate. Cal/OSHA strongly disfavors for inexperienced workers. |
| **Sequential trigger** | Tip must be depressed *first*, then trigger. One nail per pull. | Far lower injury rate. NIOSH and Cal/OSHA recommend sequential triggers as the primary, especially for trim/finish/inexperienced crews. |

NIOSH 2011-125 and the 2019 update both recommend sequential-only triggers. Cal/OSHA does not currently outright ban contact-trip guns but cites under §3203 (IIPP) and §1599 if untrained workers are operating them. **Practical policy:** sequential triggers required, no exceptions, for trim and finish; contact-trip permitted only on production sheathing/framing by trained personnel, never on overhead work or work where the user cannot see the tip clearly.

### 4.6 Cranes and Rigging (residential adjacency)

Residential GCs occasionally encounter cranes — truss sets, modular drops, steel pickup. Cal/OSHA T8 §§4885–5050 govern.

| Topic | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Crane operator certification** | National Commission for the Certification of Crane Operators (NCCCO) or equivalent. |
| **Signal person** | Qualified, single signaler at a time, standard hand/voice signals. |
| **Rigger** | Qualified to select slings/hitches. Tag, inspect each lift. |
| **Pre-lift plan** | Load chart, ground bearing, swing radius, overhead lines. |
| **Tag lines** | Required to control load swing. |
| **No personnel under suspended load** | Period. |
| **10-ft rule for cranes near power lines** | Applies. Spotter if any chance of encroachment. |

## 5. Electrical Safety

Electrocutions are the third leg of the Focus Four. On residential sites the lethal scenarios are: contact with overhead service drop while framing/roofing; cut/damaged extension cord with no GFCI; worker tying back drywall while the panel was still hot; portable generator improperly grounded.

### 5.1 GFCI on Construction Sites — T8 §2405.4

All **120 V, single-phase, 15- and 20-amp receptacle outlets** on construction sites — that are not part of the building's permanent wiring — must have **GFCI protection** for personnel. ([T8 §2405.4](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/2405_4.html))

This means:
- Every cord, tool, and outlet you use **before** the panel is energized and inspected gets GFCI.
- Use a **GFCI-protected spider box**, or GFCI cord set ("Shock-Shield" type), or GFCI receptacle in the temp pole.
- Once permanent power is energized and accepted, those receptacles fall under permanent NEC rules (still mostly GFCI in residential).

### 5.2 Assured Equipment Grounding Conductor Program (AEGCP) — Alternative

Cal/OSHA allows AEGCP as an alternative to GFCI. Requirements:

- Written program on-site.
- Each cord, plug, receptacle, and tool tested for continuity **before first use, after repair, and every 3 months** (color-coded tags by quarter).
- Damaged or failing equipment removed from service.
- Designated competent person to perform/oversee tests.

In practice, almost nobody uses AEGCP — GFCIs are cheaper, faster, more foolproof. Use GFCIs unless you have a specific high-leakage-current tool that nuisance-trips and you can engineer an AEGCP exception.

### 5.3 Extension Cords

| Spec | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Hard-service or junior-hard-service (e.g., type **S, SO, ST, STO, SJ, SJO, SJT, SJTO**). Inside-rated cords (SPT, NM) prohibited outdoors. |
| **Outdoor / wet** | **W** in the type suffix (e.g., SJTW, SOOW). |
| **Gauge for length** | 12 AWG/3-conductor is the residential workhorse. For 50+ ft runs with 15-A loads, step up to 10 AWG. |
| **Inspection** | Daily, before use. Cuts, nicks, exposed conductors, melted insulation, missing ground pin → tag out and destroy. |
| **No splices** | A spliced cord is a defective cord. Replace, don't tape. |
| **Strain relief** | At plugs and tool ends; never pull a cord by its conductor. |
| **Wet location** | All connections elevated; never lying in water; cord protectors over walkways. |

### 5.4 Overhead Lines During Framing & Roofing — Already covered §4.2.

This is where you kill people if you forget. Walk the site mentally **before** the first wall raises. Use a 10-ft pole and a spotter if you need to verify clearance. If the service drop is in the way, get the utility to drop and re-drop the line — cheaper than a funeral.

### 5.5 Generators and Temporary Power

| Issue | Best practice |
|---|---|
| **Carbon monoxide** | Never operate a generator inside a structure, garage, or near open windows. CO is colorless and kills fast. Minimum 20 ft from any opening; preferably outdoors and downwind. |
| **Grounding** | Most modern portable generators are "separately derived systems" with bonded neutral; follow the manufacturer's grounding label. If feeding a structure, follow NEC 250 — usually a ground rod. |
| **GFCI receptacles on the generator** | Required on the unit's 120 V outlets per OSHA, even at the source. |
| **Spider box** | Distribution box with GFCI on every receptacle, twist-lock feeders. Provides safe daisy-chain. |
| **Refueling** | Engine off, cool, no smoking. Funnel. Spill cleanup. |
| **Backfeed** | When connecting a generator to a house panel, a transfer switch is required to prevent backfeeding the utility (lineman killer). Plug-suicide-cord setups are illegal. |

### 5.6 Working on Energized Equipment

Cal/OSHA T8 §§2940 (electrical safety orders) requires **de-energization** before work unless de-energizing introduces additional hazards or is infeasible. On residential, **de-energize, lock out, verify, and only then work**. Use a non-contact voltage tester *and* a contact meter. Don't trust labels.

For any work above 50 V, the qualified person must wear appropriate **arc-rated PPE** if energized work is performed — but the residential answer is almost always "turn it off."

## 6. Respiratory and Chemical Hazards

### 6.1 Lead — Pre-1978 Homes

Two overlapping regimes:

| Rule | Authority | Triggers |
|---|---|---|
| **EPA RRP Rule (40 CFR 745)** | Federal EPA | Renovation, repair, painting of **pre-1978 housing or child-occupied facility** that disturbs more than **6 sq ft interior / 20 sq ft exterior / replaces windows**. Firm must be EPA-certified; lead supervisor must be a **Certified Renovator**. |
| **Cal/OSHA Lead in Construction T8 §1532.1** | Cal/OSHA | Worker-protection standard. Any construction work that may expose workers to lead. ([T8 §1532.1](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/1532_1.html)) |
| **CDPH Lead-Related Construction (Title 17 CCR §35001+)** | CA Dept of Public Health | Certification of supervisors and workers performing lead-related construction work in pre-1978 buildings. Separate from RRP. |

**Practical workflow for pre-1978 work:**

1. Get **EPA RRP firm certification** ($300, 5-yr). Anyone supervising must be a **Certified Renovator** (8-hr class).
2. Test or assume lead-based paint. **Lead Disclosure form** required to homeowner before work (separate from agent-of-record disclosure at purchase).
3. Use **lead-safe work practices**: containment (poly sheeting), no open-flame burning/torching, no power sanding without HEPA, no high-pressure water blasting, daily HEPA vacuuming, wet methods.
4. Cal/OSHA **§1532.1** worker protections:
   - **Initial exposure assessment** (presume above 50 µg/m³ Action Level for the trigger tasks until measured otherwise).
   - **Trigger tasks** (presumed high exposure): grinding, sanding, abrasive blasting, torch cutting, demolition. Default to powered air-purifying respirator (PAPR) or supplied air.
   - **Hygiene facilities** — change room, hand-wash, no eating in work area.
   - **Medical surveillance** for workers above Action Level for 30 or more days/year. Blood-lead testing.
   - **CDPH worker certification** if exposure-prone work.
5. **Cleanup verification** — visual + cleaning verification card / wipe sample.
6. **Recordkeeping** — exposure data 30 yrs; medical 30 yrs.

For an RGC who occasionally remodels older homes, **subcontract lead-disturbing work to a certified specialty contractor** unless you've invested in firm certification, training, and equipment.

### 6.2 Asbestos — T8 §1529

| Trigger | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Asbestos-containing materials (ACM)** | Vinyl floor (especially 9x9), popcorn ceiling, pipe insulation, plaster, mastic, joint compound, transite siding/pipe, roofing felt. Common in pre-1980 California construction. |
| **Pre-survey** | Pre-1981 buildings: assume ACM, sample, or use a Certified Asbestos Consultant (CAC) survey. |
| **DOSH Asbestos Contractor Registration** | Required to perform asbestos-related work ≥ 100 sq ft of ACM with > 0.1% asbestos. Annual registration with the **Asbestos Contractor Registration Unit (ACRU)**. ([DIR ACRU](https://www.dir.ca.gov/dosh/acru/acruhome.htm)) |
| **CSLB ASB classification** | C-22 Asbestos Abatement Contractor's license required for abatement (separate from B-General). |
| **Notification** | Notify the nearest Cal/OSHA district office **24 hours** before starting work at a temporary worksite involving ACM. |
| **AQMD notification** | South Coast AQMD (Rule 1403) and other local air districts have separate **10-working-day** notifications for demolition or renovation involving regulated ACM. |
| **Worker training** | Class I (TSI/surfacing), Class II (resilient floor, roofing, drywall), Class III (small repair), Class IV (custodial). Each tier has different training hour and respirator requirements. |
| **Air monitoring** | Personal sampling per shift. Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL): 0.1 f/cc 8-hr TWA; Excursion limit 1.0 f/cc 30-min. |
| **Worker medical surveillance** | Pre-placement, annual. |

For a residential GC: **never disturb suspected ACM without testing**. If positive, hire a licensed abatement contractor. Do not allow your framers, demo workers, or roofers to chip into popcorn ceilings, sand vinyl mastic, or saw transite pipe.

### 6.3 Silica — T8 §1532.3

Crystalline silica from cutting, grinding, drilling concrete, masonry, stone, tile, or stucco. Silicosis is irreversible and increasingly common because high-RPM tools generate dust faster than humans expel it. ([T8 §1532.3](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/1532_3.html))

**PEL:** 50 µg/m³ as 8-hr TWA. **Action level:** 25 µg/m³.

You comply by either:

(a) **Following Table 1** of §1532.3 — specified engineering controls and respiratory protection for each listed task, OR
(b) **Performance option** — measure exposures, implement controls, document below PEL.

**Table 1 (most residential)**: use these and you don't have to monitor.

| Task | Engineering control | Respirator |
|---|---|---|
| **Stationary masonry saw** (e.g., wet tile saw) | Integrated water delivery system continuously to blade | None required |
| **Handheld power saw** (concrete, masonry) outdoors | Tool with integrated water-delivery, OR HEPA-filtered dust collection with hood | None ≤ 4 hr/shift outdoors; APF 10 (half-mask) > 4 hr |
| **Handheld power saw** indoors | Water OR HEPA shroud + vacuum (98%+ collection) | APF 10 required when used > 4 hr/shift |
| **Walk-behind concrete saw** | Continuous water | None outdoors |
| **Drilling (concrete) — handheld** | HEPA-shrouded drill with integrated dust collection | None usually |
| **Jackhammers / impact** | Water OR HEPA dust collection | APF 10 (half-mask) — sometimes APF 25 indoors |
| **Handheld grinder for mortar removal (tuckpointing)** | Shroud + HEPA vacuum | APF 25 (PAPR or full-face) |
| **Handheld grinder for other uses** | Shroud + HEPA vacuum, OR water | APF 10 indoors > 4 hr |
| **Drywall sanding / cleanup of silica dust** | HEPA vacuum, wet sweeping | Cannot use compressed air or dry sweeping for silica dust |

**Other §1532.3 requirements regardless of Table 1 use:**

- **Written Exposure Control Plan** (silica-specific; separate from IIPP or incorporated).
- **Competent person** designated to make changes and inspections.
- **Medical surveillance** for workers required to use respirators ≥ 30 days/year (chest X-ray, spirometry, baseline + every 3 years).
- **Hazard communication / training** annual.
- **Recordkeeping** — exposure 30 yrs, medical for duration of employment + 30 yrs.

### 6.4 Wood Dust

Hardwood dust is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen. T8 §5155 lists nuisance dust PELs (15 mg/m³ total / 5 mg/m³ respirable inhalable as default). Practical controls:

- HEPA vacuum on sanders, miter saws, table saws.
- Local exhaust on stationary tools.
- N95 minimum for cleanup; P100 for prolonged hardwood sanding.
- Wet methods where compatible.

### 6.5 Spray Finishing

VOCs, isocyanates (catalyzed urethanes), HAPs. Cal/OSHA §1539 + §5150 + South Coast AQMD/SCAQMD product limits in California.

- **Cartridge respirator** (organic vapor + P100 pre-filter) at minimum for catalyzed/oil-based product. For isocyanates, **supplied air** is the only correct answer (cartridges do not adequately protect).
- **Ventilation** — explosion-proof fan, never recirculate.
- **Ignition sources** — bond/ground containers, no smoking, intrinsically-safe electrical.
- **Skin protection** — nitrile gloves, full sleeve, eye/face for spray operations.

### 6.6 Respirator Selection (T8 §5144)

You cannot just hand out a respirator. Cal/OSHA §5144 requires a **written respiratory protection program**:

- Hazard assessment (what, how much).
- Selection (NIOSH-approved, correct cartridge/filter, correct APF).
- **Medical evaluation** (questionnaire reviewed by PLHCP) before use.
- **Fit-testing** (qualitative or quantitative) annually and whenever facial features change.
- **Training** annual.
- **Maintenance, cleaning, storage**.
- **Recordkeeping** — medical 30 yrs, fit test 1 yr.

| Hazard | Respirator |
|---|---|
| Nuisance dust (drywall, wood) | N95 |
| Lead, silica (low) | Half-mask APF 10 with P100 |
| Lead, silica (higher), some asbestos work | Full-face APF 50 with P100 (or PAPR APF 1000) |
| Asbestos Class I | PAPR or supplied air APF 1000 |
| Confined space, IDLH, unknown atmosphere | **Supplied-air SCBA only** |
| Pesticides, VOC spray finishes | Organic vapor cartridge + P100 |
| Isocyanates | Supplied air |

Beard policy: any facial hair that crosses the sealing surface of a tight-fitting respirator disqualifies use. Loose-fitting PAPR or supplied-air hoods are the workaround.

## 7. Heat Illness Prevention — T8 §3395

Cal/OSHA's heat illness standard is one of the strictest in the country. Outdoor work at **80°F or above** activates it; additional **high-heat** procedures activate at **95°F**. ([T8 §3395](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/3395.html))

### 7.1 The Four Core Requirements

| # | Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Water** | Fresh, pure, suitably cool drinking water. **1 quart per worker per hour** for the entire shift (so ~2 gallons/worker for an 8-hour day). Located **as close as practicable** to the work areas. Never let a crew "share one cooler" 100 ft from the work. |
| 2 | **Shade** | When ambient ≥ 80°F, shade must be **present** at the worksite. Sufficient for **all** workers on recovery / meal periods at the time, accommodate them in a seated position, not deter use. ≤ 25% of the body's skin surface in direct contact with the hot surfaces. Below 80°F, shade must be **promptly available** on request. |
| 3 | **Cool-down rests** | Any worker requesting a cool-down rest must be allowed at least **5 minutes** in the shade. No retaliation. Encouraged proactively. |
| 4 | **Emergency response** | Procedures to contact EMS, transport, provide first aid; supervisors trained to recognize heat illness. |

### 7.2 Written Heat Illness Prevention Plan (HIPP)

Required, in writing, **in English and the language of the majority of workers**. Made available at the worksite to employees and Cal/OSHA on request.

Must include:
- The four requirements above translated into site procedures.
- **High-heat procedures** (95°F).
- **Acclimatization** procedures.
- **Emergency response procedures** (who calls 911, who escorts to ambulance, clear address of worksite, who notifies family, who notifies Cal/OSHA if it becomes a serious injury).

Sample HIPP at dir.ca.gov/dosh/etools/08-006.

### 7.3 High-Heat Procedures (≥ 95°F)

Additional requirements:

- **Effective communication** — voice, observation, or electronic — between supervisor and crew.
- **Observation** — supervisor or designated observer for signs of heat illness. One supervisor per ≤ 20 employees, OR mandatory buddy system, OR regular check-in (every 30 min minimum), OR other equally effective measures.
- **Pre-shift meeting** — review HIPP, water, shade, right to cool-down.
- **Mandatory cool-down rest** — at least **10 minutes every 2 hours** in shade, not optional, not deductible from regular breaks (a separate cool-down rest can coincide with a meal break).
- **Authorization to call EMS** — at least one designated worker on site authorized to call 911; in the absence, *any* employee may call.

### 7.4 Acclimatization

| Worker category | Acclimatization rule |
|---|---|
| **New worker** | Must be closely observed for first **14 days** of employment in conditions of heat. |
| **Returning worker** | Closely observed if absent from heat work for **7 or more days**. |
| **All workers** | Closely observed during a heat wave (≥ 80°F when there's been a 10°F or more increase over the prior 5-day average), regardless of tenure. |

"Closely observed" means a supervisor or co-worker monitors for symptoms; new workers should not be in full production until acclimatized. This is one of the elements Cal/OSHA *will* ask about on inspection — and one of the most-cited gaps.

### 7.5 Recognition and First Response

| Condition | Signs | First response |
|---|---|---|
| **Heat cramps** | Muscle spasms, sweaty | Rest, water, electrolytes, monitor. |
| **Heat exhaustion** | Heavy sweat, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, normal-to-elevated temp | Move to shade, cool with water/ice on neck/wrists/groin, hydrate, call EMS if symptoms persist > 15 min or worsen. |
| **Heat stroke** | **Confusion, altered mental status, hot/dry OR hot/sweaty skin, temp > 103°F, collapse** | **CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY.** Aggressive active cooling — immersion in cold water if available, or ice packs on neck/armpits/groin, soaked sheets. Heat stroke is a true medical emergency; brain damage and death within minutes. Do not wait for symptoms to "improve." |

Train every supervisor to recognize the distinction between heat exhaustion (urgent) and heat stroke (911). Confusion or altered mental status = heat stroke until proven otherwise.

### 7.6 Indoor Heat Standard (effective July 2024)

T8 §3396 covers indoor work environments. Triggers at **82°F indoors**; high-heat at **87°F** (or 82°F with radiant heat sources / vapor-impermeable clothing). Most residential work that triggers is hot-weather attic, basement, or unconditioned-structure work. Treat attic work as triggering even if outside is moderate — attics easily reach 130°F.

## 8. Tool Safety and Power Equipment

### 8.1 Circular Saws

- **Lower guard** must retract on entry into work and **spring back** instantly on completion. Tie-back, jam, or removal is a citation.
- **Riving knife or splitter** (modern saws have riving knife) prevents kickback from binding.
- **Anti-kickback pawls** on table saws.
- **Plunge cuts** require lifting the lower guard with the dedicated lever — fingers off the blade path; cut should not start over a knot or nail.
- **Don't override the safety pin** that prevents the guard from being fixed open.
- Blade for material: framing blades (24T) for rough cuts, finish (60T+) for trim. Do NOT use abrasive masonry wheels in a saw not rated for them.

### 8.2 Angle Grinders

The most under-respected tool on a residential site. They cause catastrophic injuries.

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Match wheel RPM rating ≥ tool max RPM** | An undersized-RPM wheel will explode. Read the wheel before mounting. |
| **Side guards installed** | Required, position to deflect debris away from the operator. Removing the side guard for "access" is the #1 grinder citation. |
| **Wheel inspection** | Ring test on bonded abrasive: tap with non-metallic handle — dull thud = cracked, discard. |
| **Face shield + safety glasses** | Both, not either. |
| **Hearing protection** | Above 85 dBA (most grinders). |
| **Anti-restart switch / paddle switch** | Tools must not auto-start on power restore. |
| **Two-hand operation** | Both side and trigger handle. |
| **No cutting wheels on grinding tools** | Different bond, different speed rating; mismatched cutting wheels are a common shrapnel injury. |

### 8.3 Powder-Actuated Tools (Hilti, Ramset, Powder-Actuated Fasteners)

T8 §1693 requires:

- **Operator certification card** issued by the tool manufacturer or authorized trainer. Carry the card while operating.
- **Tool inspection** before each shift.
- **Loaded tool never pointed at anyone** — even unloaded, treat as loaded.
- **Misfire procedure** — hold tool against work surface for 30 seconds, then carefully unload; place defective load in water container.
- **Eye and ear protection** mandatory; hard hat for nearby workers.
- **Don't fire through soft material** — cartridge may penetrate and exit.

### 8.4 Aerial Work Platforms (Scissor Lifts, Boom Lifts)

T8 §3637 et seq. (Subchapter 7 Aerial Devices).

| Topic | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Operator training** | Required, machine-specific, documented. ANSI A92 / general industry compliant. |
| **Pre-shift inspection** | Walk-around + function test, documented daily. |
| **Fall protection in scissor lifts** | Guardrails are the primary protection; **personal fall arrest required when guardrails are down for any reason** or when worker leans over rail. |
| **Fall protection in boom lifts** | **Always required** — harness with lanyard attached to manufacturer-designated anchor point inside the bucket. |
| **Outriggers / chocks** as required by manufacturer. |
| **Ground conditions** — firm, level, away from edges (3× height rule for stability). |
| **Never use as a crane** | Don't suspend loads from the bucket unless rated. |
| **Travel position** | Boom down, bucket centered, when moving on flat ground only. |
| **Overhead lines** | Same 10-ft rule — most boom-lift electrocutions are line contacts. |

### 8.5 Concrete Saws

Silica controls per §1532.3 Table 1 — almost always wet cutting. Walk-behind saws: water supply must reach blade continuously; do not run dry "just for a minute." Indoor cutting requires additional vacuum dust collection. CO from gas-powered saws indoors = poisoning risk.

### 8.6 Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

T8 §3380–3385 and §1514. Employer pays for required PPE.

| PPE | Standard | When |
|---|---|---|
| **Hard hat** | ANSI Z89.1 Type I (top impact) Class C/G/E | Anywhere overhead hazard exists — practically always on a job site with active overhead work. |
| **Safety glasses** | ANSI Z87.1 + | Continuous on jobsite per most company policies; required for any cutting, grinding, fastening, demolition. Side shields. |
| **Face shield** | ANSI Z87.1 + | Over safety glasses for grinding, chipping, high-velocity work. |
| **Hearing protection** | NRR rated | 85 dBA 8-hr TWA action level; 90 dBA PEL. Most power tools exceed this. |
| **Foot protection** | ASTM F2413 (steel/composite toe, EH electrical hazard option) | Construction sites — required by most contractors; Cal/OSHA cites under §3385. |
| **Gloves** | ANSI/ISEA 105 cut levels A1–A9 | A4+ for sheet metal, framing; cut-resistant for any blade work. Never gloves near rotating tools (table saw, drill press) — entanglement. |
| **High-vis** | ANSI/ISEA 107 Class 2/3 | Near vehicle traffic. |
| **Respirator** | NIOSH-approved | Per §5144 program. |
| **Fall protection harness** | ANSI Z359 | When at heights — see §2. |

PPE is the **last** layer of protection — not the first. Elimination, engineering controls, and administrative controls come first.

## 9. Toolbox Talks and Safety Culture

### 9.1 Toolbox Talks — Cal/OSHA Code of Safe Practices

T8 §1509(e) requires the employer to **hold a "tailgate safety meeting" at least every 10 working days** to emphasize safety. The industry standard is **weekly** (5–10 minutes, beginning of shift). Daily is even better; it's what high-performing residential contractors do.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Length** | 5–10 minutes. |
| **Topic** | Site-specific or recent near-miss. Rotate through: falls, ladders, heat, silica, GFCI, housekeeping, eye protection, lift assist, vehicle/traffic, struck-by, PPE inspection. |
| **Documentation** | Date, topic, attendees signed in, instructor, key points. Keep with IIPP records ≥ 1 year. |
| **Language** | Conducted in language workers understand. |
| **Two-way** | Ask: any near-misses this week? Any conditions to fix? Workers should leave comments. |

This is the **good-faith defense** — when Cal/OSHA finds a violation by an employee, your documented training and recent toolbox talk on that exact topic helps support an **employee-misconduct affirmative defense**. Without it, you own the violation.

### 9.2 Incident and Near-Miss Investigation

Every recordable injury **and every near-miss** should be investigated. Near-misses are free lessons — investigate as if they had killed someone, because next time they might.

A simple template:
1. **What happened** — narrative, timeline.
2. **Where, when, who, what equipment, what materials, what conditions** (weather, lighting, fatigue).
3. **Immediate cause** — what unsafe act or condition led directly?
4. **Root cause** — 5-Why. Why was the act/condition possible?
5. **Corrective action** — engineering, administrative, training; specific responsible person; deadline.
6. **Follow-up verification** — was the corrective action actually implemented?

§3203 requires hazard correction documentation. Keep these.

### 9.3 Safety Incentive Programs — What Works vs. What Backfires

**Don't:** Reward "X days without an injury" with cash/jackets/raffle. This creates pressure to **not report** injuries. Cal/OSHA and OSHA both view such programs as evidence of underreporting and have cited employers whose injury logs were suspiciously low.

**Do:**
- Reward **leading indicators** — toolbox talk attendance, near-miss reporting, hazard observations submitted, training completion.
- Reward **behaviors** — wearing PPE, performing pre-shift inspections, completing JHAs.
- Recognize **safe crews** through pizza lunches, public mentions in toolbox talks, peer recognition.
- Discipline progressively for unsafe behavior — verbal → written → final → termination. Document.

### 9.4 Subcontractor Safety Management

The GC is almost always the **controlling employer** under multi-employer worksite doctrine. Even when a sub's worker gets hurt because of the sub's lousy practices, you can be cited.

| Practice | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prequalification** | Before contracting: Cal/OSHA log, EMR (workers' comp experience modifier — under 1.0 is good), IIPP copy, license, insurance, written safety program, training rosters. |
| **Contract language** | Sub must comply with all OSHA/Cal/OSHA standards and your IIPP; sub indemnifies for safety violations; right to remove unsafe workers; right to stop work for unsafe conditions; require subs to report incidents to you within 24 hours. |
| **Pre-job safety conference** | Before sub's mobilization: walk the site, identify hazards, exchange emergency procedures, confirm sub's competent persons, confirm sub's PPE. |
| **Daily oversight** | GC superintendent walks the site daily, addresses unsafe conditions immediately, documents. |
| **Stop-work authority** | Communicate at pre-job: any worker — yours or sub's — has authority to stop work for an imminent danger, no retaliation. |
| **Documentation of corrections** | If you correct a sub's hazard, document. Establishes "reasonable care" defense to controlling-employer citation. |

### 9.5 New-Employee Orientation

Before the new worker swings a hammer:

1. **IIPP overview** — your safety policy, their rights, your responsibilities.
2. **Site-specific hazards** — what's on this job (lead? overhead lines? trenches? roof work?).
3. **Emergency procedures** — exits, muster points, address of site, who calls 911.
4. **First-aid location** — kit, supplies, AED if any, designated first-aiders.
5. **Heat-illness training** — water, shade, cool-down, acclimatization (per §3395).
6. **Hazard-communication overview** — where SDSs live, what chemicals.
7. **PPE issuance** — hard hat, glasses, gloves, etc. Fit-test if respirator.
8. **Tool safety expectations** — what they will operate, training required first.
9. **Fall-protection overview** — equipment, anchor points, rescue plan.
10. **Reporting** — how to report a near-miss or injury; no retaliation.

Document attendance with signature. Keep with IIPP.

## 10. Cal/OSHA Inspections and Citations

### 10.1 What Triggers an Inspection

| Trigger | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Imminent danger** | Highest priority. Cal/OSHA can be at your site within hours. |
| **Fatality / serious injury** | Always inspected. You called it in per §342 (or someone else did). |
| **Worker complaint** | Anonymous complaints are common; phone, online, in-person. Cal/OSHA prioritizes by hazard severity. |
| **Referral** | From other agencies (fire, EMS, building department), media, or third-party referrals. |
| **Programmed inspection** | Random or targeted at high-hazard industries; construction is "high-hazard." |
| **Follow-up** | Verify abatement of prior citations. |

### 10.2 Your Rights and Obligations During an Inspection

The inspector (Cal/OSHA Associate Safety Engineer / Industrial Hygienist) will:

1. **Present credentials** at the gate. Always ask to see them; verify.
2. **Hold an opening conference** — explain the scope, expected duration, your rights.
3. **Conduct a walkaround** — accompanied by an employer representative and an authorized employee representative. Take own photos/measurements/notes. Ask employees questions privately.
4. **Hold a closing conference** — informal review of apparent violations and discussion of abatement; not the final word.

**Your rights:**
- Request a warrant. (You can. It's not generally advised unless something feels very off — Cal/OSHA can get one quickly, and the relationship sours.)
- Have your own representative on the walkaround.
- Limit scope: inspector should confine to the area of complaint (in most cases). Push back if they expand without cause.
- Take parallel notes and photographs.
- Receive a copy of any sampling results.
- Caucus before answering questions.

**Your obligations:**
- Provide reasonable access to the worksite and to records (IIPP, training, 300 log, SDS).
- Don't tamper with the site after notice (for fatalities, before notice).
- Don't retaliate against the complainant — felony.

**Best practices for the GC during inspection:**
- Be polite, professional, factual. Don't volunteer.
- Designate **one** representative as the inspector's liaison. Other supervisors continue work.
- Document parallel everything the inspector documents — measurements, samples, photos, conversations.
- Correct obvious hazards immediately during the walkaround; ask to note correction on the spot ("good faith abatement").
- Don't admit fault. Stick to facts.
- Get the inspector's card; ask when to expect citations; ask about informal settlement conference availability.

### 10.3 Citation Categories and Penalties (2025)

| Class | Definition | Max civil penalty per violation (2025) |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory** | Recordkeeping, posting, reporting (e.g., late §342 report; missing 300 log; no posted poster). | **$16,285** |
| **General (Other-than-Serious, OTS)** | Direct relationship to safety but unlikely to cause death/serious harm. | **$16,285** |
| **Serious** | Substantial probability of death or serious physical harm; employer knew or should have known. | **$25,000** |
| **Willful** | Intentional disregard or plain indifference. Min penalty exists. | Min **$11,632**; Max **$162,851** |
| **Repeat** | Same or substantially similar violation within prior 5 years. | Up to **$162,851** |
| **Failure to Abate** | Continuing violation past abatement date. | Up to **$16,285 per day** beyond abatement date |

([2025 penalties](https://www.dir.ca.gov/DIRNews/2025/2025-10.html))

Penalties are subject to **adjustments** based on size of employer, good faith (do you have an IIPP? Trained workers? Toolbox talks?), prior history. The base "gravity" penalty for a serious violation is typically $18,000 with reductions/adjustments applied — but cap at $25,000 per violation. Serious-willful for an injury or fatality where Labor Code 6427.7 applies has higher minimums.

### 10.4 Abatement

Each citation includes a **proposed abatement date** by which the employer must correct the hazard. You must:

- Correct the hazard.
- Submit **abatement verification** (employer affidavit, photos, receipts, training records).
- Post the citation at or near the affected area for **3 working days** or until corrected, whichever longer.

Failure to abate accrues per-day penalties.

### 10.5 Appeals

If you disagree with a citation:

1. **Informal conference** (request within 15 working days of citation receipt). Often the **Informal Settlement Agreement (ISA)** is reached here — typical outcomes include reclassification (Serious → General), reduction of penalty, extension of abatement, conversion of repeat-eligible to non-repeat. Most citations end here.
2. **Formal Appeal** to the **Cal/OSHA Appeals Board (OSHAB)** — must be filed within **15 working days** of receipt of the citation. Free, written form. Stops penalty accrual.
3. **Hearing** before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ). You may represent yourself or use counsel; subpoena witnesses; introduce evidence.
4. **Decision** by ALJ.
5. **Petition for Reconsideration** to the Appeals Board (within 30 days of ALJ decision).
6. **Writ of Mandate** to **California Court of Appeal** for judicial review.

Strategy: most disputes settle at the informal conference. The Cal/OSHA District Manager has authority to negotiate. Bring your evidence (training records, IIPP, photos), be respectful, propose specific reclassifications, document any abatement already completed.

### 10.6 Multi-Employer Worksite — Who Gets Cited?

Cal/OSHA follows a multi-employer citation policy parallel to federal OSHA's. Each employer at a worksite is evaluated for one or more roles: ([T8 §336.10](https://www.dir.ca.gov/title8/336_10.html))

| Role | Who | Liable when |
|---|---|---|
| **Creating** | Caused the hazard. | Any employer who created the hazard. |
| **Exposing** | Has employees exposed to the hazard. | Has obligation to protect them — even if didn't create. |
| **Correcting** | Responsible for fixing the hazard (often the trade specialist). | Failed to fix when responsibility assigned. |
| **Controlling** | General supervisory authority over the worksite (typically the **GC**). | Failed to exercise reasonable care to prevent/detect/correct violations by others. |

As the GC, you are almost always the **controlling employer**. The "reasonable care" standard means Cal/OSHA looks at:

- Did you have a **written safety program** covering the hazard?
- Did you conduct **regular inspections** of the site?
- Did you **enforce** when subs created hazards?
- Did you **respond** to known issues?

A documented program, daily superintendent walks, written hazard notices to subs, and progressive enforcement (warning → stop-work → removal of worker) constitute reasonable care. Lack of any of these makes you the most attractive citation target.

A **subcontractor's** Cal/OSHA citation can also be cited to the GC if the GC was the controlling employer and failed reasonable care — but the GC can defeat the citation by demonstrating reasonable care. This is why your subcontractor management documentation matters so much.

## 11. Quick Reference — The First 90 Days

If you've just become responsible for the safety program and don't know where to start, here is the 90-day plan that gets you to compliant:

### Days 0–14: Stop the Bleeding

- [ ] Cal/OSHA poster (DLSE/DOSH) and labor-law posters posted at every site office and yard.
- [ ] Get a copy of any existing IIPP. If none, download the Cal/OSHA model.
- [ ] Confirm workers' comp carrier; request their safety consultant's contact.
- [ ] Confirm who can dial 1-800-963-9424 to report an §342 event — owner, super, office manager. Train all three.
- [ ] Buy GFCI cord sets / spider boxes for every active site.
- [ ] Buy hard hats, Z87 safety glasses, Class 2 vests, hearing protection. Issue at orientation.
- [ ] Inventory chemicals; download SDSs to a site binder + cloud folder.

### Days 15–45: Build the Program

- [ ] Customize the IIPP with your company name, sites, hazards, names.
- [ ] Written **Heat Illness Prevention Plan**, in English + Spanish if applicable.
- [ ] Written **HazCom** program.
- [ ] Written **Respiratory Protection** program if you do any silica/lead/painting work.
- [ ] Written **Fall Protection** plan with site-specific anchor points and rescue plan.
- [ ] Identify a **competent person** for excavations, scaffolds, fall protection (often you).
- [ ] First **weekly toolbox talk** — pick a topic, run it, document, file.
- [ ] First **monthly safety inspection** — walk every active site, photo any hazard, write a corrective-action notice.

### Days 46–90: Operationalize

- [ ] Begin or update **300 Log** entries; post 300A by Feb 1.
- [ ] Run **new-employee orientation** for every new hire — even if you've been "running it informally."
- [ ] **Subcontractor prequalification packet** — IIPP request, EMR, license, insurance, training records.
- [ ] Conduct an **emergency drill** (heat illness response, injury response, fire) at one site.
- [ ] Schedule **annual training renewals** — fall protection, silica, lead, respirator fit-test, scaffold-user, powder-actuated tool re-cert, first aid/CPR.
- [ ] Run an **internal mock Cal/OSHA inspection** with your workers' comp consultant. Identify gaps.
- [ ] Review your **work auto insurance** — does it cover hot work / extra-hazardous activities?
- [ ] Create or update a **site safety binder** template that includes: IIPP excerpt, HIPP, SDSs, toolbox talk attendance, daily inspection, emergency contacts, posted citations (if any), permit copies. One per active site.

## 13. Five Decisions That Prevent Most Residential Fatalities

If you only do five things differently after reading this:

1. **Plan fall protection before framing starts.** Anchor points pre-installed. PFAS issued and inspected. Roof brackets and SRLs staged. The framer steps onto a roof already protected.
2. **Walk the overhead lines before the wall raises.** If a service drop crosses where a wall will go up, get the utility to drop it. Cost $0; benefit, one less coffin.
3. **Never let a worker enter an unprotected trench, ever.** No "I'll just be in there 30 seconds." No "the soil looks fine." Trench box, hydraulic shore, or slope back. Period.
4. **Run heat illness like it kills people — because it does.** Water at the work, shade at the work, cool-down rest available, supervisor watching the new guy for 14 days. Acclimatize new hires gradually.
5. **Train, document, repeat.** Daily toolbox talk, weekly inspection, monthly review. Document everything. The IIPP is a living document, not a binder. The crew that talks about safety every morning is the crew that goes home every night.
