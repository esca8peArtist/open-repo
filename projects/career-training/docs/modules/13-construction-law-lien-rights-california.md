# Construction Law and Lien Rights for the California Residential GC

> **Purpose.** This is a working reference, not a treatise. Pull it open the moment a payment problem appears, when a customer says "I'm not paying for that," when a sub demands a release before you've been paid yourself, or when a homeowner threatens to sue. Each section is built around the practical question: *what do I do, by when, and what is the citation I will quote when I do it?*

> **Disclaimer.** This is education, not legal advice. California construction law is highly technical, and a single missed deadline can extinguish a six-figure claim. When the dollars get serious — usually anything above CSLB-arbitration limits, or anything involving structural defects, injuries, or repeat non-payers — call a construction attorney. The cost of one hour of legal review ($350–$600) is trivial compared to losing your lien rights.

---

## 0. Mental Model — How the System Actually Works

California's construction payment system is built around one core problem: **the people who add value to a building (labor, materials, equipment) are not usually the same people who hold the money (owner, lender).** The statutes exist to bridge that gap by giving the value-adders a *security interest* — a legal hook into the property itself — that survives the failure of any single party in the payment chain.

The system has **three parallel remedies**, each pointing at a different pocket:

| Remedy | Targets | Filed With | Best Used When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mechanic's Lien** | The real property (owner's equity) | County Recorder | Owner has equity; no construction loan; or general nonpayment |
| **Stop Payment Notice** | The construction loan funds | Construction lender | There is a construction loan with undisbursed funds |
| **Payment Bond Claim** | The surety (on bonded jobs) | Surety + principal | Public works or bonded private jobs |

For a **residential GC**, the mechanic's lien is the workhorse. Stop notices matter on owner-builder jobs or new construction with a loan. Payment bonds rarely show up on residential remodels.

Everything in the lien statutes flows from one principle: **timing is jurisdictional**. Miss a deadline by one day and your lien is void — not voidable, not "discretionary with the judge," but void as a matter of law. Calendar discipline is the single most important habit in this entire body of law.

## 1. California Mechanic's Lien Law — The Basics

### 1.1 What a mechanic's lien actually is

A mechanic's lien is a **security interest against real property** in favor of someone who has contributed labor, services, equipment, or materials to a work of improvement. It is *not* a money judgment — it is a cloud on title that gives the claimant the right to **force a sale of the property** if the underlying debt is not paid, with proceeds first applied to the lien.

The practical leverage of a lien is not usually the foreclosure itself (foreclosures are slow, expensive, and rare). The leverage is that **the lien sits on the title until released**, which means:

- The owner cannot refinance.
- The owner cannot sell.
- The lender (if any) gets nervous and may freeze draws.
- Title insurance becomes unavailable for any subsequent transaction.

That blocking effect is what gets you paid.

### 1.2 Constitutional and statutory basis

California's mechanic's lien is one of the very few **constitutionally guaranteed** civil remedies in the United States.

- **California Constitution, Article XIV, §3**: "Mechanics, persons furnishing materials, artisans, and laborers of every class, shall have a lien upon the property upon which they have bestowed labor or furnished material for the value of such labor done and material furnished." This means the Legislature cannot abolish the lien — only regulate its exercise.
- **Civil Code §8000 et seq.**: The 2012 Mechanic's Lien Reform Act (effective July 1, 2012) replaced the old Civil Code §3082 et seq. scheme. The reorganization renumbered virtually every section, and the old numbers are still cited in older cases — when you see "§3097," translate to the current §8200 series.

Key structural sections of the post-2012 code:

| Code Range | Subject |
|---|---|
| §§8000–8062 | Definitions, general provisions |
| §§8100–8190 | Notice requirements and service rules |
| §§8200–8216 | Preliminary Notice (20-day notice) |
| §§8400–8494 | Mechanic's Liens |
| §§8500–8560 | Stop Payment Notices |
| §§8600–8614 | Payment Bonds |
| §§8800–8848 | Prompt payment, suspension of work |
| §§8132–8138 | Statutory waiver forms (the four mandatory forms) |

### 1.3 Who can file a mechanic's lien

Civil Code **§8400** is the gatekeeper. A claimant has lien rights if they provided labor, services, equipment, or materials to a work of improvement under a direct contract with the owner *or* under a subcontract with someone who has a direct contract with the owner.

| Claimant Type | Lien Rights? | Common Pitfalls |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct (prime) contractor** | Yes, if licensed | License must be active and properly classified — Bus. & Prof. Code §7031 bars unlicensed contractors from any recovery |
| **Subcontractors** | Yes | Must serve preliminary notice; chain of contract must be intact |
| **Sub-subcontractors (2nd tier)** | Yes | Same — preliminary notice critical |
| **Material suppliers** | Yes | Must show materials were *delivered to* (or specifically fabricated for) the project |
| **Equipment lessors** | Yes (§8200(a)) | Lien limited to reasonable rental value during use on the job |
| **Laborers (W-2 employees)** | Yes | No preliminary notice required for laborers |
| **Design professionals (architects, engineers, surveyors)** | Yes, with caveats | Special "design professionals lien" under §§8300–8319 if work doesn't proceed; standard mechanic's lien if it does |
| **Suppliers to suppliers** | **No** | The chain breaks at the second tier of supply |
| **Off-site fabricators (custom work)** | Yes, if specifically fabricated for the project | Stock items generally don't qualify |
| **Unlicensed contractors** | **No — total bar** (Bus. & Prof. Code §7031) | Even partial unlicensed status during the project can void all rights |

The **§7031 trap** deserves emphasis. If your license lapses for *any portion* of the work — even one day mid-project — you may lose the right to recover compensation for the entire job, and the owner can sue to recover everything they have already paid you (disgorgement). Subscribe to CSLB renewal reminders. Set calendar alerts 90 and 30 days before expiration.

### 1.4 The lien claimant hierarchy and priority

Multiple claimants can lien the same property. Two priority questions arise: priority **among lien claimants**, and priority **against other encumbrances** (mortgages, deeds of trust).

**Among lien claimants** (Civil Code §8450):
- All mechanic's liens for a single work of improvement share priority **equally**, regardless of recording order.
- If the property sells for less than the total liens, claimants share **pro rata**.
- There is no first-to-file advantage among mechanic's lien claimants — racing to the recorder doesn't help.

**Against other encumbrances** (Civil Code §8450–§8458):
- Mechanic's liens have priority over any encumbrance recorded **after the work of improvement commenced**, regardless of when the lien itself is recorded.
- "Commencement of work" is a fact question: the first visible work on site (excavation, demo, material delivery in some cases).
- This is why lenders demand a "no work" affidavit and a recorded deed of trust **before any work begins** — to preserve the lender's priority.

Practical implication for a GC: if you started work before the construction loan recorded (even tearing down a fence), your lien beats the lender's deed of trust. This dramatically increases leverage.

### 1.5 Owner's property rights and the homestead

A mechanic's lien attaches to the **owner's interest** in the property. If the owner only has a leasehold (tenant), the lien attaches only to that leasehold unless the work was authorized by the fee owner (Civil Code §8442).

**Homestead exemption does NOT defeat a mechanic's lien.** A homeowner cannot hide behind the homestead to block a foreclosure for unpaid construction work. This is a frequent misunderstanding — a homeowner may tell you "you can't lien my house, it's my homestead." Wrong. CCP §704.730 et seq. exempts homestead value from *general* creditors, not from *purchase-money or improvement* liens.

### 1.6 Criminal vs. civil consequences

Most lien law violations are civil only — the wronged party sues for damages or for release of an improper lien. Two scenarios cross into criminal territory:

| Violation | Statute | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Diversion of construction funds** (taking owner's payment and not paying subs/suppliers) | Penal Code §484b | Misdemeanor or felony depending on amount; can also trigger CSLB license revocation |
| **False or fraudulent lien** (knowingly inflating amount, or filing without basis) | Civil Code §8422; Penal Code §115 | Criminal misdemeanor for filing false document; civil liability for slander of title, abuse of process, attorney fees |
| **Forged lien releases** | Penal Code §470 | Felony forgery |
| **Contractor practicing without a license** | Bus. & Prof. Code §7028 | Misdemeanor; CSLB enforcement |

The civil exposure for an improperly recorded lien is real: under §8424, an owner can petition the court for an expedited release, and if the lien is found frivolous, the claimant pays the owner's **attorney fees**. Don't lien out of anger. Lien only when the underlying claim is solid and properly documented.

## 2. Preliminary Notice (the "20-Day Notice")

### 2.1 Why it exists and what it does

The preliminary notice is the single most important piece of paper in California construction. It is not a demand for payment. It is a **statutory prerequisite to lien rights**. No preliminary notice (when one is required), no lien — period.

The notice exists because owners and lenders need to know **who is on the job** so they can verify everyone has been paid before disbursing draws or closing out the project. The 1969 reform (carried forward in the 2012 Act) made notice mandatory to balance the surprise-lien problem against the subcontractor's right to security.

### 2.2 Who must serve and who is exempt — Civil Code §8200

**Required to serve:**
- All subcontractors
- All material suppliers (whether to the GC or to a sub)
- Equipment lessors
- Design professionals working under contract with anyone other than the owner

**Exempt from serving:**
- **Wage claimants** (W-2 employees) — §8200(e)(1)
- **Direct (prime) contractor** with a direct written contract with the owner — §8200(e)(2)

### 2.3 The prime contractor exception — read carefully

The prime contractor exception in §8200(e)(2) is **narrow and conditional**. The prime contractor is exempt from serving preliminary notice **on the owner**, but must still serve **the construction lender** if there is one. This is a frequent and expensive mistake.

| If you are the GC and... | You must serve... |
|---|---|
| Owner-financed (no construction loan), direct contract with owner | No preliminary notice required |
| There is a construction loan | **Preliminary notice on the lender required** (failure forfeits lien priority over the lender's deed of trust and forfeits stop notice rights) |
| You have a contract with a developer/GC who is not the fee owner | Full preliminary notice required (you are effectively a sub in the eyes of the statute) |

**Best practice: serve preliminary notice on every project, period.** It costs $5 in certified mail. It eliminates a category of risk that has destroyed many claims. Even when you are the prime contractor on an owner-direct job, send a courtesy preliminary notice — it costs nothing and harms nothing.

### 2.4 Timing — the 20-day rule (this is widely misunderstood)

The "20-day" in "20-day preliminary notice" is **not a deadline from project start**. It defines a **retroactive look-back window**.

The rule, in plain language:
- A preliminary notice protects lien rights for **all labor and materials furnished from 20 days before service forward**, plus all future work.
- If you serve on Day 1 of the job, you are covered for the entire job.
- If you serve on Day 30, you lose lien rights for Days 1–10 (the work done more than 20 days before service), but preserve rights for Days 11 onward.
- If you serve on Day 200, you preserve rights for Days 180–200 and forward, and you have **lost** the lien for the first 180 days.

This means you **can** serve preliminary notice late and still preserve some rights — but the longer you wait, the more rights you forfeit.

**Best practice: serve on the day of mobilization, before the first work begins.** Most California lien-tracking services (NCS, LienItNow, SunRay, Levelset/Procore Pay) will generate and mail the notice for $20–$50 per project — cheap insurance.

### 2.5 Content requirements — Civil Code §8202

A preliminary notice must contain:

1. **Description of the labor, services, equipment, or materials** furnished or to be furnished, and an estimate of the total price.
2. **Name and address of the person furnishing** the labor/services/equipment/materials (you).
3. **Name of the person to whom or for whom** the labor/services/equipment/materials are furnished (your direct contracting party — usually the GC if you are a sub, or the owner if you are the GC).
4. **Description of the jobsite** sufficient to identify it (street address is usually sufficient for residential; legal description preferred and required for liens).
5. **Statutory warning language** in bold-faced type (substantively this is the "NOTICE TO PROPERTY OWNER" block — see §8202(a)(5)). Get the language exactly right. Form available from CSLB and most legal stationers.

A defective preliminary notice (missing content) can be challenged, but courts have generally been forgiving of minor errors that do not mislead. Get the warning language verbatim. Estimate the total price honestly — overstating is permitted, but a wild overestimate ("$10M" on a $50K job) invites challenge.

### 2.6 Service methods — Civil Code §8110, §8116, §8118

Three valid methods:

| Method | Effective Date of Service | Proof Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Certified or registered mail, return receipt requested** | Date of mailing (not delivery — this is critical) | Proof of mailing (the receipt the post office stamps) — no signed return receipt needed |
| **Personal delivery** | Date of delivery | Declaration of service |
| **First-class mail with certificate of mailing** | Date of mailing | Certificate of mailing from USPS |

The **certified mail proof-of-mailing** point is one of the most reassuring features of California's scheme: **you do not need to prove the recipient received it.** You only need to prove that *you mailed it correctly to the right address on the right date.* If the homeowner refuses to sign for it, that's their problem, not yours.

Keep the green USPS receipt with the postmark forever. Scan it. Store it in two places. If a dispute arises three years later, that scrap of paper is your evidence.

### 2.7 Who to serve

For a typical residential project, serve **all three** of these parties (each gets their own copy):

1. **Owner of record** — verify via county recorder (free public records), not via what the homeowner tells you. Joint tenants: serve both. Trust ownership: serve the trustee.
2. **Prime contractor** — if you are a subcontractor.
3. **Construction lender** — if any; identify via the recorded deed of trust. If you are uncertain whether there is a construction loan, ask the owner in writing. If there is one and you don't notice the lender, you lose stop notice rights even if your lien rights remain.

### 2.8 What you lose if you miss the notice

| Missed Notice Scenario | Consequence |
|---|---|
| No preliminary notice ever served (by a sub or supplier) | **Total loss of lien rights** |
| Preliminary notice served late | Loss of lien rights for work done more than 20 days before service |
| Preliminary notice served on owner and GC but not lender | Lien rights preserved against property; **stop notice rights lost**; lien priority against lender's deed of trust may be lost |
| Defective notice content | Possible challenge — depends on whether defect is material |

### 2.9 Preliminary notice also preserves stop notice rights

A single preliminary notice, properly served, supports **both** mechanic's lien and stop notice rights. This is one notice doing two jobs. You do not need to serve a separate "stop notice preliminary." This makes serving the lender essentially free additional protection — do it.

## 3. The Mechanic's Lien Itself

### 3.1 Timing — Civil Code §8412 (general contractor) and §8414 (sub or supplier)

This is the most critical timing rule in the entire statutory scheme. Read it twice.

**General contractor (direct contract with owner):**
- **90 days after completion of the work of improvement** — if no Notice of Completion or Cessation is recorded.
- **60 days after recording** of a valid Notice of Completion or Cessation — if one was recorded. (§8412)

**Subcontractor or supplier:**
- **90 days after completion** — if no Notice of Completion or Cessation.
- **30 days after recording** of a valid Notice of Completion or Cessation. (§8414)

| Trigger Event | GC Deadline | Sub/Supplier Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| No NOC or Cessation recorded | 90 days after actual completion | 90 days after actual completion |
| Valid Notice of Completion recorded | 60 days after recording | 30 days after recording |
| Valid Notice of Cessation recorded | 60 days after recording | 30 days after recording |

The **trap**: an owner who suspects a payment dispute will record a Notice of Completion to **shorten everyone's deadline**. If you are a sub and the owner records on a Friday, you may have only **30 days** to file. Calendar this on every job.

### 3.2 What counts as "completion" — Civil Code §8180

Completion occurs upon the earliest of:

1. **Actual completion** of the work of improvement (substantial completion plus punch list usually counts).
2. **Occupation or use by the owner** accompanied by cessation of labor.
3. **Cessation of labor for a continuous period of 60 days** (or 30 days following a recorded Notice of Cessation).
4. **Acceptance by the owner** if the contract so provides.

For multi-phase residential remodels, "completion" can be ambiguous. Document the dates: if the homeowner moves back in, photograph it. If work stops, log it daily.

### 3.3 Notice of Completion — Civil Code §8182

- Filed by the **owner** in the county recorder's office.
- Must be recorded within **15 days of actual completion** to be effective (§8182(a)).
- Triggers the shortened lien deadlines (60 days for GC, 30 days for subs).
- Must be served on the original contractor and any claimant who served a preliminary notice (§8190) within 10 days, or it does not shorten the deadline as to that claimant.

A NOC that is late (more than 15 days after completion) or that omits required content (§8182(b)) is **invalid** and does **not** shorten the deadline. If you receive a NOC and suspect it's defective, calendar both the shortened deadline (in case it's valid) and the 90-day deadline (in case it's not). File earlier of the two.

### 3.4 Notice of Cessation — Civil Code §8188

- Filed by the **owner** after **30 continuous days** of labor cessation.
- Same effect as Notice of Completion: shortens lien deadlines.
- Used when a project is paused indefinitely (financing problem, dispute) but not technically complete.

### 3.5 Content of the lien — Civil Code §8416

The lien must be a **verified statement** (signed under penalty of perjury) containing:

1. Amount of the demand after deducting just credits and offsets.
2. Name of the owner or reputed owner.
3. General statement of the kind of labor, services, equipment, or materials furnished.
4. Name of the person by whom the claimant was employed or to whom the claimant furnished.
5. Description of the site sufficient for identification — **legal description strongly preferred**. A street address may be sufficient if no ambiguity exists, but case law has voided liens over property description defects. Pull the legal description from the deed (county recorder) or a current title report.
6. Proof of service affidavit showing service on the owner (required under §8416(a)(7) within the same 90/60/30-day window; failure to serve voids the lien).
7. The statutory **"NOTICE OF MECHANICS LIEN"** warning to the owner — §8416(a)(8) — must appear in bold 10-point type at the top of the lien.

A lien with defective content can be challenged via §8480 (action to remove) or §8424 (petition to release frivolous lien). Get the form right — use a current form from a legal stationer or California Land Title Association template, not a 2010 form predating the reform.

### 3.6 Recording — Civil Code §8416(a)

- File at the **County Recorder** in the county where the property is located.
- Recording fee: typically $20–$30 plus per-page fees.
- The recording date is the operative date for the 90-day foreclosure deadline (see §3.8 below).

### 3.7 Lis pendens effect — Civil Code §8461

Recording the lien automatically creates a cloud on title. You do **not** need to record a separate Notice of Pendency of Action (lis pendens) at the time of recording — the lien itself is constructive notice. However, **once you file the foreclosure lawsuit**, you must record a lis pendens within 20 days (§8461) referencing the suit, or the lien becomes unenforceable against a bona fide purchaser.

### 3.8 Foreclosure deadline — Civil Code §8460

**A lawsuit to foreclose the lien must be filed within 90 days of the date the lien was recorded.** Miss this, and the lien is **automatically void** by operation of law (§8460(b)) — and the owner can demand a release without paying you a cent.

This is the second jurisdictional deadline, after the recording deadline. Calendar it the moment you record the lien. Many GCs record a lien for leverage but never plan to actually sue — that's fine *as a negotiation tactic*, but if 90 days pass and no settlement has been reached, you must either:

- File the foreclosure suit (preserves the lien indefinitely), or
- Negotiate an extension (the owner agrees in writing to extend — rare; or both parties stipulate to extend), or
- Release the lien voluntarily and pursue a contract claim only.

A few attorneys will record a lien, let the 90 days lapse, and quietly let the lien die — this is **malpractice** and **bad faith** (the lien continues to cloud title until released, even after it's legally void, and the owner can sue for slander of title).

### 3.9 Release of lien — the four statutory waiver forms

Once paid, you **must** release the lien. The release forms are statutorily mandated (Civil Code §§8132–8138 — see Section 5 below). For a recorded lien specifically, use a **Mechanic's Lien Release** (often integrated with an unconditional waiver on final payment) and record it at the county recorder.

Under **Civil Code §8488**, if you fail to release a satisfied lien within **10 days of a written demand from the owner**, you are liable for the owner's damages and attorney fees, plus a statutory penalty.

### 3.10 Lien release bond — Civil Code §8424

The owner can **substitute a bond** for the lien in an amount equal to 125% of the claim. The bond is recorded; the lien is then automatically released from title (the cloud is gone). Your claim is now against the surety, not the property.

For the GC claimant, this is mostly neutral — sometimes better. You still have a secure claim (now against a regulated surety with deep pockets), and the owner now has motivation to settle because they are paying bond premiums (~1–3% per year of the bond amount). For an owner trying to sell or refinance, the bond is the fastest way to clear title.

If an owner offers a bond, accept it. Verify the surety is A-rated and registered with the California Department of Insurance.

## 4. Stop Payment Notice ("Stop Notice")

### 4.1 What it is and why it matters

A stop notice is **filed with the construction lender**, not the county recorder. It directs the lender to withhold money from the next construction loan disbursement to cover the claimant's unpaid amount. The lender, having received the notice, must hold those funds until the dispute is resolved or risk personal liability to the claimant.

The stop notice exists because the **lien is sometimes inadequate**: if a property is leveraged to (or above) its value, foreclosing a lien yields nothing — the senior deed of trust eats the proceeds. The stop notice bypasses the property and reaches into the loan funds directly, freezing money before it ever reaches the (potentially insolvent) GC or owner.

### 4.2 When stop notices outperform liens

| Scenario | Lien Power | Stop Notice Power |
|---|---|---|
| Owner has substantial equity, no construction loan | Strong | N/A |
| Owner financing through cash | Strong | N/A |
| Construction loan with undisbursed funds | Weaker (junior to lender) | **Very strong** |
| Owner bankrupt, no equity | Weak | **Effective if funds still undisbursed** |
| Property already sold | Weak (subject to BFP defenses) | Effective if loan hasn't fully disbursed |

For a sub working on a new construction project, **the stop notice is often more powerful than the lien**. For a remodel GC working on an owner-equity job, the lien is usually sufficient and the stop notice may not apply.

### 4.3 Who can file — Civil Code §8520

Anyone who could file a mechanic's lien can also file a stop notice (subs, suppliers, equipment lessors, design professionals). The **prime contractor** can file a stop notice but only on **public works** projects (Civil Code §9350 et seq. — the public works equivalent). On private works, the prime contractor's leverage with the lender is through other means.

### 4.4 Timing — Civil Code §8550, §8552

Stop notice deadlines mirror the mechanic's lien:

- **90 days after completion** — no NOC or Cessation.
- **30 days after recording** of NOC or Cessation (for non-prime claimants).

Service is on the **lender** — typically by certified mail to the lender's branch handling the loan, with a copy to any branch handling disbursements.

### 4.5 Content — Civil Code §8536

Like the lien, a stop notice must be verified (sworn) and must state:

1. The amount of the claim and a general description of the work.
2. Name of the owner.
3. Name of the contracting party.
4. Identification of the property and the construction loan if known.

### 4.6 Bonded vs. unbonded stop notice — the critical distinction

This is where stop notices get powerful or weak depending on a single decision.

**Unbonded stop notice (private works):**
- The lender **may** withhold funds at its discretion.
- In practice, most lenders ignore unbonded stop notices on private works because they have no compulsion to act.
- Effectively a notice of claim only.

**Bonded stop notice (private works) — Civil Code §8532:**
- The claimant posts a bond equal to **125% of the claim**.
- The lender **must** withhold funds equal to **125% of the claim** (the lender's withhold matches the bond, ensuring the claimant cannot weaponize the notice frivolously).
- The lender becomes **personally liable** to the claimant if it disburses despite the notice.

The bond costs the claimant about 1–3% per year of the bond amount. On a $50,000 claim, the bond is $62,500, costing ~$1,000–$1,500 in premiums. For a sub with a strong claim and a deadbeat owner, **this is the highest-leverage move in residential construction finance**. It freezes the lender's funds and forces a resolution.

### 4.7 Release of stop notice

Same four statutory waiver forms (§§8132–8138). When paid, deliver an unconditional waiver and release on final payment, and notify the lender in writing that the stop notice is withdrawn.

## 5. Lien Waivers — The Four Forms (Civil Code §§8132–8138)

### 5.1 Why this matters more than most GCs realize

Most payment disputes in residential construction don't begin with the homeowner refusing to pay — they begin with **a missing or improperly executed waiver in the paper trail**. A homeowner with a careful contractor or attorney advisor will demand waivers at each draw. A GC who casually signs the wrong waiver type, or signs before the check clears, may give up six figures of lien rights for an unpaid invoice. **Treat the four forms like a four-cycle engine: each waiver type belongs in exactly one moment of the payment cycle, and using the wrong one breaks everything.**

### 5.2 The four mandatory forms

California **prohibits** any waiver of mechanic's lien rights except via the four statutory forms (Civil Code §8122 — the anti-waiver provision). A waiver in any other form, including a custom form drafted by an owner's attorney, is **void and unenforceable**. The forms are reproduced verbatim in the Civil Code; legal stationers and lien services have them.

| Form | Code Section | When to Use | What It Releases |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment** | §8132 | Submitted with a progress payment request, before payment received | Releases lien rights for the specific amount **only when the check clears** |
| **Unconditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment** | §8134 | Given **after** a specific progress payment has cleared | Releases lien rights for that specific amount, no conditions |
| **Conditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment** | §8136 | Submitted with the final payment request, before payment received | Releases all lien rights **only when the final check clears** |
| **Unconditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment** | §8138 | Given **after** the final payment has cleared | Releases all lien rights, no conditions |

### 5.3 The cardinal rule: never give an unconditional waiver before the money clears

The unconditional waiver is **binding regardless of whether you actually get paid**. If you sign an unconditional waiver on Monday and the check bounces on Tuesday, your lien rights are gone. The check bouncing is your problem to chase via small claims — but the **lien**, your most powerful collection tool, is irretrievably released.

**Rule: conditional waivers come first; unconditional waivers come only after the funds have settled in your account.** "Settled" means cleared, not deposited. ACH and wire transfers typically settle same day or next day. Personal checks take 1–10 business days depending on the issuing bank. Cashier's checks settle quickly but can still be revoked if fraudulent.

A safe practice:

1. With each pay application, attach a **conditional waiver on progress payment** for the amount of that application.
2. After the check clears (verify with your bank, not just by inspection of the check), provide an **unconditional waiver on progress payment** for the cleared amount.
3. At the final payment, repeat with the final-payment versions.

### 5.4 How to exchange lien waivers with owners

The typical residential draw cycle:

| Day | Event | Document |
|---|---|---|
| Day 0 | Submit Draw #3 invoice for $25,000 | Cover letter + invoice + **Conditional Waiver on Progress Payment for $25,000** |
| Day 7 | Owner pays Draw #3 | Receive check |
| Day 12 | Check clears bank | Deliver **Unconditional Waiver on Progress Payment for $25,000** to owner |
| Day 14 | Submit Draw #4 with prior unconditional attached | Cover letter + invoice + Unconditional for #3 + **Conditional for #4** |

The unconditional from the prior draw is your evidence to the owner (and to the owner's lender, if any) that you have been paid through that point. The conditional for the current draw is your request for the new payment and your promise to release upon receipt.

### 5.5 How to collect waivers from your subs

You are the **owner-equivalent** to your subs. Mirror the same discipline upward:

- **Every sub progress payment**: require a conditional waiver with the invoice; require the prior-draw unconditional from each sub before issuing the next check.
- **At final**: require unconditional final waivers from every sub and supplier before issuing the final check to that party.
- **No waiver, no check** — this is a firm rule and should be in every subcontract.

A practical checklist (printed and used by office staff or PM):

```
SUBCONTRACTOR PAYMENT PACKET (per draw):
[ ] Subcontractor invoice
[ ] Conditional Waiver on Progress Payment (current draw amount) - signed
[ ] Unconditional Waiver on Progress Payment (prior draw amount) - signed
[ ] Updated lien waiver log
[ ] Preliminary notice on file (verify date)
[ ] License current (verify CSLB)
[ ] Workers comp current
[ ] Insurance certificates current
```

### 5.6 Custom lien waivers are illegal

Civil Code **§8122** prohibits requiring a claimant to waive lien rights except via the statutory forms. Owners — especially commercial owners trying to apply commercial practices to residential — sometimes insert custom waiver language into their contracts ("Contractor hereby releases all claims..."). **This language is void.** You cannot be required to sign it, and if you do sign it, the waiver is still ineffective. But: don't sign it anyway — it creates confusion and litigation expense even if ultimately unenforceable.

If an owner's contract requires custom waivers, redline the contract: "All lien waivers shall be in the statutory forms set forth in Civil Code §§8132–8138." If the owner refuses, walk away or proceed knowing you've identified a serious sophistication or good-faith problem.

### 5.7 Joint check agreements

A **joint check** is a check made payable jointly to the GC and a supplier (or sub and sub-supplier). Both parties must endorse for the check to be negotiable. This protects:

- **The owner**, who knows the money is reaching the actual labor/materials supplier and not just the GC.
- **The supplier**, who is guaranteed to see the money before the GC can spend it elsewhere.

Joint check agreements are common with material suppliers on jobs where the GC's credit is questionable, or where the supplier insists on direct access to payments. They are **not** lien waivers — receiving a joint check does not automatically release lien rights. The supplier should still execute conditional/unconditional waivers in the standard cycle.

A joint check arrangement is documented by a **joint check agreement** signed by all three parties (owner or GC, sub/supplier, and the writer of the check). Keep the agreement and the endorsed checks in the project file.

## 6. Contract Disputes and Breach

### 6.1 Contract notice requirements — the silent claim-killer

Most construction contracts (AIA A105/A201, ConsensusDocs, custom contracts drafted by owner's attorney) contain **notice provisions** requiring written notice of any claim within a short window — typically **7 to 30 days** of the event giving rise to the claim. Failure to give timely notice **waives the claim**, often regardless of merit.

Examples of events that trigger notice requirements:

- Discovery of differing site conditions (concealed conditions revealed during demo).
- Owner-directed changes that affect cost or schedule.
- Delays caused by owner, owner's separate contractors, or owner's consultants (architect).
- Force majeure events.
- Defective owner-supplied materials.

**Rule: when anything happens that could affect cost or schedule, send a written notice within 7 days regardless of what your contract says.** Sending early is harmless; sending late is fatal.

A simple template:

> "Pursuant to Section [X] of the contract dated [date], this letter provides notice of a [claim / change / delay / differing condition] arising from [event] which occurred on [date]. We estimate this will affect the [cost / schedule] by approximately [$X / Y days]. We will provide a detailed claim within [10] business days. This letter is provided to preserve our rights and is without prejudice to ongoing efforts to resolve this matter cooperatively."

Send certified mail or via email with read receipt. Save the proof.

### 6.2 Material vs. minor breach

Not every contract violation justifies stopping work or terminating. California recognizes:

- **Material (substantial) breach**: a violation that defeats the central purpose of the contract or substantially impairs the non-breaching party's expected benefit. Example: owner stops paying entirely for three consecutive draws.
- **Minor (partial) breach**: a violation that does not defeat the contract's central purpose. Example: owner pays a draw 5 days late.

A **material breach** generally allows the non-breaching party to:
- Suspend performance.
- Terminate the contract.
- Sue for total damages (including expectation damages — what they would have made on the rest of the contract).

A **minor breach** allows only:
- A claim for actual damages caused by the specific violation.
- Continued performance under the contract (no termination right).

**This determination is fact-specific and risky to make unilaterally.** Suspending or terminating on what a court later calls a minor breach exposes you to liability for breach yourself. When in doubt, document, give written notice, demand cure, and consult an attorney before suspending.

### 6.3 Suspension of work for non-payment — Civil Code §8800

California gives **statutory** rights to suspend for non-payment that exist independently of the contract. **Civil Code §8800** provides:

- An original (prime) contractor may **stop work** if the owner fails to make a progress payment within **35 days** of the date payment is due under the contract.
- The contractor must give the owner **written notice of intent to stop work** at least **10 days** before stopping (so a total of ~45 days from the original due date before work actually halts).
- The contractor is **not liable** for delay damages caused by the stoppage.

This is enormous. A non-statutory suspension may itself be a breach by the contractor. A §8800 suspension is **protected by statute** — you cannot be sued for stopping under §8800 if you followed the procedure.

**The script (residential remodel):**
- Day 0: Progress payment due under contract.
- Day 30: Payment still unpaid. Send first written demand.
- Day 35: Payment threshold under §8800 reached. Send formal **"Notice of Intent to Stop Work Pursuant to Civil Code §8800"** stating that work will stop if payment is not received within 10 days.
- Day 45: If still unpaid, work stops. Document the stoppage with photos.

### 6.4 Anticipatory repudiation

If the owner unequivocally states they will not pay future obligations or will not perform their part of the contract (e.g., refuses to make decisions, refuses to provide access), the contractor may treat this as a **present breach** even before the obligation is due. The contractor may:

- Suspend performance immediately.
- Sue for damages.
- Mitigate (find other work).

The repudiation must be **clear and unequivocal**. A homeowner saying "I'm not sure I can pay next month" is not repudiation. "I will not pay you any more money on this project" is repudiation.

### 6.5 Damages — types and limits

| Damage Category | What It Covers | Typical Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct (compensatory)** | The cost to complete the work, unpaid invoices, retainage, the value of work in place | Almost always recoverable |
| **Lost profit** | The profit margin you would have earned on the unfinished portion of the contract | Recoverable for material breach; calculation often disputed |
| **Consequential** | Indirect downstream damages — e.g., the owner's lost rental income, your lost bonding capacity, your reputational damage | **Almost always waived** in modern contracts; if not waived, narrowly construed and hard to prove |
| **Liquidated damages** | A pre-agreed amount (often per-day delay damages) | Enforceable if a reasonable forecast of damages, not a penalty |
| **Attorney fees** | Legal costs of enforcement | Only recoverable if the contract has a prevailing-party fee clause, OR a statute provides for fees (CCP §1717 makes one-way clauses bilateral) |
| **Interest** | Prejudgment interest at 10% (Civil Code §3289) on liquidated sums; 7% constitutional rate otherwise | Recoverable on liquidated claims |
| **Punitive** | Punishment for malicious conduct | Not recoverable for breach of contract alone — only on tort claims (fraud, conversion) |

### 6.6 Why the consequential damages waiver matters

A clause reading "Neither party shall be liable to the other for consequential damages" is in most modern contracts. **You want this clause.** Without it, an unhappy homeowner could potentially claim:

- Lost rental income during delayed move-in.
- Temporary housing costs.
- Diminution in value.
- Lost business if the property is income-producing.

These can dwarf the contract value. A consequential damages waiver caps your exposure roughly at the contract price. **Insist on a mutual consequential damages waiver in every contract.** Be aware: AIA forms include this by default; some attorney-drafted custom contracts delete it — read carefully.

### 6.7 Mitigation

Both parties have a duty to **mitigate damages** — to take reasonable steps to reduce losses, even when the other side is at fault. For a GC whose owner has breached:

- Promptly seek replacement work for crew and equipment.
- Sell or store materials.
- Do not let damages accumulate passively.

Failure to mitigate reduces recoverable damages by the amount that could have been avoided.

### 6.8 Constructive change orders

A **constructive change** occurs when the owner (or their agent, like the architect or homeowner's spouse-acting-as-PM) directs work outside the contract scope without issuing a formal written change order, and the contractor performs.

This is one of the most common residential dispute patterns: "Can you just move the outlet two feet?" → contractor moves it → owner refuses to pay extra because there was no signed CO.

**Defense via documentation:**

1. **Immediate written notice** the same day: "Per our conversation today at 2:15 PM on site, we are proceeding with the outlet relocation as directed. We estimate this will cost an additional $X and add Y hours. Please confirm via written change order. We will proceed with the work to maintain schedule, with the understanding that pricing for this change will be agreed upon promptly."
2. **Photo documentation** of before and after.
3. **Time tracking** of labor specifically on the change.
4. **Material receipts** specifically tagged to the change.

A constructive change order claim, properly documented, is recoverable as a contract claim. Without documentation, it becomes a he-said-she-said dispute that almost always favors the homeowner in residential litigation.

### 6.9 Cardinal change doctrine

When the cumulative effect of many changes is so substantial that it **fundamentally alters the nature of the original contract**, the contractor may invoke the **cardinal change doctrine**: treat the original contract as voided by the changes, and seek compensation on a **quantum meruit** (reasonable value of work performed) basis rather than at original contract rates.

A typical residential pattern: owner contracts for a kitchen remodel at $80,000. Over the course of the project, owner adds bathroom remodel, then changes structural layout, then adds a deck, then changes the kitchen finishes twice. Cumulative CO total now exceeds the original contract. **The original contract no longer describes the actual work**, and the contractor may argue cardinal change.

This is more an argument than a remedy — there's no statute, just common-law doctrine — but it's a useful frame in negotiation and litigation. The threshold is high: changes must be **outside the reasonable scope of changes contemplated by the parties at contracting**. Document carefully.

## 7. Dispute Resolution — Costs, Speed, and Realism

### 7.1 The escalation ladder

| Step | Cost (typical) | Time | Outcome Binding? | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Negotiation** | $0 (or attorney letter ~$500–$1,500) | Days to weeks | Only if settled | Almost every dispute starts here; should be tried before escalation |
| **Mediation** | $1,500–$5,000 (split) | 1–2 days | No, voluntary settlement | Disputes where both sides want resolution but need a neutral to help |
| **CSLB Arbitration** | $25–$300 filing fee | 4–8 months | Binding | Disputes ≤ $12,500 (mandatory) or ≤ $50,000 (voluntary if both consent) |
| **Small Claims Court** | $30–$75 filing fee | 30–90 days | Binding | Individual claims ≤ $12,500; business claims ≤ $6,250 |
| **Arbitration (AAA, JAMS)** | $5,000–$50,000+ | 6–18 months | Binding, very limited appeal | Required by contract; faster than litigation for mid-sized disputes |
| **Superior Court Litigation** | $20,000–$200,000+ | 2–4+ years | Binding | Large claims, complex disputes, or when arbitration is unavailable |

### 7.2 Negotiation — always first

The math is unforgiving. Even on a strong claim, by the time you net legal fees, expert witnesses, lost time, and the personal toll of litigation, **anything less than a 60% recovery via lawsuit is worse than a 50% settlement today**. Most experienced construction attorneys will say the same: "I would rather settle for 70¢ on the dollar today than win 100¢ in 24 months."

Tactics for negotiation:

- **Lead with the strongest documented item**. Don't argue the weakest piece of your claim first — establish credibility with what is irrefutable.
- **Acknowledge the other side's reasonable points**. This is not concession; it is positioning the conversation toward solvable issues.
- **Make the cost of litigation explicit** without threatening: "Look, you'll spend $20K, I'll spend $20K, and after a year of stress neither of us will be happy with the result. We're $15K apart today. Let's split that."
- **Document the settlement in writing**, signed by both parties, including a clear release of further claims for the specific items resolved.

### 7.3 Mediation

A **non-binding** process where a neutral mediator helps the parties reach a voluntary settlement. The mediator does not decide anything — they shuttle between the parties and propose resolutions.

Construction mediation is highly effective: **success rate often quoted at 70–80%**. Most construction contracts (AIA A105/A201, ConsensusDocs) require mediation as a precondition to litigation or arbitration.

Practical pointers:
- **Pick a mediator with construction experience.** AAA, JAMS, and local construction-specialist mediators are typical. A retired construction judge is often ideal.
- **Half-day mediations** ($1,500–$3,000) for simpler disputes; **full-day** ($3,000–$6,000) for complex ones.
- **Mediation briefs**: each side typically submits a short (5–10 page) brief in advance describing the dispute and their position.
- **Settlement at mediation is enforceable** under CCP §664.6 if reduced to a signed writing — get the settlement on paper before leaving the room.

### 7.4 Arbitration — fast, binding, hard to appeal

Most residential contracts (especially AIA) provide for **binding arbitration** under the **AAA Construction Industry Arbitration Rules**. Key features:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Decision-maker | One arbitrator (claims under $1M) or three (larger) — often a retired judge or construction lawyer |
| Discovery | Limited — usually one round of document requests, limited depositions, no interrogatories absent special order |
| Hearing | Quasi-trial; rules of evidence relaxed; arbitrator decides facts and law |
| Timeline | 6–18 months typical, vs. 2–4 years for litigation |
| Appeal | **Extremely limited** — only for arbitrator misconduct, exceeding authority, or manifest disregard of law. Wrong factual or legal decisions are generally NOT appealable |
| Cost | AAA filing fee scales with claim size ($1,000–$10,000+); arbitrator fees $300–$700/hr |
| Privacy | Private — not part of public record |

**Practical reality**: arbitration is faster and more private than litigation, but the costs are real and the "no appeal" feature cuts both ways. If you get a bad arbitrator who misapplies the law, you are stuck.

For residential contracts, arbitration is often **mandatory** if the contract calls for it. California law generally enforces arbitration clauses, with limited exceptions:

- Arbitration clauses in **adhesion contracts** (consumer contracts with one-sided terms) may be unenforceable if **unconscionable** (both procedurally and substantively). California courts scrutinize these.
- **Class action waivers** in arbitration clauses are enforced under federal law (*Concepcion*).

### 7.5 Litigation in Superior Court

For larger disputes or where arbitration is unavailable:

- **Filing fee**: ~$435 in California (limited civil) or $435+ (unlimited civil — claims over $25,000).
- **Pleadings**: Complaint, Answer, Cross-Complaint typical.
- **Discovery**: Full discovery — interrogatories, document requests, depositions, expert designation.
- **Motions**: Demurrers, motions to compel, motions for summary judgment, motions in limine.
- **Trial**: Bench (judge) or jury. Construction trials average 5–15 court days.
- **Appeal**: Yes — Court of Appeal then potentially Supreme Court. Adds 1–3 more years.

**Cost reality**: a construction case through trial in California typically costs **$50,000–$300,000 per side**, depending on complexity. Reserve for claims **over ~$100,000** where settlement has failed.

### 7.6 Small Claims Court

| Limit | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Individual claim limit** | $12,500 (CCP §116.220) |
| **Business entity claim limit** | $6,250 |
| **Attorneys** | Not allowed (CCP §116.530) — but you can consult one before filing |
| **Filing fee** | $30–$75 depending on amount |
| **Timeline** | Hearing typically 30–70 days from filing |
| **Appeal** | Plaintiff cannot appeal; defendant can appeal to Superior Court (de novo retrial) |

**Practical use**: small unpaid invoices ($1,000–$12,500). The process is genuinely fast and the lack of attorneys means you must present your own case — but for a clear unpaid invoice with good documentation, this is the cheapest, fastest collection method.

For a corporation or LLC, you must send an officer/owner — not an attorney. Bring contract, invoices, all written communications, photos, and the lien waivers showing prior payments (to establish the unpaid balance).

### 7.7 CSLB complaint and arbitration process

The **Contractors State License Board** (CSLB) operates a complaint and arbitration program for disputes involving licensed contractors.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Complaint** | Any consumer can file a complaint with CSLB about a licensed contractor |
| **Mandatory arbitration** | For disputes ≤ $12,500 — both sides required to participate |
| **Voluntary arbitration** | For disputes between $12,501 and $50,000 — both parties must agree |
| **Decision** | Issued by an independent arbitrator; binding |
| **Effect on license** | Failure to pay an arbitration award can result in **license suspension** — this is the leverage |
| **Filing** | Through CSLB website (cslb.ca.gov) |

For a contractor, the CSLB process is double-edged:

- **You as claimant** (against another contractor, e.g., a sub): can be effective for smaller claims.
- **You as respondent** (homeowner complaining about you): **take this seriously**. A CSLB finding against you affects your license. Respond promptly, provide complete documentation, and consider an attorney even though the CSLB process is informal.

### 7.8 California statute of limitations

| Claim Type | Statute | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Written contract | CCP §337 | **4 years** from breach |
| Oral contract | CCP §339 | **2 years** from breach |
| Open book account (running account) | CCP §337 | 4 years from last entry |
| Mechanic's lien foreclosure | Civil Code §8460 | **90 days** from lien recording |
| Stop notice action | Civil Code §8550–§8556 | **90 days** from expiration of period to record stop notice |
| Personal injury / property damage | CCP §335.1 | **2 years** |
| Patent construction defect | CCP §337.1 | **4 years** from substantial completion |
| Latent construction defect | CCP §337.15 | **10 years** from substantial completion |
| Fraud | CCP §338(d) | 3 years from discovery |
| Negligent professional services (architect, engineer) | CCP §339, §337.15 | Varies |

The **10-year latent defect window** (CCP §337.15) is the long tail of residential construction risk. Years after substantial completion, a homeowner can sue for latent (hidden, not reasonably discoverable) defects — typically structural, waterproofing, or hidden mechanical issues. This is why post-completion document retention matters: **keep your project files for at least 11 years**.

## 8. Common Residential Disputes and How to Prevent Them

Each of the patterns below kills relationships and creates lawsuits. Each is preventable with discipline at the beginning of the project.

### 8.1 Scope disputes — "We didn't agree to that"

**Pattern**: Homeowner expects something to be included that the contractor priced as excluded (or never priced at all). "Of course painting was included — why else would I hire a remodeler?"

**Prevention**:
- **Written scope of work** in the contract, with explicit **inclusions** and **exclusions**.
- For residential, include a separate **exclusions list** in plain English: "This contract does NOT include: appliances, light fixtures, window coverings, landscaping, ..."
- Walk the scope with the homeowner before signing; initial each page.
- If the architect's drawings have ambiguity, identify it in the contract with a stated assumption ("Floor finishes assumed to be 5/8" engineered hardwood; alternates require change order").

### 8.2 Change order disputes — unsigned work and verbal approvals

**Pattern**: Homeowner directs a change verbally; contractor performs; homeowner refuses to pay because there was no written CO.

**Prevention**:
- **No work without written CO** — make this a contract term and a hard rule.
- Use a one-page change order form: scope, price, schedule impact, signature, date.
- If the homeowner won't sign before work, send a same-day **"Confirmation of Direction"** letter (see §6.8 above) documenting what was directed and your understanding of the cost — this creates a paper trail even without a signed CO.
- Track all CO's in a CO log on the project; reconcile monthly.

### 8.3 Payment disputes — missing draws, disputed retainage

**Pattern**: Homeowner withholds a draw because they're "not happy with something"; contractor stops work; both sides escalate.

**Prevention**:
- **Contractual payment schedule** with clear milestones: each draw tied to specific completed work (e.g., "Draw 4 due upon completion of rough framing inspection sign-off").
- **Retainage** capped (5–10% standard); explicit release at substantial completion with a defined punch list process.
- **Mid-project payment certifications** signed by both parties (sometimes by architect).
- **Late payment**: send formal notice on Day 30; invoke §8800 procedure on Day 35.

### 8.4 Delay claims — owner-caused delays blamed on GC

**Pattern**: Homeowner can't decide on tile (4 weeks); change order changes structural plan (2 weeks); inspector finds something on owner's existing structure (1 week). Owner then complains "you're 7 weeks behind schedule!"

**Prevention**:
- **Daily logs** documenting work performed, materials received, weather, visitors, AND **delays caused by owner decisions or owner-supplied items**.
- **Written delay notices** at the moment a delay occurs: "On [date], owner had not yet selected tile, delaying tile installation. We estimate a 3-week schedule impact unless selection is made by [date]."
- **Schedule updates** monthly, with revised completion dates documented.
- A baseline schedule with logic ties showing critical path is gold-standard. Even a simple Gantt chart with predecessor relationships is far better than nothing.

### 8.5 Defect claims — post-completion warranty issues

**Pattern**: Months after substantial completion, homeowner complains of a leak / crack / squeak / sticky door and demands free repair, escalating to "the whole thing is defective."

**Prevention**:
- **Inspection records** kept for every inspection (city, third-party, in-house).
- **Test results** for waterproofing, pressure tests, etc.
- **Substantial completion walkthrough** documented with photos and a signed punch list.
- **Warranty language** in the contract: clear coverage period (typically 1 year on workmanship, manufacturer terms on materials), procedure for claims (written notice, opportunity to inspect, opportunity to cure).
- After-occupancy photographs of completed work — establishes baseline condition.
- For latent defects (the 10-year tail), document **everything** during construction.

### 8.6 Material substitution disputes

**Pattern**: Specified material is back-ordered; contractor proposes substitution; homeowner verbally OK's it; later complains the substitution was inferior.

**Prevention**:
- **Written substitution approval** every time, no exceptions. Email is sufficient if it identifies the original spec and the substitute by name/model number.
- For a phone or in-person conversation, send a same-day email confirming: "Confirming our conversation today: due to back-order of XYZ, we are substituting ABC at the same price. Please confirm via reply."
- **Manufacturer cut sheets** for both original and substitute, with notes on differences.

### 8.7 Abandonment claims — when the GC stops work

**Pattern**: GC stops work due to non-payment. Homeowner sues for abandonment, claiming the GC's stoppage was wrongful and caused damage to the unfinished project.

**Prevention** — this is where **Civil Code §8800** protects you:
- Document the non-payment carefully (invoices sent, due dates, no payment received).
- Send the formal §8800 notice **10 days before stopping**.
- After stopping, **secure the site** (locked, weather-protected, materials accounted for) — abandonment claims often turn on whether the site was left in a hazardous state.
- Continue communicating: a §8800 stoppage is a pause, not a termination. Make clear in writing that you remain ready to resume on payment of the outstanding amount.

A §8800 stoppage followed by a recorded mechanic's lien is the standard escalation sequence for serious non-payment.

## 9. Documentation Systems — Protecting Yourself Day by Day

The single biggest predictor of who wins a construction dispute is **who has the better contemporaneous documentation**. Not who is right, not who is more sympathetic, not who has the better lawyer — **whose paper trail is more complete and credible**.

### 9.1 Daily log — non-negotiable

A daily log entry should capture:

| Field | Notes |
|---|---|
| Date and weather | Including high/low temp, precipitation, wind if relevant |
| Workers on site | By trade and headcount (e.g., "4 framers, 2 electricians, 1 plumber") |
| Hours worked | Start/end times |
| Work performed | Specific tasks completed, with reference to drawing locations |
| Materials received | Quantities, delivery tickets attached |
| Visitors | Owner, architect, inspector, subs not in production, etc. |
| Inspections | Type, inspector, result |
| Photos | Multiple per day, including any out-of-ordinary conditions |
| Issues / RFIs | Anything that came up requiring resolution |
| Delays | Owner-caused, weather, sub no-shows |
| Safety incidents | Anything from minor to OSHA-reportable |

**Tools**:
- **Buildertrend** ($500–$700/mo for small GCs): residential-focused, integrates with accounting, owner portal, daily logs.
- **Procore** (enterprise pricing): commercial-grade, larger residential GCs.
- **CompanyCam**: photo-focused, cheap, GPS-tagged photos.
- **Raken**: daily reports specifically.
- **Google Drive + Sheets + Photos**: free, works if you have discipline — folder per project, log spreadsheet, daily photos.

A daily log is **not optional**. It is the cheapest insurance you can buy against any future dispute.

### 9.2 Notice letters — "covering your file"

Whenever any dispute, delay, or unusual event occurs, send a written letter or email that day. This is called "covering your file" — building a contemporaneous record of your position so that, months later, your version of events is the documented one.

**Standard notice letter template**:

```
[Date]
To: [Owner / GC / Architect]
Re: [Project name and address]
Subject: [Issue]

This letter is to document [event] which occurred on [date].

The relevant facts are:
1. [Fact]
2. [Fact]
3. [Fact]

Our position is: [position]

We request: [action, decision, payment]

We reserve all rights under the contract and applicable law.

Please respond by [date].

Sincerely,
[Name]
```

Send via certified mail or email with read receipt. File a copy.

### 9.3 Change order discipline

Already covered in §6.8 — **never perform out-of-scope work without a signed change order or a same-day documented confirmation.** If you have to choose between a 1-hour delay to get a signature and a future $5,000 dispute, take the 1-hour delay every time.

### 9.4 Lien waiver tracking

A simple log per project:

| Date | Draw # | Amount | Conditional Sent | Date Paid | Date Cleared | Unconditional Sent |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mar 1 | 3 | $25,000 | Mar 1 | Mar 8 | Mar 12 | Mar 13 |
| Mar 15 | 4 | $30,000 | Mar 15 | (pending) | | |

Mirror this for every sub: each sub gets a row per draw, columns for conditional/unconditional received.

### 9.5 Photo documentation

Photos are the second-most-important documentation after the daily log. Best practices:

- **Daily progress photos** — minimum 5–10 per day, covering the full work area.
- **Before-cover photos** — extensive coverage before drywall, before backfill, before siding, before flashing is concealed. This protects you against later allegations that something wasn't installed correctly.
- **GPS-tagged photos** (built into modern phone cameras and apps like CompanyCam) — proves location and time.
- **Date-organized storage** — folders by date or auto-organized by app.
- **Backup** — never rely on a single device. Cloud sync, twice.

A residential project should generate **1,000–5,000 photos** over its lifecycle. If you have fewer than 100, you have inadequate documentation.

### 9.6 Meeting minutes

For any meeting where decisions are made — OAC (owner-architect-contractor) meetings, on-site reviews, decision meetings with the homeowner — write up minutes the same day and email them to all attendees:

```
Meeting: [Type] — [Project] — [Date]
Attendees: [Names]

Discussion points:
1. [Topic]
2. [Topic]

Decisions made:
1. [Decision] — responsibility: [name]
2. [Decision]

Action items:
1. [Action] — owner: [name] — due: [date]

Please reply within 48 hours with any corrections.
```

Silence after sending becomes effective ratification of the minutes. If the homeowner later says "I never agreed to that," you have a written record they did not dispute.

### 9.7 Document retention

Keep all project files (contract, drawings, daily logs, photos, RFIs, change orders, lien notices, lien waivers, correspondence, inspection records, warranty documents) for **at least 11 years** after substantial completion (covers the 10-year latent defect window plus a buffer).

Cloud storage costs are trivial; the value of a complete file 8 years later when a leak appears is enormous.

## 10. Working with a Construction Attorney

### 10.1 When to hire one

| Situation | Hire an Attorney? |
|---|---|
| Reviewing a large new contract ($500K+) | **Yes** — 1–3 hours, often $1,000–$3,000, pays for itself |
| First time using AIA forms | **Yes** — onboarding investment |
| Lien enforcement (foreclosure suit) | **Yes** — procedural complexity, fee-shifting possible |
| Owner sues you | **Yes** — immediately |
| You want to sue an owner for > $25,000 | **Yes** |
| Owner files CSLB complaint against you | **Strongly consider** — license at stake |
| Settlement negotiation on a 6-figure dispute | **Yes** — leverage and structure |
| Business formation, partnership, succession | **Yes** |
| Small payment dispute (< $12,500) | Usually no — use small claims |
| Routine subcontract drafting (after initial template) | No, but have an attorney develop the template once |
| Routine lien filing | No, but use a lien service or attorney-reviewed template |

### 10.2 What to expect on hourly rate

| Specialty | Range (California, 2025) |
|---|---|
| General business attorney handling construction | $300–$450/hr |
| Construction-specialist attorney | $400–$600/hr |
| Senior partner at construction firm | $600–$900/hr |
| Construction litigation specialist (trial) | $500–$700/hr |
| CSLB defense attorney | $350–$500/hr |

**Tips**:
- Ask for a **flat fee** on discrete matters (contract review, demand letter, lien preparation). Many construction attorneys will quote $500–$2,500 flat.
- **Retainer agreements** are standard — typically $2,500–$10,000 against which hourly is billed.
- **Itemized billing** is required by ethics rules — review monthly.
- **Negotiate** — many attorneys will discount routine work or cap fees for repeat clients.

### 10.3 Alternatives to a full attorney engagement

| Alternative | Best For |

**Vetting questions for any prospective attorney**:
1. What percentage of your practice is construction law?
2. How many residential cases like mine have you handled in the last 3 years?
3. What's your typical fee structure for a matter like mine?
4. Who else in the firm will work on this matter, and at what rates?
5. What's your assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of my case in 5 minutes?

Question 5 is the litmus test. A construction attorney with real experience will give you a candid initial read in a free consultation. A vague answer ("hard to say without more information") is fine for the first call; but the attorney should be able to identify the **key issues** quickly.

### 11.4 Red flags during a project (act now, not later)

| Red Flag | Action |
|---|---|
| Owner late on first draw | Send reminder same day; tighten approach |
| Owner changes mind frequently | Insist on written CO for every change |
| Owner brings in a "consultant" or attorney mid-project | Tighten documentation; consider attorney review |
| Owner stops responding to RFIs | Send written delay notice each time |

| Subs threaten to lien | Pay them or address dispute immediately; protect your relationship with owner |

## 12. Closing Principles

1. **Calendar discipline is the foundation.** Every deadline in this body of law is jurisdictional. Use a system — Buildertrend, a CRM, a paper calendar, whatever you'll actually maintain — and put every deadline in it twice.

2. **Documentation is cheaper than litigation by three orders of magnitude.** An hour of daily logs costs $50. A week of trial costs $50,000. Spend the hour.

3. **The lien is a negotiation tool, not a weapon.** Record liens when the underlying claim is real and the deadline is approaching. Use the cloud on title to bring the owner to the table. Foreclosure is a last resort.

4. **Use the statutory forms.** California's four-form waiver scheme exists precisely so that ordinary contractors don't need to draft legal documents. Use them. Refuse to sign anything else.

5. **The conditional/unconditional distinction protects your cash flow.** Never give an unconditional waiver before the check clears. Never accept a custom waiver from anyone, ever.

6. **§8800 is your friend.** When the owner stops paying, the statute gives you the right to stop working without breach liability. Follow the procedure (35-day, 10-day notice), document, and stop.

7. **Mediation works.** Construction disputes settle at mediation 70–80% of the time. Try it before arbitration or litigation in every case.

8. **Get an attorney early on big matters.** A $2,000 contract review can prevent a $200,000 lawsuit. The math is not subtle.

9. **Don't lien out of anger.** A frivolous lien exposes you to attorney-fee liability under §8424 and slander-of-title damages. Lien only when the claim is solid and documented.

10. **Keep the file for 11 years.** The 10-year latent defect tail (CCP §337.15) is the longest exposure you have. The cost of cloud storage is zero. There is no excuse for losing project documents.

The contractors who survive payment disputes — and they all face them eventually — are not the ones with the toughest stance or the meanest attorney. They are the ones whose documentation is so thorough, whose notices are so timely, and whose lien posture is so technically correct that the dispute resolves quickly in their favor because **the other side's attorney advises settlement after seeing the paper trail**.

That is the goal: not to win in court, but to make sure you never have to.
