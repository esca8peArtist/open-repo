# Residential Construction Finance and Lending for the General Contractor

## Why This Module Exists

Construction lending is the unseen plumbing of nearly every project a residential GC touches. The client signs your contract, but the lender writes the checks. If you do not understand how a construction loan works — how draws are funded, when retainage is released, what triggers an inspector visit, what a stop payment notice does to a recalcitrant lender — you will be the one floating payroll while your clients wait for "the bank."

This module is written for the working California GC who needs to:

1. **Manage cash flow** through draw cycles without bleeding personal credit lines.
2. **Protect lien rights** in a triangular owner–lender–contractor relationship governed by the California Civil Code.
3. **Advise clients** intelligently when they come to you with a Pinterest board, a lot, and a vague idea about "getting a loan."
4. **Finance their own deals** — first a personal home build, then a spec, then perhaps a small infill development.

The financial literacy required to run a residential GC business in California is roughly equivalent to that of a community-bank loan officer. This document closes that gap.

## 1. Construction Lending Basics

### 1.1 Construction Loans vs. Permanent Mortgages

A **permanent mortgage** is what most people call "a mortgage" — a fully amortizing loan of 15 to 30 years secured by a completed, occupiable structure. The lender's collateral is a finished house on a finished lot. Risk is comparatively low; the market for the asset is well established; the borrower pays principal and interest from day one.

A **construction loan** is something else entirely. It funds a **process**, not a finished asset. The lender is exposed to:

- **Completion risk** — the house may never get built.
- **Cost overrun risk** — the budget may not be sufficient.
- **Collateral risk** — a half-built house is worth far less than a finished one.
- **Mechanic's lien risk** — unpaid subs and suppliers can file liens that compete with the lender's deed of trust.

Because of these risks, construction loans are structured very differently:

| Feature | Construction Loan | Permanent Mortgage |
|---|---|---|
| Term | 6–18 months (residential), 18–36 months (commercial / multi-unit) | 15, 20, or 30 years |
| Payment structure | Interest-only on **disbursed** balance | Principal + interest on full balance |
| Disbursement | Drawn in stages tied to completion | Lump sum at closing |
| Rate | Variable (Prime + spread) or short-term fixed; typically higher than perm | Fixed or ARM; typically lower |
| Underwriting | LTC and as-completed LTV; project budget, plans, GC credentials | LTV and DTI of the borrower |
| Collateral monitoring | Active — inspections at each draw | Passive — annual escrow / tax review |
| Exit | Must be paid off or converted to perm at completion | Amortizes to zero over term |

The interest-only structure during construction is what makes a build financeable for most homeowners. They are typically still living in (and paying for) their existing house. A fully amortizing loan on top of their existing mortgage would be untenable.

### 1.2 Construction-to-Permanent (C-to-P) Loans

A C-to-P loan packages both the construction loan and the permanent mortgage. There are two flavors:

**Single Close (One-Time Close, OTC)**

- One application, one underwriting, one closing, one set of fees.
- Permanent rate locked at closing (sometimes with a one-time float-down option at conversion).
- At substantial completion, the loan automatically "modifies" or "converts" — no second closing.
- Borrower qualifies once based on as-completed value and projected DTI.
- Best when rates are stable or trending up, and when the borrower wants simplicity.

**Two-Time Close (Two-Step)**

- Construction loan closes first; permanent mortgage closes separately at completion.
- Two underwriting cycles, two appraisals (one as-completed, one final), two sets of closing costs.
- Borrower must re-qualify at conversion — risk if their financial picture changes (job loss, dependent, etc.).
- Permits "shopping" the perm mortgage at completion — can capture better rates if they have fallen.
- Best for sophisticated borrowers, complex custom builds, longer timelines, or rate-decline environments.

**Practical implication for the GC**: With a single-close C-to-P, the loan is already in place and the client's "permanent" rate is fixed. They are less likely to balk at a change order that delays substantial completion. With a two-close, the client is racing the clock against rate volatility and re-qualification — they are more likely to push hard on schedule and budget.

### 1.3 What the Lender Wants

A construction lender has three core anxieties. Every policy, form, and process flows from these:

1. **That funds are used for construction** — not diverted to other debts, vacations, or the GC's last project. Hence: draw inspections, lien releases, title updates.
2. **That the project is actually completed** — a half-finished house is nearly worthless. Hence: completion guarantees, contingency reserves, contractor vetting, schedule monitoring.
3. **That the as-completed collateral covers the loan** — if the borrower defaults, the lender needs to sell the finished house and recover principal plus costs. Hence: LTV limits, conservative appraisals, market analysis.

### 1.4 LTC vs. LTV

Construction lenders underwrite on **two** ratios simultaneously. The loan must satisfy **both** caps. The lower of the two governs.

**Loan-to-Cost (LTC)** = Loan Amount / Total Project Cost

- Total Project Cost includes land, hard costs (labor + materials), soft costs (permits, design, engineering), financing costs (interest reserve, points), and contingency.
- Tells the lender how much "skin in the game" the borrower has.
- Higher LTC = lender takes more risk; lender requires more borrower equity.

**Loan-to-Value (LTV)** = Loan Amount / As-Completed Appraised Value

- Based on appraiser's projection of what the finished home will sell for in current market.
- Tells the lender how much cushion exists if they have to foreclose and sell.
- Higher LTV = less cushion; harder to recover principal in distress.

**Typical California Residential Ranges (2025–2026 market)**:

| Loan Type | LTC | LTV (as-completed) |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional owner-occupied (bank / credit union) | 75–85% | 75–80% |
| Conventional owner-occupied (post-2025 tightening) | 65–75% | 65–75% |
| Jumbo owner-occupied | 70–80% | 70–75% |
| Investor / spec (bank) | 65–75% | 60–70% |
| Hard money / private (spec) | 75–85% (sometimes 90%) | 65–75% |
| FHA 203(k) | 96.5% LTV (with mortgage insurance) | Per FHA limits |
| VA construction | Up to 100% LTV (entitlement-based) | N/A |

The post-2024 tightening reflects construction cost volatility, appraisal uncertainty, and bank concerns about the regional economic cycle. Treat any "up to 85% LTC" advertisement as a ceiling for ideal borrowers, not a default.

### 1.5 Construction Loan Timeline

A typical owner-occupied California construction loan, application to first draw:

| Phase | Typical Duration | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-application & shopping | 1–4 weeks | Borrower compares lenders, gathers documents, selects GC |
| Application & document collection | 1–2 weeks | Income, assets, plans, contract, budget, schedule, GC credentials |
| Underwriting | 2–4 weeks | Lender reviews borrower, project budget, GC qualifications |
| Appraisal (as-completed) | 1–3 weeks | Appraiser values the project as if completed today |
| Loan approval & disclosures | 1 week | Final terms, CD issued, 3-day rescission for owner-occ |
| Title, insurance, recording | 1–2 weeks | Title search, prelim, builder's risk binder, deed of trust prep |
| Loan closing | 1 day | Documents signed, deed of trust recorded |
| Construction commencement | Same day or per schedule | Critical: GC must NOT commence before deed of trust records |
| First draw request | 30–45 days after commencement | After enough work to justify a draw inspection |
| Mid-construction draws | Monthly | Subject to inspection, lien releases, title updates |
| Substantial completion | Per schedule | Final inspection, CofO, final draw less retainage |
| Final draw + retainage release | 30–60 days post-completion | After all lien release periods expire |
| Conversion to permanent (if C-to-P) | At completion or 30–60 days after | OTC: automatic modification; two-close: full refi |

**Application to first draw: 60–90 days is realistic. Tell your clients this.** Many homeowners assume "we have a loan" means money is available next week. It does not.

## 2. The Draw Process — The GC's Primary Interface with the Lender

This is where the GC actually lives. Everything else in this document supports getting this section right.

### 2.1 How a Draw Actually Works

The basic flow:

1. **GC completes work** to a milestone or percent-complete threshold.
2. **Owner (sometimes GC) submits a draw request** to the lender with supporting documentation (invoices, lien releases from prior draw, photos).
3. **Lender orders an inspection** — typically within 1–3 business days.
4. **Inspector visits the site** and certifies the percent-complete of each budget line item.
5. **Lender reconciles** inspector's report against the budget, deducts retainage and any prior overdraws, and approves the disbursement amount.
6. **Lender funds the draw** — either directly to the owner (who then pays the GC and subs) or, in California, often through the title / escrow company.
7. **GC pays subs and suppliers** from the funds received.
8. **At the next draw**, GC produces signed lien releases from subs and suppliers proving the prior draw funds reached them.

The cycle repeats roughly monthly. Each cycle is 10–21 days end-to-end (request to funding), with the inspection-to-funding leg typically 5–10 business days.

### 2.2 Draw Schedule vs. Cost-to-Complete

This is the single biggest source of friction between GCs and construction lenders.

**The GC thinks**: "I just paid the framer $80K. I need $80K from the next draw to refill cash."

**The lender thinks**: "What percent of total framing budget is now complete? If framing is 60% done, and total framing budget is $120K, I can fund up to $72K against framing, minus 10% retainage, minus any prior framing draws."

These two views often diverge. The GC's expense in a given period may be front-loaded (large material orders, deposit payments). The lender funds based on percent of physically observable, in-place work. Materials sitting in the driveway or on a shelf may or may not be fundable — see "stored materials" below.

The single most useful schedule a GC can produce for a construction loan is a **Schedule of Values (SOV)** that breaks the contract sum into 15–40 line items, each with a dollar value summing to the contract total. The lender funds against percent-complete of each line. Aligning the SOV with the lender's standard cost categories — instead of forcing the lender to map your line items to their categories — accelerates every draw.

**SOV best practices for getting paid faster**:

- Break out **mobilization** and **demolition** as their own line items (early money).
- Separate **rough** from **finish** in each trade (paid earlier).
- Use **material delivery + install** sub-lines for long-lead items (cabinets, windows, HVAC equipment) so material delivery alone can support a partial draw.
- Keep **General Conditions** as a discrete line that draws monthly in proportion to schedule elapsed, not percent-complete — most lenders accept this.
- Avoid huge "Final / Punch / Closeout" lines, which become trapped behind retainage.

### 2.3 The Inspector

Three common models:

**Bank-employed inspector** (rare in 2026; mostly large national lenders): An employee of the lender. Less responsive to the GC; tends to be conservative on percent-complete; insulated from the GC relationship.

**Third-party construction inspector** (most common): A field inspection company (Land Gorilla, Granite Inspection, GreenStreet, NTS, etc.) contracted by the lender. Reports digitally with photos and line-item percentages. Generally professional and accountable.

**Title-company disbursement inspector** (common in California): Title companies offering construction control / disbursement services use their own field reps. The same firm orders the title update and handles the disbursement.

What the inspector checks at each visit:

- Physical percent-complete of each SOV line.
- Visible quality and code-compliance flags (not a substitute for the building inspector — the lender's inspector is checking that the work was done, not that it passed).
- Site security, weather protection, and obvious safety issues.
- Photos of every major area for the file.
- For some lenders: stored materials count and protection.

**Working with the inspector**:

1. Walk the inspector — do not let them wander unaccompanied. You will end up calibrating their percent-complete in real time.
2. Have the SOV printed and your own percent-complete marked. Discuss any line where you disagree.
3. Show them progress in areas they cannot see — concealed plumbing, blocking, mechanical work in the attic.
4. Provide photos of work covered since the last visit (e.g., foundation rebar before pour).
5. Be professional and consistent. The same inspector will probably visit every month.

### 2.4 Draw Schedule vs. GC Payment Schedule — The Cash Flow Trap

This is where GCs go broke.

**The lender's reality**: Pays in arrears, after inspection, against percent-complete, less retainage.

**The GC's reality**: Pays subs progress payments after work is done (typically Net 10–30), pays material suppliers on delivery or Net 30, makes deposits weeks in advance for long-lead items.

The gap looks like this for a typical monthly draw cycle:

| Day | Event |
|---|---|
| 1 | Start of draw period |
| 5 | Place cabinet deposit ($15K out) |
| 10 | Framer mid-month progress invoice ($25K out, due Net 15) |
| 15 | Lumber delivery ($18K out, COD) |
| 25 | Plumbing rough invoice ($12K out, due Net 30) |
| 30 | End of period — submit draw |
| 32 | Inspection ordered |
| 36 | Inspection performed |
| 40 | Lender approves $60K draw (after retainage and reconciliation) |
| 42 | Funds released to owner / escrow |
| 44 | Owner pays GC $60K |

From day 5 to day 44 — about six weeks — the GC has fronted up to $70K. Across multiple jobs, this is six-figure working capital under the best of circumstances.

**Tools for managing the gap**:

- **Business line of credit** sized to 1.5–2x peak project gap (see Section 9).
- **Sub payment terms aligned to draws**, not invoice dates: contract language tying sub payment to GC's receipt of corresponding draw funds ("pay-when-paid"). Note: California limits "pay-if-paid" but permits "pay-when-paid" with a reasonable time qualifier. Review with counsel.
- **Material supplier credit accounts** with Net 30 or Net 45 terms — pushed out past the draw cycle.
- **Owner deposits at contract signing** — typically capped at 10% or $1,000 for home improvement contracts in California (Bus. & Prof. Code § 7159), but on new construction contracts the cap does not apply the same way; structure carefully with counsel.
- **Bi-monthly draws** on larger jobs — twice as much paperwork but halves the float window.
- **Front-loaded SOV** (not over-loaded — see §2.6) — captures early money for cash position.

### 2.5 Draw Frequency

| Frequency | When Used | Cash Flow Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly | Default for most lenders, owner-occ and investor | Standard 30–45 day cycle |
| Bi-monthly (semi-monthly) | Larger projects (>$1M), some commercial | Better cash flow; double the paperwork |
| Per-milestone (foundation, framing, etc.) | FHA 203(k), some VA, some hard money | Worst cash flow; only 4–6 draws total |
| Cost-to-cost on receipt | Rare; bonded jobs only | Best cash flow; uncommon in residential |

Lock the draw frequency at contract / loan origination. Many lenders will allow a one-time switch from monthly to bi-monthly on request mid-project if the GC demonstrates the need and there are no draw disputes pending. Ask early — it can save the project.

### 2.6 Stored Materials

A perpetual flashpoint. Most California construction lenders will fund **some** stored materials under specific conditions:

| Lender Type | Stored Materials Policy (typical) |
|---|---|
| Conventional bank, owner-occ | 50–80% of invoice value; must be on-site, secured, insured |
| Off-site stored (e.g., cabinet shop, custom millwork) | Often refused; some allow with bonded warehouse and UCC filing |
| Long-lead items (HVAC equipment, windows) | More flexible; lenders recognize the supply chain reality |
| Hard money | More flexible; some fund 100% of stored on-site |
| FHA 203(k) | Generally not fundable until installed |

The GC's defensive playbook for stored materials:

1. **Identify long-lead items at contract** and flag them in the SOV as separate "material delivery" lines.
2. **Ask the lender's policy in writing** before ordering — get the percent fundable, the documentation required (bill of sale, photos, on-site delivery receipt), and the storage / insurance requirements.
3. **Get the supplier paid** off the stored-materials draw before installation. If the lender will not fund the full invoice, the GC needs a plan for the gap.
4. **Title to stored materials**: ensure your supplier contract transfers title on delivery (not installation). Otherwise, you cannot prove ownership and the lender will refuse the draw.

### 2.7 Documents Required at Each Draw

A typical draw package:

| Document | Who Provides | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Draw request form (lender-specific) | Owner / GC | SOV line items with prior, current, and to-date percent and dollars |
| GC's pencil schedule / itemized invoice | GC | Backup for the request |
| Sworn statement / draw affidavit | GC (sometimes Owner) | "All bills for prior draw are paid, no unrecorded liens" |
| Conditional lien release — current draw | GC, subs, suppliers | Cal. Civ. Code § 8132 or § 8136 (final) format |
| Unconditional lien release — prior draw | GC, subs, suppliers | Cal. Civ. Code § 8134 or § 8138 (final) format |
| Title update / date-down endorsement | Title company (ordered by lender) | Confirms no new liens recorded |
| Photos of progress | GC or inspector | Lender file documentation |
| Change order log | GC | Reflects any approved CO impact on budget |
| Building department inspections passed | GC | Some lenders sync to permit milestones |
| Insurance certificates (current) | GC | Builder's risk, GL, WC — must be current |

**California lien release forms — get this right**:

| Form | Civil Code | When Used | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment | § 8132 | Submitted with **this** draw request | Releases lien rights through "through date" **only when payment clears** |
| Unconditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment | § 8134 | Submitted at **next** draw, covering prior draw | Releases lien rights **regardless** of payment status |
| Conditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment | § 8136 | At final draw / retainage release | Releases all lien rights **only when final payment clears** |
| Unconditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment | § 8138 | After final payment received | Releases all lien rights immediately |

The forms must substantially follow the statutory language. A non-conforming release is not enforceable against the claimant — but it is also not enforceable to release the lien. Many California title companies and lenders provide pre-filled statutory forms; use them. Module 13 covers the lien-release flow in greater depth.

### 2.8 Retainage

Construction lenders typically hold back **10% of each draw** until substantial completion. On private residential projects in California, **as of January 1, 2026, Civil Code § 8811 caps retention at 5% for most private projects**, including residential — a significant change from the traditional 10%. This applies to the owner's withholding from the GC and the GC's withholding from subs. The lender, however, is contractually distinct: the construction loan documents may still authorize the lender to retain 10% of each disbursement as security for completion. Read your loan agreement carefully.

**Implications for the GC**:

- The GC's draw from the owner is now capped at 5% retention; the lender may still hold 10% of disbursements. Therefore the GC may **receive 95% of earned value while the lender disburses 90%** — the owner must cover the difference from equity, or the GC carries it.
- Plan for retention release as a separate cash event 30–60 days after substantial completion, after the lien release period expires.
- Retainage is the lender's leverage to enforce punch list completion. Substantial completion ≠ final completion ≠ retention release. Track all three dates separately.
- Sub-tier retention: GC withholds 5% from subs; this must align with what GC receives from owner. Do not over-withhold (you become the bank to your subs) or under-withhold (you lose leverage on punch list).

## 3. Lien Rights Protection in a Construction Loan Context

The construction loan adds a powerful third party — the lender — to the lien-rights triangle. Understanding the lender's position is essential to protecting your own.

This section assumes familiarity with the core California mechanic's lien regime covered in Module 13. The focus here is the construction-loan-specific overlay.

### 3.1 The Lender Is Not a Neutral Party

The construction loan is secured by a **deed of trust** recorded against the property at loan closing. The lender (the **beneficiary** of the deed of trust) is a first-priority secured creditor with foreclosure rights. If the borrower defaults, the lender can sell the property to recover.

But mechanic's liens can compete for that collateral. A GC or sub with a perfected lien can force a sale that effectively pushes the lender behind them in priority. This is why the lender cares so much about lien releases, preliminary notices, and stop payment notices — they are protecting their lien position.

### 3.2 Priority and the Relation-Back Doctrine

The California rule, expressed simply:

> A mechanic's lien attaches **as of the date of commencement of the work of improvement** — not the date the individual claimant started work, and not the date the lien is recorded. All liens "relate back" to commencement.

The competing rule for the construction loan:

> A deed of trust takes priority over mechanic's liens **if and only if it is recorded before commencement of the work**.

The interaction is sharp:

| Sequence | Priority Result |
|---|---|
| 1. Deed of trust recorded. 2. Work commences. 3. Mechanic's liens filed. | Deed of trust is senior; all mechanic's liens are junior to the construction loan. |
| 1. Work commences. 2. Deed of trust recorded. 3. Mechanic's liens filed. | **All** mechanic's liens (even from subs who started later) "relate back" to commencement and are senior to the deed of trust. |

**"Commencement" is the visible-to-the-eye test.** Demolition, tree removal, dirt deliveries, foundation staking, power pole installation, and material deliveries to the site all qualify. Surveying alone typically does not.

**Why this matters to the GC**:

1. **Never start work, or deliver materials, before confirming the loan has recorded.** The lender's title company will hold the loan recording if there is any visible evidence of commencement. Calling the lender after you have already broken ground can blow up the closing — they may require a subordination or a re-bid of priorities.
2. **At the start of every loan-financed project**, get written confirmation from the title company that the deed of trust has recorded. Then mobilize.
3. **If you are mid-project and the owner refinances or takes a second loan**, the new lender's lien is junior to your already-attached lien (as long as your work started before the new recording). This can be leverage if you have unpaid balances.

### 3.3 The 20-Day Preliminary Notice

Mandatory in California for nearly every claimant who does not have a direct contract with the owner. Some specifics for construction-loan projects:

- The GC who has a direct contract with the owner does **not** need to serve a preliminary notice to preserve mechanic's lien rights against the owner. But the GC **does** need to serve one on the construction lender if it wants to preserve **stop payment notice** rights against the lender.
- Subs, suppliers, equipment lessors, and design professionals **must** serve preliminary notice on owner, direct contractor (GC), and construction lender to preserve lien and stop notice rights.
- Service must be within 20 days of first furnishing labor or materials. Late service preserves rights only for labor / materials furnished in the 20 days **before** service.
- Send by certified mail with return receipt, or by personal service / proof of service. Keep proof — it is a constant audit item.
- The lender's identity is on the prelim report and is publicly recordable; ask the owner or title company in writing if you cannot find it.

**A GC's preliminary notice on the lender does two things**:

1. Preserves stop payment notice rights (see § 3.4).
2. Puts the GC on the lender's draw-disbursement radar — the lender now knows you exist and may even copy you on draw correspondence.

### 3.4 Stop Payment Notice — The Hammer

The single most powerful remedy the GC has when the owner is not paying. A stop payment notice forces the lender to **withhold disbursements** from the construction loan equal to the unpaid amount.

**How it works**:

1. The GC (or sub) is owed money. The mechanic's lien is a claim against the property; the stop payment notice is a claim against undisbursed loan funds.
2. The GC serves a stop payment notice on the construction lender, identifying the unpaid amount, the property, and the work performed.
3. Under California Civil Code § 8520 et seq., the lender **must** withhold up to 125% of the claim from further disbursements **if the stop payment notice is "bonded"** — accompanied by a surety bond equal to 125% of the claim amount (Civ. Code § 8532).
4. The lender continues to fund only the portion of the loan not subject to the stop notice.
5. The dispute is resolved by payment, interpleader, or litigation. The funds are held in escrow until then.

**Critical mechanics**:

- **Unbonded stop notices** on construction lenders are not binding — the lender may, but is not required to, withhold. Always bond a stop notice on a private construction lender.
- **Timing matters**: A stop notice served after the lender has disbursed all loan funds is meaningless. Serve early once a payment dispute crystallizes.
- **GC vs. sub trade-off**: A stop notice from a sub effectively forces the lender to police the GC's payment behavior. As the GC, you do not want your subs serving stop notices on your lender — it triggers underwriting review, future-loan implications, and reputational damage with lenders. Pay your subs.
- **Compared to mechanic's lien**: A mechanic's lien attaches to the property and can force a sale, but if the property is underwater (loan exceeds value), recovery is limited. A stop notice captures undisbursed loan funds — money that is sitting at the lender. Often the stop notice is the better remedy when there is unfunded loan budget remaining.

**Decision matrix — which remedy to use**:

| Situation | Best Remedy |
|---|---|
| Owner not paying; project early; significant undisbursed loan funds | Bonded stop payment notice (captures lender funds) |
| Owner not paying; project complete; loan fully disbursed | Mechanic's lien (only remaining target is property equity) |
| Owner has equity but cash-poor | Mechanic's lien (forces sale or refinance to clear lien) |
| Public project | Stop notice + payment bond claim (no mechanic's liens on public property) |
| Best practice in serious disputes | All available remedies preserved in parallel; pursue the most cost-effective first |

### 3.5 Completion Guarantees and Reserves

Lenders may require either or both:

**Completion Guarantee**: A personal guaranty by the borrower (sometimes the GC, on larger spec projects) that the project will be completed for the budgeted amount. If the project goes over budget, the guarantor must fund the gap. Triggers liability beyond just the loan balance.

**Interest Reserve**: A line item in the loan budget that covers interest payments during construction. The lender disburses from the reserve to pay itself the monthly interest charge. Sized to cover construction term + a buffer. If the project runs long, the reserve depletes — the borrower then pays interest out of pocket.

**Contingency Reserve**: 5–15% of hard costs, held back to cover unforeseen costs and change orders. Sometimes administered by the lender (released on documented need) and sometimes by the owner (released to the GC for approved change orders).

GCs should understand which reserves exist in their client's loan and how they are released. The contingency in particular is often misunderstood by clients — it is **their money** that is set aside to handle the unforeseen, not the lender's padding or the GC's slush fund.

## 4. Owner-Occupied Construction Lending — Helping Your Client

### 4.1 Types of Owner-Occupied Construction Loans

| Loan Type | Best For | LTV Cap | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conventional C-to-P (conforming)** | Standard custom home up to county loan limit (~$1.2M Bay Area, $806K elsewhere in CA 2025) | 75–80% as-completed | Fannie / Freddie eligible; OTC or two-close |
| **Jumbo C-to-P (non-conforming)** | Above conforming limit; many CA projects | 70–80% | Portfolio lender; stricter UW; larger reserve requirements |
| **FHA 203(k) Standard** | Renovation / major rehab; first-time buyers; lower credit | 96.5% | Renovation only; not ground-up; HUD consultant required; 5 draws |
| **FHA 203(k) Limited** | Minor rehab ($35K cap) | 96.5% | Cosmetic / repair only; no consultant |
| **FHA One-Time Close Construction** | Ground-up, lower equity buyers | 96.5% | Specific FHA-approved lenders; tight GC approval |
| **VA Construction Loan** | Veterans / eligible spouses | Up to 100% | Rare lender pool; VA-approved builder required |
| **USDA Construction Loan (Single Close)** | Rural-designated properties | Up to 100% | Income-capped; USDA-approved areas (parts of CA) |
| **State-bond / first-time-buyer** | Income-qualified first-timers | Varies | CalHFA-adjacent programs; check current availability |

### 4.2 The Owner-Builder Exception

Most California construction lenders **require a licensed general contractor**. The owner-builder exception (where the owner self-performs as their own GC) exists in California building law but is heavily restricted by lenders:

- Most banks **will not lend** to an owner-builder for new construction.
- Some portfolio lenders and credit unions will lend to owner-builders with substantial construction experience and net worth, but require:
  - 30–40% equity (vs. 20–25% with a GC).
  - A construction manager or "consulting GC" of record.
  - Tight inspection and disbursement controls.
  - Personal financial reserves to cover overruns.

The GC's relevance: when a client floats "I might just be my own contractor," explain that almost no lender will fund the project under that structure, and the few that will charge a premium. The GC's license, experience, and financial backing are part of the loan's collateral package.

### 4.3 What the Lender Wants From the GC

When the client's loan goes to underwriting, the GC will be asked to produce:

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Signed construction contract | Establishes scope, price, and parties |
| Cost breakdown / budget (line-itemized) | Lender's SOV input; basis for draws |
| Construction schedule (Gantt or milestone) | Loan term, interest reserve sizing |
| Plans and specifications | Appraiser and inspector reference |
| GC license verification (CSLB lookup) | Current, active, classification (B / B-2) appropriate |
| Bond and / or LLC bond | Per CSLB requirements |
| Certificate of Insurance: GL, Auto, Workers' Comp | Lender named as additional insured on GL where applicable |
| GC financial references (sometimes) | Bank, supplier, prior owner references |
| Prior projects portfolio | Photos, addresses, contact for verification |
| W-9 | For 1099 reporting where applicable |
| Lien release sample (your form) | Lender vets against statutory CA forms |

Larger or jumbo lenders may also request:

- GC's personal financial statement (rare for owner-occ; common for spec).
- Tax returns (rare for owner-occ; common for spec).
- Sample SOV from a prior similar project.
- Written construction control / payment procedure.

**Building a "GC packet"** that you can hand to a lender within 24 hours of a client's request is a competitive advantage. It signals professionalism and removes friction from your client's loan process.

### 4.4 Approved Contractor Lists

Some lenders maintain approved-GC lists. Getting on one is more art than science but generally requires:

- **Multiple successful projects** with that lender's borrowers (3+ projects, clean draw history, no stop notices).
- **A relationship with the construction lending team** — not just the front-end loan officer.
- **Standard documentation** on file: license, bond, COI, financials, references.
- **Proven track record** of on-time draws, clean lien releases, and no completion problems.

Being on a lender's approved list short-circuits underwriting time on your clients' loans, gives you preferred-builder marketing rights with their borrowers, and can occasionally provide referrals from the lender's loan officers.

The reverse exists too — getting **removed** from an approved list (or quietly blacklisted) follows a single bad project. Protect this aggressively.

### 4.5 Loan Timeline Implications

The 60–90 day application-to-first-draw window is the most under-communicated reality in residential construction. The GC's job is to set the client's expectations:

1. **Application package complete** — set a target date with the client; chase missing documents.
2. **Plans + permits + final budget** — required before underwriting locks; do not start permits assuming the loan is closing in two weeks.
3. **Mobilization timing** — schedule subs to be available 60–90 days after loan application, not 30 days. False starts kill sub relationships.
4. **Site preparation timing** — the no-work-before-recording rule means demolition, tree removal, and site prep wait until after closing.

A pre-construction services agreement (PCSA) bridges this gap. The GC bills for plans coordination, value engineering, permit expediting, and preconstruction services during the loan underwriting period. The PCSA fee is typically paid by the client outside the construction loan and is non-refundable. This protects the GC from working for free during loan limbo.

### 4.6 The Contingency Reserve — Explaining It to Clients

Lenders typically require a contingency of **5–15% of hard costs** for new construction (often 10%) and **10–20% for renovation**. The contingency is held in the loan budget as a discrete line item.

What clients commonly misunderstand:

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "The contingency is the bank's safety net." | The contingency is borrower funds (or borrowed money the borrower owes back) reserved for project use. Unused contingency reduces the loan balance or goes back to the borrower. |
| "The GC has access to the contingency." | The GC cannot draw against contingency without a documented change order and lender approval. |
| "Contingency means we can change anything we want." | Contingency funds approved change orders and unforeseen conditions, not scope creep. Spending contingency on upgrades depletes the safety net for actual unforeseens. |
| "If we don't use it, we lose it." | Unused contingency typically reduces the final loan balance at conversion. The borrower benefits. |

Walking the client through the contingency at contract signing — what it is, what it covers, who controls it — prevents the most common mid-project fights.

## 5. Non-Owner-Occupied and Investment Construction Lending

### 5.1 Rental Property Construction

For investor borrowers building a single-family rental or small multifamily:

| Loan Type | Structure | Key Metric |
|---|---|---|
| **Conventional investment** | Fannie / Freddie investor guidelines; higher rate than owner-occ | LTV 70–75% as-completed; investor DTI |
| **DSCR loan** (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) | Qualifies on the **property's** projected rental income, not borrower income | DSCR ≥ 1.0 (rent ≥ debt service); typically 70% LTV |
| **Portfolio investor** | Bank holds loan on its own books; flexible UW | Custom; relationship-driven |
| **Hard money / private** | Asset-based, short term, expensive | LTV / LTC focus; less doc-intensive |
| **Commercial construction (5+ unit)** | Treated as commercial real estate | DSCR, debt yield, LTC; SBA possibilities for owner-occ commercial |

Rates on investment construction run 100–200 bps higher than owner-occupied; LTC and LTV caps are tighter.

### 5.2 ADU Construction Financing

The ADU lending market expanded sharply 2022–2025 and has consolidated. Key options:

| Product | Notes |
|---|---|
| **CalHFA ADU Grant ($40K)** | Paused as of 2024 due to fund exhaustion; check CalHFA for current status. When active, reimburses pre-development costs for income-qualified homeowners. |
| **HELOC against existing equity** | Most common ADU funding method. Borrower draws as needed; GC paid in stages from HELOC draws. Variable rate. |
| **Cash-out refinance** | Converts existing equity to ADU build budget at closing; locks rate. Reduces flexibility but provides certainty. |
| **ADU-specific construction loans** | Specialty lenders (RenoFi, Acorn Finance partner network, some CalHFA-aligned credit unions) underwrite on **post-ADU appraised value**, allowing higher loan-to-current-value than typical. |
| **Conventional renovation loan (Fannie HomeStyle / Freddie CHOICERenovation)** | Combines purchase or refi with renovation funds, including ADU. Underwrites on as-completed value. |
| **FHA 203(k)** | Possible for ADU as part of a primary-residence rehab if FHA limits permit. |
| **Self-funded** | Common for sophisticated homeowners; no lender draw process, but GC still uses formal SOV and lien releases for own protection. |

ADU lending complications:

- The **as-completed appraisal** must support the post-ADU value. In areas where ADU comps are thin, the appraisal may come in low and limit the loan.
- Some jurisdictions still treat ADUs differently for property tax reassessment — confirm with the client.
- The ADU's projected rental income can sometimes be used to boost DTI qualification (Fannie / Freddie permit a percentage of projected rent on certain loan types).
- HELOCs are draw-funded but the GC's payment process is informal — the GC should still use a formal SOV, lien releases, and percent-complete tracking even if the lender does not require it. This protects both the GC and the homeowner.

See Module 15 for the construction side of ADU work.

### 5.3 Small Multifamily (2–4 Units)

- **Owner-occupied 2–4 unit**: Fannie / Freddie treat as primary residence with adjusted LTV (up to 75–85% depending on units). Construction-to-permanent loans available similar to single-family.
- **Investment 2–4 unit**: Investor LTV caps (70–75%); higher rates.
- **5+ unit**: Commercial multifamily; DSCR and debt-yield underwriting; typically requires commercial construction loan, often through portfolio lender or HUD program.

### 5.4 HELOC for Renovation

A HELOC against the existing property's equity is the lightest-weight financing for a renovation:

| Feature | Typical |
|---|---|
| Loan-to-value cap | 80–85% (combined first + HELOC) |
| Rate | Variable, Prime + spread (0.5–2.0%) |
| Draw period | 10 years |
| Repayment | Interest-only during draw, then amortizing |
| Closing time | 2–4 weeks |
| Fees | Often $0–500; some require minimum draw |

From the GC's perspective, a HELOC-funded renovation feels different from a construction loan:

- **No formal draw inspection** — the homeowner writes checks from the HELOC as the GC invoices.
- **No lender involvement in lien releases** — though the GC should still collect them for the homeowner's protection.
- **Cash flow risk shifts to the GC** — if the homeowner stops drawing or maxes the line, the GC has no lender to appeal to.
- **Recommend formal contract and SOV** anyway — protects both parties.

## 6. Spec Construction Financing — GC as Developer

This section is for the GC stepping over the line from contractor to developer.

### 6.1 The Spec Risk Profile

A spec build means the GC (alone or with partners) commits to construct a house without a committed buyer. Risks compound:

- **Construction risk**: Cost overruns, schedule delays, weather, subs, code issues. Same as any custom build, except the GC absorbs all of it.
- **Market risk**: The completed home may sell for less than projected. Comps move. Interest rates change. Months on market.
- **Carrying risk**: Every month the home sits unsold costs interest, taxes, insurance, HOA, utilities, maintenance.
- **Liquidity risk**: GC capital is tied up; cannot pursue other opportunities.

Reward profile: the GC captures both the **contractor's profit** (typically 10–20% of construction cost) **and** the **developer's profit** (typically 10–25% of project margin). On a $1.5M build that sells for $2.2M, the spread can be $300–500K — but on a bad build that sells for $1.8M, the GC may lose six figures.

### 6.2 Financing the Spec

Spec financing is a different animal from owner-occupied. Banks are more cautious; the borrower is an LLC or developer entity; there is no occupant. Options:

| Source | Rate (2026 indicative) | Points | Term | LTC | Speed to Close |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Local bank / portfolio lender** | Prime + 1–3% (~8.5–10.5%) | 1.0–1.5 | 12–18 months | 65–75% | 45–75 days |
| **Credit union (rare for spec)** | Prime + 1–2% | 0.5–1.5 | 12–18 months | 60–70% | 30–60 days |
| **Private money / individual investor** | 9–13% | 1.0–3.0 | 12–24 months | 65–80% | 10–30 days |
| **Hard money fund** | 10–13% | 2.0–4.0 | 12–18 months | 70–85% | 5–14 days |
| **Online private lenders (Kiavi, Easy Street, Lima One, etc.)** | 10–12% | 1.5–3.0 | 12–24 months | 75–85% | 14–30 days |
| **Specialty construction lenders (e.g., Builders Capital)** | 9–11% | 1.5–2.5 | 12–18 months | 75–85% | 21–45 days |
| **Joint venture equity partner** | Profit split, not interest | N/A | Project | 100% (no debt) | Variable |

### 6.3 What Spec Lenders Underwrite

A spec lender focuses on:

1. **The deal**: Land cost, hard + soft cost, total project cost, as-completed value, ARV (after-repair value if rehab). The deal must "pencil" with adequate margin (typically 20%+ projected profit on cost).
2. **The location and product**: Will the house actually sell at projected price in that market? Comps, days on market, absorption rate.
3. **The borrower (GC)**: Experience (how many completed projects?), financial strength (liquidity, net worth), credit, prior spec history.
4. **The exit**: How does the loan get paid off — sale, refinance to perm by buyer, refinance to landlord loan if the GC rents it?

A clean deal package for a spec lender typically includes:

- Land purchase contract or deed (if owned).
- Plans, specs, permits (or status).
- Detailed line-item budget with subs identified.
- Construction schedule.
- Comparable sales analysis (broker's CMA, ideally 3–6 comps).
- Pro forma: total cost, projected sale price, projected profit, sensitivity analysis.
- GC's resume and prior project portfolio.
- GC's personal financial statement (PFS).
- GC's tax returns (last 2 years).
- GC's bank statements (last 2–3 months).
- LLC operating agreement and EIN.
- Liability and builder's risk insurance binders.

### 6.4 The GC's Equity Requirement

Most spec lenders require the GC to have 15–25% of total project cost in the deal as cash or equity (free-and-clear land at appraised value counts).

| Total Project Cost | Loan @ 80% LTC | GC Equity Required |
|---|---|---|
| $1.0M | $800K | $200K |
| $1.5M | $1.2M | $300K |
| $2.5M | $2.0M | $500K |

Free-and-clear land bought below market is the cheapest equity. Cash from prior project sales is the most flexible. Promissory notes from family / friends are sometimes accepted but should be subordinated to the construction lender.

### 6.5 Spec Cash Flow

Spec cash flow has three distinct phases:

**Phase 1 — Land acquisition and pre-development (months 1–6)**: GC's cash going out for land, plans, permits, soft costs. No revenue. Interest reserve has not started accruing because construction loan has not closed.

**Phase 2 — Construction (months 6–18)**: Construction loan funded; draws cover sub and material costs. GC receives the **builder's fee** within the draws (typically 10–15% of hard costs, drawn as a "general conditions / overhead and profit" line item). Interest reserve depletes monthly.

**Phase 3 — Sale (months 18–24)**: Listing, marketing, showing, contract, closing. Carrying costs continue. Sale closes; loan paid off; GC captures remaining equity as developer profit.

**Cash flow forecast** for a $1.5M spec:

| Month | Event | Cash Flow Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Land purchase ($300K, GC pays cash from reserves) | -$300K |
| 2–6 | Plans, permits, soft costs ($75K, GC cash) | -$75K |
| 6 | Construction loan closes ($1.2M committed) | +$0 (committed, not drawn) |
| 7–18 | Monthly draws fund construction; GC fee included in draws | Net positive: GC fee net of overhead |
| 7–18 | Interest reserve depletes ($60K total over 12 months) | Tracked in loan balance, not out-of-pocket |
| 18 | Substantial completion; retainage held back | Final draw delayed |
| 18–24 | Carrying costs ($5K/month: interest, taxes, insurance, utilities, HOA) | -$30K |
| 22 | Sale closes at $2.2M | Net proceeds: $2.2M − $1.2M loan − $80K closing costs = $920K to GC |
| 22 | Repays GC's $375K cash equity | Net profit: ~$545K |

The forecast looks great. The reality is that **months 18–22** is where many GC-developers blow up. If the house does not sell in 90 days, carrying costs eat margin. If the market shifts down 5%, profit shrinks by $110K. Always sensitivity-test on both **time to sale** and **sale price** before committing.

### 6.6 Selling the Spec

Two strategies:

**Sell during construction**: List the property mid-build. Allows the buyer to make late-stage selections (some). Often the buyer's mortgage closes at completion as a permanent loan, simultaneously paying off the GC's construction loan. The GC gets a faster, cleaner exit but typically captures less margin (buyers expect a discount for an in-progress home).

**Sell at completion**: Build out to a market-tested spec (kitchens, baths, finishes that broadly appeal). List when complete. Maximizes margin if the market is hot; maximizes carrying risk if not.

A hybrid: aggressive marketing from substantial completion forward, with willingness to negotiate during the final punch list.

The agent relationship matters: a listing agent who knows the area's spec market, who has buyer relationships, and who will price aggressively saves more than the commission percentage costs. Interview agents before the build starts.

## 7. Hard Money and Private Lending

### 7.1 What Hard Money Is

**Hard money** is short-term, asset-based real estate financing extended by private investors, private funds, or specialty non-bank lenders. The lender's primary underwriting question is **the property**, not the borrower.

Typical hard money profile:

| Feature | Typical |
|---|---|
| Rate | 9–13% |
| Points (origination fee) | 1.0–4.0 |
| Term | 6–24 months |
| Amortization | Interest-only |
| LTV | 65–75% (sometimes higher on strong deals) |
| LTC | 70–85% |
| Recourse | Usually recourse to borrower / guarantor; some non-recourse |
| Closing time | 5–14 days |
| Documentation | Light; property focused; borrower credit reviewed but secondary |
| Prepayment penalty | Sometimes (90–180 day minimum interest guarantee) |

### 7.2 When Hard Money Makes Sense

| Use Case | Why Hard Money Fits |
|---|---|
| **Speed-critical purchase** (auction, off-market, distressed) | 7-day close vs. 45 for bank |
| **Bridge between completion and perm financing** | Avoids forced sale at completion |
| **Spec project the bank will not finance** | Unique site, complex scope, GC without 5+ year track record |
| **Credit-impaired borrower with strong asset** | Underwrites the property, not the credit score |
| **Cash-out for next project capital** | Quick liquidity on owned asset |
| **Foreclosure / short sale acquisitions** | Banks cannot close fast enough |
| **Rehab and flip** | Short-term, asset-based, exit by sale |

### 7.3 When Hard Money Does Not Make Sense

| Use Case | Better Option |
|---|---|
| **Owner-occupied primary residence** | Conventional / FHA / VA — far cheaper |
| **Long-hold rental** | DSCR loan or conventional investor — 30-year fixed |
| **Slow projects with no exit pressure** | Bank construction loan — half the cost |
| **Thin-margin deals** | Don't borrow at 12% on a deal that only pencils at 7% |

### 7.4 Finding Hard Money

| Source | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Local mortgage broker specializing in hard money** | Knows local lenders, terms; can shop the deal | Adds 0.5–1.0 point in fees |
| **Real estate investor associations / clubs** | Direct private investor relationships | Variable terms; relationships take time to build |
| **Online platforms (Kiavi, Lima One, RCN Capital, Easy Street, Roc Capital)** | Fast online application, programmatic UW | Less flexibility; rate / fee structure |
| **Specialty construction lenders (Builders Capital, BridgeInvest, RoundPoint Construction)** | Construction-specific products | Larger deal size minimums |
| **Family office / private fund** | Flexible, relationship-driven | Hard to access without introduction |

### 7.5 The GC's Role in Hard Money

Hard money lenders increasingly scrutinize the GC even when not the borrower. Expect to provide:

- GC license, bond, insurance.
- Construction contract and scope.
- Detailed budget.
- Schedule.
- GC project portfolio (especially similar projects in the area).
- Sometimes a draw process / lien release sample.

A strong GC presentation can move a marginal deal from "decline" to "approve." Conversely, a sketchy GC packet can kill an otherwise good deal. Treat hard money lenders as repeat customers — they fund 5–50 deals a year per officer and have long memories.

## 8. Understanding the Title Company's Role

The title company is the often-invisible third leg of the construction loan stool. In California especially, it plays an active operational role.

### 8.1 Title Insurance

Title insurance protects the owner (owner's policy) and lender (lender's policy / ALTA policy) against title defects — undisclosed liens, errors in prior recordings, forgery, fraud, encumbrances that were missed.

The **lender's policy** is required by every construction lender. It protects the lender's lien position. It is paid for once at closing and lasts as long as the loan is in place.

The **owner's policy** is optional but strongly recommended for the homeowner. It protects against title defects in perpetuity (or until the owner sells).

Mechanic's lien coverage is sometimes available as a specific endorsement to the lender's policy, providing the lender additional protection against the priority risks discussed in § 3.2.

### 8.2 The Preliminary Report ("Prelim")

Before closing, the title company issues a **preliminary report**. It is not a title insurance policy — it is an offer to insure, conditioned on the items disclosed being cleared or excepted.

Structure:

| Schedule | Contents |
|---|---|
| **Schedule A** | Insured (lender / owner), policy type and amount, vesting, legal description |
| **Schedule B – Part I** | Pre-printed standard exceptions (rights of parties in possession, encroachments not shown by survey, etc.) |
| **Schedule B – Part II** | Specific exceptions: existing liens, easements, CC&Rs, deeds of trust, mechanic's liens, judgments, tax liens |

**What the GC reads in a prelim**:

- **Existing deeds of trust** that need to be paid off (refinance / construction loan).
- **Easements** — utility, access, drainage — that may affect what / where you can build.
- **CC&Rs** — covenants, conditions, restrictions — that may impose setbacks, design requirements, ARC approvals.
- **Mechanic's liens or notices of pendency** — any prior unresolved construction claims on the property.
- **Tax liens** — unpaid property taxes that must be cleared before construction loan funds.
- **Boundary / survey issues** — exceptions noting "encroachment" or "survey exception" that may require a new survey.

A GC who reads the prelim before bid finalization catches dealbreakers — a 10-foot drainage easement running through the planned footprint, a CC&R prohibiting two-story additions, a prior mechanic's lien that the seller did not disclose.

### 8.3 Construction Loan Disbursement Through Title

In California, many construction lenders use the title (or "escrow") company as a **disbursement agent**. The loan is set up so:

1. Lender funds disbursements to the title company's construction escrow.
2. Title company holds the funds.
3. At each draw, the title company:
   - Confirms the inspection report.
   - Collects lien releases.
   - Runs a "date-down" title update (confirming no new liens recorded).
   - Disburses to the owner, GC, or directly to subs as instructed.

**Benefits for the GC**:

- More transparent disbursement (the title company has fiduciary obligations to all parties on the escrow instructions).
- Sometimes faster — title is set up to disburse quickly once instructions clear.
- Title company manages the lien release collection, often providing forms.

**Considerations**:

- The title company charges disbursement fees (per-draw fee, typically $200–500).
- Each draw runs through escrow instructions — disputes over the instructions delay payment.
- Direct-pay to subs (where the title company cuts a check straight to the framer) is sometimes available and reduces GC float risk — but requires the GC's consent and proper instruction setup at loan origination.

### 8.4 Reading Mechanic's Lien Activity on the Prelim

The Schedule B exceptions can show prior construction activity. Items to look for:

- **Notice of completion** — recorded by prior owner; resets lien filing deadlines for any pending claimants.
- **Mechanic's lien** — active claim that must be cleared.
- **Notice of cessation** — prior project halted; lien deadlines accelerated.
- **Notice of nonresponsibility** — owner disclaiming responsibility for work ordered by a tenant; relevant on rental property remodels.

If any of these appear, get an explanation before closing the construction loan. A surprise lien at the start of your project is a six-week problem at minimum.

## 9. The GC's Own Business Financing

The construction loan funds the **project**. The GC's working capital funds the **gap between paying subs and getting paid**. They are different.

### 9.1 Business Line of Credit

The single most important non-project financing tool for a residential GC.

**How it works**: A revolving credit facility — the GC has a credit limit, draws as needed, pays back as cash comes in, and pays interest only on the outstanding balance.

| Feature | Typical |
|---|---|
| Limit | $50K–$1M+ depending on revenue and credit |
| Rate | Prime + 1–4% (variable) |
| Term | Renewable annually; some revolve indefinitely |
| Collateral | Often unsecured for smaller lines; A/R or business assets for larger |
| Use | Bridge gap between sub payment and draw receipt |
| Underwriting | 2 years business history, financial statements, credit |

A GC running $3–5M annual volume typically needs a $200–500K line of credit. Smaller GCs can start with an SBA Express line ($25K–$500K) or a regional bank line ($50K–$250K) and grow with revenue.

**Discipline**: A line of credit funded **as a working capital bridge** should be paid down between projects. A line that gets to 80% utilization and stays there is a structural problem, not a bridge — the business is undercapitalized.

### 9.2 Business Credit

Building business credit separates personal liability from business operations and creates access to better terms:

| Tool | Notes |
|---|---|
| **EIN + DUNS number** | Foundational for business credit profile |
| **Business credit score (Dun & Bradstreet PAYDEX, Experian Business, Equifax Business)** | Tracks payment history on trade lines |
| **Trade lines with suppliers** | Lumber yards, plumbing supply, electrical supply — pay on time, build history |
| **Business credit cards** | Use and pay off; AMEX, Capital One Spark, Brex |
| **Bank relationship** | A primary bank that knows your business, sees deposits, can underwrite quickly |

Building business credit takes 24–36 months. Start before you need it.

### 9.3 Equipment Financing

GCs accumulate equipment: trucks, trailers, scaffold, lifts, compressors, generators, tools.

| Option | When It Makes Sense |
|---|---|
| **Cash purchase** | Small items, low-utilization, equipment you'll own > 7 years |
| **Equipment loan** | Mid-size purchases ($10–100K); 3–5 year term; equipment is collateral |
| **Equipment lease (operating)** | Newer equipment, expected useful life shorter than term; lease ends with return |
| **Capital lease / lease-to-own** | Like financing; ends with title transfer |
| **Manufacturer financing** | Often subsidized rates on new equipment; check for promos |
| **Section 179 deduction** | Tax incentive to purchase (vs. lease) — equipment up to limit can be fully expensed in year of purchase |

Rule of thumb: finance equipment that produces revenue; cash-purchase equipment that supports operations.

### 9.4 SBA Loans

The Small Business Administration guarantees loans made by approved lenders. For GCs, the two relevant programs:

**SBA 7(a)** — General-purpose loans up to $5M:
- Working capital, equipment, business acquisition, real estate.
- 10–25 year terms depending on use.
- Rate: Prime + spread (currently around 10–12%).
- Personal guaranty required for owners ≥20%.
- 6–12 weeks to close.

**SBA 504** — Real estate and major equipment up to $5.5M:
- Owner-occupied commercial real estate or large equipment.
- 20–25 year terms.
- Lower fixed rates on the CDC portion.
- 50/40/10 structure (bank 50%, CDC 40%, borrower 10%).

SBA loans are heavy in documentation but provide longer terms and lower down payments than conventional bank financing. The application includes business plans, three years of tax returns, personal financial statements, and project budgets. Worth the effort for the right deal.

### 9.5 Factoring

**Accounts receivable factoring** — selling unpaid invoices to a factor at a discount for immediate cash.

| Feature | Typical |
|---|---|
| Advance rate | 75–90% of invoice value upfront |
| Factoring fee | 1.5–5% of invoice, depending on aging and volume |
| Effective rate | Equivalent to 15–60% annualized |
| Recourse | Usually recourse (GC liable if customer doesn't pay) |
| Use | Cash emergencies, growth capital, distressed cash flow |

Factoring is expensive money. For a residential GC, it should be the last resort — by the time you are factoring construction draws, the lender relationships are already strained. Better to size the line of credit appropriately.

The exception: factoring **specific receivables** from slow-paying institutional or commercial clients (school district remodels, government contracts) where the payer is creditworthy but slow. This is closer to "invoice purchase" and can be a useful tool.

### 9.6 Personal vs. Business Credit

The temptation when starting out is to fund the business on personal credit cards and HELOCs. This works for a while; it never works long term.

| Consideration | Personal Credit | Business Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Liability | You are personally liable for everything | Limited (in theory) to business; personal guarantees common |
| Tax deduction | Limited if personal card used for business | Business expenses fully deductible |
| Credit score impact | Business borrowing hits personal FICO | Business credit, doesn't affect personal FICO directly |
| Limits | Personal credit limits cap business growth | Business credit can scale with revenue |
| Cost of capital | Personal cards: 18–28% | Business cards / lines: 8–14% |
| Audit / accounting | Mixed personal + business is an accounting nightmare | Clean separation |

The discipline: **separate from day one**. Business bank account, business credit card, business EIN, business legal entity (LLC or S-corp). Treat the business as a real business even when you are the only employee.

See Module 12 for business entity setup specifics.

## 10. Key Terms Glossary

### Real Estate Finance

**Deed of Trust** — California's preferred security instrument for real estate loans (substitute for "mortgage"). Three parties: trustor (borrower), trustee (neutral third party, usually title company), beneficiary (lender).

**Trustor** — The borrower. Grants the deed of trust to secure the loan.

**Trustee** — Neutral third party holding bare legal title. Can foreclose non-judicially under the power of sale.

**Beneficiary** — The lender. Owns the debt and is the named beneficiary on the deed of trust.

**Recourse Loan** — Lender can pursue borrower's other assets if the property collateral is insufficient.

**Non-recourse Loan** — Lender's recovery is limited to the property collateral; other borrower assets are protected. Common in commercial; less common in residential construction.

### Loan Ratios

**LTV (Loan-to-Value)** — Loan / appraised value. In construction, usually **as-completed LTV**.

**LTC (Loan-to-Cost)** — Loan / total project cost. Construction-specific metric.

**DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)** — Property net operating income / annual debt service. Investment lending metric; ≥1.0 means property covers its debt.

**DTI (Debt-to-Income)** — Borrower's monthly debt / gross monthly income. Owner-occupied qualification metric.

### Fees and Pricing

**Points** — 1 point = 1% of loan amount, paid upfront. Used as origination fee, discount fee, or both.

**Origination Fee / Origination Points** — Lender's compensation for underwriting and processing.

**Discount Points** — Optional points paid to buy down the rate. 1 point typically buys down 0.25% in rate.

**Interest Reserve** — Loan budget line for paying construction-period interest. The lender disburses to itself from the reserve.

### Loan Structures

**Construction-to-Permanent (C-to-P)** — Loan that funds construction and converts to a permanent mortgage at completion.

**One-Time Close (OTC)** — A C-to-P with a single closing at the start; converts automatically.

**Two-Time Close** — Construction loan that is paid off and replaced by a separate permanent mortgage at completion.

**Completion Guarantee** — Personal guaranty that the project will be completed for the budgeted amount.

### Draws

**Draw Request** — Borrower's request for disbursement of construction loan funds.

**Draw Inspection** — Third-party verification of percent-complete used to size the draw.

**Disbursement** — Actual transfer of funds from lender (or title escrow) to borrower / GC / subs.

**Schedule of Values (SOV)** — Line-item breakdown of contract sum used as the basis for draws.

**Retainage** — Percentage held back from each draw until completion (or substantial completion). California cap: 5% on most private projects (Civ. Code § 8811, effective Jan 1, 2026); lender may still retain 10% per loan terms.

**Cost-to-Complete** — Remaining budget required to finish the project. Lender uses this to confirm the loan can carry to completion.

### California Lien Statutes (Critical)

**Mechanic's Lien (Civ. Code § 8400 et seq.)** — Statutory right of contractors, subs, and suppliers to record a lien on improved property for unpaid work.

**Preliminary Notice (Civ. Code § 8200 et seq.)** — 20-day notice that preserves lien and stop notice rights; required of subs and suppliers (and direct contractors for stop notice rights).

**Stop Payment Notice (Civ. Code § 8500 et seq.)** — Notice served on owner or construction lender that forces withholding of undisbursed funds.

**Notice of Completion (Civ. Code § 8180 et seq.)** — Owner's filing that shortens the lien-filing window (30 days for direct contractor, 60 for everyone else after recording).

**Notice of Cessation (Civ. Code § 8188)** — Filed when work stops for 30+ days; similar effect to notice of completion for shortened deadlines.

**Conditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment (Civ. Code § 8132)** — Statutory form releasing lien rights through a specific date, effective only when payment clears.

**Unconditional Waiver and Release on Progress Payment (Civ. Code § 8134)** — Statutory form releasing lien rights immediately, regardless of payment.

**Conditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment (Civ. Code § 8136)** — Final-payment version, effective only when final payment clears.

**Unconditional Waiver and Release on Final Payment (Civ. Code § 8138)** — Final-payment version, immediate effect.

## Practical Checklists

### New Project Onboarding — Construction Loan Client

- [ ] Confirm loan type (C-to-P single close, two-close, FHA 203(k), VA, HELOC, hard money).
- [ ] Obtain lender contact (construction lending officer, processor, draw administrator).
- [ ] Obtain title / escrow contact and disbursement model (direct to owner vs. through title).
- [ ] Confirm scheduled close date and target construction start.
- [ ] Confirm DO NOT START WORK until deed of trust records — written confirmation in file.
- [ ] Submit GC packet (license, bond, COI, financials, schedule, budget) to lender as soon as requested.
- [ ] Receive loan documents (loan agreement, draw procedures) and read them — flag any unusual disbursement, retainage, or completion-guaranty terms.
- [ ] Align SOV with lender's preferred format.
- [ ] Confirm draw frequency (monthly default) and inspection vendor.
- [ ] File preliminary notices to all required parties (owner, lender, etc.) within 20 days of first labor or materials.

### Monthly Draw — GC Checklist

- [ ] Update SOV with current percent-complete per line.
- [ ] Pull sub progress invoices through draw period end.
- [ ] Collect conditional lien releases (§ 8132 format) from all subs and suppliers for current draw.
- [ ] Collect unconditional lien releases (§ 8134) for the **prior** draw — proof prior funds reached recipients.
- [ ] Sign GC's own conditional release for current draw.
- [ ] Prepare and submit draw request package to lender / title.
- [ ] Schedule inspector site visit; walk inspector personally.
- [ ] Resolve any percent-complete disputes within 24 hours.
- [ ] Track disbursement receipt date; alert subs to expected payment.
- [ ] Pay subs and suppliers within agreed terms; collect unconditional releases for next draw.
- [ ] Update insurance certificates if any have lapsed; resubmit to lender.

### Spec Project Go / No-Go Checklist (GC as Developer)

- [ ] Total project cost identified within ±5% (land, hard, soft, financing, contingency).
- [ ] As-completed value supported by **at least 3 comparable sales** within last 6 months, within 1 mile.
- [ ] Projected profit ≥ 18% of total project cost after all financing and selling costs.
- [ ] Sensitivity tested: hold profit positive at 5% lower sale price **and** 90-day longer hold.
- [ ] GC cash equity available for 15–25% LTC requirement.
- [ ] Construction loan term ≥ realistic build time + 6 months (sale buffer).
- [ ] Carrying cost budget ≥ 9 months of taxes, insurance, interest, utilities, HOA.
- [ ] Listing agent identified and interviewed; pricing strategy validated.
- [ ] Exit strategy includes both sale **and** refinance-to-rental as backup.
- [ ] GC's overall portfolio can absorb a 6-month loss of distributable cash from this project.

## Closing Note

Construction lending is regulated finance overlaid onto skilled trade work. The GC who masters both sides — knowing how the framer sets the bottom plate **and** how the lender funds the framing draw — wins more bids, runs healthier cash flow, and builds the optionality to become a developer.

The single skill that ties this whole module together is **reading the loan documents and the title prelim at the start of every project**. Most GCs do not. The 30 minutes spent on this at project kickoff prevents 30 weeks of avoidable cash flow pain.
