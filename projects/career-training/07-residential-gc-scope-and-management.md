# Residential General Contracting: Scope, Trades, and People Management
*The single-document daily reference for someone running their first residential jobs as GC*

This guide is written for a trades-fluent builder — framer, plumber, electrician, super, or seasoned remodeler — stepping into the chair where you own the whole job: scope, schedule, money, people, inspections, and the owner. The assumption is that you can read a tape, read a plan, and read a person. What you may not yet have done is sequence twenty trades on a single-family home, hold a homeowner's expectations in line for nine months, and absorb the consequences of every decision because the contract has your name on it.

Residential is its own discipline. It is not "small commercial." The owner often lives in the house being remodeled or down the street watching the new build. Decisions happen in hours, not weeks. Your subs are usually small businesses — one-truck operations, two-brother crews, a guy and his apprentice. Cash flow is tighter, margins are thinner, and the buffer between "fine job" and "called the licensing board" is one bad bathroom.

Use this document as a deck of checklists. Carry it. Mark it up.

---

## 1. Residential GC Scope Overview

### 1.1 What the GC Owns vs. What the Subs Own

The simplest mental model: **the GC owns the building. The subs own their work.** That sounds obvious until a roof leaks at a chimney cricket the roofer installed but the mason flashed against. Both subs point at the other. The GC owns the leak, owns the call, owns the fix. Plan for this and write contracts that allocate the gray areas.

| GC Owns (cannot delegate) | Sub Owns (within their scope) |
|---|---|
| Permit, inspections, certificate of occupancy | Their license, their crew, their tools |
| Master schedule and trade sequencing | Their daily plan within the GC's window |
| Means and methods of coordination between trades | Means and methods within a single trade |
| Site safety program (overarching) | Crew-level safety, PPE, training |
| Site security, fencing, trash, sanitation | Cleaning up their own mess each day |
| Temporary utilities (power, water, port-o-john) | Cords, hoses, and the tools they plug in |
| Owner communication and change orders | RFIs to GC, not to owner |
| Submittals and material approvals | Submitting product data on time |
| Lien waivers, payment, retainage | Pulling their own permits when required (electrical, plumbing, HVAC in most jurisdictions) |
| Cleanup between trades, final cleaning | Cleanup at end of their phase |
| Insurance over the whole site | Their own GL and WC certificates |
| Quality of the finished house | Quality of their installation |

Note the trick: **subs pull their own MEP permits** in most jurisdictions because the trade license is theirs, not yours. You don't sign their permit, but you absolutely schedule and coordinate their inspections. If a plumber's rough fails because their pipe is too close to your framer's stud, that is a coordination failure — your problem.

### 1.2 Typical Project Phases

These phases are roughly serial, but with overlap at the transitions. The transitions are where projects die.

| # | Phase | Typical Duration (2,000 SF new build) | Key GC Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Pre-construction (design, contract, permit) | 4–12 weeks | Signed contract, approved permit set, schedule, budget |
| 2 | Site work, utilities, foundation | 2–4 weeks | Foundation inspection passed |
| 3 | Framing and roof dry-in | 2–4 weeks | Framing inspection passed, building weathertight |
| 4 | Windows and exterior doors set | within framing | "Dried-in" — no water entering |
| 5 | Rough plumbing, electrical, HVAC | 2–3 weeks | Rough MEP inspections passed |
| 6 | Insulation | 2–4 days | Insulation inspection passed |
| 7 | Drywall (hang, tape, finish, prime) | 2–3 weeks | Walls ready for paint |
| 8 | Interior finishes (paint, floors, cabinets, trim) | 4–8 weeks | Substantial completion |
| 9 | MEP trim, fixtures, appliances | overlapping w/ finishes | Final MEP inspections passed |
| 10 | Exterior: siding, roofing (if not done), paint, decks | overlapping w/ interior | Exterior complete |
| 11 | Landscaping and flatwork | last 2–3 weeks | Final grade, drives, sod |
| 12 | Final inspection, punch list, CO | 1–3 weeks | Certificate of Occupancy, owner walk |
| 13 | Closeout and warranty start | ongoing | Manuals, warranties, lien releases delivered |

For a renovation/addition, phases 1–3 compress, but **demo + selective framing** introduces hidden conditions that blow the schedule. Always carry a hidden-conditions allowance on a remodel.

### 1.3 The GC's Critical-Path Decisions

These are the decisions that **only the GC** can make. Subs cannot decide them. Owners should not decide them alone. Architects often opine but rarely take responsibility.

- **Sequence of trades** — who is on site this week and next, who must finish before whom.
- **Inspection timing** — when to call for rough framing vs. waiting for a missing strap.
- **Material delivery sequencing** — when to bring windows, when to bring cabinets (rule: not before drywall is primed).
- **Hidden-condition response** — rotten sill, undersized header, plumbing in a wall to be removed. Stop work, document, price, get owner approval, proceed.
- **Quality reject or accept** — the painter's caulk lines, the framer's headers, the tile setter's grout color. Once you accept, you own it.
- **When to call the architect or engineer** — if the field doesn't match the plan, you decide whether to RFI or field-correct.
- **Money calls** — pay-app approval, retainage release, sub cash advances, change-order pricing.

### 1.4 How Residential Differs From Commercial / Industrial

The cultural and operational gap is wider than people new to the role expect:

| Dimension | Residential | Commercial / Industrial |
|---|---|---|
| Owner presence | Owner often on site daily, sometimes living in it | Owner is a corporation, represented by an agent |
| Decision speed | Hours to a day | Days to weeks (RFI process) |
| Sub crew size | 1–6 people per trade | 10–100 per trade |
| Sub paperwork sophistication | Often handshake + 1-page proposal | AIA contracts, schedule of values, submittals |
| Payment cycle | Monthly, sometimes 50% deposit + draws | Monthly pay app, 30–60-day terms |
| Schedule discipline | Look-ahead by phone | Pull plan, Last Planner, 4-week look-ahead in P6 |
| Inspection cadence | Building dept. inspector, 4–8 visits | Owner's QA + jurisdictional + third-party |
| Margins | 10–20% on cost-plus; 15–25% gross on lump sum | 3–8% net is common, with EPC bigger |
| Risk allocation | Largely on GC; AIA A105/A102 or custom | Heavily negotiated; LDs, performance bonds, retainage |
| Cash flow risk | High — small subs can bankrupt you if you front-load | Lower — larger subs carry more float |
| Documentation | Email, photos, daily logs are often the contract record | Formal RFI/submittal/change order process |

The single most important difference: **on residential, you are the contract**. There is no procurement department, no quality manager, no scheduler, no contracts attorney sitting next to you. Every role lands on the GC. Build personal systems early or the work eats you.

## 2. Trade-by-Trade Scope Reference

For each trade below: the work itself, what the GC must oversee, coordination points, quality checkpoints, and the common failure modes. Memorize the failure modes — they are where money disappears.

### 2.1 Site Work and Demo

**Scope.** Tree removal and protection, clearing, rough grading to subgrade, erosion control (silt fence, inlet protection), demo of existing structures, haul-off, utility daylighting and trenching, foundation excavation, backfill and compaction, final rough grade prior to flatwork.

**GC oversight.**
- **Demo permit** separate from building permit in many jurisdictions. Check before demo day.
- **Utility locates** — call 811 (or local one-call) 2–3 business days before any digging, every time. Re-call if you stop and restart.
- **Asbestos and lead** surveys on any structure built before 1980 (asbestos) or 1978 (lead). Pre-demo testing is often legally required and the disposal manifest must be retained.
- **Tree protection fencing** at the dripline, not the trunk. Owners will fire you if you kill a mature tree because the trackhoe parked under it.
- **Erosion control** is the SWPPP inspector's #1 fail point — silt fence trenched in 6", not surface-stapled.
- **Compaction** — verify lifts of 8" max with proctor testing on engineered fill. On a residential slab, this is the difference between a flat floor and a cracked floor in year three.

**Coordination points.**
- Excavator must hold finished floor elevation (FFE) within a fraction of an inch — confirm benchmark with surveyor.
- Coordinate utility trench depths with the plumber (sewer slope) and electrician (depth-of-cover rules per NEC 300.5).
- Don't backfill against a foundation wall until concrete has cured per engineer and waterproofing/drain board is installed.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Subgrade flat and compacted to spec (typically 95% standard proctor for slab support).
- Drain rock and footing drain installed before backfill on basements/walkout foundations.
- Final grade slopes away from the house at minimum 6" in the first 10 ft per IRC R401.3.

**Common problems.**
- Wrong FFE. Set the benchmark, photograph it, and have the excavator confirm in writing before digging.
- Buried utilities cut. Always have the 811 ticket on site. Photograph the marks before the dig.
- Backfill too soon, foundation cracks. Wait for engineer release and waterproofing.
- Excavator damages neighbor's tree or drive. Pre-construction photos of adjacent property are mandatory.

### 2.2 Concrete and Foundations

**Scope.** Footings, foundation walls, slabs-on-grade, basement floors, post-tension slabs (where used), porch and stoop slabs, exterior flatwork (often a separate phase), waterproofing, drain board, sub-slab vapor barrier and insulation.

**Foundation systems decision matrix:**

| System | When to use | Pros | Cons | Inspection points |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Slab-on-grade | Warm climates, level lots, slab-on-grade homes | Low cost, fast | No utility access below, hard to remodel plumbing | Pre-pour: rebar, vapor barrier, sub-slab MEP, isolation joints |
| Crawlspace (vented or conditioned) | Mixed climates, sloped lots | Service access, easy MEP rework | Moisture risk, pest entry | Footing, stemwall, sill, vapor barrier; conditioned crawl needs continuous insulation and mechanical air |
| Basement (full or walkout) | Cold climates, expensive lots, owners wanting more SF | Maximum usable SF, mech room location | Cost, water risk, excavation | Footing, drain tile, wall pour, damp/waterproof, drain board, dewatering plan |
| Post-tension slab | Expansive soils (TX, parts of CA, Front Range) | Resists cracking on expansive clay | Specialty trade, cannot drill or cut without map | Tendon layout, stressing, capping; chair heights critical |
| Pier-and-beam | Coastal, flood zones, very sloped lots | Elevates above flood, access below | Insulated subfloor required, vibration | Pier embed, beam connection, hurricane straps |

**GC oversight.**
- **Embeds before pour.** Anchor bolts (IRC R403.1.6: 1/2" min, 6 ft o.c. max, within 12" of corners), hold-downs, sleeves for plumbing and electrical, post-tension layout if applicable. Walk this with the framer's lead and the plumber before the truck arrives.
- **Pre-pour inspection.** Most jurisdictions require an inspection before any concrete touches forms or rebar — footing inspection. Do not schedule the pour until you've passed.
- **Concrete mix design.** Confirm strength (typ. 2,500–3,000 psi for residential footings, 3,000–4,000 psi for slabs, higher for exposed flatwork in freeze zones), slump, air entrainment (5–7% for freeze-thaw exposure), and aggregate. Get the ticket from every truck.
- **Pour sequence and weather.** Don't pour into a forecasted hard rain in the first 4 hours. Don't pour onto frozen subgrade. Hot weather (>85°F) — add retarder, mist forms, plan night pours if needed (ACI 305). Cold weather (<40°F) — blankets, heated enclosures, no calcium chloride in PT or reinforced (ACI 306).
- **Curing.** Wet cure or curing compound minimum 3 days for slabs. Strip forms after walls have reached design strength (often 24–48 hours for vertical forms, longer for suspended).

**Coordination points.**
- Plumber stubs DWV and water lines before slab pour. The plumber's rough is part of the pre-pour inspection.
- Electrician stubs conduit through footings/slabs for service entrance, EV, exterior outlets.
- HVAC: in-slab radiant tubing if applicable — leak-test under pressure during the pour and document.
- Framer's mudsill bolt layout must match what was placed in the wet concrete. If anchors are off, you'll be epoxy-anchoring later (expensive, slow, and the inspector may flag it).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Top of footing flat and level (gauge with a tape from a string line every 10 ft).
- Wall thickness consistent, no honeycomb at the bottom of pours.
- Anchor bolts straight, plumb, and at correct height above concrete (4–7 turns of nut engagement required).
- Slab finish: float, trowel, broom or hard-troweled per spec. Hard-troweled slabs are slick when wet — bad for garages without sealer.
- Slab flatness for hardwood/tile — call out F-numbers if a flat floor matters (FF 25 / FL 20 is typical residential; FF 35+ for long hardwood runs).

**Common problems.**
- Honeycomb at wall base from inadequate vibration. Patch only with engineer approval.
- Cold joints from interrupted pour. Plan continuous pours; if interrupted, plan a keyway or roughen the surface.
- Slab cracks from no/improper control joints. Saw-cut within 4–12 hours of pour; depth = 1/4 of slab thickness; spacing = 24–36 × thickness in feet.
- Wrong anchor bolt locations — slab line wrong, walls don't land on the footing. Check with the framer's plans before the pour.
- Inadequate curing in hot/dry climates leading to dusty, weak slab surface.

### 2.3 Framing

**Scope.** Sill plates, floor systems, wall framing, roof framing (stick or truss), sheathing, hardware (hangers, straps, hold-downs), shear walls, stairs, blocking for cabinets/grab bars/handrails, temporary bracing.

**Lumber and engineered wood reference:**

| Member | Common Species/Grade | When Used | Key Sizing Rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| Studs | SPF Stud grade, DF #2 in west | Wall framing | 2x4 to 10 ft, 2x6 to 16 ft, check IRC R602 tables |
| Joists (dimension) | DF #2, SPF #2, SYP #2 | Floor joists short spans | Check IRC R502 tables |
| Joists (engineered) | I-joist (TJI, BCI, LPI) | Floor joists long spans | Manufacturer span tables — non-substitutable |
| Beams | LVL, PSL (Parallam), Glulam | Long spans, point loads | Engineer-stamped; LVL more economical, PSL for hidden or moisture exposure |
| Rim board | Engineered rim (LSL, LVL rim), 1-1/8" | Edge of floor system | Must transfer vertical and shear load |
| Headers | LVL or built-up 2x | Over openings | Span tables for built-up; engineering for long spans |
| Posts | PSL, SCL post, or built-up studs | Under beam bearing | Continuous load path — must land on a beam or footing below |
| Sheathing | 7/16" or 1/2" OSB / CDX plywood | Walls and roof | Spans 24" rafter spacing; nailing per shear wall schedule |

**Hardware reference — these get inspected:**

| Hardware | Function | Location |
|---|---|---|
| Joist hangers (Simpson LUS, HUS) | Joist to beam connection | Every joist to flush beam |
| Hurricane ties / rafter ties (H1, H2.5A) | Roof to wall uplift | Every rafter/truss to top plate |
| Strap ties (CS, MST, MSTC) | Tension continuity across openings, stories | Over headers, story-to-story, drag struts |
| Hold-downs (HDU, HD, PHD) | Shear wall overturning | Ends of shear walls, both stories |
| Post bases / caps (ABU, BC, CC) | Post connection to foundation/beam | Every column |
| Mudsill anchors (MASA) when no AB | Sill to foundation | Alternative to anchor bolts |

**Shear walls** transfer lateral load (wind, seismic) from roof and floor diaphragms to the foundation. They are nailed per a specific schedule (e.g., 8d at 4" o.c. edges, 12" o.c. field; or 6"/12"; or 3"/12" for heavy walls). **The inspector counts the nails.** Spray-paint shear wall sheets a distinctive color before nailing to keep crews honest.

**Trusses vs. stick framing:**

| Factor | Trusses | Stick / Cut Roof |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Set in 1–2 days | 1–2 weeks |
| Cost | Often less, large clear spans cheap | More labor |
| Storage attic | Limited (web members in the way) | Full usable attic with cut roof |
| Modifications | Cannot field-modify without engineering | Field-flexible |
| Mechanical routing | Plan ducts in trusses (open-web, attic-truss) | Easier in stick |
| Lead time | 4–8 weeks engineered, delivered | None |

**Temporary bracing.** Trusses must be braced per BCSI (Building Component Safety Information) — lateral, diagonal, and continuous lateral restraint of bottom chords. Truss collapses kill people every year. The truss erector owns the bracing during erection; the GC owns it after.

**GC oversight.**
- Walk the plate layout before walls go up. Check window/door rough openings against the window schedule.
- Verify all engineered lumber matches the structural plans — receive the truss/I-joist package and compare to plans.
- Verify hardware is on site before framing reaches that elevation. A two-week wait for HDQ8 hold-downs is unforgivable.
- Pre-rock blocking: cabinets (uppers at 84" AFF backing, lowers at 36" backing), grab bars (every accessible bath, 33–36" centered), TV mounts, handrails, towel bars. Blocking is cheap to install now and impossible later.
- Confirm fire blocking per IRC R302.11 (every 10 ft horizontal, at floor/ceiling, around chimneys/penetrations).
- Confirm draftstopping per IRC R302.12 for floor assemblies.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Walls plumb (within 1/4" over wall height) — check with 6 ft level on every corner.
- Top of foundation/floor/walls level — set a laser early and shoot reference at every floor.
- Headers correct size and orientation per plan. Verify built-ups are nailed per code (typ. 16d at 16" o.c., both faces, staggered).
- Roof sheathing nailing — H-clips between rafters/trusses if 24" o.c. and sheathing is 7/16".
- All hardware installed before insulation. Punch list this room-by-room before drywall.

**Common problems.**
- Wrong roof slope — set the ridge before sheathing, verify with framing square or laser.
- Missing hardware — straps over headers, hold-downs at shear wall ends. Inspectors will fail this.
- Out-of-plumb stair walls — finish carpenter and tile setter will pay for it. Plumb stairs before tying off.
- Notched and bored joists exceeding code (IRC R502.8). One bored hole in the middle third for a vent stack can fail a floor.
- Cutting an I-joist top or bottom flange. This is never allowed. Specify hole charts before MEP cuts anything.

### 2.4 Roofing

**Scope.** Underlayment (felt or synthetic), ice-and-water shield, drip edge, valleys, flashing (step, counter, kick-out, apron, chimney crickets), pipe boots, ridge venting, the roofing material itself (asphalt, metal, tile, slate, synthetic), and tear-off on a remodel.

**Underlayment and ice protection.**
- IRC R905.1.2 requires **ice barrier** in regions where average January temperature is 25°F or less — from eave edge, extending up to a point 24" inside the exterior wall line (not the eave). On low slopes, expand coverage.
- Underlayment: 15# felt or synthetic. Synthetic is more durable, lays flat, doesn't wrinkle. Two layers required on slopes 2:12 to 4:12 per IRC R905.
- Drip edge required at eaves and rakes per IRC R905.2.8.5. Sequence: drip edge at eave goes **under** underlayment; drip at rake goes **over** underlayment.

**Roofing material decision:**

| Material | Lifespan | Slope Min | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3-tab asphalt | 15–20 yr | 2:12 | Rare on new construction; budget repair only |
| Architectural asphalt | 25–30 yr | 2:12 (double underlay 2:12–4:12) | Default residential. Class A fire when over fire-rated deck |
| Standing seam metal | 40–70 yr | 1:12 (mech-seam) / 3:12 (snap-lock) | Premium look. Substrate flat. Coordinated with snow guards |
| Metal shingle/tile | 40–60 yr | 3:12 | Lighter than tile, expensive |
| Concrete tile | 50+ yr | 4:12 | Heavy (8–11 psf); engineer must size structure |
| Clay tile | 50–100 yr | 4:12 | Heaviest, fragile during install |
| Slate | 75–150 yr | 4:12 | Specialty trade; not all roofers |
| Cedar shake | 20–30 yr | 4:12 | Class C unless treated; banned in many WUI zones |
| TPO/EPDM (low slope) | 20–30 yr | flat | Detached residential flat roof, ADUs, modern flat roofs |

**Flashing — the leak prevention system.**

| Flashing Type | Where | Common Mistake |
|---|---|---|
| Step flashing | Roof-to-wall at slope | One piece per shingle course; siding/stucco laps over, never sealant only |
| Counter flashing | Over step flashing, into wall material | Must be set into reglet on masonry, behind siding on framed walls |
| Apron flashing | Roof-to-wall at horizontal junction | Bottom flange laps over starter course; top laps under WRB |
| Kick-out (diverter) flashing | Bottom of step flashing where roof terminates at a wall | Most-skipped flashing on residential. Without it, water runs behind the siding and rots the corner. **Mandatory IRC R903.2.1.** |
| Chimney cricket / saddle | Behind chimneys > 30" wide | Required IRC R1003.20. Solder copper or fold step + pan |
| Pipe boots | Around vent stacks | EPDM cracks at 8–10 yr; use lead or storm collar on long-life roofs |
| Valley | Where two roof planes meet | Open metal valley = lowest leak risk; woven and closed-cut valleys cheaper but riskier |

**Ventilation.** Per IRC R806, attic vent area = 1/150 of attic SF, or 1/300 if balanced 50/50 high (ridge) and low (soffit) with a vapor retarder. Don't mix ridge vent with gable vents (short-circuits airflow). Confirm soffit vents are not blocked by insulation — use baffles.

**GC oversight.**
- Confirm decking nailed off before underlayment (8d ring-shank at 6" edges / 6" field is common high-wind requirement).
- Walk roof after dry-in to verify no exposed nails, all valleys complete, all flashings present **before** the roofer leaves for the next job.
- Roofing on the rough framing inspection? Sometimes — depends on jurisdiction. Usually the building inspector wants to see sheathing and nailing. The roofing material itself is often final-inspected.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Drip edge present at all eaves and rakes.
- Kick-out flashings at every roof-to-wall termination (check it explicitly — it's the most-missed item).
- Pipe penetrations have proper boots, ridge vent continuous, snow guards installed where specified.
- No "shiner" nails (visible from below in soffit) — count of exposed nails through sheathing.
- Hip and ridge cap shingles installed (often a separate SKU from field shingles).

**Common problems.**
- Tear-off scope under-priced — the roofer assumed one layer, the house has three. Get the existing condition on the contract.
- Underlayment exposed too long before shingles — UV degrades synthetic in 30–180 days depending on brand. Shingle within the manufacturer's window.
- Wind damage during installation. Tarp the dried-in roof at end of every day if shingles aren't on.
- Trash burned the lawn. Magnetic sweep for nails before the roofer demobilizes.

### 2.5 Windows and Exterior Doors

**Scope.** Rough openings (framer's responsibility, GC verifies), pan flashing, WRB integration, window/door installation, sealants, interior trim coordination.

**The rough opening (RO) rule.** RO is window unit dimension + 1/2" both directions (per most manufacturers; verify per submittal). Too tight, the window doesn't fit. Too loose, the shimming gets sloppy and air sealing fails.

**The flashing sequence — this is the single most miscoordinated detail in residential construction. Do it wrong and the wall rots in five years.** ([VERTEX guide](https://vertexeng.com/insights/window-flashing-101-the-proper-way-to-flash-a-residential-finned-window/))

Correct sequence for a **nail-fin window** (most residential vinyl/composite windows):

1. **Sill pan first.** Bottom of rough opening gets a sill pan — formed pan, peel-and-stick membrane, or liquid flashing. Slope to exterior. Pan must wrap up the jambs 4–6". This is the lifeboat — if all else fails, water lands here and exits.
2. **WRB cut as an inverted-Y.** Cut the WRB above and below the opening, fold flaps in or temporarily tape up. Side flaps wrap into the opening (or wrap to the inside, depending on detail).
3. **Window set in opening, shimmed plumb/level/square, nailed off through fin per manufacturer.** Sealant: bead at jamb and head fin, **but NOT at sill** (leave sill open so the pan can drain).
4. **Jamb flashing tape** — over the side fins, lapping onto the pan and up past the head 4–6".
5. **Head flashing tape** — over the head fin, lapping onto the jamb tape, extending 4–6" past each side.
6. **WRB head flap** drops down over the head flashing and is taped at the top with a horizontal piece of tape — completing the shingle lap.

**Shingle principle:** every layer on a wall is lapped so water on the outside flows down and out, never back to the sheathing. If you can run your hand down the wall and feel any edge facing up, water can get behind it.

**Mulled units.** Multi-window units (2 or 3 windows mulled into one frame) ship from the factory mulled or are field-mulled. Factory-mulled is preferred — the manufacturer warranties the mull joint. Field-mulled requires their mulling kit and detail. The RO is bigger; verify framer used the **mulled RO**, not the sum of individual ROs.

**Installation tolerances.**
- Plumb: ≤ 1/8" over the window height.
- Level: ≤ 1/8" over the sill width.
- Square: diagonals equal within 1/8".
- Operable units must operate **after** install — open and close the sash before walking away.

**GC oversight.**
- Window order confirmed against approved schedule (size, swing, operation, glass spec, grille pattern) **before** ordering. A wrong window is 6–10 weeks of delay.
- Pre-install meeting with framer, installer, and siding installer (later) so all three know the flashing detail.
- Photograph every window flashing before WRB closes it. This protects you against future leak claims.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Operable, plumb, square, sealed correctly.
- Sill pan present and visible from inside before drywall.
- Head WRB flap dropped and taped.
- Sealant at head and jambs only (sill is open to drain).

**Common problems.**
- "Reverse lap" at head — WRB tucked under the head flashing rather than over. Water enters the wall.
- Pan flashing missing or not sloped. Five-year rot guarantee.
- Wrong-size window ordered. Reorder is 6–10 weeks; field-modifying RO is risky.
- Sealant at sill — traps water inside the pan.
- Painter caulks the sill bead joint shut from inside. This is fine cosmetically but argue against painter caulk on the exterior sill — the assembly needs to weep.

### 2.6 Rough Plumbing

**Scope.** DWV (drain-waste-vent) system, water supply (hot and cold), gas piping (sometimes a separate sub), water heater rough-in, hose bibs, irrigation rough-in (sometimes), sewer/septic connection, pressure testing.

**DWV.** Lay out the system in 3D before any cuts. Vents must serve every fixture and rise through the roof (or use AAVs where allowed). Slope is **1/4" per ft** for 2"–3" lines (1/8" per ft acceptable for 4"+ in IPC; UPC is stricter at 1/4" per ft for everything 4" and under). Cleanouts at every 100 ft and at every change of direction > 45°.

**Water supply systems:**

| Material | Pros | Cons | Where Used |
|---|---|---|---|
| PEX-A (Uponor, expansion fit) | Flexible, frost-tolerant, fewest fittings (home run) | Pricier, rodent risk in unprotected runs | Most new residential; preferred |
| PEX-B (crimp/clamp) | Cheaper, widely available | More fittings restrict flow; smaller ID at fittings | Volume builders |
| Copper Type L | Long lifespan, holds shape, no rodent issue | Expensive, solder skill, recycling theft target | Premium remodels, exposed runs |
| CPVC | Cheap | Brittle with age, glue joints | Some southern states; declining |

**Home-run vs. trunk-and-branch.** Home-run (PEX manifold with dedicated runs to each fixture) gives even pressure, easy shut-off, no hot-water wait at far fixtures with appropriately sized recirculation. Trunk-and-branch is cheaper but pressure drops on simultaneous use. For premium homes, home-run is the default.

**Pressure testing.**
- DWV: 10 ft water column (5 psi air, or fill the system with water). Hold for the inspector — typically 15 minutes minimum.
- Water supply: 100 psi air or water for 15 minutes (jurisdictional). Hold gauge on the system through inspection.
- Gas: typ. 10 psi air for 15 minutes (some require longer, some lower). Check local code.

**Water heater rough-in:**

| System | Rough Considerations |
|---|---|
| Tank gas | Gas line sized for BTU input, B-vent or PVC (condensing), combustion air per IRC G2407, T&P relief drain to exterior or safe location |
| Tank electric | 240V 30A circuit, drip pan + drain in finished space |
| Tankless gas | High BTU (180k–199k), dedicated gas line (often 3/4"), Cat IV venting (sealed combustion), condensate drain, isolation valves |
| Heat pump water heater | 240V circuit, condensate drain, located in conditioned or semi-conditioned space with 700+ CF or ducted, drip pan |

**GC oversight.**
- Walk the slab/floor stub-outs against the cabinet shop drawings before the slab pour or floor close-in. Stub locations are the #1 punch-list problem.
- Confirm sewer connection elevation against city tap or septic field. If the city tap is too high, you need an ejector pump.
- Coordinate hose bib placement with siding and exterior trim.
- Schedule the plumbing rough inspection as soon as DWV and supply are pressurized; don't wait for trim items.
- For a tankless installation, confirm gas line capacity from meter. Many homes need a gas meter upgrade (utility lead time 2–8 weeks).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All fixtures stubbed (count against fixture schedule).
- Vents penetrate the roof at correct locations; minimum 6" above roof per IPC, 12" in snow zones.
- Pressure tests held, gauges visible.
- Bath rough-in heights consistent: toilet flange 12" from finish wall, tub spout 24" off finished floor (rough higher; trim sets final), shower valve 48" off finished floor, hand-held 72".
- Hose bibs: anti-siphon vacuum breakers, frost-free if exposed to freezing.

**Common problems.**
- Toilet flange off by 1/2" — toilet won't sit right or won't fit between vanity and tub. Verify before drywall.
- Vent stack runs through a cabinet pull-out. Pre-coordinate with cabinet shop drawings.
- Tub overflow drain leak — sealed in the wall, hard to find. Pressure-test the tub waste BEFORE drywall.
- Gas line undersized for tankless. Check BTU load and pipe size against pipe sizing tables (IFGC 402).

### 2.7 Rough Electrical

**Scope.** Service entrance, panel and subpanels, branch circuit rough-in, boxes for switches/receptacles/lighting, exterior receptacles, low-voltage rough (structured wiring), bonding and grounding, EV-ready, solar/battery prep, generator interlock, smoke/CO rough.

**Panel sizing.** NEC 220 load calculation. Most new single-family homes: **200 A minimum** for all-electric or houses with EV. **400 A** for large homes (>4,000 SF), all-electric with HPWH + heat pump + EV + induction. Subpanels for detached garages, ADUs, or specific zones (kitchen, basement).

**Branch circuit basics:**

| Circuit Type | Size | Where Required |
|---|---|---|
| 15 A general lighting | 15 A | Bedrooms, living, general (NEC 210.12 — AFCI required) |
| 20 A small appliance | 20 A | Kitchen counter receptacles (NEC 210.11(C)(1)) — min 2 |
| 20 A laundry | 20 A | Laundry area (NEC 210.11(C)(2)) |
| 20 A bathroom | 20 A | Bathrooms (NEC 210.11(C)(3)) |
| 20 A dedicated | 20 A | Dishwasher, disposal, microwave, fridge (some) |
| 30 A | 30 A | Dryer (240V), water heater electric |
| 40–50 A | 40–50 A | Range, EV charger (level 2) |
| 50–60 A | 50–60 A | Subpanel feed to detached structure, HPWH 240V |

**AFCI / GFCI map (NEC 210.8 and 210.12 — 2023 NEC current):**
- **AFCI:** virtually all 120V branch circuits in dwellings, including kitchens, laundry, dining, family rooms, bedrooms, hallways. Exception: bathrooms (GFCI handles).
- **GFCI:** bathrooms, kitchens (all counter receptacles), garage, outdoor, basement, laundry, dishwashers, within 6 ft of sinks, pool/spa, crawlspace lighting.
- Many circuits are **AFCI + GFCI dual-function** breakers — laundry, kitchens (most), basements.

**Home run discipline.** Each circuit returns to the panel as a single home run, no junctions in inaccessible spaces. Multi-wire branch circuits (shared neutral) — handle ties required, no longer ambiguous in NEC.

**EV-ready (NEC 625, IRC N1103.5 in some jurisdictions and CALGreen requires it on new builds).** At minimum: a dedicated 240V circuit to the garage, sized for at least 40 A continuous (50 A breaker), or an empty raceway with capacity for future. Verify local AHJ requirements — some require fully installed, not just stubbed.

**Solar / battery prep.** Per CALGreen and many AHJs: 200-amp main panel with at least 4 backed-up branch positions, conduit to the roof, room for inverter and battery in garage/utility room. NEC 705 governs grid interconnection.

**Low voltage / structured wiring.** Cat6 / Cat6A to each room, coax to TV locations, structured wiring panel (SWP) in a central location near the electrical panel. Pre-wire is cheap; retrofit is expensive. Doorbell, alarm contacts, security cameras, AV, network — pre-wire even if the owner doesn't have a system yet.

**GC oversight.**
- Walk the rough with the electrician room-by-room against the lighting plan. Verify outlet height (typ. 16" to bottom AFF), switch height (typ. 48" AFF or matched to backsplash), kitchen counter receptacles at 44" AFF for backsplash applications.
- Confirm all boxes are properly nailed off and to the correct depth for the finish material (drywall vs. wainscot vs. tile).
- Confirm the panel is in an accessible location with code clearances (NEC 110.26: 30" wide, 36" deep working space, 6'6" headroom).
- Verify load calc supports the connected loads. Don't run out of panel space at trim-out.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All boxes plumb and to the correct depth.
- Cable secured per NEC 334.30 (within 8" of a single-gang plastic box, 12" of all other boxes; supported every 4-1/2 ft).
- Holes drilled in studs at center (not edge); nail plates at every cable within 1-1/4" of stud edge per NEC 300.4.
- Ground rods driven, bonding to water service and gas (where required).
- Smoke/CO rough-in to NFPA 72 / IRC R314, R315: smokes in every bedroom, outside every sleeping area, every floor; CO outside every sleeping area and every floor with combustion appliances or attached garage. Interconnected (hardwired or wireless), with battery backup.

**Common problems.**
- Boxes set to drywall depth, then tile or wainscot is added — boxes are recessed too far (requires box extender).
- Light switch on the hinge side of a door because the framer flipped the door swing without telling the electrician.
- Panel filled to capacity at rough — no room for AFCI/GFCI breaker upsizing or future loads.
- Missing nail plates — inspector fails and you drywall after.

### 2.8 HVAC Rough

**Scope.** Equipment selection and sizing (Manual J/S/D), duct layout, refrigerant line sets, condensate drains, combustion air, fresh-air ventilation (ASHRAE 62.2), exhaust fans (bath, kitchen, dryer), thermostat rough.

**Manual J, D, S — these are the design trinity.**

| Manual | What It Does | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Manual J (ACCA) | Room-by-room heating/cooling load | Sizes the system. Oversized = short cycling, humidity issues, noise. Undersized = can't meet load on design day. |
| Manual S (ACCA) | Equipment selection from Manual J | Specific make/model to meet load with appropriate sensible/latent capacity |
| Manual D (ACCA) | Duct design | Sizes ducts and registers for static pressure and CFM at each room |

If your HVAC sub doesn't perform Manual J/S/D and the AHJ requires it (most do per IRC M1401.3 and IECC), you'll fail the mechanical permit application. Even if not required, demand it — undersized or oversized HVAC is the #1 owner complaint at year one.

**System type decision:**

| System | Best For | Trade-offs |
|---|---|---|
| Forced air gas furnace + AC | Cold-to-mixed climates with gas | Cheapest, easy to service; relies on gas |
| Heat pump (air-source, ducted) | Mixed climates, all-electric | Higher efficiency, lower op cost; supplemental heat strips for cold |
| Cold-climate heat pump | Zones 5+ all-electric | Designed for -5°F to 5°F; premium cost |
| Mini-split / VRF | Additions, ADUs, no duct space | Zoned, high efficiency, multi-head systems can be complex |
| Hydronic (boiler + radiators / radiant) | Premium, comfort-focused, masonry homes | Highest first cost, slowest response, no cooling |
| Hybrid (heat pump + gas furnace) | Cold climates needing redundancy | Best comfort + economy in cold zones; controls complex |

**Duct sizing rule of thumb (use Manual D, but for sanity-check):** 400 CFM per ton of cooling. Round duct preferred where space allows (less friction). Trunk lines: rectangular ≤ aspect ratio 3:1.

**Refrigerant line sets.** Match manufacturer-specified line sizes by tonnage and length. Long line sets need additional charge per manufacturer. Insulate the suction line continuously — bare suction line in a chase will sweat and rot drywall. Braze with nitrogen purge; pressure-test to manufacturer spec (typ. 300+ psi with nitrogen for 15 min), then pull vacuum to 500 microns and hold.

**Combustion air (IRC G2407 / IFGC).** Atmospheric appliances in confined spaces need outdoor combustion air. Sealed-combustion appliances do not (preferred for new builds — fewer carbon monoxide risks).

**Fresh air per ASHRAE 62.2-2022.** Whole-building ventilation rate: `Qfan = 0.03 × Afloor + 7.5 × (Nbr + 1)` CFM, where Afloor is conditioned floor area and Nbr is bedroom count. ([ASHRAE 62.2 calculator](https://hvacdesignpros.com/ashrae-62-2-construction-calculate-required-amount-ventilation/))

| Home Size | Bedrooms | Required Qfan (CFM) |
|---|---|---|
| 1,500 SF | 3 | 0.03×1500 + 7.5×4 = 75 |
| 2,000 SF | 3 | 60 + 30 = 90 |
| 2,500 SF | 4 | 75 + 37.5 = 112 |
| 3,500 SF | 5 | 105 + 45 = 150 |

Implementation: supply ventilation, exhaust only (bath fan running continuously), balanced (HRV/ERV — preferred in tight homes), or integrated with the air handler (CFIS).

**Local exhaust.**
- Bath fans: 50 CFM intermittent or 20 CFM continuous (ASHRAE 62.2).
- Kitchen range hood: 100 CFM intermittent or 5 ACH continuous. Hoods over 400 CFM trigger makeup-air requirements (IRC M1503.6).
- Dryer vent: minimum 4" rigid metal, max length per manufacturer (typ. 25 ft minus elbows). Booster fan for longer runs.

**GC oversight.**
- Equipment delivered to site: verify model numbers against approved submittal. HVAC subs occasionally substitute and you eat the difference.
- Ducts sealed with mastic (not tape only) at every joint per IECC. Leakage test is required in many jurisdictions (≤ 4 CFM/100 SF for total leakage; ≤ 4 CFM/100 SF for leakage to outside in some climate zones).
- Confirm refrigerant line set route is concealed and accessible at termination.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All registers sized and placed per plan; balanced after trim.
- Returns sized for the system (often under-sized — should be 2× the supply trunk area for low-static design).
- Thermostat location not on an exterior wall, not in direct sun, not above a heat source.
- Bath fans vented to **outside**, not into attic or soffit (IRC M1505.2).
- Refrigerant lines insulated continuously, condensate drain trapped and pitched to a safe termination.

**Common problems.**
- Oversized AC = short cycles, doesn't dehumidify, comfort complaints.
- Returns too small = high static, low airflow, comfort and energy issues.
- Duct in unconditioned attic without proper insulation (R-8 minimum per IECC) — massive energy loss.
- Manual J skipped or copy-pasted from another house — the system doesn't match the building envelope.

### 2.9 Insulation

**Scope.** Cavity insulation (walls, floors, ceilings), continuous insulation (CI) at exterior, attic and rim joist insulation, vapor retarder where required, air sealing at penetrations, sound batts at interior walls where specified.

**R-value requirements (IRC N1102 / IECC).** Varies by climate zone (1–8). Check your jurisdiction's adopted code; sample (2024 IECC prescriptive, IRC N1102.1.3):

| Climate Zone | Wood Wall | Ceiling | Floor over Unconditioned | Basement Wall | Slab |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | R-13 | R-30 | R-13 | none | none |
| 2 | R-13 | R-49 | R-13 | none | none |
| 3 | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-49 | R-19 | R-5 | none |
| 4 (except marine) | R-20 or R-13+5ci | R-60 | R-19 | R-10 | R-10, 2 ft |
| 4 marine + 5 | R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci | R-60 | R-30 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 2 ft |
| 6 | R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci | R-60 | R-30 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 4 ft |
| 7–8 | R-20+5ci or R-13+10ci | R-60 | R-38 | R-15ci or R-19 | R-10, 4 ft |

Source: [UpCodes IECC table reference](https://up.codes/s/r-value-alternative); always verify with your AHJ's adopted version.

**Insulation type matrix:**

| Type | R per inch | Best Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fiberglass batt | R-3.0 to R-4.3 | Standard cavity fill | Sensitive to install quality; grade I install critical |
| Mineral wool batt | R-3.7 to R-4.2 | Cavity fill, fire / sound rating | More expensive; dense, easier to install square |
| Blown cellulose | R-3.5 | Attic, dense-pack walls | Densest install; settling control needed |
| Blown fiberglass | R-2.5 to R-3.7 | Attic | Cheaper; settling less than cellulose |
| Open-cell SPF | R-3.6 to R-3.9 | Vaulted, retrofit, air sealing | Vapor-open; cheaper than closed-cell |
| Closed-cell SPF | R-6.0 to R-7.0 | High R in small cavity, basement walls, rim joists | Vapor barrier, structural, expensive |
| Rigid foam (XPS, EPS, polyiso) | R-3.8 (EPS) to R-6.5 (polyiso) | Continuous insulation, foundations | Polyiso loses R at low temps; XPS for below-grade |

**Vapor retarder rules by climate** (simplified — actual is per IRC R702.7 / R702.7.1):

- Climate zone 5+ (cold): Class II vapor retarder (kraft facer, smart membrane) on **warm side** of insulation (interior face of stud wall).
- Climate zone 4 (mixed): No vapor retarder required on most walls; use a Class III (latex paint).
- Climate zone 1–3 (hot/humid): No interior vapor retarder; some details put a smart membrane on the exterior. **Never put a vapor barrier on both sides** — moisture will be trapped.
- Crawlspaces: vapor retarder on the ground (6 mil min, often 10–20 mil for conditioned crawls).
- Basements: poly between concrete wall and stud framing is acceptable only with continuous insulation (XPS) directly against the concrete to prevent dew point in the cavity.

**Air sealing (critical and often missed).** Caulk or foam at:
- Top plate to drywall (drywall is the air barrier on top floor ceilings).
- Bottom plate to subfloor.
- All electrical box rims (use airtight boxes or sealing gaskets).
- Around plumbing penetrations.
- Around HVAC penetrations.
- Rim joist (closed-cell SPF preferred).
- Attic kneewalls (back side must be sheathed and air-sealed).

**GC oversight.**
- **Pre-insulation walkthrough.** Required by inspector in most jurisdictions: all framing complete, all MEP rough complete, all blocking present, all penetrations sealed. Walk before the insulator arrives.
- **Insulation inspection.** Required before drywall in most jurisdictions. Inspector verifies R-values, air sealing, vapor retarder where required, fire-blocking.
- **HERS/blower door test** if required by code (CA Title 24, IECC blower door at ≤ 5 ACH50 for new construction; tighter in some jurisdictions). Schedule the rater.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Grade I installation: insulation fills cavity, no gaps, voids, or compressions. Batts cut around wires and pipes, not stuffed.
- Blown insulation depth markers visible from attic access — confirm depth matches design R-value.
- Spray foam — depth gauge readings. SPF must cure before drywall (typ. 24 hr, vapor-open in inhabited spaces during cure).
- Air sealing visible: caulked top plates, foam at penetrations, gasketed boxes.

**Common problems.**
- Batts stuffed behind wires instead of cut — compression destroys R-value.
- Missed air sealing at top plates and electrical boxes — biggest source of blower-door failure.
- Wrong vapor barrier direction or doubled up — moisture trapped in cavity.
- Insulator left rim joist for "later" — gets forgotten, gets drywalled over with no insulation, becomes a permanent cold spot.

### 2.10 Drywall

**Scope.** Hanging gypsum board, taping (mudding), finishing, priming (sometimes), corner bead and trim accessories, fire-rated assemblies where required.

**Board choice:**

| Type | Use | Thickness |
|---|---|---|
| Regular 1/2" | General walls and ceilings (16" oc framing) | 1/2" |
| 5/8" | Ceilings 24" oc, garage ceiling under habitable, fire-rated assemblies | 5/8" |
| Type X (fire-rated 5/8") | Garage-house separation, attached ADU walls, 1-hr assemblies | 5/8" |
| Type C (high-rated) | 2-hr assemblies (rare in residential) | 5/8" |
| Mold-resistant (purple, green) | Wet rooms (behind tile uses backer instead) | 1/2" or 5/8" |
| Cement board (HardieBacker, Durock) | Wet wall tile substrate | 1/4" floor, 1/2" wall |
| Sound-rated (Quietrock, Type S) | Theater walls, shared walls | 5/8" |
| Sag-resistant ceiling | Ceiling 24" oc, especially in humid areas | 1/2" |

**Levels of finish (Gypsum Association GA-214):** ([Gypsum Association reference](https://gypsum.org/2019/04/ga-214-2017-quick-reference-guide-levels-of-finish/))

| Level | Description | Where Used |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Hung only, no tape | Temporary or undecided |
| 1 | Tape embedded in joint compound, no coats | Concealed areas (above ceilings, plenums) |
| 2 | Tape + 1 coat over joints/fasteners | Tile backer, garages, storage |
| 3 | Tape + 2 coats over joints and accessories, 1 coat fasteners | Heavy textures (knockdown, orange peel) |
| 4 | Tape + 3 total coats over joints, 2 coats on fasteners; smooth | Standard residential — flat or eggshell paint |
| 5 | Level 4 + skim coat over entire surface | Critical lighting, gloss paint, dark colors |

**Most residential is Level 4 + texture, or Level 4 smooth for premium homes. Level 5 is a meaningful upcharge — clarify in scope.**

**Fire-rated assemblies.**
- Garage-to-living-space wall: per IRC R302.6 — 1/2" gypsum on the garage side at minimum; 5/8" Type X if there's habitable space above; ceiling under habitable space must be 5/8" Type X.
- Garage-attached ADU separation: 1-hour fire wall and ceiling.
- All penetrations through rated assemblies must be firestopped — UL-listed firestop putty, sealant, or sleeves.

**Corner bead options:**

| Type | When | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Metal corner bead (nail-on) | Standard outside corners | Strongest, dents resistant; nail flat |
| Paper-faced metal (no-coat) | Standard, premium look | Less prone to crack |
| Vinyl (off-angle, bullnose) | Off-angle, curves | Flexible, glue + staple |
| Tape (paper) | Inside corners | Use a corner trowel |

**GC oversight.**
- Confirm hangers' (the people, not the hardware) work before tapers arrive — straight joints, screwed off properly, no waves or step-offs at butt joints.
- Walk for ceiling joint placement — long ceilings should have butt joints staggered, not in a line.
- Confirm fire-rated walls receive the correct board (mark walls with paint or tape during framing).
- Schedule drywall delivery for load-in immediately after rough inspection passes — don't store more than 1–2 days on site (humidity damage).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Hanging: 1/2" gap at the floor, gaps under 1/4" at joints, no nail/screw pops, no over-driven fasteners.
- Taping: tight, no bubbles, feathered edges, sanding smooth (200-grit minimum for Level 4).
- Inside corners: tight, no gaps.
- Outside corners: straight, no waves.
- Critical lighting test (Level 4 areas): hold a strong work light parallel to the wall at finish; ridges and shadows should not be visible from 3 ft.

**Common problems.**
- Butt joints crowned at ceilings — visible forever. Use a back-blocking technique or stagger butt joints.
- Mud applied over dust — paint peels. Vacuum before primer.
- Wrong board in fire-rated area (1/2" where 5/8" Type X is required) — inspector fails and you re-hang the wall.
- Drywall stored in unconditioned space, absorbs moisture, sags on ceilings.
- Primer skipped — finish paint shows lap marks; texture flashes.

### 2.11 Exterior Finishes (Siding and Cladding)

**Scope.** WRB (already up under windows), drainage plane, lath/furring where needed, the cladding material itself, trim, soffits, fascia, eaves, sealants, paint or stain.

**Cladding decision:**

| Material | Cost (relative) | Lifespan | Climate Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vinyl siding | $ | 20–40 yr | Avoid in fire zones; UV fade in south |
| Fiber cement (HardiePlank, James Hardie) | $$ | 50+ yr | Most popular premium; specific install (gap rules, painted ends) |
| Engineered wood (LP SmartSide) | $$ | 30–50 yr | Cheaper than fiber cement, must be sealed |
| Wood lap or shake (cedar) | $$$ | 20–40 yr stained | Pre-stain backs and ends |
| Stucco (3-coat traditional) | $$$ | 50+ yr | Code rain-screen detail required IRC R703.6 |
| One-coat / synthetic stucco | $$ | 30–50 yr | Faster install; weep screed and lath critical |
| Brick veneer | $$$$ | 100+ yr | 1" air gap, weeps, lintels at openings |
| Stone veneer (adhered) | $$$ | 30–50 yr | Two-layer WRB required IRC R703.12 |
| Stone (full bed) | $$$$$ | 100+ yr | Steel lintels, weep system |
| Metal siding (corrugated, standing seam) | $$$ | 40+ yr | Premium look on modern homes |

**Universal exterior rule: drainage plane behind every cladding.**

The wall is a system: framing + sheathing + WRB + (rainscreen gap in many systems) + cladding. Water that gets past the cladding hits the WRB, runs down, and exits at the weep screed or base flashing. Cladding is **never** the primary water barrier.

**WRB and rainscreen rules by cladding:**

| Cladding | WRB | Rainscreen Required? |
|---|---|---|
| Vinyl | One layer house wrap | No (vinyl self-vents) |
| Fiber cement | One layer house wrap (or 60-min Grade D paper) | Recommended; required in some climate zones for low drying |
| Wood siding | House wrap + furring (1/4"–3/4" gap) recommended | Strongly recommended |
| Stucco (3-coat) | Two layers Grade D paper (IRC R703.6.3) | Inherent drainage from 2-layer paper |
| Stone veneer (adhered) | Two layers Grade D paper or drainable WRB | Drainage required by code |
| Brick veneer | One layer WRB | 1" air gap is the rainscreen, weep holes at base |
| Stucco (one-coat) | Two layers Grade D paper or rated synthetic WRB | Per manufacturer; usually one rated layer |

**Critical exterior details:**
- Weep screed at the base of stucco walls, minimum 4" above grade, 2" above paved surfaces (IRC R703.6.2.1).
- Kick-out flashings at every roof-to-wall termination.
- Z-flashing over horizontal trim, at floor lines on sided walls, over windows without integral head flashing.
- Sealant joints: at the perimeter of windows and doors, between dissimilar materials, at sealant-only details (back-rod and tooled, not just smeared).
- Soffit ventilation: continuous, baffled in attic.

**Stucco specifics (3-coat).**
1. Two-layer Grade D paper or rated WRB.
2. Self-furring lath fastened per code (often 6" o.c. vertical, 16" o.c. horizontal, into framing — not just sheathing).
3. Scratch coat: 3/8" thick, scored horizontally for bond.
4. Brown coat: 3/8" thick, leveled. Cure for 7 days minimum (sometimes 14).
5. Finish coat: 1/8" thick, with color or to be painted.

Total thickness ≈ 7/8". Color coat applied wet-on-wet only with experienced crew. Control joints required at expansion areas and at panel intersections (every ~144 SF, max 18 ft any dimension).

**GC oversight.**
- WRB integration with windows: re-check before siding starts. Photograph everything.
- Confirm fasteners are correct for the material (stainless or hot-dipped for cedar, ring-shank for fiber cement, etc.).
- Verify color/profile against approved sample.
- Walk the field every few days — corners, terminations, sealant joints.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Straight courses, consistent reveals.
- All terminations sealed correctly with backer rod and sealant where applicable.
- Fastener pattern correct (no over-driven, no surface nails on fiber cement).
- Painted siding: ends and cut ends primed before install (HardiePlank requirement).
- Stucco: control joints in place, no hairline cracking from improper cure, weep screed exposed and not paved over.

**Common problems.**
- Caulk used instead of flashing at horizontal trim. Caulk fails in 5 years.
- Siding installed before WRB head flap dropped over window — water entry.
- Stucco painted before scratch and brown coats fully cured — color failure, hairline cracks.
- Fiber cement scratched and painted over without primer.

### 2.12 Tile and Flooring

**Scope.** Substrate prep, waterproofing for wet areas, underlayment, tile installation, grout, hardwood/LVP installation, transition strips, baseboards (coordinate with finish carpenter).

**Substrate requirements:**

| Floor Material | Substrate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ceramic / porcelain tile | 1-1/4" total subfloor + underlay (deflection L/360 minimum, L/720 for natural stone) | Cement board, uncoupling membrane (Schluter Ditra), or mud bed |
| Stone tile | Stiffer subfloor (L/720) | Cement backer or mortar bed required |
| Engineered hardwood | Plywood subfloor, dry (≤ 12% moisture) | Glue-down or nail-down depending on product |
| Solid hardwood | Plywood or OSB subfloor, dry (≤ 12% moisture) | Nail-down on wood; floating only with certain products |
| LVP / SPC | Smooth, flat (1/8" in 6 ft), dry | Floating; underlayment per manufacturer |
| Carpet | Smooth subfloor, no nail heads | Tackless strips and pad |
| Polished concrete | Slab itself | Power-trowel-finished slab with FF35+ for best result |

**Waterproofing wet areas.**

Shower walls and floor: cement board alone is NOT waterproof (it's water-resistant). Use:
- Schluter Kerdi membrane (sheet), or
- Hydro Ban / Aquadefense liquid waterproofing, or
- Wedi panels (foam-core integrated waterproof), or
- Hot-mopped lead/pan or PVC liner (traditional mud bed).

Build a curbless shower? Drain location, slope, and waterproof linear drain require pre-coordination with framer (drop in floor structure).

**Tile layout sequence.**
1. Find the center of the room, snap chalk lines.
2. Dry lay tile from center to see where cuts fall at walls.
3. Adjust to avoid small slivers (<1/3 of a tile) at sight-lines and avoid bad meeting at thresholds.
4. Set first row to a screed board (straight 2x4 nailed temporarily); fill back to wall last.
5. Maintain consistent grout joints (1/16" minimum for rectified tile; 1/8" or larger for non-rectified).

**Hardwood vs. LVP installation sequence.**
- Hardwood: acclimate in conditioned space for **5–14 days** (per NWFA), test subfloor moisture, install perpendicular to floor joists when possible, expansion gap at perimeter (3/4" typical), nailed at 8" o.c. through tongue.
- LVP: acclimate per manufacturer (often 48 hr), maintain expansion gap at perimeter, transition strips at door openings if changing material.

**Sequence with other trades.**
- **Hardwood goes after drywall, paint primer; sometimes after finish paint depending on dust tolerance.** Most premium builds: hardwood after final paint to protect floor from spills, but cover with rosin paper before any finish work returns.
- **Tile goes after drywall, before cabinets** in bathrooms (tile-up-to wall, set vanity on top of tile). In kitchens, floor tile sometimes goes under cabinets, sometimes not — discuss with the kitchen designer.
- **Wood floor finishing on site:** the dustiest phase of the project; protect everything.

**GC oversight.**
- Subfloor moisture readings (use a pin or pinless meter) before any flooring goes down. Document.
- Confirm tile layout dry-run before mortar.
- Walk waterproofing **before** tile — flood-test pans (24 hr standing water test) per ANSI A108.
- Schedule HVAC before flooring; the house must be conditioned for at least 5 days before hardwood, ideally a full conditioning season.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Tile flat: 1/16" lippage maximum (ANSI A108 standard).
- Grout fully filled, no cracks, color uniform.
- Hardwood: no squeaks (test by walking), no gaps in lock joints, transitions tight.
- LVP: no curling at seams (manufacturing defect, replace at install).
- Stair landings and treads: solid, no flex, transitions sound.

**Common problems.**
- Lippage on large-format tile. Use leveling clips and a flat substrate.
- Cracked tile from substrate flex. Re-check L/360 deflection — joist span often the culprit.
- Hardwood gaps in winter (installed too wet) or cupping (installed too dry, then humidity rises). Acclimation discipline solves this.
- Floor not flat enough for LVP — manufacturer denies warranty. Self-level the substrate first.

### 2.13 Cabinetry and Millwork

**Scope.** Delivery and storage, layout verification, installation, scribing, integrating with countertops, hardware, integration with plumbing/electrical/HVAC.

**Receiving and storage.**
- Don't accept delivery until the house is **dried-in, drywalled, primed, and conditioned**. Cabinets are veneer or paint-grade wood and they move with moisture.
- Inspect every box at delivery: pull a sample piece, check for damage, mark damage on the BOL.
- Store off the floor, away from drywall mud / sanding dust, in a conditioned space.

**Installation sequence (kitchen).**
1. Confirm walls plumb and floor level. Establish high spot of floor and a level reference line at ~34-1/2" for base cabinet tops.
2. Install **uppers first** (some pros do lowers first — both methods work, but uppers-first avoids leaning over base cabinets). Hang from a temporary ledger.
3. Install lowers, shim level, anchor through cabinet back into stud or solid blocking. Use cabinet screws, not drywall screws.
4. Install islands — anchor to floor with cleats or hidden brackets.
5. Final filler strips, light rails, toe kicks, end panels.
6. Template for counters (24–48 hr after install).
7. Counter install (typically a separate sub — granite/quartz/solid surface).
8. Hardware (knobs, pulls) after paint touch-up.
9. Plumbing trim, appliance install last.

**Plumb and level discipline.** A cabinet line that is 1/4" out of level over 12 ft will look terrible when the backsplash goes up. Shim every cabinet to the same level reference, not to the floor.

**Coordination with paint and trim.**
- **Crown molding** on cabinets is installed by either the cabinet installer or the finish carpenter — clarify in scope.
- **Base shoe** at toe kicks — finish carpenter installs after flooring.
- **Touch-up paint** required at the cabinet-to-wall joint, every shim location, every nail hole.

**GC oversight.**
- Verify cabinets are correct against approved shop drawings BEFORE install. Wrong cabinets = 8–12 weeks to reorder.
- Pre-install meeting with installer, plumber, electrician, counter fabricator — confirm sink cutout, outlet locations, range hood, dishwasher dimensions.
- Confirm appliance dimensions against cabinet openings (especially overlay vs. inset, panel-ready dishwashers, custom range hoods).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All cabinets level (within 1/16" across run), plumb, doors aligned with consistent gaps (1/8" typical).
- Soft-close hardware functional on all doors and drawers.
- All scribes tight to wall (no visible gaps).
- Toe kick continuous, no gaps.
- Crown and light rail aligned, miters tight.

**Common problems.**
- Cabinets that don't fit because the room is out of square — measure the actual room, not the plan.
- Plumbing/electrical doesn't line up with cabinet openings. Always template from the cabinet shop drawings.
- Damaged cabinet panels delivered. Refuse damaged pieces; document at delivery.
- Counter template before final cabinet install — counters won't fit at install day.

### 2.14 Painting

**Scope.** Surface prep, primer, finish coats (interior and exterior), trim and door painting (factory finish vs. site finish), wall texture, stain/lacquer on natural wood.

**Scope clarifications you must spell out.**
- Walls only, or walls + ceilings?
- Trim and doors, or factory-finished?
- Closet interiors, or unpainted?
- One color throughout, or multiple colors (each color is $)?
- Touch-up after other trades, or punch-list-only?
- Stain on stair treads / handrails (often a separate scope or a finish carpentry add).
- Exterior: number of coats, prep, masking, gutters, fascias, garage doors.

**Sheen by room (recommendations):**

| Sheen | Where | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Flat / matte | Ceilings, low-traffic walls | Hides imperfections |
| Eggshell / satin | Most walls (residential default) | Cleanable, slight sheen |
| Semi-gloss | Trim, doors, bathrooms, kitchens | Cleanable, mildew-resistant |
| Gloss | Specialty trim, cabinets | Most durable; shows every flaw |

**Premium homes increasingly use:**
- Walls: Level 5 drywall + matte or eggshell with light spec ([Benjamin Moore Aura], [Sherwin-Williams Emerald]).
- Trim: water-based alkyd (Advance, ProClassic) for hard finish; lacquer or pre-cat for true factory feel.
- Cabinets: shop-finished is best; site-finished cabinets always have brush marks.

**Schedule — when does paint happen?**

There are two valid sequences in residential:

| Sequence A (common) | Sequence B (premium) |
|---|---|
| 1. Drywall finish + prime | 1. Drywall finish + prime |
| 2. Paint walls and ceilings (first finish coats) | 2. Trim install (unpainted) |
| 3. Trim, doors, cabinets install | 3. Caulk, fill nail holes |
| 4. Trim painted on site (caulk, fill, brush) | 4. Spray trim and walls (best finish quality) |
| 5. Flooring install | 5. Flooring install |
| 6. Touch-up | 6. Touch-up |

Sequence B (spray after trim) is the highest-quality finish but requires masking everything and uninhabited site. Sequence A is more common; the trim paint is brushed on site.

**Spray vs. roll/brush.**
- Spray: best for ceilings, smooth walls, cabinets, doors off the hinges. Requires masking everything else.
- Roll: walls, most common.
- Brush: cut-in, trim, sash, touch-up.

**GC oversight.**
- Approve color samples on the actual wall, in actual light, with all coats.
- Pre-paint walkthrough: drywall punch list (nail pops, ridges, scratches). The painter only paints what's prepped.
- Confirm primer matches the substrate (PVA primer on new drywall, stain-blocking primer on knots, bonding primer on slick surfaces).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Coverage even (no thin spots — hold light parallel to wall).
- No roller stipple in eggshell, no brush marks in trim (unless brush specified).
- Lines straight at ceiling/wall, wall/trim, color change boundaries.
- Closet interiors painted to ceiling.
- Hardware unpainted (doors painted off the hinges, hinges and locksets removed and reset).

**Common problems.**
- Color shift between rooms because cans weren't boxed (mixed) — always box paint.
- Lap marks from rolling too dry. Maintain wet edge.
- Bleed-through over knots or markers. Prime with shellac (BIN) on troublesome stains.
- Caulk and paint over construction adhesive smears, drywall mud splatter — finish never recovers. Pre-paint walkthrough is mandatory.

### 2.15 Plumbing Trim-Out

**Scope.** Setting fixtures (sinks, toilets, tubs, showers), faucets, supply stops, drain trim, water heater connections, ice maker line, washing machine valves, hose bibs.

**Sequence within trim-out.**
1. Set tubs and showers if not done at rough (some are set early during framing).
2. Hang vanities and pedestals; cabinets must be in place.
3. Counter and sink (often a counter sub task; the plumber connects after).
4. Faucet install with supply lines.
5. Drain trim, P-traps.
6. Toilet install — last, after flooring.
7. Water heater connections (if not done at rough).
8. Washer/dryer hookups.
9. Final pressure check, water flow at every fixture.

**Coordinate with the tile setter:**
- Tub spout and shower valve trim plates installed AFTER tile.
- Shower head extends through tile — pipe must be cut to right length before trim.
- Tile around tub flange and tub apron — caulk, not grout (movement joint).

**GC oversight.**
- Confirm fixture submittals match what was ordered. Faucet finishes (chrome, polished nickel, matte black, brushed gold) all look different at fixture compared to door hardware.
- Schedule the plumbing final inspection after all fixtures are set and operating.
- Coordinate water heater start-up — gas pressure tested, vented, ignited, temperature set (≤ 120°F at fixture per CPSC; some jurisdictions 140°F max with mixing valve).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All supply stops accessible and operating.
- Toilets sealed to flange with wax ring or rubber gasket; no rocking.
- Drains flowing without slow drainage.
- No leaks under any fixture (verify each individually).
- Faucets at correct centers; spouts not too short for the sink.

**Common problems.**
- Wrong faucet finish or model — owner notices instantly.
- P-trap doesn't fit because rough was too high or too low — re-rough or accept a tall trap.
- Hose bib leaks at the through-wall seal — needs frost-free repair.
- Toilet rocks because flange is recessed below tile (must be flush or slightly above). Use a flange extender ring.

### 2.16 Electrical Trim-Out

**Scope.** Receptacle and switch device installation, dimmers, light fixtures, ceiling fans, range hood electrical connection, panel labeling, smoke/CO devices, exterior lighting, low-voltage trim.

**Sequence.**
1. After drywall is painted (or after primer at minimum) — devices and plates go in cleanly.
2. Light fixtures installed after ceiling paint is complete.
3. Dimmers and smart controls — confirm load type (LED, incandescent, low-voltage) for each.
4. Panel schedule labeled with every circuit; the inspector wants it readable.
5. Smoke / CO devices installed and tested for interconnection.

**Code-driven device placement (NEC, current cycle):**
- Receptacles every 12 ft of wall (so no point on the wall is > 6 ft from a receptacle); every wall ≥ 2 ft must have a receptacle.
- Kitchen counter receptacles: every 4 ft along counter, no point > 2 ft from a receptacle; on a 20 A small-appliance circuit; GFCI required.
- Bathrooms: receptacle within 36" of basin; GFCI required.
- Hallways ≥ 10 ft: receptacle required.
- Stair landings: lit switching at top and bottom.
- Outdoor: GFCI receptacle at front and back, accessible from grade level (NEC 210.52(E)).

**Smoke and CO (IRC R314, R315).**
- Smokes in every bedroom, outside every sleeping area, every floor including basements (but not crawls or attics).
- CO outside every sleeping area, and every floor with combustion appliances or attached garage.
- Combination units (smoke + CO) acceptable; some jurisdictions require photoelectric, ionization, or dual.
- Interconnected — hardwired with battery backup, or wireless interconnected (newer code allowance).
- 10-year sealed battery units acceptable as replacement in retrofit; new construction wants hardwired interconnect.

**GC oversight.**
- Verify all light fixtures on site, unbroken, with correct trims and bulbs.
- Confirm dimmer / smart switch programming if it's a smart-home job.
- Confirm panel labeled per NEC 408.4 (each circuit identified by clear description) — handwritten in pencil is unacceptable on premium jobs.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- All devices plumb and flush with the wall surface (not recessed, not proud).
- Plates straight and tight.
- All dimmers and smart switches functioning.
- GFCI / AFCI test buttons confirmed.
- Exterior lights weatherproof, gaskets installed.

**Common problems.**
- Recessed cans cut into a joist (must be IC-rated and air-tight in insulated ceilings — NEC 410.116 / IECC).
- Outlet boxes too deep or shallow for the finish surface.
- Smart switches sold to owner but installed without programming or commissioning — call-backs forever.
- Smoke / CO failed test at final — wiring issue or one detector defective. Test before the inspector arrives.

### 2.17 HVAC Trim-Out

**Scope.** Registers and grilles installed, thermostat (smart or basic) installed and commissioned, equipment start-up, refrigerant final charge, system balancing, filter media installation, final commissioning, owner training.

**Equipment start-up.**
- Outdoor unit: voltage check, refrigerant pressure check, fan rotation, super-heat / sub-cooling per manufacturer.
- Indoor unit: airflow at all registers, temperature split (typ. 18–22°F across the coil in cooling).
- Combustion appliances: CO test, gas pressure, flue draft.
- Heat pump: defrost cycle test, supplemental heat lockout settings.

**Balancing.** A proper balance requires hood flow measurements at every supply and return, and adjusting dampers to design CFM (from Manual D). On most residential jobs, balancing is rudimentary at best — fight to get an actual balance report on premium builds.

**Filter media.** MERV 8 minimum for IAQ; MERV 11–13 preferred for tight homes. Confirm filter rack accessible for owner replacement.

**Fresh air ventilation.** Confirm operating per ASHRAE 62.2 — HRV/ERV running on schedule, supply ventilation damper opening, or CFIS calling on the air handler at intervals. Many jobs install the equipment but never commission it.

**GC oversight.**
- Schedule the start-up after **all duct boots are connected and registers are in.** Operating without filters or with construction dust through coils destroys the equipment.
- Confirm thermostat location, programming, and owner training.
- Confirm condensate pump (if used) wired and tested; secondary drain pan and float switch on attic units (mandatory IRC M1411.3).

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Registers all painted or finished, screws straight.
- Thermostat plumb, level, calling and operating both heat and cool.
- Static pressure within manufacturer range.
- No condensate leaks at the coil.
- Bath fans tested at each location; airflow at the fan (use a flow hood, not just feel).

**Common problems.**
- Bath fan runs but airflow is half rated CFM because the duct has too many elbows. Re-route, replace with rigid duct.
- Thermostat installed on an exterior wall — runs heat all day. Move.
- Refrigerant low charge — installer didn't pull full vacuum. Comfort suffers; eventually compressor fails.
- ERV/HRV installed but never balanced or scheduled — owner doesn't know how to operate.

### 2.18 Finish Carpentry

**Scope.** Door hanging (pre-hung and slab), casing, base, crown, chair rail, wainscot, built-ins, mantels, stair handrail and balusters, closet shelving.

**Door hanging.**
- Pre-hung: install in rough opening, plumb the hinge jamb first, shim, screw to jamb, hang the rest of the jamb. Operate the door — should hold any position without swinging.
- Slab: hinges mortised on site, fitted to existing jamb. Tighter fits but more skill.
- Pocket doors: framer roughed; finish carpenter installs the door, soft-close hardware, edge pulls.
- Barn doors: track installed into solid blocking (added at framing — verify).

**Trim sequence.**
1. **Doors hung first.** Without doors, you don't know what level the casings need to be.
2. **Casings** — sides, then head. Tight to jamb.
3. **Base** — install before flooring if flooring is solid hardwood that goes under base; install after flooring if LVP/tile/carpet (flooring is the reference line).
4. **Crown / cornice** — independent of base.
5. **Built-ins, mantels, paneling** — staged after primary trim is up.
6. **Base shoe / quarter round** — last, after final flooring.

**Sequence: base before or after flooring?**

| Flooring | Base Sequence | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Solid hardwood (site-finished) | Base before sanding/finishing | Sand right to base; clean line |
| Pre-finished hardwood | Base after | Flooring shows around base, expansion gap covered by base |
| LVP / SPC | Base after | Expansion gap covered by base |
| Tile | Base after (or use a base tile) | Tile sets the floor reference |
| Carpet | Base before, then carpet tucked under | Cleaner look |

**Stair rail.** Per IRC R311.7.8: handrail height 34"–38" above tread nosing. Graspable cross-section (cylindrical 1-1/4" to 2" OD, or equivalent for non-circular). Returns at top and bottom, continuous on stairs > 1 riser. Balusters: max 4" sphere clearance (IRC R312.1.3).

**GC oversight.**
- Confirm all material on site before the finish carpenter starts. Stained vs. paint-grade. Profile of casing and base matches approved sample.
- Pre-trim walkthrough: walls plumb, doors operable, flooring substrate ready.
- Schedule painter behind finish carpenter for caulk and fill.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Doors open and close cleanly, latches engage.
- Casing miters tight, no gaps.
- Base scribed to floor where uneven, tight to wall (caulk seam where painted).
- Crown straight across the room, mitered tight.
- Stair handrail solid; balusters straight; max 4" sphere passes.

**Common problems.**
- Floors not flat enough — base scribed too much; visible gap.
- Casing mitered without joints back-cut — opens in dry weather.
- Door not plumb because RO out of plumb — finish carpenter has to choose: jamb plumb (door swings open or closed) or door plane plumb (gap inconsistent at head). Plane is usually correct.
- Stairs creak (subfloor + tread interface). Glue and screw treads; eliminates 80% of squeaks.

### 2.19 Landscaping and Flatwork

**Scope.** Final grade, driveway, walkways, patios, retaining walls (if any), planting, sod/seed, irrigation, exterior lighting.

**Sequence.**
1. **Final grade** before flatwork. Confirm slopes away from house at 6" in first 10 ft per IRC R401.3.
2. **Forms for flatwork** — driveways, walkways, patios. Verify control joint spacing (every 8–10 ft for 4" residential slabs).
3. **Concrete pour** — sequence around access (driveway last so trucks can still reach).
4. **Irrigation rough**, then sleeves under flatwork. Trench separate from flatwork to allow planting later.
5. **Topsoil** (4–6" minimum for sod/seed; deeper for plantings).
6. **Sod or seed** — sod for instant lawn, seed for cost.
7. **Plantings** — trees, shrubs, perennials.
8. **Mulch** in beds (2–3" depth, not against trunks).
9. **Exterior lighting** — low-voltage (most common) or line-voltage.

**Driveway specifications.**
- Concrete: 4" minimum, 5" preferred; 6" for heavier vehicles. 6-mil vapor barrier on grade (optional); reinforcement (#3 rebar 18" o.c. or fiber mesh).
- Asphalt: 2" wear + 2" binder over 6" compacted aggregate.
- Pavers: 1" sand bed over 6" compacted aggregate; restraint at edges.

**Patio drainage.** Slope at least 1/4" per ft away from the house. Concrete patios — control joints every 8–10 ft. Stone or paver patios — pitch the substrate, not just the finish.

**GC oversight.**
- Confirm utility locates again before any deep planting or digging post holes.
- Verify final grade and drainage before flatwork — once concrete is in, fixing grade is destructive.
- Photograph the site before sod / planting (for warranty disputes later).
- Plant warranty: typical 1 year if installed by the landscape sub, often only if irrigation operates.

**Quality checkpoints.**
- Grade pitches away from foundation everywhere; no flat spots within 10 ft of house.
- Flatwork control joints in correct locations and depth.
- Sod laid tight (no gaps), watered immediately.
- Plants set at proper depth (root flare visible, not buried).
- Exterior lighting on a timer or photocell, properly aimed.

**Common problems.**
- Settling backfill around foundation — owner sees a moat after the first rain. Over-fill backfill so it can settle 6–12 months.
- Sod dies because the homeowner didn't water. Pre-final discussion with owner: who's watering, on what schedule, for how long.
- Concrete cracks where no control joint was placed. Saw-cut within hours of pour.
- Irrigation lines too shallow — winter freeze damage on day-light frost.

## 3. The Inspection Sequence

The building department is your last quality gate. Treat the inspector as a partner, not an adversary. Inspectors who like you will tell you what to fix and re-inspect in days. Inspectors who don't like you will write up everything and make you wait.

### 3.1 Permits Required

| Work Type | Typical Permit Required? | Triggers |
|---|---|---|
| New single-family / ADU | Yes | Always |
| Addition (new floor area) | Yes | Any new conditioned space |
| Interior remodel (non-structural) | Often yes | Most jurisdictions require for any kitchen/bath remodel; usually if MEP is involved |
| Cabinet swap, paint, flooring | Usually no | "Like for like" cosmetic |
| Re-roof | Yes (most jurisdictions) | Sheathing exposed |
| Window / door replacement | Yes if egress changes; sometimes always | Different size, structural changes |
| Deck > 30" off grade | Yes | Structural; egress |
| Demolition | Yes (separate permit) | Hazardous materials, utility disconnects |
| Electrical / plumbing / HVAC scope of work | Yes — trade permit by sub | Anything more than fixture swap |
| Solar PV / battery | Yes | Always |
| Fence (over a height threshold) | Sometimes | Jurisdictional |

**Critical:** If you start without a permit and an inspector catches you, you typically pay 2× the permit fee, may face a stop-work order, and may have to expose covered work for inspection. Don't start without a permit.

### 3.2 Typical Residential Inspection Sequence

| Order | Inspection | What They Look For | Triggers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Temporary power / pole | Service to a temp pole, GFCI receptacle, grounding | Before construction begins, sometimes |
| 2 | Footing | Excavation depth, soil bearing, rebar size/placement, anchor bolt prep | Before footing pour |
| 3 | Foundation wall (basements) | Vertical reinforcement, waterproofing, drainage | Before backfill |
| 4 | Underslab plumbing | DWV layout, depth, slope, test ball | Before slab vapor barrier |
| 5 | Underslab electrical (if applicable) | Conduit depth, sleeves | Before slab |
| 6 | Slab pre-pour | Vapor barrier, sub-slab insulation, rebar, post-tension if applicable, embeds | Before slab pour |
| 7 | Rough framing | Member size/grade, hardware, shear walls, fire-blocking | After framing, before insulation |
| 8 | Rough plumbing | Vent locations, pipe slope, pressure test on water and DWV | After framing, with framing or separate |
| 9 | Rough electrical | Box locations, wire support, AFCI/GFCI mapping, smoke/CO rough | After framing |
| 10 | Rough mechanical | Duct/refrigerant routing, combustion air, exhaust terminations | After framing |
| 11 | Roof / sheathing | Nailing pattern, hurricane straps (in some climates), flashing | Often combined with rough framing |
| 12 | Insulation | Cavity fill, R-values, air sealing, vapor barrier | After all rough, before drywall |
| 13 | Drywall nailing (some AHJs) | Fastener pattern on fire-rated assemblies | After hanging, before tape |
| 14 | Final plumbing | Fixtures installed, water heater, hose bibs, no leaks | At final |
| 15 | Final electrical | Devices, panel labeled, smoke/CO operational | At final |
| 16 | Final mechanical | Equipment start-up, ventilation operating | At final |
| 17 | Final building | Stairs, handrails, egress, smoke/CO, grade, address visible | At final |
| 18 | Energy / HERS rating (where required) | Blower door, duct leakage, lighting compliance | Before final building |
| 19 | Certificate of Occupancy | Issued after all finals pass | Owner can move in |

Some jurisdictions combine rough inspections (one visit covers framing + MEP); others split. Confirm with your inspector at the pre-construction meeting.

### 3.3 Scheduling Inspections to Not Delay the Job

- **Most jurisdictions require 24–72 hours lead time** for an inspection request. Some are online (instant scheduling); some by phone (slower).
- **AM vs. PM slots:** AM slots fill first. If you must have a same-week inspection, schedule on Monday morning for that week.
- **Re-inspection fees** apply in most jurisdictions (typ. $75–$200 each). Three re-inspections often trigger a hard re-inspection with a higher fee or a meeting with a senior inspector.
- **Window:** inspectors arrive in a 2–4 hour window in most jurisdictions, full-day in some. Plan the day around the window — don't have a critical trade racing to finish 30 minutes before the call.
- **Cancelation:** if you call to cancel by 8 AM the same day, usually no fee. After that, you may pay a no-show fee.

### 3.4 What Inspectors Look For (Top Failure Reasons)

**Footing fails:**
- Missing or wrong-size rebar.
- Footing too shallow (frost depth in cold climates).
- Anchor bolts wrong location, height, or spacing.
- Soil too soft or organic.

**Rough framing fails:**
- Missing fire-blocking.
- Missing hold-downs or hurricane straps.
- Headers undersized.
- Notches and bored holes exceeding allowance.
- Shear wall nailing pattern wrong.
- Stair geometry (rise/run, headroom).

**Rough MEP fails:**
- Plumbing vent missing or undersized.
- Pressure test gauge not on system.
- Electrical boxes too deep / shallow.
- Cable not supported, no nail plates.
- Smoke / CO locations missing.
- HVAC duct in unconditioned space without insulation.

**Insulation fails:**
- R-value below code.
- Vapor barrier wrong side or doubled up.
- Air sealing missing at top plates, boxes, penetrations.
- Crawlspace vapor retarder not on ground.

**Final fails:**
- Handrail too low, missing returns.
- Guardrail spacing > 4".
- Address numbers not visible from street.
- Grade not pitched away from house.
- GFCI doesn't trip on test.
- Smoke/CO not interconnected or not all installed.

### 3.5 Managing Corrective Action Between Inspection and Re-inspection

1. **At the inspection itself:** politely ask the inspector to walk every item with you. Take written notes. Photograph each fail location.
2. **Confirm the correction:** if you're unsure of the fix the inspector wants, ask. "If I do X, will that pass?" Don't guess.
3. **Document the correction:** photograph every corrected item with a tape measure or before/after view.
4. **Call for re-inspection:** schedule the re-inspect after the corrections are done. Don't call too soon ("I think it's done") — costs a re-inspection fee.
5. **At re-inspection:** point the inspector at the corrected items. Don't make them search. They're busy.

If you disagree with an inspector's call (rare, but it happens): ask politely for the code section, document, then escalate to the chief building official with calm written technical argument. Do not argue with the inspector on site — you'll lose every battle that way.

## 4. Scheduling a Residential Project

### 4.1 Building the Schedule From the Critical Path

The residential critical path (new construction):

```
Permit → Site/Foundation → Framing/Roof → Windows/Dry-in
   → Rough MEP → Rough Inspections → Insulation
   → Drywall → Cabinets/Trim/Tile → Paint
   → MEP Trim → Floor Finishes → Final → CO
```

Anything not on this path is **off-critical** and can be worked in parallel or floated. Examples of off-critical paths: landscaping (often runs parallel from rough through finish), exterior siding (parallel to interior rough), exterior paint (parallel to interior finishes).

### 4.2 Trade Sequencing Rules and Typical Durations

For a 2,000 SF single-family home, single-story or simple two-story, normal complexity:

| Phase | Crew Size | Duration | Predecessors |
|---|---|---|---|
| Site / excavate / form | 2–4 | 5–10 days | Permit |
| Foundation pour + cure | 3–5 | 3–7 days; 7-day wait if continuing | Footing inspection |
| Framing (incl. sheathing, dry-in) | 4–6 | 10–15 days | Foundation cured |
| Windows / exterior doors | 1–2 | 2–4 days | Framing dried-in |
| Roofing (if not built into framing) | 3–5 | 3–5 days | Framing dry-in |
| Rough plumbing | 2–3 | 5–8 days | Framing complete |
| Rough electrical | 2–3 | 5–8 days | Framing complete (parallel w/ plumbing if coordinated) |
| Rough HVAC | 2–3 | 5–8 days | Framing complete (parallel w/ above) |
| Inspections — rough | n/a | 1–3 days | All rough work complete |
| Insulation | 2–3 | 2–4 days | Rough inspections pass |
| Drywall hang | 3–5 | 3–5 days | Insulation pass |
| Drywall finish | 2–3 | 5–10 days | Hang complete |
| Drywall prime | 1–2 | 1–2 days | Tape dry |
| Cabinets | 2 | 3–5 days | Prime |
| Trim carpentry | 2 | 5–10 days | Cabinets, or parallel for non-cabinet rooms |
| Tile (kitchen/bath) | 2 | 5–10 days | Prime; sometimes earlier in shower walls |
| Counter template + fab + install | 1–2 | 2–4 weeks calendar | Cabinets set |
| Paint (walls + trim) | 2–3 | 5–10 days | Trim caulked, holes filled |
| Hardwood / LVP install | 2–3 | 3–5 days | Paint final, conditioned |
| MEP trim | 2 each | 3–5 days each | Paint complete, flooring complete |
| Punch + final cleaning | varies | 5–10 days | Substantial completion |
| Inspections — final | n/a | 1–3 days | Finals from trades |
| Landscape / flatwork | 3–5 | 5–10 days | Often parallel with finishes for siding/paint exterior; final grade at the end |

**Rule of thumb:** a 2,000 SF new build runs **5–9 months** depending on complexity and climate. Custom homes run 10–18+ months. Renovations are highly variable — a kitchen + 2 baths runs 8–14 weeks; a whole-house remodel can run a full year.

### 4.3 Float Management

**Where to build buffer.**
- 1–2 weeks between rough inspection pass and start of insulation (in case re-inspection is needed).
- 1 week between cabinets set and counter install (template, fab time).
- 2 weeks between final inspection and target move-in (punch list, owner walk).

**Weather buffer.** In a rain-heavy region (PNW, NE, SE), add 2–4 weeks across the schedule for weather days during the foundation/framing phase. In a freeze-heavy region (Midwest, NE), add 2–3 weeks for winter productivity loss if foundation pours fall in November-February.

**Hidden-condition buffer (remodels).** Always 10–20% schedule buffer on remodels. The wall you open has wires you didn't expect, plumbing you have to move, or a structural issue requiring an engineer.

### 4.4 Look-Ahead Scheduling

Run a **2-week look-ahead** weekly. Every trade lead on the job — the framer, the plumber, the electrician, the HVAC sub, the drywaller — gets called or texted with the same format:

```
[Trade]: Confirm
- Last day on site: [date]
- Off-site days this week: [list]
- Material delivery: [what, when]
- Manpower next week: [crew size, days]
- Issues / questions: [open list]
```

Compile into a single look-ahead document. Send to the owner Friday afternoon. Hold a 15-minute Monday huddle on site with whoever is active.

The look-ahead is **not the schedule**. The schedule is the contract baseline. The look-ahead is the operational tool. They must reconcile; if they don't, something has changed and a schedule update is in order.

### 4.5 Common Schedule Killers

| Killer | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Permit delay (plan review longer than expected) | Submit permit application as early as possible; respond to plan check comments within 48 hours |
| Owner indecision (paint colors, fixtures) | "Decision deadline" memo at kickoff with dates that must be hit |
| Material lead times (windows 6–10 wk, cabinets 8–14 wk, appliances varies) | Order at contract signing for long-lead; track in a delivery log |
| Failed inspection requiring rework | Pre-inspection self-walk; train trades to self-check |
| Weather (rain, freeze, heat) | Buffer in schedule; tent + heat for foundations in winter; tarp for framing |
| Sub flake / no-show | Multiple bidders on file; pre-construction meeting with backup options identified |
| Hidden conditions | 10–20% schedule buffer on remodels; allowance line in contract |
| Change orders mid-project | Strict CO process; pause work on impacted area until signed |
| Owner-furnished material delay | Get owner-furnished delivery commitments in writing; track separately |

## 5. Managing Subcontractors

### 5.1 Writing Scopes That Prevent Disputes

A scope of work that prevents disputes has six elements:

1. **What's included** — a list of every operation the sub will perform. "Furnish and install all plumbing fixtures and trim per attached fixture schedule" is better than "Plumbing finish."
2. **What's excluded** — items explicitly NOT in this scope. Excluding "cleanup of other trades' debris" and "removal of any concrete or masonry from the floor area" prevents fights.
3. **What's owner-furnished** vs. sub-furnished — the fixture schedule, who buys what.
4. **Schedule** — mobilization date, completion date, number of mobs (mobilizations) included.
5. **Price** — lump sum, time + material, or unit rates. Cap if T&M.
6. **Specific quality and code references** — "Install per manufacturer instructions and 2024 IRC. PEX-A piping required."

A 1-page residential scope template:

```
SCOPE OF WORK: [TRADE] — [PROJECT ADDRESS]

INCLUDES:
- [Operation 1, with specs]
- [Operation 2]
- ...

EXCLUDES:
- [Item, with who is responsible]
- [Cleanup of other trades' debris]
- [Permit (held by [GC or sub])]

OWNER-FURNISHED ITEMS (sub installs only):
- [List with delivery date]

SCHEDULE:
- Mobilize: [date]
- Substantially complete: [date]
- Number of mobilizations included: [N]

PRICE: $[Amount] lump sum / $[Rate]/hr T&M not to exceed $[Cap]
PAYMENT: [Terms]

CODE / STANDARD:
- [IRC 2024 / NEC 2023 / etc.]
- [Manufacturer install per spec]
- [Quality level / GA-214 Level 4 / etc.]

GENERAL TERMS:
- Insurance: GL $1M, WC as required
- Lien waivers: Required with each payment
- Warranty: 1 year workmanship
- Change orders: Written, signed before performing
```

### 5.2 Bidding and Leveling

For each trade, **get three real bids** from prequalified subs (more than three on big trades like framing, MEP). Levelize the bids — compare apples to apples.

A bid leveling sheet (example, framing):

| Item | Bidder A | Bidder B | Bidder C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sill plates and floor system | $X | $X | $X |
| Wall framing | $X | $X | $X |
| Roof framing (stick or truss) | $X | $X | $X |
| Stairs | $X | $X | $X |
| Sheathing labor | $X | $X | $X |
| Hardware install | $X | $X | $X |
| Cleanup | included | NOT INCLUDED | included |
| Dumpster | NOT INCLUDED | included | NOT INCLUDED |
| Hoisting (crane day for trusses) | included | NOT INCLUDED | included |
| **Total** | $X | $X | $X |
| **Total adjusted for missing items** | $X | $X+$Y | $X+$Z |

Pre-award check:
- Current GL and WC certificates on file.
- License current.
- Reference calls to 3 recent jobs (not the sub's pick — your pick from their reference list).
- Walk one of their current jobs if possible.
- Confirm crew availability for your dates.

### 5.3 Contracts — Clauses That Matter on Residential

A residential subcontract on a single-family home doesn't need to be a 50-page AIA A401. A 3–5 page contract is appropriate. Critical clauses:

- **Scope** — reference to the attached SOW, plans, specs.
- **Payment terms** — typically draws against percent complete, with retainage. Suggested: 30%/30%/30%/10% retainage. Or progress payments biweekly per agreed percent.
- **Insurance** — GL minimum $1M each occurrence / $2M aggregate; WC if employees; vehicle if driving.
- **Lien waivers** — conditional with each payment, unconditional upon final.
- **Indemnity** — sub indemnifies the GC and owner for sub's negligence.
- **Warranty** — 1 year workmanship; manufacturer warranties pass through.
- **Right to terminate** — for cause (default after notice) and for convenience (with appropriate compensation).
- **Change orders** — written and signed before work; no oral change orders accepted.
- **Schedule** — mobilization and substantial completion dates; sub agrees to coordinate with other trades.
- **Cleanup** — daily cleanup by sub; final cleanup by GC.
- **Hold-harmless / cross-indemnity** depending on your insurance broker's advice.
- **Lien rights notice** — required in many states (CA, TX, etc.) and must be served at start of work; not a clause but a separate notice the sub may need to send.

### 5.4 Directing Subs on Site

**Daily check-in.** Walk every active trade's work area each day. 5–15 minutes per trade. Topics:
- What did you do today?
- What's tomorrow?
- Any blockers?
- Anything from me?

**Conflict resolution between trades.** Most conflicts are coordination failures, not personalities:
- Electrician needs to drill the same stud the plumber just notched.
- Drywaller is ready to hang but the inspector hasn't passed insulation.
- Cabinets blocked by tile not yet started.

Get both leads in the same conversation (in person or on speakerphone). State the conflict, ask each what they need, decide which one moves first or how the sequence shifts. Document the decision.

**Authority lines.** You — the GC — direct the sub's foreman or owner. You do NOT direct the sub's craft labor directly. If you tell a journeyman electrician to move an outlet, the journeyman will do what you say, but their foreman will charge you for the change and probably resent the workaround.

### 5.5 Managing Quality

**Pre-work meeting** at the start of each trade. 15–30 minutes. Cover:
- Scope review.
- Plan / detail walk.
- Schedule and milestones.
- Coordination with adjacent trades.
- Quality expectations (defects that will require rework, finish levels).
- Safety expectations.

**Milestone inspections.** Walk the work at clear milestones:
- Framing: when each floor's walls are up; when sheathing is on; when roofed and dried-in.
- Rough MEP: midway through (catch wrong sequence before they finish); at completion before insulation.
- Drywall: after hang, after tape, after sand, after prime.
- Tile: after waterproofing, after layout dry-run, after grout.
- Trim: after each room is hung.

**Correction process.** Verbal first ("hey, that miter is open"). If not addressed, written. If not addressed twice, a meeting with the sub's owner and you and the foreman. If still not addressed, scope or stop pay.

### 5.6 Managing Cash Flow

Residential cash flow is brutal because owners pay you, you pay subs, and the gap is short.

**Owner draws:**
- 10% at contract / deposit (sometimes 20–30% on lump sum, less on cost plus)
- Progress draws — typically monthly against percent complete, or against milestone events (foundation complete, dry-in, drywall, substantial completion)
- 10% retainage held until punch + warranty period (in some states, mandated; in others, negotiated)
- Final payment within 30 days of CO

**Sub payments:**
- Typically same percentage-complete logic as owner draws, but timing offset
- Net 30 is common; net 15 on smaller subs; 50% deposit + progress + final on long-lead items (cabinets)
- Lien releases required with each payment (conditional progressive; unconditional final)
- 10% retainage on subs is harder to enforce on residential — most subs won't accept it; if you do hold retainage, hold it on the labor portion only

**The critical timing rule:** **never pay a sub before the owner has paid you** for that portion. If you do, you're financing the job out of pocket and one delayed owner draw bankrupts you.

### 5.7 Dealing With Problem Subs

| Problem | Sign | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Slow sub | 50% of expected daily output, no manpower bump | Meeting with owner; demand crew bump or you'll backfill at their cost |
| Flaky sub (no-shows) | Skips Mondays, doesn't return calls | One written warning; if it happens twice, find a backup |
| Disappears mid-job | Two weeks no contact | Send written notice of default per contract; mobilize replacement sub; back-charge the original at their final invoice |
| Quality refusal | Won't fix defects | Withhold final payment in proportion; document with photos; warranty period covers some, defects are your right |
| Asking for more money mid-job | "We didn't see that" mid-installation | Treat as a change order request; you can deny if it's clearly within scope; sometimes you split the difference to keep things moving |

The two pieces of advice: (1) have a list of backup subs for every trade before the project starts, and (2) don't let one bad sub catch up with you — fire fast on residential because the rest of the schedule depends on it.

## 6. Managing Direct Employees and Day Labor

### 6.1 Role Definitions on a Residential Site

| Role | Responsibility | Typical Span |
|---|---|---|
| Superintendent | Site operations, sub coordination, inspections, quality, safety on site | 1–3 active homes |
| Foreman | Crew lead for self-performed work (your own framing crew, your own laborers); reports to super | 2–6 craft |
| Lead carpenter | Senior craft, runs day-to-day in absence of foreman | Self + 1–2 |
| Laborer | General work — cleanup, material handling, simple framing assist | Self |
| Apprentice / helper | Learning trade, supervised work | Self |

On a small operation, the GC IS the super on small projects, with maybe one full-time laborer / apprentice. As the company scales, the GC backs out of daily site presence and supers run individual jobs.

### 6.2 Daily Huddle Structure

A daily huddle, 10–15 minutes, every morning, on site, with whoever is active:

1. **Yesterday:** what got done.
2. **Today:** what each crew is doing (state the goal: "drywall room 1 finished, room 2 hung").
3. **Tomorrow:** what's coming, any prep needed.
4. **Blockers:** what's stopping any crew.
5. **Safety:** any condition to be aware of, any near-miss yesterday.

If a trade isn't on site, text them a summary of the huddle decisions that affect them.

### 6.3 Giving Clear Direction

**Verbal direction is the default.** Walk the work area, point at the work, state what you want, ask if it's clear.

**Written direction** when:
- Instructions are complex (multiple operations, sequence-dependent).
- A change is being directed (especially scope or method).
- You expect to need a record (problem sub, problem condition).

**Daily assignment** for direct employees: at the morning huddle, write each person's day on a whiteboard or in a shared note. At end of day, brief check-in: what's done, what's tomorrow.

### 6.4 Handling Conflict Between Workers

On a small site, two people can wreck the chemistry of the whole crew. Address conflict immediately:

1. Talk to each side individually first.
2. Bring them together if appropriate, with you as moderator. State the conflict, ask each to describe it, agree on a resolution.
3. If it's a values issue (theft, dishonesty, harassment) — separate immediately, escalate, do not negotiate.
4. If one party is consistently the source — they're not the right fit. Cut the loss. Replacing one person is easier than fixing a toxic site.

### 6.5 Safety Management on Residential Sites

OSHA 1926 applies to all residential construction. The major exposures:

**Fall protection (Subpart M).** Per [29 CFR 1926.501](https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1926/1926.501): fall protection required at **6 feet** on residential construction. Methods: guardrails, safety nets, personal fall arrest. STD 03-11-002 historically allowed a "fall protection plan" on residential where conventional fall protection was infeasible, but the burden of proof is on the contractor and OSHA expects conventional methods as the default. ([OSHA Subpart M](https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/regulations/standardnumber/1926/1926SubpartM))

In practice:
- Roofing: harness with rope grab and anchor point on every roofer above 6 ft.
- Floor framing 2nd floor: guardrails or harnessed once the joists are decked.
- Window openings > 6 ft above grade: covered, harnessed, or guardrailed.
- Floor holes (stair openings) covered with secured, marked, load-rated cover.

**Electrical (Subpart K).** GFCI on every temp circuit. Don't tap into the home's panel for tools until covered and inspected.

**Stairs and ladders (Subpart X).** Ladder 3-point contact. Don't stand on the top step. Use a stair tower or scaffolding on multi-day repetitive elevated work.

**PPE.** Hard hats when overhead work is in progress. Safety glasses always. Hearing protection at saws and nail guns continuous. Cut-resistant gloves when handling metal flashing. Respirators for fiberglass, silica, lead, mold.

**Silica (Subpart Z, 1926.1153).** Cutting concrete, masonry, tile = silica exposure. Wet methods or vacuum-shroud tools required.

**Toolbox talks.** Weekly 10-minute safety meeting. Topics rotate: fall protection, ladder safety, PPE, hand tools, power tools, hot work, weather, hazardous materials. Document with sign-in sheet.

**Workers comp.** Cover ALL employees. Day labor — verify the agency provides WC; if not, you're the employer of record and pay it through your policy. Carry an umbrella over your GL.

**Workers comp classification.** Residential framing is typically code 5645 / 5651; carpentry is 5403 / 5645. Misclassification (calling framers "carpentry, NOC") can result in audit and back-premiums.

### 6.6 Employee vs. Subcontractor Classification

The IRS / DOL classification test (simplified, **6-factor economic-reality test** under the 2024 DOL rule):

1. **Opportunity for profit or loss** depending on managerial skill.
2. **Investment** by worker and employer.
3. **Permanence** of the relationship.
4. **Nature and degree of control** the employer exercises.
5. **Integrality** to the employer's business.
6. **Skill and initiative** of the worker.

A worker who only works for you, on your schedule, with your tools, doing your trade — is an employee. Pay them on W-2, withhold taxes, carry WC, pay your share of FICA.

A sub who works on multiple jobs for multiple GCs, with their own tools, license, and crew — is a 1099 contractor. They pay their own taxes; you carry no WC for them but verify they carry their own.

Misclassification penalties on residential:
- Back taxes (your share + theirs) for up to three years.
- Workers comp premium recalculation.
- DOL fines for FLSA violations (overtime).
- State-level penalties (CA AB5 is severe).
- Personal liability of the GC owner in many states.

When in doubt, treat as employee. The IRS bias is toward employee classification.

## 7. Working With the Owner

### 7.1 Setting Expectations at Kickoff

The kickoff meeting with the owner — held within a week of contract signing — establishes the rules of engagement for the entire project. Outputs:

- **Communication plan:** weekly meeting (when, where), weekly written update (Friday email), urgent decision window (24 hours).
- **Decision deadlines:** memo listing every owner decision (paint colors, fixtures, finishes), with the date by which the decision must be made to not delay the schedule. Have the owner sign this.
- **Site access rules:** when the owner can be on site (typically after hours, or with an appointment), where they can park, that they should not direct subs (only the GC directs the subs).
- **Change order process:** any change goes through a written CO. The GC prices it, the owner signs it, then it's executed. No oral changes.
- **Allowance items:** any allowances (tile, fixtures, appliances) — what's included in the allowance, how overages are handled.
- **Payment schedule:** dates and percentages.
- **Owner-furnished items:** delivery dates, who unloads, who's responsible for damage.

### 7.2 Change Orders

A change order has four elements:

1. **Description** of the change.
2. **Cost** — labor, material, equipment, markup. Be transparent.
3. **Schedule impact** — days added or "no impact."
4. **Signature** — owner signs before the work is performed.

**Pricing.** Time + material with markup is honest but unpredictable. Lump sum with markup is predictable but you must include contingency for the unknown. On most residential work, use lump sum on clear changes (add a window, swap a faucet to a more expensive one) and T&M with cap on unclear changes (hidden conditions in a remodel).

**Markup.** On residential, 15–25% on direct cost is typical. Some GCs use 10% overhead + 10% profit (stated separately).

**Documentation.** Email is the modern CO. Send a CO template, owner replies "approved," done. Save the email.

### 7.3 Owner-Furnished Materials

OFM (sometimes called OFCI — owner-furnished, contractor-installed) is common on residential — owners want to buy their own faucets, lights, tile, appliances. The risk to the GC:

- **Wrong item delivered:** owner buys a faucet that doesn't fit the rough; you eat the rework or pass it to the owner.
- **Damaged item:** who's responsible — the owner, the GC who unloaded, or the freight company?
- **Lead-time delay:** owner doesn't order in time; the trade is on standby.
- **Warranty:** when the owner-furnished faucet fails in year two, who replaces it?

Protect yourself in the contract:
- **Owner is responsible for ordering, delivery, and damage** until the GC accepts in writing.
- **GC has 24–48 hours to inspect at delivery.** If GC accepts, GC is responsible for damage thereafter.
- **GC is not warrantying owner-furnished material** — only the installation.
- **GC has the right to reject installation** if material doesn't meet code or manufacturer install requirements.

### 7.4 Owner-Caused Delays

Document, document, document. Email is your friend.

Common owner-caused delays:
- Indecision on finish selection.
- Late delivery of OFM.
- Mid-construction scope additions without CO.
- Failure to grant access (locking the gate, refusing entry).
- Failure to make payments (you stop work; this is a contract right; consult counsel).

When you see a delay forming, send a written notice immediately:
- "We need [decision/material/payment] by [date] to maintain schedule. After [date+1], we will [stop work / reassign crew / charge standby / extend completion date by X days]."

Then deliver on what you said. Owners learn fast.

### 7.5 Keeping the Owner Engaged Without Disrupting the Job

The owner wants to be involved. Channel it productively:
- **Weekly walkthrough** with the owner — Saturday morning, 30 minutes, with you (not subs). Update on progress, decisions needed, anything to flag.
- **Photo updates** every few days — texts of milestones.
- **Decision packets** — group decisions and present together rather than one-by-one. "Here are the four bath tile choices in your range — please pick by Friday."
- **Off-hours selection meetings** with vendors (tile showroom, lighting showroom) on Saturday mornings.

What to discourage:
- Owner on site during work hours, directing subs.
- Owner texting subs directly.
- Owner sending design changes via Pinterest screenshots without CO process.
- Owner inviting friends and family for tours during work (insurance issue).

## 8. Estimating a Residential Project

### 8.1 Takeoff Sequence

Work through the plan systematically, room by room and assembly by assembly:

1. **Site** — clearing area, demo SF, excavation CY, haul CY, erosion control LF, utility trench LF.
2. **Concrete** — footing LF, footing CY, wall CY, slab SF, flatwork SF, rebar lbs.
3. **Framing** — wall LF × height for studs, sheathing SF, roof rafters/trusses, floor joist LF, hardware count.
4. **Roofing** — squares of shingles, valley LF, flashing LF, vent EA.
5. **Windows / doors** — count by size and type; reference the schedule.
6. **MEP** — fixture count for plumbing, device count for electrical, ton count and duct LF for HVAC; price as sub bid not unit.
7. **Insulation** — SF by assembly and R-value.
8. **Drywall** — SF of board (1.05 × wall + ceiling SF for waste).
9. **Exterior** — siding SF, trim LF, paint SF, soffit SF.
10. **Interior finishes** — paint SF, tile SF, hardwood SF, carpet SY.
11. **Cabinets / millwork** — LF of base/upper, count of doors/drawers; price as quote.
12. **Trim** — door and casing count, base LF, crown LF.
13. **Appliances** — count.
14. **Landscape / flatwork** — SF/CY.

### 8.2 Key Units and Productivity Reference

| Trade | Common Unit | Typical Productivity (rough) |
|---|---|---|
| Excavation | CY hauled | 80–150 CY/day on a residential foundation |
| Concrete (footings) | CY placed | 5–10 CY/hr for residential pour |
| Framing | LF of wall / SF of floor | 200–400 LF of wall/day for a 3-person crew |
| Roofing | Squares (100 SF) | 2–4 squares per roofer per day on asphalt |
| Drywall hang | SF/day per crew | 1,000–1,500 SF/day for a 2-person crew |
| Drywall finish | SF/day per crew | 600–1,000 SF/day per finisher |
| Paint walls | SF/day | 1,000–1,500 SF/day (one coat, roll) |
| Tile floor | SF/day | 100–250 SF/day per setter |
| Hardwood install | SF/day | 250–500 SF/day per installer |
| Trim | LF/day | 80–150 LF of trim per finish carpenter |
| Electrical rough | Devices/day | 30–60 devices roughed per electrician |
| Plumbing rough | Fixtures/day | 4–8 fixtures roughed per plumber |

### 8.3 Sub Bid Review — What's Often Left Out

When reviewing a sub bid, check for these commonly-omitted items:

- **Cleanup** of their own trade's debris.
- **Dumpster** — GC's or sub's?
- **Temporary power and water** — usually GC's, confirm.
- **Permit fees** — sub gets MEP permit; GC gets building.
- **Hoisting** — crane for trusses, cabinets to second floor.
- **Travel / mobilization** if the sub is from out of area.
- **Backcharges** for damage to other trades' work.
- **Warranty** scope and duration.
- **As-builts** — drawings reflecting field changes (often required for MEP).
- **Specific scope items** the plans show but the sub didn't break out (e.g., hose bibs not on the plumbing fixture schedule; one in the front, one in the back).

### 8.4 Markup, Overhead, Contingency

Components of GC pricing on residential:

| Component | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Direct cost (subs + material + your labor) | 100% baseline | Sum of cost |
| General conditions (super, port-o-john, dumpsters, temp utilities, site office, supervision) | 8–15% of direct cost | Time-based; longer schedule = more GCs |
| Overhead (your office, insurance, owner's salary) | 10–15% of direct cost | Allocated across your projects |
| Profit | 8–15% of direct cost | Margin you keep |
| Contingency | 3–10% of direct cost | Higher on remodels and unknowns |
| **Total markup over direct cost** | **25–55%** | Sum of the above |

In practice, residential GCs often state "15% overhead + 10% profit" or "20% on cost-plus" for transparency. On lump sum, the markup is buried in the line items and the contingency is invisible to the owner.

### 8.5 Presenting the Estimate to the Owner

Two formats:

**Line item (cost-plus or transparent lump sum):**
```
1. Site / Excavation       $X
2. Foundation              $X
3. Framing                 $X
...
12. Final cleaning         $X
Subtotal direct           $X
Overhead (10%)             $X
Profit (10%)               $X
Contingency (5%)           $X
TOTAL                      $X
```

**Summary (lump sum, less transparent):**
```
Project Total: $X
   Site work, foundation, structure: $X
   MEP rough and finishes: $X
   Interior finishes: $X
   Site improvements: $X
   
Allowances:
   Tile: $X
   Plumbing fixtures: $X
   Lighting: $X
   Appliances: $X
   Cabinetry: $X
```

Most owners want a line-item view of the major chunks, with allowance items called out separately. Be honest about what's allowance vs. fixed — allowances will fluctuate.

## 9. Closeout and Punch List

### 9.1 Walking the Punch List

The punch is the gap between "substantially complete" and "done." Done right, it's a finite list completed in 1–3 weeks. Done wrong, it's a six-month nag.

**Walk in this order:**

1. **Systems first.** Run HVAC through heat and cool. Run every plumbing fixture. Test every electrical device. Test smoke/CO. Test the garage door. Test every operable window and door.
2. **Finishes** — every wall, ceiling, trim joint. Look for nail pops, paint touch-ups, caulk gaps, scratches.
3. **Cabinets and counters** — every door and drawer operates, no scratches, hardware aligned.
4. **Floors** — squeaks, scratches, transitions, finishes.
5. **Exterior** — siding, paint, deck, roof from the ground.
6. **Site** — grade, walkways, gutters, drainage, landscape.

### 9.2 Organizing Punch Items

By trade is the most useful organization for follow-up:

```
PUNCH LIST — [PROJECT] — [DATE]
PAINTER:
- Touch up nail pops north wall master bedroom
- Caulk gap at base in kitchen island east side
- Repaint scuff on staircase wall above landing
PLUMBER:
- Tighten kitchen faucet handle (loose)
- Replace shower head hardware (chipped at delivery)
ELECTRICIAN:
- Replace burned-out can light in foyer
- Re-aim track lights over kitchen island
HVAC:
- Air balance — master bedroom under-supplied
- Replace MERV 8 filter (construction filter still in)
FRAMER / TRIM:
- Refit master bedroom door — drags at top
- Re-miter crown corner in dining room (open)
```

Also tag by **room** and **priority** — items the owner sees daily are priority A.

### 9.3 Getting Subs Back for Punch

The single biggest closeout problem on residential: subs disappear once they think they're done.

What works:
- **Hold the final 10% retainage** until punch is closed.
- **Schedule punch within 1–2 weeks of substantial completion** — fresh in everyone's mind.
- **Visit the sub's owner in person** if their crew is unresponsive.
- **Backcharge** their final invoice for items you complete yourself.
- **Reference call** — let them know future referrals depend on punch performance.

What doesn't work:
- Weekly emails reminding them.
- Threatening lien releases (you already needed those at progress draws).
- Calling the same foreman who's now on the next job.

### 9.4 Final Inspections and Certificate of Occupancy

Sequence:
1. Self-walk for known final items (smokes work, address number visible, grade pitched, GFCIs trip, handrail returns).
2. Schedule final inspections — building, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, sometimes separate.
3. Pass finals.
4. Energy / HERS final (if required).
5. Building department issues CO (certificate of occupancy) or CFO (final).

**Without CO, the house cannot be legally occupied** (in most jurisdictions). Some jurisdictions issue Temporary CO (TCO) if minor items remain (landscape sod not in due to season, etc.).

### 9.5 Warranty Period

The standard residential warranty is **1 year on workmanship** from the date of substantial completion or CO. Manufacturer warranties run longer:
- Roofing: 25–50 years per manufacturer.
- Windows: 10–25 years per manufacturer.
- Equipment (HVAC, water heater): 5–10 years parts, 1 year labor (sometimes longer with extended).
- Structural (in some states, statutorily extended to 10 years).

**What you owe in warranty:**
- Workmanship defects (settling cracks, drywall pops, paint touch-up after the first season is debatable).
- Coordinate manufacturer claims (call the manufacturer's rep for the owner; don't make the owner do it).
- One scheduled warranty walk at 11 months (right before warranty expires).

**What to exclude in the contract:**
- Owner-caused damage.
- Normal wear and tear.
- Settling cracks under 1/16" (cosmetic).
- Items affected by owner's failure to maintain (HVAC filters not changed, gutters not cleaned).
- Owner-furnished items.

**How to handle warranty calls:**
- **24-hour response standard.** Even if you can't fix it that day, acknowledge.
- **Quick assessment** — is this a defect or wear/maintenance?
- **Document each call** in a warranty log (date, issue, resolution).
- **One scheduled return visit** with all warranty items batched (not 17 separate trips).
- **At 11 months:** scheduled walk; address legitimate items; close out warranty.

## 10. Daily and Weekly Cadence — Putting It Together

This is the rhythm of running a residential job. Use it as a template; adjust to your context.

### 10.1 Daily

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 6:30–7:00 | Site prep — unlock, lights on, port-o-john checked, materials staged |
| 7:00–7:15 | Daily huddle with active trades |
| 7:15–11:00 | Walk active trades, address issues, schedule deliveries |
| 11:00–12:00 | Office work — emails, paperwork, owner update, CO drafting |
| 12:00–1:00 | Lunch (eat at the site if possible — accessible to subs) |
| 1:00–4:00 | Walk again, hold pre-work meetings, take photos |

## 12. Closing — The Disposition of a Residential GC

Three habits separate the GCs who survive their first year from the ones who don't.

**1. Write everything down.** Daily log, photos, change orders, sub directions, owner decisions. Memory fails. Paper wins arguments. Email is paper. If a sub does something on a verbal direction and it goes wrong, the next sub will swear they were told something else. The GC who has the photo dated and time-stamped wins.

**2. Walk the work.** Email tells you what people say is happening. The site tells you what is actually happening. Walk three times a day at minimum. The difference between an okay GC and a great one is how many issues they catch by eye before the inspector or the owner does.

**3. Hold the schedule.** The schedule is your tool against every party — the owner who wants to add scope, the sub who wants an extra week, the supplier who wants to deliver late. Once the schedule slips and is allowed to slip, the entire project loses discipline. Defend it.

The trades will respect you for being fair and clear. The owner will respect you for being honest about cost and time. The inspector will respect you for being prepared. None of them need you to be the smartest person in the room — they need you to be the one who is reliably present, organized, and decisive.

The work is the work. The product is a house. The discipline is everything else.
