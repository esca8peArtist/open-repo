# Architecture for the General Contractor

A working reference for the residential and light-commercial GC who has built buildings but has not previously worked shoulder-to-shoulder with a design team. The focus is reading, interpreting, and coordinating architectural drawings and specifications — not designing them. By the end of this you should be able to pick up an unfamiliar drawing set, walk it from cover to detail in under an hour, identify the highest-risk coordination gaps, and run a clean RFI/submittal process with the architect of record.

> **Bottom line first**: The architect's drawings are a *legal instrument of service*, not a build sheet. They show design intent and minimum performance, and they delegate means and methods to you. The GC who reads the General Notes, Division 01, and the wall sections — in that order — before opening the floor plans will avoid 80% of the disputes that sink small-firm projects.

## Part 1 — The Architect's Role and the Design Process

### 1.1 Phases of Service — What You Get and When

The AIA structures the architect's basic services in five phases. As a GC, the phase the documents are in determines what you can rely on, what you can price, and what you should not be installing yet.

| Phase | Abbrev. | Deliverable | Typical % of design fee | What the GC sees |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Schematic Design | SD | Diagrammatic plans, single-line elevations, massing, narrative spec | ~15% | Concept only. Useful for ROM ("rough order of magnitude") pricing ±25–30% |
| Design Development | DD | Dimensioned plans, sections, outline spec, MEP single-lines, key details | ~20% | Permittable in some residential jurisdictions; pricing ±10–15% |
| Construction Documents | CD | Full bid set: drawings + specifications, sealed | ~40% | This is what you bid and build from |
| Bidding / Negotiation | — | Addenda, bid analysis, contract award | ~5% | Q&A period, substitution review |
| Construction Administration | CA | Site visits, RFI/submittal review, ASIs, pay app certification, punch list | ~20% | The phase you work with the architect every week |

**Why GC involvement matters at each phase**:

- **SD**: If you have a preconstruction contract (CM-style), this is where you flag major cost drivers — site access, complex geometry, signature finishes — *before* the design hardens around them. Once geometry is set in DD, value engineering becomes amputation.
- **DD**: The right moment for constructability review. Wall types are chosen, MEP zones are reserved, structural concept is locked. Push for clarity on building envelope, weatherproofing, and any unusual assembly. Get the spec outline so you can start subcontractor pre-qualification.
- **CD**: The drawings get sealed and stamped. This is the legal scope. Read the entire set. Read the entire spec. Issue questions in writing as RFIs against the bid set — they become contract addenda if answered before bid close.
- **CA**: The architect is the owner's eyes and ears. Your relationship here determines whether the job runs smoothly or by ambush.

### 1.2 The Architect as Owner's Agent — Formal Workflow

Under AIA A201 (General Conditions) and B101 (Owner-Architect Agreement), the architect is the owner's *agent* for design intent interpretation, but is **not** the owner's representative for cost or schedule decisions unless explicitly contracted. The architect:

- **Can**: Interpret drawings, reject non-conforming work, certify pay applications, recommend rejection of substitutions, issue ASIs that don't change cost or time.
- **Cannot**: Direct the means/methods, accept work, waive contract requirements, authorize change orders (only the owner can), or stop the work (only the owner can — though architects can recommend it).

**The four formal documents you'll exchange weekly**:

| Document | Initiated by | Purpose | Binds whom |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RFI** (Request for Information) | GC | Clarify ambiguity, missing info, conflict in docs | Architect's response becomes part of the contract docs |
| **Submittal** (shop drawing, product data, sample, mock-up) | GC (via sub) | Demonstrate compliance with design intent before fabrication | "Approved" / "Approved as Noted" releases for fabrication, not for changed scope |
| **ASI** (Architect's Supplemental Instructions) | Architect | Minor clarifications, no cost or time impact | Binds GC unless GC responds within contract time with a CCD/PCO claim |
| **CCD / PR** (Construction Change Directive / Proposal Request) | Owner via architect | Directs a change before price is negotiated | Pricing follows under change order |
| **Change Order (CO)** | Owner | Formal change of contract sum/time | Signed by owner, architect, GC |

**Critical**: An ASI is not a license for free work. If the architect issues an ASI that adds cost or time, you must respond in writing — usually within 7 to 21 days per the contract — with a Change Order Request (COR) or you waive the claim. Read your A201 Article 7 ("Changes in the Work") and Article 15 ("Claims and Disputes") and post the time limits on the trailer wall.

### 1.3 Design-Bid-Build vs. Design-Build — Relationship Differences

| Aspect | Design-Bid-Build (DBB) | Design-Build (DB) | CM at Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contract relationship | Owner holds two contracts (architect, GC) | Owner holds one contract with DB entity (which holds architect as sub or partner) | Owner holds two but GC is on board during design |
| Architect reports to | Owner | DB entity (often the GC) | Owner |
| GC enters | After CDs complete, via bid | Day one | During DD typically |
| Liability for design errors | Architect to owner | DB entity to owner — GC absorbs design risk | Architect to owner |
| Substitutions | Hard — must prove equivalence | Easy — architect works for you | Medium |
| Adversarial risk | Highest | Lowest in theory; depends on internal politics | Low |
| Best for | Public work, clear scope | Fast-track, complex MEP, owner wants single point of responsibility | Owner wants cost certainty mid-design |

In **residential DBB**, the architect is the homeowner's advocate and will scrutinize every variance from the documents. In **DB**, you are often *hiring* the architect — be careful: the architect still owes a duty of care to the owner under most state licensing law even when contractually subordinate to you. Don't ask them to do something that puts their license at risk.

### 1.4 AIA B101 — What the Architect Is and Isn't Responsible For

**AIA B101-2017** is the standard Owner-Architect agreement. Key clauses a GC should know:

- **§3.6.2.1 Site visits**: The architect visits "at intervals appropriate to the stage of construction" — typically 1–4 visits per month on a residence. They are **not** continuous inspectors. If you build it wrong between visits, that's on you.
- **§3.6.2.2 Means and methods**: Architect is "not responsible for the construction means, methods, techniques, sequences or procedures, or for safety precautions and programs." This is your scope, completely.
- **§3.6.4.1 Rejection of work**: Architect has authority to reject work that does not conform to the documents.
- **§3.6.4.2 Submittals**: Architect's review is "only for the limited purpose of checking for conformance with information given and the design concept" — NOT for accuracy of dimensions, quantities, or coordination with other trades. That's the GC's job. Read this clause until it's tattooed.
- **§3.6.6 Certificates for payment**: The architect's certification is based on observation and is not a guarantee that you've installed it correctly. Just that they haven't seen evidence you haven't.

The architect carries **professional liability** (errors and omissions) insurance, typically $1M–$2M per claim. You carry **commercial general liability** and **builder's risk**. They cover different things — neither covers the other.

### 1.5 When the GC Pushes Back vs. When the GC Complies

**Comply (and document)**:
- Aesthetic preferences within tolerance — paint sheen, grout color, trim profile.
- Design intent that's buildable but more expensive than your preferred method.
- Code-compliant assemblies even when you think they're overbuilt.
- Architect's interpretation of ambiguous drawings, *provided* you got it in writing as an RFI response.

**Push back (in writing, via RFI)**:
- Code violations — you can be cited as the installer regardless of the stamp on the drawing.
- Constructability impossibilities (insufficient clearance, missing structure for shown weight).
- Manufacturer instruction conflicts that void warranty (especially window/door flashing, roofing, waterproofing).
- Drawings vs. spec conflicts — the spec usually governs unless the contract says otherwise (AIA A201 §1.2.1.1 is ambiguous; most contracts amend to "specifications govern" or "more stringent governs").
- Anything that puts a trade subcontractor in an OSHA-violating position.

**Never**:
- Make an unauthorized field change. A field change without an RFI response or ASI is your money — and if the change harms anyone, your liability.
- Tell the homeowner the architect is wrong without copying the architect. Triangulation poisons every job.

## Part 2 — Reading Architectural Drawing Sets

### 2.1 Sheet Organization — The Standard Order

A complete CD set is assembled in discipline order, then by sheet number within discipline. The **U.S. National CAD Standard (NCS)** sheet numbering convention:

`[Discipline Designator][Sheet Type].[Sheet Sequence]`

| Discipline | Letter | Common sub-disciplines |
|---|---|---|
| General | G | G0.x (cover/index), G1.x (code summary) |
| Architectural | A | A0.x (general), A1.x (plans), A2.x (elevations), A3.x (sections), A4.x (enlarged plans), A5.x (details), A6.x (interior elevations), A7.x (schedules), A8.x (interior details) |
| Civil | C | Site, grading, utilities |
| Landscape | L | |
| Structural | S | |
| Mechanical (HVAC) | M | |
| Plumbing | P | |
| Electrical | E | |
| Fire Protection | FP or F | |
| Technology / Low Voltage | T | |
| Equipment | Q | Kitchen, lab |
| Interiors | I | Finishes, FF&E |

**Sheet type number meanings** (NCS):

| Number | Type |
|---|---|
| 0 | General — cover sheet, index, abbreviations, symbols, general notes |
| 1 | Plans (horizontal cuts) |
| 2 | Elevations (vertical exterior views) |
| 3 | Sections (vertical cuts) |
| 4 | Large-scale views — enlarged plans, enlarged elevations |
| 5 | Details |
| 6 | Schedules and diagrams |
| 7 | User defined — often interior elevations |
| 8 | User defined — often interior details |
| 9 | 3D representations |

So **A2.03** is the third architectural elevation sheet. **A5.21** is detail sheet 21 in the architectural set. Once you know this, you can navigate any project's drawings without asking.

**The canonical reading order for a new set**:

1. **Cover / Index (G0.0)** — confirms what's in the set; check that nothing referenced elsewhere is missing
2. **Code summary / Life Safety (G1.0)** — occupancy, construction type, allowable area, egress
3. **General Notes (A0.0)** — the legal small print; assumptions you must verify
4. **Site Plan / Civil** — where the building goes, setbacks, utilities
5. **Floor Plans (A1.x)** — overall layout
6. **Exterior Elevations (A2.x)** — what it looks like from outside, cladding zones
7. **Building Sections (A3.x)** — story heights, structural relationships
8. **Wall Sections (A3.x or A5.x)** — assembly, envelope, the GC's most-read sheet
9. **RCPs (A1.x late series, e.g. A1.5)** — ceilings, MEP coordination zone
10. **Enlarged Plans (A4.x)** — kitchens, baths, stairs
11. **Interior Elevations (A6.x or A7.x)** — cabinets, tile, trim layout
12. **Schedules (A7.x or A6.x)** — doors, windows, finishes, hardware
13. **Details (A5.x, A8.x)** — connections, transitions, terminations

### 2.2 Title Block Anatomy

Every sheet has a title block, conventionally on the right margin. Read it in this order:

| Block element | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Project name and address | Confirm you're holding the right job |
| Architect's name, address, license number, state seal | Architect of Record; the seal is the legal signature |
| Issue date and issue type | "Issued for Permit" / "Issued for Bid" / "Issued for Construction" — these are NOT the same set |
| Revision block (rev #, date, description) | Every revision since the original issue, in chronological order |
| Drawn by / Checked by / Approved by | Internal QC; useful when calling the firm |
| Sheet number and sheet title | Self-explanatory |
| Project number | Architect's internal number; use it on every RFI |
| Scale | Drawing scale — **always verify it matches what you're scaling**; printer reductions are common |
| Key plan / north arrow | Where this sheet is in the building, which way is up |

**Revision clouds and triangles**: A cloud (irregular bubble) outlines a revised area; the triangle (delta) with a number inside identifies which revision. Match the triangle number to the revision block.

> **Trap**: An "Issued for Permit" set is for AHJ review, not for construction. If you build from it, you are building from a non-construction set. Always confirm the latest "Issued for Construction" stamp before mobilizing.

### 2.3 Symbols and Conventions

Universal architectural symbols you must read fluently:

**Section cut**: A heavy line across the plan with a flag at each end. The flag is a circle (or hexagon) with two numbers — top = section number, bottom = sheet number where it appears. Arrow on the flag indicates view direction.

```
 (3/A3.1) ─────────────►
 ▲ section 3 on sheet A3.1, looking in arrow direction
```

**Elevation marker**: A circle with a triangle pointing to a wall. Interior elevation tags often use a four-sided "compass rose" with a number on each side showing which elevation you'll find for that wall on which sheet.

**Detail callout**: A bubble (circle, hexagon) with a leader to a specific location. Top number = detail #, bottom = sheet #.

**Door tag**: Usually a circle, hexagon, or oval with the door number (often matches the room number it opens into; e.g. 102A = first door into room 102).

**Window tag**: Hexagon or special shape with letter or number tying to the window schedule.

**Room tag**: Bubble or rectangle with room name and number — e.g., `KITCHEN / 105`.

**North arrow**: True north and project north can differ; the project north is rotated to keep the drawing rectangular. The architect's plans use project north; the civil/survey plans use true north. Match before locating.

**Scale bar**: A graphic bar showing 0, 5, 10 ft etc. — use this not the stated scale, because reductions distort.

**Match line**: Heavy dashed line indicating where one sheet ends and continues on another (large buildings).

**Break line**: Zigzag indicating drawing is truncated for clarity.

### 2.4 Keynote Systems

Two flavors:

- **General keynote list** (sometimes called a legend): Numbers or letters apply across the entire set. `KN-12 = 5/8" Type X gypsum board on metal furring` would mean the same thing on every sheet.
- **Sheet keynotes**: Numbers apply only to that sheet. The keynote list lives in a column on the same sheet, and the numbers reset on the next sheet.

The keynote list typically references a **specification section** in parentheses or brackets — e.g., `8. WINDOW, SEE SCHED (08 50 00)`. This is your bridge from the drawing to the spec book. Always follow the keynote to the spec section before pricing or installing.

### 2.5 Reading Schedules

Schedules are tables that move repetitive information off the drawings. The four you'll use constantly:

**Door schedule** columns typically include:
- Door number / tag
- Size (W × H × thickness)
- Material (SCWD = solid core wood door, HM = hollow metal, etc.)
- Type / elevation reference (door type drawing)
- Frame material and type
- Fire rating (20 min, 45 min, 60 min, 90 min)
- Hardware set (HW-1, HW-2 etc., referencing the hardware specification)
- Head/jamb/sill detail references
- Remarks (smoke seal, weather strip, vision lite, etc.)

**Window schedule**:
- Window number, size, type, frame, glazing, U-value, SHGC (for Title 24 in California), sill height, head/jamb/sill details, screen, remarks.

**Room finish schedule**:
- Room number and name, floor finish, base, north/south/east/west wall finishes, ceiling finish, ceiling height, remarks.

**Hardware schedule** (more often in the spec, Division 08 71 00):
- Hardware set number (HW-1)
- Listing of every piece: hinges (BB1191 4.5 × 4.5), lockset (Schlage L9050), closer (LCN 4040), threshold, weather seal, etc.
- ANSI/BHMA grade

**How a GC uses schedules during construction**:

| Phase | Schedule use |
|---|---|
| Buyout | Quantify doors/windows/hardware for sub bids; verify lead times |
| Submittal | Generate one consolidated submittal package, not per-door |
| Rough framing | Cross-reference rough opening sizes against door/window schedule before framing inspection |
| Pre-finish | Confirm finish selections in field; chase down "TBD" entries 4 weeks before drywall |
| Punch list | Walk the schedule; verify every door, window, finish matches |

### 2.6 Dimensions — A Common Source of Conflict

The dimension you scale and the dimension the architect intends are not always the same plane. Three conventions to memorize:

| Convention | Where used | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| **Face of stud / face of concrete (FOS, FOC)** | Most residential and light commercial framing plans | Structural dimensioning convention |
| **Face of finish (FOF)** | Interior layouts, ADA clearances, cabinet/appliance fits | What the homeowner sees |
| **Grid line / centerline** | Heavy commercial, steel-framed | Structural grid is the spine; everything else relative |

The drawings should state which is used in the General Notes. If they don't, **issue an RFI before laying out**. Common scenarios where this bites:

- Architect dimensions to "FOF" for a 36" ADA door clear width — you lay out to FOS — once drywall and frame go in, the clear width is 33.5". Fail.
- Dimensions stack: three rooms at 12'-0" each = 36'-0", but the exterior dimension shows 36'-2". The 2" is "hold" / "balance" / "field verify" and must be assigned to one wall — usually a closet or chase. Always reconcile.
- A "string dimension" line shows partial dimensions adding to an overall. **Compare the sum of the partials to the overall on every plan**. If they don't match, RFI immediately. Architects sometimes leave the overall correct and let one partial absorb the difference — sometimes they don't.

> **GC discipline**: Before framing any wall, lay it out on the slab in chalk, then walk the architect through it. Two hours of layout review prevents two weeks of demo.

### 2.7 Line Weights and Layers

Drawings communicate hierarchy through line weight:

| Weight | Use |
|---|---|
| Heavy (0.7 mm+) | Cut lines — the edges of objects cut by the drawing plane (walls cut in plan, items cut in section) |
| Medium (0.35 mm) | Visible edges of objects not cut (cabinet faces in elevation, door swings in plan) |
| Light (0.18 mm) | Hidden lines, surface texture, hatching, dimension lines |
| Very light | Construction lines, grid lines |

**Hatching conventions** (residential and light commercial common ones):

| Pattern | Material |
|---|---|
| Diagonal hatching | Concrete |
| Stippled / dotted | Sand, gravel, or earth |
| Wood-grain | Wood (finish) |
| Wood with diagonal slash | Wood (framing or rough) |
| Dense diagonal cross | CMU / masonry |
| Insulation symbol (curly) | Batt insulation; rigid is straight diagonal lines or labeled "RI" |

CAD layers and BIM categories (more relevant when you receive the model) typically follow the AIA layer guidelines: discipline-major-minor (`A-WALL-FULL`, `A-DOOR`, `A-GLAZ`). You don't need to author these but knowing them helps when you ask the architect to turn on or off a layer for a coordination print.

## Part 3 — Plan Types in Depth

### 3.1 Floor Plans

A floor plan is a horizontal cut, conventionally taken at 4'-0" above finish floor (so it cuts through doors, windows, and standard outlets). What you should systematically check on every floor plan:

| Check | Why |
|---|---|
| Column grid lines and dimensions | Backbone — everything else relates to these |
| Wall types (W1, W2, etc.) keyed to wall type legend | Different STC, fire rating, thickness — affects layout |
| Room names and numbers | Match the finish schedule |
| Door swings and tags | Verify operability — does that door clear the toilet? |
| Window locations and tags | Confirm tag matches schedule type |
| Dimension chains | Partials sum to overall? Always check |
| Plumbing fixture symbols | Confirm against P drawings — same count and locations |
| Stair direction arrows (UP / DN) | Stair runs and landings consistent with section |
| Elevation/section markers | All callouts lead to a real sheet |
| Slab edges, slopes, depressions | Tile recess at showers, slope to floor drains, etc. |

**Wall type legend** lives on its own sheet (often `A0.2` or near the floor plans). Each type designates:
- Width
- Studs: size, spacing, material (2x4 wood @ 16" oc, 3-5/8" 25 ga metal @ 16" oc)
- Sheathing both sides (5/8" Type X GWB ea side)
- Insulation
- Fire rating (1-hr, 2-hr) with UL listing number
- STC (sound transmission class) for separations
- WRB, vapor retarder if exterior

**Partition heights**: Floor plans rarely show partition height; check the RCP. Walls are commonly noted as:
- "Slab to deck above" (full height)
- "To 6" above ceiling" (acoustic but not full)
- "To ceiling" (cosmetic only)

### 3.2 Reflected Ceiling Plans (RCP)

An RCP is the view looking *down* on the ceiling as if a mirror were on the floor — so the plan reads in the same orientation as the floor plan. RCPs coordinate:

- Ceiling materials and heights (AFF = above finished floor)
- Light fixtures (with fixture type tags keyed to a lighting schedule on E drawings)
- HVAC supply, return, exhaust diffusers (with neck size and CFM if shown by architect; usually delegated to M)
- Sprinkler heads (concept locations; the FP sub will field-locate)
- Access panels (where? what size? what's behind?)
- Ceiling transitions, soffits, coves, recessed slot diffusers
- Speakers, smoke/CO detectors, occupancy sensors

**Hard-lid vs. accessible (T-bar) ceilings**:

| Type | Construction | Coordination concern |
|---|---|---|
| Hard-lid (drywall) | Framed soffit with gyp board | Anything above ceiling that needs service requires an access panel; locate them now or cut them in later (expensive) |
| Accessible (ACT / T-bar) | Suspended grid with lay-in tile | Easy access but light fixture and diffuser layouts must register with grid |
| Wood plank / specialty | Architect specifies | Often custom; flag long lead in submittal |

**The RCP is where MEP and architecture meet** — and where coordination problems live. Lay an RCP, mechanical, electrical, and lighting plan over the same area and clash-check. In residential, a single 1×8 wood ceiling beam often conflicts with a recessed light or a duct that "fits in the joist bay."

### 3.3 Roof Plans

Roof plans show the building from above. Critical elements:

- **Slope arrows and pitch**: Residential typically "X:12" (e.g., 4:12 = 4" rise per 12" run). Commercial low-slope typically "1/4":12" or "1.5%" — the same thing, different notation. Always verify minimum 1/4":12" (~2%) on low-slope to prevent ponding.
- **Roof drainage**: Primary drains, scuppers, gutters. **Overflow drains required** by IPC (and CRC R903) — secondary system, typically 2" higher inlet or separate scupper, dumping where someone will notice (above an entry, classically).
- **Crickets and saddles**: Triangular framing behind chimneys, parapet returns, large curbs to direct water around obstructions. Required when an obstruction is wider than ~30" on a low slope.
- **Penetrations**: Plumbing vents, exhaust fans, RTUs, skylights, hatches. Each requires a detail showing flashing/curb.
- **Equipment curbs**: Pre-fabricated metal curbs for RTUs, exhaust fans. Size and spacing must match the equipment submittal — RFI any discrepancy.
- **Roof access**: Hatch, ladder, or door. Required by some codes when mechanical equipment is on the roof.
- **Parapet height and coping**: Heights must match the elevations; coping must be detailed in section.
- **Walk pads**: Required for service routes on membrane roofs (TPO, EPDM) to protect from foot traffic.

### 3.4 Site Plans — Architectural vs. Civil

Two different plans, often conflicting. Know which governs.

| Element | Architectural site plan | Civil site plan |
|---|---|---|
| Building footprint | Yes — definitive shape and dimensions | Shows building outline from architectural |
| Setbacks | Often shown | Definitive, from survey |
| Property lines | Shown | Definitive, from survey |
| Topography / contours | Existing / proposed simplified | Definitive grading, drainage |
| Driveways, parking | Layout shown | Engineered with slopes, ADA |
| Utilities | Service entry points only | Definitive routing, depths, inverts |
| Landscaping | Conceptual | Often a separate L sheet |
| Erosion control | No | Yes — SWPPP, BMPs |

**Rule of thumb**: Architectural site plan shows *what* and *where* at the building scale. Civil plan shows *how to build* the site work. **Civil governs grades, utilities, and stormwater** unless the contract says otherwise.

### 3.5 Building Sections — Mental Walkthrough

A building section cuts vertically through the entire building. To read one:

1. **Find the cut line on the floor plan** (the section marker) — orient yourself: which way are you looking?
2. **Identify the bearing elements** cut by the plane — walls, footings, columns, slabs, roof structure. These are the heaviest lines.
3. **Identify story heights** — finish floor to finish floor, finish floor to ceiling, ceiling to structure above.
4. **Locate vertical penetrations** — stairs, shafts, mechanical chases.
5. **Read the roof slope and parapet** — does the section match the roof plan?
6. **Trace the building envelope** continuously — foundation to roof, no breaks in the air barrier, water barrier, thermal barrier.

> Common story heights for residential: 9'-1" plate (8'-0" ceiling with 12" floor system). Light commercial: 10'-0" to 12'-0" plate. Verify before framing.

### 3.6 Wall Sections — The GC's Most Important Detail

A wall section is a vertical cut through the exterior wall from footing to roof, typically at 3/4" or 1" scale. It is the assembly drawing. Read it from outside to inside, bottom to top:

**From outside in (exterior wall)**:
1. Cladding (siding, stucco, brick, fiber cement)
2. Rainscreen gap or fastening base (if applicable)
3. Weather-resistant barrier (WRB)
4. Sheathing (OSB, plywood, gyp sheathing)
5. Structural framing with cavity insulation
6. Vapor retarder (climate-dependent: warm side in cold climates, omit or smart-vapor-open in mixed/hot)
7. Interior finish (drywall, paneling)

**From bottom up**:
1. Footing and foundation wall
2. Capillary break (sill seal, sill gasket)
3. Sill plate (treated where in contact with concrete)
4. Wall framing with fire-blocking, draft-stopping per code
5. Top plate(s)
6. Floor system (if multi-story)
7. Roof framing, eave detail, gutter, fascia

**The five barriers a wall section must show continuity of**:

| Barrier | Material example | What kills it |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Studs, sheathing | Bad fastening, missing blocking |
| Water (drainage plane) | WRB + flashings | Reverse laps, missing kick-out flashing |
| Air | Continuous taped sheathing, ADA membrane, or interior poly | Penetrations untaped |
| Thermal | Insulation | Compression, voids, thermal bridges |
| Vapor | Vapor retarder, depends on climate | Wrong side, double vapor barrier creating sandwich |

**Flashing hierarchy** (high to low — water must always travel from a higher overlap to a lower):
1. Roof flashing (highest priority)
2. Through-wall flashings at window heads, parapets
3. Window head, jamb, sill flashing — sill flashing pan first, then jamb, then head
4. Foundation / base flashing

The GC who reads every wall section before framing — and every detail referenced from those wall sections — catches 90% of the envelope problems that result in leaks.

## Part 4 — Details and Specifications

### 4.1 Three Types of Details

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Wall section / typical section** | Show full assembly | Standard exterior wall, foundation to roof |
| **Connection detail** | Show how two systems meet | Window head flashing to WRB, deck-to-house ledger |
| **Feature detail** | Show architectural feature | Built-in cabinet niche, custom railing, feature wall |

Each detail has:
- A title and number matching the callout
- A scale (1-1/2" = 1'-0" or 3" = 1'-0" most common; full size for tricky transitions)
- Material call-outs via keynote or leader
- Dimensions when critical
- Reference to spec section

### 4.2 CSI MasterFormat — How Specs Are Organized

The Construction Specifications Institute **MasterFormat** is the universal spec numbering convention (current edition: 50 divisions; older spec books may use 16-division MasterFormat 1995 — rarely seen now on new work).

| Division | Subject | What's in it (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| 00 | Procurement and Contracting Requirements | Bid forms, contract, bonds, conditions |
| **01** | **General Requirements** | **Read every word — see 4.3** |
| 02 | Existing Conditions | Demolition, remediation, site clearing |
| 03 | Concrete | Cast-in-place, precast, accessories |
| 04 | Masonry | CMU, brick, stone |
| 05 | Metals | Structural, miscellaneous, fabrications |
| 06 | Wood, Plastics, Composites | Rough carp, finish carp, casework |
| 07 | Thermal and Moisture Protection | Insulation, WRB, roofing, sealants — envelope-critical |
| 08 | Openings | Doors, windows, glazing, hardware, skylights |
| 09 | Finishes | Gyp, tile, flooring, paint, ceilings |
| 10 | Specialties | Toilet partitions, signage, fire extinguishers |
| 11 | Equipment | Appliances, kitchen equipment, residential equipment |
| 12 | Furnishings | Window treatments, casework, FF&E |
| 13 | Special Construction | Pools, saunas, integrated systems |

Each spec section is internally formatted in **CSI 3-Part Format**:

**Always read Part 1 of every relevant section before bidding** — that's where the submittals list, mock-up requirements, and warranty obligations hide.

### 4.3 Division 01 — General Requirements

Division 01 is the **operating manual for the project**. The whole GC team should read it before mobilizing. Typical sections:

| Section | Number | Why a GC reads it |
|---|---|---|
| Summary | 01 11 00 | Project scope, work by owner, phasing |
| Price and Payment Procedures | 01 20 00 | Schedule of values, allowances, unit prices, application for payment |

| Submittal Procedures | 01 33 00 | Submittal log, formatting, electronic vs. paper, lead times |
| Quality Requirements | 01 40 00 | Mock-ups, testing, inspection, special inspections |
| Temporary Facilities | 01 50 00 | Trailers, fencing, utilities, signage, parking, restrooms |
| Product Requirements | 01 60 00 | Substitution procedures, basis of design, "or equal" |
| Execution and Closeout | 01 70 00 | Cleaning, starting and adjusting, closeout submittals, training, warranties |

**The two Division 01 sections that prevent the most disputes**: 01 33 00 (Submittals) and 01 60 00 (Substitutions). If you do not follow the format and timing they prescribe, the architect can reject your submittal or substitution on procedural grounds alone.

### 4.4 Shop Drawing Review — What Stamps Mean

When you submit a shop drawing (millwork shop, steel fabrication drawings, custom windows, etc.), the architect returns a stamp. The wording is critical:

| Stamp | Meaning | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **No Exceptions Taken** / **Approved** | Fabricate as shown | Release for fabrication |
| **Approved as Noted** / **Make Corrections Noted** | Fabricate, applying the architect's red-line corrections; resubmittal not required | Mark up shop's working copies with the notes; release for fabrication |
| **Revise and Resubmit** | Significant non-conformance; not approved for fabrication | Correct and resubmit; **do not fabricate** |
| **Rejected** | Reject completely — wrong product, wrong approach | Start over, with RFI if scope unclear |
| **For Information Only** / **Received** | Architect reviewed for record but did not approve | Limited use; clarify before fabricating |

**Legal weight of "Approved as Noted"**: The architect's review (per AIA B101 §3.6.4.2 and A201 §3.12.8) is for *design intent only*. If the architect misses a dimension error or a code violation, **you are still responsible**. The shop drawing review does not transfer liability for accuracy or coordination from the contractor to the architect.

Practical rule: Have your project engineer or PM red-line submittals *before* the sub forwards them up. The architect should see a coordinated submittal, not raw shop output.

### 4.5 Keeping the Record Straight — Addenda, ASI, RFI Response, Bulletin, CO

| Document | When issued | Cost / time impact | How it changes the contract |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Addendum** | During bidding, before contract signed | Embedded in bid | Becomes part of contract documents |
| **RFI response** | During construction | No (clarification) — unless followed by COR | Becomes part of contract docs (interpretation) |
| **ASI** | During construction | Architect asserts no cost/time impact | Minor change; GC must dispute in writing if there's actually cost |
| **Bulletin** (some firms) | During construction | Maybe (architect's term for a packet of changes) | Either ASI-equivalent or PR-equivalent; read the cover sheet |
| **PR / CCD** (Proposal Request / Construction Change Directive) | During construction | Yes — pricing requested | CCD directs work to proceed; CO follows |
| **Change Order (CO)** | After scope and price agreed | Yes — modifies contract sum/time | Signed by owner, architect (recommends), GC |

> **Filing discipline**: Maintain a master document log with every addendum, ASI, RFI, PR, and CO. Cross-reference each one in your daily reports and pay applications. When the project ends and a dispute arises three years later, this log is your defense.

## Part 5 — Building Systems Coordination

### 5.1 Structural vs. Architectural Coordination

The architect designs the building; the structural engineer designs the structure that holds the building up. They work together, but their drawings are independent. The GC must reconcile them.

| Conflict type | Architectural says | Structural says | Resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wall thickness | 6" wall | 4" CMU + 2" of who-knows-what | Verify wall type and dimension; usually structural governs the structural element, architectural governs the finished thickness |
| Bearing vs. non-bearing | Often not labeled on A | Labeled on S | If S shows a header, the wall is bearing. Don't remove or modify without S engineer approval |
| Opening sizes | Door schedule says 36" × 80" RO | Header sized for 36" rough | A-side rough opening must allow door + frame + shim; S-side must size header for the rough |
| Grid layout | Grid lines at column centerlines on A | Grid lines at column centerlines on S | Should match; check carefully on residential where A may use "off-grid" dimensioning |

**Critical rule**: Any wall that holds up something — joist, beam, header, roof rafter — is bearing. Even a wall *parallel* to joists may be load-bearing if it supports a beam above. Never remove a wall without S engineer review, even if the architect's plan looks innocent.

### 5.2 MEP Coordination — Architectural Layer

The architect's drawings typically show:
- Mechanical room and electrical room locations and sizes
- Major shaft locations
- Plumbing fixture locations (count and type)
- Diffuser, grille, light fixture locations on the RCP
- Equipment that affects architecture: water heaters in closets, electrical panels recessed in walls, exhaust hood penetrations

The MEP engineers' drawings define the sizes, routing, capacities, and connections. Coordinate by:

1. **Overlaying plans** — put A, M, P, E plans on the same scale and check for conflicts: shaft sizes large enough for ducts, ceiling space deep enough for the deepest duct + slope + insulation + structure clearance.
2. **Plenum vs. hard-lid** — in an accessible ceiling, MEP can be looser. In a hard-lid ceiling (drywall soffit), every penetration needs an access panel. Locate them before drywall.
3. **Mechanical equipment access** — verify clearance for service: pulling a coil, replacing a fan motor, accessing a panel.
4. **Electrical panel clearances** — NEC 110.26 requires 36" working depth in front of any panel, 30" wide minimum, 6.5' high clear. Architects routinely place panels in 24" closets. RFI before framing.

### 5.3 BIM Coordination

On larger residential and light commercial, the architect models in Revit (or ArchiCAD, sometimes Vectorworks for residential). The GC may receive:

- **The federated model** — a combined model with A, S, M, P, E, FP overlaid
- **IFC exports** — open-standard model format
- **Coordination drawings** — 2D output from the model

**Clash detection** workflow:

1. Models are aggregated in Navisworks, Solibri, BIM 360, or similar.
2. Software identifies geometric collisions: pipe through beam, duct through joist, fixture in wall.
3. Coordination meeting reviews each clash — hard clash (physical), soft clash (clearance), workflow clash (sequencing).
4. Decisions tracked in an issue log; model authors revise; rerun.

**Roles**:
- **Model authors**: Architect, structural, MEP — each owns their model.
- **BIM coordinator / manager**: Often the GC, especially in DB. Hosts the federated model, runs clash detection, schedules meetings.
- **Trade detailers**: Subs (mech, fire protection) often produce shop-level BIM models that get coordinated into the federated model before fabrication.

On residential projects under ~5,000 sf, BIM is rare. On light commercial under ~20,000 sf, you may receive Revit files but not run a full BIM workflow. Don't pretend to BIM you can't run; ask the architect for 2D coordinated overlays instead.

### 5.4 Accessibility — ADA and Beyond

Two parallel regimes:

- **ADA Standards for Accessible Design (2010)** — federal civil rights law, applies to commercial and public facilities (Title III). Not enforced by the building department; enforced by private lawsuit or DOJ.
- **IBC Chapter 11 / ICC A117.1** — building-code accessibility, enforced by the AHJ at permit and CO.

In **California**, **CBC Chapter 11A** (residential) and **11B** (public) are stricter than ADA in many places. CBC 11B controls public accommodations even where ADA would otherwise apply.

**The dimensions a GC should commit to memory**:

| Element | Min / Max |
|---|---|
| Clear door width | 32" min (door open 90°, measured between face of door and stop) |
| Push side door clearance | 12" min beyond latch (with closer) |
| Pull side door clearance | 18" min beyond latch on pull side |
| Hallway / accessible route width | 36" min (44" CBC commercial) |
| Wheelchair turnaround | 60" diameter circle, or T-turn 60" × 60" with 36" arms |
| Reach range (forward, unobstructed) | 15" min / 48" max AFF |
| Reach range (side, unobstructed) | 15" min / 48" max AFF |
| Counter height (accessible portion) | 34" max AFF |
| Toilet centerline from wall | 16"–18" (ADA), 17"–18" (CBC 11B) |
| Grab bars at toilet | Rear 36" min, side 42" min, mounted 33"–36" AFF |
| Ramp slope | 1:12 max (8.33%) with 30' max run, 60" landings |
| Door threshold height | 1/2" max with 1:2 bevel above 1/4" |

**Catch ADA violations *before* drywall**: Once the wall is built, moving a toilet centerline 2" is a $2,000 fix that costs $20,000. Lay out every accessible toilet, lavatory, shower, and door clearance in chalk on the slab before framing. Photograph it. Have the architect or owner sign off if there's any doubt.

References: [ADA Standards](https://www.ada.gov/law-and-regs/design-standards/), ICC A117.1 (purchase from ICC), [California CBC Chapter 11B](https://www.dgs.ca.gov/DSA/Resources/Page-Content/Resources-List-Folder/Access-Compliance-Reference-Materials).

## Part 6 — Residential Architecture Specifics

### 6.1 California Residential Codes

California publishes the **Title 24** suite, updated on a 3-year cycle (current edition: 2022, in effect since 2023; 2025 edition coming into effect). Key parts a residential GC must coordinate:

| Code | Title | Subject |
|---|---|---|
| Part 1 | CA Admin Code | Administrative |
| Part 2 | **California Building Code (CBC)** | Based on IBC; commercial and multifamily Group R-1, R-2 |
| Part 2.5 | **California Residential Code (CRC)** | Based on IRC; one- and two-family dwellings up to 3 stories |
| Part 3 | **CEC** (California Electrical Code) | Based on NEC with CA amendments |
| Part 4 | CA Mechanical Code (CMC) | Based on UMC |
| Part 5 | CA Plumbing Code (CPC) | Based on UPC |
| Part 6 | **California Energy Code (Title 24 Part 6)** | Energy efficiency — **the big one** |
| Part 9 | CA Fire Code (CFC) | |
| Part 11 | **CALGreen** | Mandatory green building |
| Part 12 | CA Referenced Standards | |

**CRC path vs. IBC path**:
- **CRC**: For one- and two-family dwellings, townhouses ≤3 stories. Prescriptive, easier to permit. Most custom homes.
- **CBC / IBC path**: For dwellings outside CRC scope (4+ story residential, mixed-use, ADU complexes), or when an architect chooses CBC because the design exceeds CRC prescriptive limits.

**Title 24 Part 6 (Energy)** drives:
- **Envelope**: U-factors for windows, R-values for insulation, air leakage testing
- **HVAC**: Equipment efficiency (AFUE, SEER2, HSPF2), duct sealing/testing
- **Water heating**: Heat pump water heater requirements
- **Lighting**: All high-efficacy (LED essentially mandatory)
- **Solar PV**: Mandatory on new low-rise residential (with exceptions)
- **Electric-ready**: Pre-wire and stub-out for future electrification

The **Title 24 compliance documents** (CF1R for residential — formerly Form 3R, soon-to-update) are part of the permit set. A HERS rater performs in-field verification (duct test, blower door, refrigerant charge). The GC must coordinate access for the rater at the right milestones — typically post-rough-in for duct leakage and pre-final for blower door.

**CALGreen** mandates:
- Construction waste reduction (65% diversion minimum from landfill)
- Indoor water use reduction (low-flow fixtures)
- Low-VOC adhesives, sealants, paints, carpet, composite wood
- Moisture protection, framing-moisture content
- HVAC commissioning checklist (residential is simpler than commercial)

### 6.2 Residential Floor Plan Reading

Residential floor plans hide complexity in adjacencies and finishes. Walk every plan looking for:

| Adjacency | Concern |
|---|---|
| Garage to house | Fire separation — 5/8" Type X on garage side of common wall and ceiling under habitable space (CRC R302.6); self-closing door 1-3/8" solid wood, solid/honeycomb steel, or 20-min rated |
| Bedroom to bathroom | Acoustic — at least one layer of insulation in the wall; consider double drywall on noisy walls |
| Laundry above habitable | Pan, drain, leak detection — and structural support for stacking washer-dryer |
| Kitchen to dining | Ventilation make-up air if range hood ≥400 CFM (CMC); structural header for wide opening |
| Bedroom emergency egress | EERO window per CRC R310 — 5.7 sf clear (5.0 sf at grade floor), 24" clear height, 20" clear width, sill ≤44" AFF |
| Stair to landing | Headroom 6'-8" min, riser 7-3/4" max, tread 10" min (CRC R311.7) |

**Garage-to-house interface** is the most-bungled detail in residential. Required:
- 1/2" gypsum on garage walls, 5/8" Type X on the wall between garage and dwelling and on garage ceiling if habitable space above
- Floor of garage sloped to drain or door
- Door is 1-3/8" solid or 20-min rated, self-closing, self-latching, *cannot* open into a sleeping room (CRC R302.5.1)
- No HVAC duct openings in the garage
- Penetrations sealed to maintain separation

### 6.3 Exterior Envelope — Cladding, WRB, Flashing

Cladding choices and their gotchas:

| Cladding | Typical assembly | GC watchouts |
|---|---|---|
| **Stucco** (3-coat traditional) | Lath over 2 layers Grade D paper (or single layer 60-min building paper per CRC R703.7); scratch, brown, finish coats | 2-layer WRB required (CA), weep screed at base 4" above grade / 2" above paving, control joints |
| **Stucco (one-coat)** | Proprietary system, single coat over lath | Follow ICC ESR — assembly must match the listing; otherwise it's not approved |
| **Fiber cement** (Hardie, etc.) | WRB + fiber cement plank or panel; vented rainscreen optional but recommended | Strict nailing requirements; manufacturer details for corners, butt joints, kick-out flashings |
| **Brick veneer** | Brick on a 1" air gap with weeps, ties to backup wall every 16" vertical, 24" horizontal | Lintel sizing, flashing at all openings and at base — water *will* enter the cavity |
| **Wood siding** | WRB + furring (rainscreen) or direct | Back-prime, end-cut treat; clearances per CRC R317 |
| **Manufactured stone veneer** | Lath + scratch coat + stone | Same WRB as stucco; lots of failures from missing kick-out flashing; clearances to grade and paving |

**WRB (Weather-Resistant Barrier) hierarchy** (CRC R703.2 requires WRB; CA requires 2 layers behind stucco):
1. WRB lapped shingle-fashion — upper laps over lower
2. Flashing always laps **over** WRB at the head, **under** WRB at the sill of any opening
3. Sill pan flashing first; jamb flashing laps the sill flashing pan; head flashing laps the jambs; WRB laps the head flashing
4. Kick-out flashings at every roof-to-wall termination where a sloped roof meets a wall

**Window installation** depends on the type:
- **Nail-fin / flange windows**: WRB cut and flapped; sill pan installed; window set in sealant; flange nailed; head and jamb taped/flashed over the flange; WRB head flap dropped over flashing.
- **Block-frame / replacement windows**: Different sequencing; manufacturer's instructions must be followed verbatim or warranty voids.
- **AAMA 2400 / ASTM E2112** are the consensus standards; the architect's detail should reference one of these and the window manufacturer's installation guide. **Read both before installing**.

### 6.4 Interior Finish Schedules — Sequence and Coordination

Finish installation sequence (typical, top-down then bottom-up):

1. Ceiling drywall (texture if applicable)
2. Wall drywall
3. Texture (knockdown, smooth, orange peel)
4. Primer
5. Trim install (base, casing) — sometimes after first coat of paint
6. First finish coat
7. Cabinet install
8. Countertop template, fabricate, install
9. Tile (floors, showers, backsplash) — sequencing varies; in wet areas, tile substrate goes in before drywall finish
10. Hardwood / engineered wood floor (acclimation 1–2 weeks on site before install)
11. Plumbing trim (faucets, drains, toilets)
12. Electrical trim (devices, fixtures, plates)
13. Appliances
14. Carpet (last to avoid damage)
15. Final paint touch-up
16. Final clean

Read the finish schedule against this sequence. Common conflicts:
- Cabinet height + countertop thickness vs. window sill height — if backsplash tile extends to window stool, the dimensions must work to the eighth
- Tile substrate (cement board, Schluter Kerdi, GoBoard) called out in spec but not on plan — confirm before drywall
- Transition strips (Schluter T, J, L profiles) between flooring materials — usually GC-coordinated, not always specified

### 6.5 Kitchen and Bath Coordination

The two highest-density rooms in any house. Coordinate:

**Kitchen**:
- Cabinet shop drawings vs. floor plan vs. window/door locations
- Appliance specs: refrigerator depth (counter-depth vs. standard 34"), range width (30", 36", 48"), range hood CFM and make-up air, dishwasher rough plumbing and electrical, microwave drawer or above-range, wall oven rough-in dimensions (mfr-specific)
- Plumbing: sink location, dishwasher supply and drain, ice maker line to fridge, pot filler
- Electrical: small-appliance branch circuits — minimum two 20A SABCs for kitchen counter receptacles (CEC/NEC 210.52(B)); GFCI within 6' of every sink (and now throughout the kitchen — CEC 210.8(A)); dedicated circuits for dishwasher, disposal, microwave, refrigerator (recommended); under-cabinet lighting circuit; pendant locations laid out from cabinet plan, not architectural plan
- Ventilation: range hood ≥100 CFM; if ≥400 CFM, make-up air required (CMC); duct routing constrained by joists, soffit, structure

**Bath**:
- Toilet centerline from finished wall — 15" min (CRC), 17"–18" for ADA in 11B contexts
- Vanity rough-in (sink, faucet, P-trap height)
- Tub and shower valve heights — typically 28" (tub spout) / 48" (shower head) for stand-up, but verify against the actual fixture and architect's elevation
- Bath fan ventilation — 50 CFM intermittent or 20 CFM continuous (CMC), vented to outside (not attic)
- Electrical: GFCI required at any receptacle within 6' of basin; bath circuit dedicated 20A for receptacles; fan/light combo dedicated 15A or 20A; humidity sensor on fan often required by Title 24
- Shower waterproofing: pan, membrane, niche flashing — coordinate with tile sub before drywall

## Part 7 — Common Architect-GC Conflicts and How to Navigate Them

### 7.1 Design vs. Constructability

When you spot a constructability problem (the architect drew something that can't be built or can't be built to standard), the question is *when and how* to raise it.

**Before bid**: Submit an RFI during the bid Q&A period. Architect typically issues an addendum that becomes part of the contract.

**Between award and mobilization**: Submit RFI. Architect issues an ASI or revised drawing. Cost/time may follow if scope changes.

**During construction**: RFI. If the architect's response changes the work, follow with a Change Order Request (COR) within the contract's time bar.

**Never verbal**: A verbal direction from the architect is worth nothing in a dispute. If they tell you something at a walk-through, send a "confirming RFI" or a meeting minutes entry that captures the direction and ask for written confirmation.

### 7.2 Incomplete or Ambiguous Drawings

Every drawing set is incomplete to some degree. The question is *how* incomplete and what you do about it.

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Detail referenced but not drawn | RFI immediately; note in bid as exclusion if pre-bid |
| Two details conflict (e.g., A5.1/3 shows one thing, A5.2/7 shows another) | RFI; quote the more expensive interpretation; let architect rule |
| Drawing and spec conflict | RFI; usually spec governs (or "more stringent governs"), but get a written ruling |
| Information shown on one sheet contradicts another | RFI; price the more demanding requirement |
| Quantity unclear | RFI with assumed take-off; ask architect to confirm |

**Pre-bid clarifications protect you**: If you submit a bid with an unresolved ambiguity, the contract typically obligates you to provide the more stringent of the conflicting requirements without extra cost (A201 §3.2.4). Identify ambiguities pre-bid.

### 7.3 Substitution Requests

A "substitution" is a request to use a product different from what's specified. The spec almost always lists a "Basis of Design" (BoD) product and either:
- Lists 2–3 acceptable alternates (preferred), or
- Says "or equal" — but "or equal" means *the architect's determination of equal*, not yours.

**Substitution submittal package** (per typical Section 01 60 00):
- Substitution request form
- Spec section and paragraph being substituted
- Manufacturer's product data (cut sheet)
- Side-by-side comparison: performance, dimensions, capacities, finishes
- Cost difference (savings or premium)
- Schedule impact (lead time)
- Sample if requested
- Reason for substitution (cost, lead time, availability, performance)

**What architects look for**:
- Performance equivalence (not just visual)
- Aesthetic match (color, profile, sheen)
- Warranty equivalence
- Code compliance
- Compatibility with other specified products (e.g., a stucco system substitution must match the WRB, lath, accessories)
- Whether the substitution actually saves the *owner* money or just the GC

**Timing**: Most substitutions must be submitted within 30–60 days of contract award. After that, the architect can deny on procedural grounds. Read your Section 01 60 00 and put deadlines on a calendar.

### 7.4 Interpretation Disputes

When the GC and architect disagree about what the drawings mean:

1. **Read the General Conditions** (A201). Article 1 (General Provisions) and Article 4 (Architect) define the architect's authority. Article 15 (Claims and Disputes) defines the dispute resolution path.
2. **"More stringent governs"**: If two requirements conflict, the more demanding usually controls. The architect interprets; the GC complies; the owner pays if scope was expanded.
3. **Code reference disputes**: The code is the law. The architect's drawing is intent. If the drawing violates the code, the code wins — but you should not silently fix it. RFI, then proceed.
4. **Documentation**: Every interpretation should be captured in writing (RFI response). When the same kind of interpretation comes up again, reference the prior RFI in the new one.

### 7.5 Change Order Negotiations with Architect Involvement

The architect doesn't approve change orders — only the owner does — but the architect:
- Recommends approval or rejection to the owner
- Reviews pricing for reasonableness
- Verifies scope is genuinely additional and not part of the original contract
- Resolves disputes over whether work is "in scope" or "additional"

**A clean change order request**:
- Reference number (COR-001, etc.)
- Description of the change (1–3 sentences)
- Originating document (ASI-3, RFI-12 response, PR-2, owner verbal — and recover the verbal in writing)
- Itemized cost: labor, material, equipment, subcontractor (with sub's CORs attached), markup per contract
- Schedule impact (days added)
- Statement: "If not approved within X days, work will proceed under original scope" (or whatever the contract requires)

Submit COR within the contract's time bar (often 14–21 days from notice). Late CORs are routinely denied as waived.

## Part 8 — Managing Architect Coordination Day-to-Day

### 8.1 Submittal Log

The submittal log is a living document. Minimum columns:

| # | Spec section | Description | Sub | Date received from sub | Date sent to architect | Date returned | Status | Re-submittal # | Comments |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

**Lead time discipline**:
- Build the log at buyout from the spec's required submittals list
- Add lead times to every line (windows 12–16 weeks, custom millwork 8–10 weeks, ductwork 4–6 weeks, etc.)
- Plot submittal → review → fabrication → delivery → installation on the master schedule
- Architect review = 14 days typical (in the contract); re-submittals can extend
- An item with a 16-week lead time and a 14-day review window must be submitted **20 weeks before installation**, not "soon"

**Expediting**: The submittal log is also the expediting log. Weekly, the GC reviews:
- Overdue submittals (from subs)
- Overdue architect reviews
- Re-submittals approaching deadlines
- Approved submittals → POs released

### 8.2 RFI Discipline

A bad RFI is one of these:

- "How do I install this?" → Read the drawings and manufacturer's instructions first.
- "Confirm dimension" → Scale it and propose; ask for confirmation, not for the architect to calculate.
- "What is the color?" → Read the finish schedule; if not specified, ask with a proposed selection.

A good RFI:

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| Subject (one line) | Conflict between A5.2/3 (wall section) and A1.1 (floor plan) at jamb of door 102A |
| Reference | Sheets A5.2 detail 3; A1.1; Spec 08 14 00 |
| Description | A5.2/3 shows door jamb dimension as 4-7/8" from face of stud to face of finish; A1.1 dimension chain places the door 4-1/2" from the corner. The 3/8" discrepancy affects framing layout at the wall return. |
| Proposed solution | Recommend setting door 4-7/8" from corner per A5.2/3, with the 3/8" absorbed in the room dimension. |
| Schedule impact | Framing begins in this area on [date]; response needed by [date]. |
| Cost impact | None if responded by [date]. |

Number every RFI (RFI-001, RFI-002...). Log every RFI on the master RFI log. Copy the owner if cost or schedule is implicated.

### 8.3 OAC Meetings

OAC = Owner-Architect-Contractor. Weekly meeting where the project's heartbeat is taken.

**Standard agenda**:
1. Approval of prior minutes
2. Safety
3. Schedule update — look-ahead 2–3 weeks
4. RFIs — open, pending, recently answered
5. Submittals — open, pending, recently approved
6. Change orders / CORs — open
7. Owner decisions required
8. Architect decisions required
9. Field observations / site issues
10. Next meeting date

**Minutes**: Someone — usually the GC — takes minutes and distributes within 24 hours. Action items have an owner and a due date. Minutes are reviewed and approved at the next meeting. **The minutes become a contract record** — read them carefully, correct anything wrong in writing.

**Who owns what**:
- **Owner**: Decisions on finishes, allowances, COs, schedule constraints
- **Architect**: Design intent, RFI responses, submittal review, pay app certification
- **GC**: Schedule, means and methods, sub management, safety, daily reports

### 8.4 Punch List

The punch list is the final closeout of the architect's role.

**Process**:
1. **GC pre-walk**: Before notifying the architect, walk the project. Generate your own punch and have your subs correct everything you find. Don't waste the architect's punch on missing trim and paint touch-up.
2. **Architect punch**: Walk with architect, owner. Architect generates the list. Often categorized by trade or by room.
3. **GC schedule**: GC assigns each item to a sub with a deadline. Track on the punch log.
4. **Back-check**: GC verifies completion before notifying architect.
5. **Architect back-check**: Architect re-walks, signs off each item.
6. **Substantial Completion**: When the punch is complete enough that the owner can occupy and use the building for its intended purpose, the architect issues a Certificate of Substantial Completion (AIA G704). This shifts insurance, starts warranties, releases retainage in stages.
7. **Final Completion**: All punch items closed, all closeout submittals delivered (O&M manuals, warranties, as-builts, attic stock), final payment processed.

**Closeout submittals** (often the slowest part):
- As-built drawings (red-line set with all field changes)
- O&M manuals for every piece of equipment
- Warranties (manufacturer + installer)
- Attic stock (extra tile, paint, etc. — spec'd in Division 01)
- Final lien waivers
- Final certified payroll (if prevailing wage)
- Title 24 final compliance forms (CF2R, CF3R installation and verification certificates)
- HERS rater certification (residential)
- Building department final approval / Certificate of Occupancy

> **Closeout discipline**: Start collecting closeout submittals at the rough-in phase. The roofing warranty arrives months after the roof installation; if you wait until punch list, you'll be chasing 30 documents that should have been filed as you went.
