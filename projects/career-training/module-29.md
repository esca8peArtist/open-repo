# Dispute Resolution, Claims, and Schedule Delay Management

> **Purpose.** Claims and disputes are not failures — they are predictable events in any project of substance. This module is the field manual for when they happen: how to build the documentation that wins them, how to analyze schedule delays, when to escalate, and how to negotiate settlements that preserve the relationship and your margin. Read Module 13 (Construction Law and Lien Rights) first; this module goes deeper on the operational side of claims.

> **California focus.** California Public Contract Code, AIA A201-2017 general conditions, and California Code of Civil Procedure govern the legal framework cited here. Commercial and public works projects add layers; residential contracts follow Module 13 rules. Where relevant, both private and public works procedures are noted.

## 0. Mental Model — Claims as a Business Process

Most GCs treat claims as crises. The contractors who recover full money treat them as a **parallel administrative track** running alongside construction from Day 1.

The claim you win in month 18 was built on documentation from month 1. The claim you lose was unwinnable the moment you failed to log a delay on the day it happened, or performed changed work without sending written notice.

**The three-part formula for a winning claim:**

1. **Notice** — written notification to the owner within the contract's required window, usually 7–21 days after the triggering event.
2. **Documentation** — contemporaneous daily logs, photos, cost tracking, and schedule updates that prove what happened and when.
3. **Quantification** — a credible, defensible number for both time and money impact.

All three must be present. A claim with two out of three usually loses.

## 1. Claim Documentation and Procedures

### 1.1 The Daily Log — Your Primary Claim Evidence

The daily construction log is the most important document in any dispute. Courts and arbitrators consistently give more weight to contemporaneous records (written the day the event occurred) than to reconstructions created after a dispute arises.

**Required daily log fields:**

| Field | What to Record | Why It Matters in a Claim |
|---|---|---|
| Date and weather | Temp, precipitation, wind, visibility | Proves weather-caused delays; tracks force majeure events |
| Workers on site | Headcount by trade and crew | Proves manpower disruptions; establishes when acceleration began |
| Hours worked | Start, stop, breaks | Overtime tracking; acceleration cost documentation |
| Work performed | Specific activities, drawing references | Establishes progress baseline vs. schedule |
| Owner/architect presence | Who visited, what was discussed | Ties verbal directives to specific days |
| Materials received or rejected | Delivery tickets, rejected deliveries | Tracks owner-furnished material delays |
| RFIs submitted and responses received | RFI number, date sent, date answered | Documents design delay and owner-caused delay |
| Change directives received | Verbal or written, with description | Creates record of constructive changes |
| Delays encountered | Reason, duration, trades affected | Core delay claim evidence |
| Inspector visits | Inspector name, items inspected, result | Documents inspection delays outside contractor control |
| Safety incidents | All incidents, regardless of severity | Separate obligation; also relevant to productivity claims |

**Practical standard:** A daily log entry should take a superintendent 15–20 minutes to complete. If it's taking less, it's not detailed enough. If it's taking more, the format is wrong.

**Tool options:** Buildertrend, Procore, Raken (field-focused), or a structured PDF that syncs to cloud storage. The medium matters less than consistency and accessibility years later.

### 1.2 Written Claim Notices — The Notice-or-Lose Rule

Nearly every construction contract contains a **notice requirement** — a clause stating that all claims must be submitted in writing within a defined period (typically 7–21 days) of the event giving rise to the claim. Missing the notice deadline is commonly treated as a **waiver of the claim**, regardless of merit.

**Standard notice letter format:**

```
[Date]
To: [Owner / Owner's Representative]
Re: [Project Name and Address]
Contract No.: [Number]
Subject: Notice of Claim / Delay / Change

Pursuant to [Section X] of the Contract, Contractor provides notice of a 
[delay / changed condition / change in scope] arising from:

Event: [Description of what happened]
Date of occurrence: [Date]
Anticipated cost impact: [$ estimate or "to be determined"]
Anticipated schedule impact: [Days or "to be determined"]

Contractor will provide a detailed claim submission within [10–14] business 
days. This notice is provided to preserve all contractual and legal rights 
and is without prejudice to Contractor's position.

Sincerely, [Name, Title, Company]
```

**Send method:** Email with read receipt AND certified mail on significant claims. Keep both proofs of sending in the project file.

**When in doubt about whether an event requires notice, send it anyway.** A notice you didn't need costs a stamp. A notice you needed but didn't send can cost six figures.

### 1.3 Cost Tracking for Claims

A claim for additional costs must tie to **actual, documented expenditures** — not estimates, not feel, not reconstructed after the fact. The IRS-quality cost tracking system you use for tax purposes is also your claim documentation system.

**Required cost tracking for any potential claim item:**

| Cost Category | How to Track | Documents Required |
|---|---|---|
| Direct labor | Time cards by worker, by cost code | Signed time cards, payroll registers |
| Supervision/overhead labor | Superintendent time on impacted work | Logs, timesheets |
| Materials | Purchase orders, receipts, delivery tickets | Tied to specific work elements |
| Equipment | Own equipment — daily rate logs; rented — invoices | Rental agreements, operator logs |
| Subcontractor costs | Sub invoices, sub time-and-material tickets | Signed T&M tickets at end of each shift |
| Escalation costs | Vendor price quotes, before/after comparison | Original bid pricing vs. current pricing |
| Delay/standby costs | Days of unproductive crew, equipment on-site | Daily logs showing what crew was doing |
| Overhead impact | Percentage of direct costs OR extended home office overhead | Eichleay formula calculation — see below |

**The Eichleay Formula** — extended home office overhead for government contracts and many private disputes:

```
Step 1: (Contract billings ÷ Total company billings for contract period) × Total home office overhead = Allocable overhead
Step 2: Allocable overhead ÷ Contract days = Daily overhead rate
Step 3: Daily overhead rate × Compensable delay days = Extended overhead claim
```

Courts and boards of contract appeals have accepted Eichleay on federal projects; it is also used in California public works claims.

### 1.4 Organizing the Claim Package

A formal claim submission should be structured to tell a story clearly. Arbitrators and owners' attorneys read many of these; a well-organized claim is taken more seriously than a disorganized one, regardless of merit.

**Recommended claim package structure:**

1. **Executive Summary** — one page; the claim in plain English; total amount sought; events summarized.
2. **Contract and Project Background** — contract date, scope, original schedule, original price.
3. **Chronology of Events** — table format; date, event, documentation reference. This is the backbone of the claim.
4. **Entitlement Analysis** — why the owner is legally responsible (contract clause, changed condition, owner directive).
5. **Schedule Impact Analysis** — how the delay was caused and how it maps to the critical path (see Section 2).
6. **Cost Impact Analysis** — line-by-line cost documentation; totaled by category.
7. **Supporting Exhibits** — daily logs, photos, correspondence, RFIs, schedule updates, T&M tickets; tabbed and indexed.

## 2. Schedule Analysis and CPM for Delay Claims

### 2.1 Why the Schedule Is the Foundation of a Delay Claim

A delay claim without schedule analysis is an opinion. A delay claim with contemporaneous Critical Path Method (CPM) analysis is evidence. Owners' attorneys attack delay claims precisely because most GCs cannot prove that the alleged delay actually pushed the project's completion date. If the work that was delayed was not on the critical path, there is no compensable delay — regardless of how frustrating the event was.

**Key concepts:**

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Critical Path** | The longest sequence of dependent activities; any delay to a critical-path activity delays project completion by an equal amount |
| **Float** | The amount of time an activity can slip without delaying the critical path. Float belongs to the project, not the contractor — courts have generally held that owners can use contractor float for their delays |
| **Near-critical activities** | Activities with less than 2 weeks of float; monitor closely |
| **Total float** | Time an activity can slip before delaying the project |
| **Free float** | Time an activity can slip before delaying the next activity (not the project) |
| **Contemporaneous baseline schedule** | The as-planned schedule submitted at or near the start of the project — the benchmark against which delays are measured |
| **As-built schedule** | Reconstruction of what actually happened; often prepared during dispute phase |

### 2.2 Types of Schedule Delay Analysis Methods

Different methods carry different levels of persuasiveness in disputes. Most construction attorneys and expert witnesses now default to the Windows Analysis or Time Impact Analysis because they are the most transparent and verifiable.

| Method | Description | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **As-Planned vs. As-Built** | Compare the original plan to what actually happened | Simple, low cost | Does not prove causation; confuses concurrent delays |
| **Collapsed As-Built (But-For)** | Remove owner delays from as-built and recalculate what completion would have been | Strong causation argument | Requires good schedule data; can be gamed |
| **Time Impact Analysis (TIA)** | Model the schedule at the time of each delay event; measure impact prospectively | Industry gold standard; most defensible | Time-intensive; requires contemporaneous schedules |
| **Windows Analysis** | Divide project timeline into windows; analyze which party caused delay in each window | Handles concurrent delays well | Requires skilled scheduler |

**The practical minimum** for a GC managing a dispute without a forensic scheduler: maintain a monthly schedule update showing the updated critical path, the actual progress, and the reason for any schedule movement. If you have 12 monthly updates and each one notes "2-week delay due to owner's selection of tile" or "1-week delay due to rain," you have a chronological narrative that tracks the critical path's drift and its causes.

### 2.3 What Constitutes a Compensable Delay

Not every delay entitles the contractor to additional time and money. The key distinction:

| Delay Type | Description | Contractor's Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| **Excusable, Compensable** | Owner-caused: owner directives, owner's design errors, owner-supplied material delays, architect's slow RFI responses | Time extension + additional cost |
| **Excusable, Non-Compensable** | Force majeure events: earthquakes, extreme weather beyond seasonal norms, strikes, government actions | Time extension only — no additional cost |
| **Non-Excusable** | Contractor-caused: slow production, inadequate staffing, poor planning, sub failures | No time extension, no additional cost; contractor may owe owner delay damages |
| **Concurrent** | Both owner and contractor are causing delay simultaneously | Time extension possible; cost recovery disputed (see Section 4) |

**California-specific note:** California Public Contract Code §7102 provides that on public works contracts, if the contractor is delayed by acts or omissions of the public entity and the contractor provides timely written notice, the contractor is entitled to both a time extension and compensation for the delay. Private contracts depend on the specific contract language.

### 2.4 RFIs as a Delay Tracking Tool

Every Request for Information (RFI) that sits unanswered delays the contractor's work. Build an RFI log from Day 1:

| RFI # | Date Submitted | Question | Date Answered | Days to Answer | Schedule Impact? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 001 | Mar 3 | Foundation bolt layout at gridline A3 | Mar 5 | 2 | No |
| 047 | Apr 14 | Window head flashing detail for Type C | May 12 | 28 | Yes — framing at Rm 205 held 3 weeks |

An RFI log showing a pattern of slow responses (>10 business days for routine items) is compelling evidence of owner/architect-caused delay.

## 3. Constructive Acceleration and Recovery Strategies

### 3.1 What Is Constructive Acceleration

**Constructive acceleration** occurs when:
1. The contractor is entitled to a time extension (due to an excusable delay), AND
2. The owner explicitly or implicitly demands that the contractor complete on the original schedule anyway, AND
3. The contractor incurs additional costs to meet the original schedule.

The owner never issues a formal directive to accelerate — if they did, that would be a **directed acceleration** with clear entitlement to additional compensation. The "constructive" version is the implicit demand, which is what owners typically do: they acknowledge there's a delay but refuse to formally grant a time extension, leaving the contractor to either accept a late-completion penalty or accelerate at their own cost.

**Common forms of constructive acceleration demands:**
- Owner insists on original completion date despite approving scope additions.
- Owner threatens liquidated damages without acknowledging their contribution to delays.
- Owner refuses to issue a time extension for acknowledged excusable delays.
- Owner requires contractor to "catch up" without additional compensation.

### 3.2 Establishing a Constructive Acceleration Claim

To recover on a constructive acceleration claim, the contractor must prove all four elements:

1. **Excusable delay existed.** The underlying delay must qualify — owner-caused or force majeure.
2. **Contractor requested a time extension** in writing, timely.
3. **Owner denied or failed to respond** to the time extension request.
4. **Contractor accelerated** in response, incurring additional costs.

The written time extension request is critical. Without it, there is no evidence that the contractor asked to adjust the schedule. Send a formal time extension request for every excusable delay event, even if you expect to be denied.

### 3.3 Recovery Schedule Strategies

When a project is behind — whether due to excusable or non-excusable delays — the GC typically must develop a **recovery schedule**. A recovery schedule is a revised CPM that identifies how the contractor plans to complete by the contract completion date (or the new extended date).

**Recovery strategies, in order of cost:**

| Strategy | Typical Cost Premium | Best When |
|---|---|---|
| **Resequencing work** | Minimal — planning cost only | When sequences can be rearranged without quality impact |
| **Shift work / extended hours** | 15–25% labor premium for overtime | Owner-funded acceleration; controlled scope |
| **Additional crews** | Direct crew cost; mobilization overhead | Labor-intensive scopes; sufficient supervision available |
| **Pre-fabrication / modular** | Variable; often reduces on-site time significantly | Mechanical, electrical, plumbing rough-in; framing |
| **Material expediting** | Air freight, premium vendor costs | Equipment delivery delays; long-lead items |
| **Weekend work** | 20–30% premium plus supervisor overtime | Final push; inspector availability is the constraint |

**Rule:** When the owner demands acceleration after causing delays, present a written **Acceleration Cost Estimate** before commencing the accelerated work. This is a change order in concept even if the owner resists calling it that. Present it as: "To meet the [date] completion with the current [delay event], here is our estimate for additional cost. Please direct in writing if you wish us to proceed on this basis."

## 4. Contractor vs. Owner-Caused Delays — Risk Allocation

### 4.1 The Fundamental Risk Allocation Question

Every delay dispute ultimately comes down to: who caused this, and who bears the cost? The contract allocates delay risk; the daily log proves who actually caused it.

**Delays that are typically owner's risk:**

| Delay Category | Examples | Contract Clause to Cite |
|---|---|---|
| Design errors and omissions | Conflicting dimensions, missing details, specification gaps | "Changed conditions," "Owner's responsibility for design" |
| Slow RFI responses | Architect takes 30 days on a 48-hour design question | AIA §8.2.1 and §4.2 (architect's duties); project-specific RFI turnaround clause |
| Scope additions without schedule adjustment | Owner adds a bathroom without granting a time extension | "Changes in Work" clauses; COR/TIA required |
| Owner-furnished materials and equipment | Owner-supplied windows arrive 3 weeks late | Owner's obligation to coordinate deliveries; contract division of responsibility |
| Owner's separate contractors interfering | Owner's IT contractor blocks electrical rough-in access | "Owner's Separate Contractors" clause; AIA A201 §6 |
| Permitting delays when permit is owner's responsibility | Variance approval takes 6 weeks longer than planned | Explicitly stated in contract; force majeure if not |
| Defective owner-furnished information | Soils report understated groundwater; dewatering required | Differing site conditions clause |

**Delays that are typically contractor's risk:**

| Delay Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Subcontractor performance failures | Framing sub leaves job for a week without notice |
| Inadequate staffing | Crew too small for the schedule; owner told what they wanted to hear |
| Material procurement failures | GC didn't order long-lead windows until framing started |
| Poor sequencing | Insulation scheduled before rough inspections passed |
| Weather within seasonal norms | Normal winter rain in Northern California; not force majeure |
| Financial problems of subs | Sub's cash flow collapse causes stoppage |

### 4.2 Concurrent Delays — The Most Disputed Issue

**Concurrent delays** occur when both owner and contractor are causing delay simultaneously. This is the hardest delay situation to resolve, because:

- The contractor cannot claim full compensation if their own actions also delayed the project.
- The owner cannot deny all responsibility if their actions also contributed.

**California / majority rule:** When concurrent delays exist, the contractor is typically entitled to a **time extension** (so no liquidated damages), but **not additional money** for the delay period. This is called the "no compensation for concurrent delay" rule.

**Contractor strategy with concurrent delays:**
1. Document your portions of delay accurately — don't hide your own delays, because the owner's expert will find them.
2. Establish that the owner's delay was the **dominant cause** — the one that would have pushed the project regardless of your delay.
3. Use Windows Analysis to isolate periods where only the owner was causing delay (and claim for those periods) versus periods of true concurrency (claim time only).

### 4.3 Differing Site Conditions

One of the most valuable contractor protections in a public contract — and often included in private commercial contracts — is the **Differing Site Conditions** (DSC) clause.

A Type 1 DSC applies when subsurface or concealed conditions differ **materially from those indicated in the contract documents**. Example: the soils report said 1,500 psf bearing capacity; excavation reveals expansive clay requiring remediation.

A Type 2 DSC applies when conditions differ from those **ordinarily encountered** in work of the character provided for in the contract. Example: rock encountered in a location where no rock was expected or disclosed.

**To preserve a DSC claim:**
1. Stop or slow work when you encounter the unusual condition.
2. Provide written notice to the owner immediately — **do not disturb the condition** until the owner has had an opportunity to inspect.
3. Document the condition with photos and measurements before any remediation.
4. Obtain the owner's direction in writing.
5. Track all additional costs to a separate cost code from Day 1 of encountering the condition.

California Public Contract Code §7104 codifies DSC rights for public works projects.

## 5. Mediation and Arbitration Procedures

### 5.1 The Escalation Ladder — When to Use What

| Stage | Typical Cost | Timeline | Binding? | Use When |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct negotiation** | $0–$2,000 (attorney letter) | Days–weeks | Only if settled | Always try first |
| **Formal demand letter** | $500–$1,500 (attorney) | — | No | Owner is stonewalling informal discussions |
| **Mediation** | $3,000–$10,000 (split) | 30–90 days to schedule | No | Both sides willing to negotiate; required by most contracts before arbitration |
| **Arbitration (AAA/JAMS)** | $15,000–$100,000+ | 6–18 months | Yes | Contract requires; dispute exceeds $100K |
| **Superior Court litigation** | $50,000–$300,000+ | 2–4 years | Yes | Arbitration unavailable; claim exceeds $500K |

Most commercial construction contracts (AIA A201, ConsensusDocs) require mediation as a **condition precedent** to arbitration or litigation. File for mediation before the deadline in your dispute clause expires — typically 60–90 days after the claim is submitted.

### 5.2 Mediation — Preparation and Tactics

Mediation is a confidential, non-binding process where a neutral mediator helps parties find a voluntary settlement. Construction mediations settle approximately 70–80% of the time.

**Preparation steps:**

1. **Select the mediator.** Agree with the owner on a mediator with construction industry background — a retired construction judge or experienced construction attorney-mediator. AAA and JAMS maintain rosters.
2. **Submit a mediation brief** — 5–15 pages summarizing your position, the key facts, your strongest legal arguments, and what you would accept to settle. Avoid the maximalist brief that demands everything; identify your real bottom line internally before the session.
3. **Prepare your exhibits.** Bring the 10–15 documents that tell your story clearly: the key contract provisions, the notice letters you sent, the daily log entries for key days, the RFI log showing slow responses, the schedule showing the delay.
4. **Know your BATNA.** Before mediation, your team must agree privately on your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — what you will do if mediation fails, and at what cost. A GC who doesn't know their BATNA cannot negotiate effectively.

**Mediation day tactics:**

- Open with the human story, not the legal argument. Mediators are trying to find common ground; personal communication before legal positioning helps.
- In the private caucus with the mediator, be candid about your weaknesses. The mediator is not your opponent — they need to understand both sides to find a number.
- Do not make your first offer your final offer. Leave room to move.
- **Get the settlement in writing before leaving the building.** A settlement reached in the room but not reduced to a signed document often falls apart within 48 hours. CCP §664.6 makes a written settlement agreement from mediation directly enforceable.

### 5.3 Arbitration — Procedures and Preparation

**American Arbitration Association (AAA) Construction Arbitration Rules** govern most commercial construction disputes. JAMS rules apply to contracts specifying JAMS.

**Key AAA procedural steps:**

| Step | Timing | Practical Notes |
|---|---|---|
| File demand for arbitration | When mediation fails or is waived | Online at adr.org; include claim summary and contract |
| Pay filing fee | At filing | Fee scales with claim size; $950 for claims $75K–$150K; higher above |
| Arbitrator selection | Within 15–30 days of filing | Each side strikes and ranks from a list; one arbitrator for claims <$1M; three for larger |
| Preliminary hearing | Within 30–45 days | Scheduling order; discovery scope; document exchange |
| Discovery | Per scheduling order | Document requests; limited depositions; no interrogatories without agreement |
| Expert reports | Per scheduling order | Schedule analysis expert; damages expert if complex |
| Prehearing submissions | 14–21 days before hearing | Statement of claim, exhibits indexed, witness list |
| Hearing | Per scheduling order | Typically 1–5 days; informal rules of evidence |
| Award | Within 30 days of close of hearing | Written, reasoned award (optional but common) |

**Preparing for arbitration:**

- **Organize your exhibits.** A well-organized exhibit binder is more persuasive than a disorganized one. Tab and number every exhibit. Prepare a master exhibit index.
- **Prepare your key witnesses.** Your superintendent who kept the daily logs is your most important witness. They should be able to explain, without prompting, what happened on any given day the arbitrator asks about. That's a sign of authentic records.
- **Retain an expert.** For schedule disputes above $200K, a forensic scheduler is usually worth the cost ($15,000–$50,000). For below that threshold, your own detailed schedule analysis may suffice.
- **Focus on your three strongest points.** Arbitrators are human; a claim that overwhelms with 47 separate issues is less persuasive than one that hammers three irrefutable points effectively.

## 6. Negotiation Tactics and Settlement Strategies

### 6.1 The Math of Settlement vs. Litigation

Before any negotiation, run this calculation:

```
Expected litigation recovery = (Claim amount × Win probability) − (Attorney fees + Expert costs + Time cost)

Example:
Claim: $250,000
Win probability: 65%
Attorney/expert fees: $80,000
Expected litigation recovery: ($250,000 × 0.65) − $80,000 = $82,500

vs.
Settlement offer today: $130,000 → Net after no fees = $130,000
```

In this example, a $130,000 settlement today is worth $47,500 more than the expected value of winning in arbitration. **This math shifts settlement leverage strongly toward settling — and toward settling earlier rather than later**, when legal costs are lowest.

Share this calculation (privately) with your attorney and your principal before entering negotiations. It prevents emotional decision-making.

### 6.2 Pre-Negotiation Positioning

**Do before entering any settlement discussion:**

1. **Organize your documentation.** A contractor whose daily logs are complete and organized has credibility before saying a word. Signal this early: "We have full daily logs and photo documentation from every day of the project."
2. **Quantify accurately.** Overclaiming damages destroys credibility. If you claim $300,000 but your documentation only supports $180,000, the owner's attorney will expose the gap and use it to attack the entire claim.
3. **Identify the owner's pain points.** Does the owner need the dispute resolved to sell or refinance? Are they facing regulatory deadlines? Is there a tenant waiting to occupy? These create leverage.
4. **Know your minimum acceptable settlement.** Do not enter a negotiation without knowing your walk-away number. This prevents panic concessions.

### 6.3 Negotiation Tactics That Work in Construction

| Tactic | Application |
|---|---|
| **Lead with the irrefutable** | Present the clearest, most documented claim items first. Establish that your documentation is credible before arguing the disputed items. |
| **Acknowledge weaknesses first** | Proactively admitting your concurrent delays or documentation gaps takes the owner's attorney's best weapon away. "We know we had a 3-week delay with our framing crew. That's already deducted from our claim." |
| **Itemized settlement offers** | Instead of a single lump-sum demand, present a line-item claim with individual amounts. The owner can negotiate item by item, which often produces more total recovery than haggling a lump sum. |
| **The time-value offer** | "We're seeking $180,000. If settled within 30 days, we'll accept $155,000 to avoid the arbitration process." Creates urgency and incentivizes early resolution. |
| **Package with releases** | Offer a broad mutual release of all claims arising from the project in exchange for settlement. Owners often pay a premium for certainty. |
| **Involve the decision-maker** | Settlement authority often sits higher than the project manager you've been arguing with. Request that the owner's principal or board member attend mediation — they are less emotionally invested and more likely to see the math. |

### 6.4 Drafting the Settlement Agreement

A verbal settlement is not a settlement. A signed written agreement is.

**Required elements:**

1. **Identification of parties** — exact legal names of all entities.
2. **Payment amount and schedule** — total amount, due date, payment method. If payment is installments, include consequences of default.
3. **Scope of release** — "All claims arising from or related to the Project at [address], Contract dated [date]." Be specific; avoid releasing unrelated claims.
4. **Mutual release** — both sides release each other. Do not sign a unilateral release (only you release claims).
5. **Lien releases and mechanics** — conditional on receipt of payment; use California statutory forms (Civil Code §8136/8138).
6. **Non-disparagement clause** — optional but often valuable for ongoing business relationship.
7. **Confidentiality** — standard for settlement agreements; prevents the amount from becoming a benchmark in future disputes.
8. **Governing law** — California.
9. **Attorney fees provision** — each party bears their own fees (standard), or prevailing party clause if agreed.
10. **Execution** — signatures of authorized representatives; if an entity, signatory must have authority.

Have any settlement above $25,000 reviewed by a construction attorney before signing. The $500 review fee is cheap compared to an ambiguous release that generates round-two litigation.

## 7. Quick Reference Checklist

### Claim Readiness Checklist — Use from Day 1 on Every Project

**Daily operations:**
- [ ] Daily log completed by superintendent before leaving site (including weather, workers, work performed, delays)
- [ ] Photos taken daily (minimum 10), GPS-tagged, cloud-synced
- [ ] RFIs logged with submission date and response date
- [ ] Time-and-material tickets signed by owner or owner's rep at end of each shift for T&M work

**When a delay or change occurs:**
- [ ] Written notice sent to owner within contract notice period (default: 7 days if not specified)
- [ ] Event described specifically — date, duration, cause, trades affected
- [ ] Schedule impact estimated (even if stated as "TBD pending analysis")
- [ ] Cost tracking opened on new cost code immediately
- [ ] Schedule updated to show actual impact on critical path

**Monthly:**
- [ ] Schedule updated; deviations from baseline noted with causes
- [ ] Claim log reviewed: all open claims, status, supporting documentation
- [ ] RFI aging report: flag any RFI over 10 business days without response

**When dispute escalates:**
- [ ] Claim package organized per Section 1.4 structure
- [ ] Mediation demand filed before contractual deadline
- [ ] Attorney engaged if claim exceeds $50,000 or involves schedule expert
- [ ] BATNA analysis completed before any negotiation session

**Settlement:**
- [ ] Settlement amount calculated against litigation BATNA (Section 6.1)
- [ ] Written settlement agreement with all elements per Section 6.4
- [ ] Lien releases exchanged simultaneously with payment
- [ ] Project file retained for 11 years (CCP §337.15 latent defect tail)
