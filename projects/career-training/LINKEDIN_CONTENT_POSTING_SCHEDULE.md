**Subject**: How to read a construction drawing set in the first 5 minutes.

Every set of construction drawings follows the same organization. Once you know it, you can navigate any project's plan set in minutes rather than hours.

The sheet numbering convention:
- A sheets: Architectural (floor plans, elevations, sections, details)
- S sheets: Structural (foundations, framing, connections)
- M sheets: Mechanical (HVAC, ductwork, equipment schedules)
- E sheets: Electrical (panel schedules, circuit layouts, equipment locations)
- P sheets: Plumbing (drainage, supply, fixture schedules)
- C sheets: Civil (site plan, grading, utilities)

The cover sheet (Sheet 0 or Sheet G-001) contains the legend, abbreviations, and the project data block. Always read it first. Every symbol used anywhere in the set is defined there.

Module 6 (Architecture for Contractors) covers sheet organization, standard abbreviations, symbol legends, and how to read structural connection details as a GC.

**Type**: Regulatory update
**CTA variant**: "Module 14 covers this in detail — free at [SITE URL]"

---

**Subject**: California's fall protection rules changed July 1, 2025. Is your safety program current?

Effective July 1, 2025, Cal/OSHA Title 8 Section 1716.2 changed the fall protection trigger height for residential construction from 15 feet down to 6 feet.

What changed:
- Previously: residential workers on roofs over 15 feet needed active fall protection
- Now: 6 feet above any lower level triggers the requirement
- Applies to: roofing, framing, exterior finish work, and any task performed near an unprotected edge on a residential structure

What didn't change:
- Personal fall arrest systems (PFAS) and guardrail systems remain the primary compliance methods
- Safety nets are still allowed as a supplemental method
- The requirement to train workers on fall protection before the work begins

If your IIPP, toolbox talks, or site orientation materials still reference the old 15-foot threshold, they're out of date and you're exposed during an inspection.

**Subject**: Specialty sub to GC — what the transition actually looks like.

The most common path to a California GC license isn't starting as a GC. It's 8-12 years as a specialty sub, then making the jump.

What actually changes when you go from sub to GC:

**Before (specialty sub)**: You manage your own crew, your own scope, your own tools. Someone else manages the schedule, the owner relationship, and the payment chain.

**After (GC)**: You manage everyone else's schedules, scopes, and tools. You are the payment chain. Every problem that every sub has is now your problem.

The technical knowledge transfers. The contractual exposure is entirely new. Lien rights, indemnification clauses, performance bonds, owner-direct purchases — a specialty sub who becomes a GC without understanding these items is taking on risk they can't see.

### POST 11 — Week 6 content
**Type**: Module excerpt

---

**Subject**: California Title 24 compliance isn't optional — and it's more than just energy.

Title 24 is California's Building Standards Code. GCs and specialty subs who treat it as "just the energy code" miss most of what it actually covers.

Title 24, Part 6 is the Energy Code (the one most people know). But the code has 12 parts:

- Part 2: Building Code (CalADA, structural requirements)
- Part 5: Plumbing Code (state amendments to the Uniform Plumbing Code)
- Part 3: Electrical Code (state amendments to NEC)
- Part 9: Fire Code
- Part 11: Green Building Standards (CALGreen — the part most residential GCs miss)

CALGreen (Part 11) requires: mandatory 65% diversion of construction waste from landfills, low-VOC paint and adhesives on all new residential construction, and electric vehicle charging conduit in new residential garages.

If your standard scope template doesn't include waste diversion provisions and EV conduit, check whether the projects you're building require it.

### POST 12 — Week 6 content
**Type**: Regulatory update

---

**Subject**: DIR registration renewal: the license you can't let lapse.

California's DIR public works registration (required for any public works project over $25,000) must be renewed annually. The registration year runs July 1 to June 30.

Contractors who let their DIR registration lapse while actively bidding or performing public works:
- Are ineligible to work on the project during the lapsed period
- May void their bid if the lapse is discovered during the contract award process
- Face a $2,000 penalty for continuing work on a public works project without valid registration

The renewal fee as of 2026: $400 per year for contractors; $300 per year for subcontractors.

If you're a specialty sub who occasionally picks up public works projects, the DIR registration is easy to miss because it's separate from your CSLB license renewal. They're on different timelines.

**Subject**: Industrial vs. residential GC — the difference is not what most people expect.

The technical difference between industrial and residential construction: industrial sites have active processes running around you. You're tying into live systems under outage windows, coordinating with operations staff who have priorities that have nothing to do with your project.

Some residential GCs want to move into industrial work. The transition requires learning a different risk framework, not just different materials and equipment.

### POST 15 — Week 7 content
**Type**: Module excerpt

---

**Subject**: What's actually in a performance bond — and when to require one.

Performance bonds protect the owner if the contractor fails to complete the project. Most residential GCs don't carry them. Most commercial GCs work under them regularly.

What the bond actually does:
- The surety (bonding company) guarantees completion of the project if the contractor defaults
- If default occurs, the surety can: complete the project itself, hire another contractor to complete, or pay the owner the cost to complete (up to the bond amount)
- The contractor gets blacklisted with that surety and typically loses their bonding capacity

What it costs: typically 1-3% of the contract value, depending on the contractor's financial statement and project risk profile.

When a GC should require performance bonds from their subs: on any subcontract over $100,000 (for a residential GC) or over $500,000 (commercial/industrial), or when the sub is new to your team and the failure to perform would be catastrophic to your schedule.

### POST 17 — Week 8 content
**Type**: Module excerpt

---

**Subject**: ADU construction in California: what changed in 2024-2025 that most GCs still don't know.

California's ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit) laws went through two major reform cycles in 2020 and 2024. If you're doing residential work in California, you will encounter ADU projects.

Key current-state facts:
- Single-family lots can have 1 ADU and 1 JADU (Junior ADU), regardless of local zoning
- Cities must ministerially approve ADU permits within 60 days — no discretionary review allowed
- Owner-occupancy requirements for ADUs were eliminated through January 1, 2025 (this was the major 2024 change)
- Setback requirements: ADUs can now be as close as 4 feet to a side or rear property line
- Height: attached ADUs can be up to 25 feet or match the primary structure height, whichever is greater

The planning arbitrage: some California lots that were previously unbuildable for ADUs are now buildable under the revised setbacks. This has opened up work in infill neighborhoods that was previously off-limits.

Module 15 (ADU and Infill Development California) covers the full current regulatory framework, common plan check issues, and what the 2025 updates mean for residential GCs.

## External Link Strategy

LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses posts with external links in the post body. The suppression is significant — posts without links often get 2-5x more reach than equivalent posts with links.

**Recommended link handling**:
- Put the site URL in the **first comment**, not the post body
- In the post body, write "link in the first comment" or "link in my profile"
- After posting, immediately post a comment with the actual URL: "Full module at [SITE URL] — free access, no account required."
- This avoids the suppression while still making the link accessible

**Exception**: When posting a regulatory update or career story (not a case study), a link in the post body is acceptable — these posts are lower-traffic anyway and the convenience of an inline link may outweigh the algorithmic cost. Test both approaches in Week 3-4 and note which gets more impressions.
