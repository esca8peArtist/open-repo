# Setting Up a General Contracting Business in California

> **Purpose**: This is the operating manual for the *business* that does the construction — not the construction itself. It assumes you can already build. It covers everything a first-time licensed GC in California needs to set up legally, insure properly, hire safely, contract defensibly, and survive financially through the first five years.

> **Critical 2026 context**: As of 2026 the contractor license bond is $25,000 (SB 607). All licensed CA contractors must carry workers' comp by **January 1, 2028** regardless of employees (SB 216, extended by SB 1455 from the original 2026 date). Private-works retention is now capped at 5% (SB 61). Home-improvement cancellation rights now extend to email (AB 1327). Plan for these.

## 1. CSLB Licensing — The California Contractor's License

### 1.1 The Three License Families

| Class | Scope | Typical Holder |
|---|---|---|
| **Class A — General Engineering** | Fixed works requiring specialized engineering knowledge: highways, bridges, sewers, dams, pipelines, foundations as standalone work. | Heavy civil / public-works contractor. |
| **Class B — General Building** | Structures intended for support, shelter, and enclosure. Must use *at least two unrelated trades* on a project, OR frame/finish wood/metal structures (with carpentry exception). | Residential and light commercial GCs — the most common license for new GCs. |
| **Class B-2 — Residential Remodeling** | Created in 2021. Remodel-only residential work involving at least three unrelated trades on an existing structure. Cannot do new construction or structural alterations of foundation/load-bearing portions. | Remodel-focused contractors who don't do ground-up. |
| **Class C (specialty)** | One specific trade (C-10 electrical, C-36 plumbing, C-33 painting, C-39 roofing, C-20 HVAC, C-27 landscaping, etc. — ~44 classifications). | Sub-trade specialists. |
| **Class D (limited specialty)** | Niche scopes not covered by C classes (D-49 tree service, D-52 window coverings, etc.). | Niche specialists. |

**When do you need which?**
- Single-trade jobs (just roofing, just HVAC): **C-class** is sufficient and often cheaper to qualify for.
- Whole-house remodel, ADU, addition, or new build with multiple trades: **B**.
- Public works infrastructure (roads, utilities): **A**.
- You can hold multiple classifications. Adding a C-class to a B (e.g., B + C-10) lets you self-perform that specialty rather than subbing it out.

### 1.2 The "Two Unrelated Trades" Rule (B License Scope)

A B-licensee may take a prime contract only if the work involves either:
1. **Framing or carpentry** as part of the work, OR
2. **Two or more unrelated trades or crafts** (e.g., concrete + electrical, drywall + roofing).

If a job is essentially one trade (e.g., re-roof only, repipe only), the B-licensee may **not** take that prime contract — it must go to the appropriate C-class contractor or you must subcontract to a C-class. The CSLB enforces this; violation is grounds for citation.

**Half-the-work rule**: When the project involves a specialty trade requiring its own C license (notably C-10, C-36, C-46, etc.), the B contractor may **not** self-perform more than 50% of that specialty work unless they also hold the C classification. This forces GCs to subcontract specialty work to licensed subs.

### 1.3 Class B Application — Step by Step

| Step | Item | Detail / Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Application form 13A-1** | Filed with CSLB Sacramento. $450 application fee (2026). Lists the qualifying individual (you, an RMO, or an RME). |
| 2 | **Experience verification** | 4 years of journey-level / foreman / supervisor / contractor / owner-builder experience within the last 10 years. Must be in **multiple trades** consistent with B scope. Each year verified by signed certification from an employer, fellow journeyman, contractor, building official, architect, or engineer. CSLB audits ~3% randomly — keep documentation (pay stubs, W-2s, permits, photos). |
| 3 | **Education credit (optional)** | Up to 3 of the 4 years may be substituted with vocational training, military training, AS/BS in construction management, civil/architectural engineering, or a related apprenticeship. Minimum 1 year of hands-on experience always required. |
| 4 | **Posted notice / publication** | None required (unlike some states). |
| 5 | **Fingerprinting / Live Scan** | After application is accepted CSLB sends fingerprint card; Live Scan at a Department of Justice authorized site. Cost ~$50. Background check looks for prior convictions, license discipline, contracting fraud. Misdemeanors and felonies require disclosure; not automatic disqualifiers but require explanation. |
| 6 | **Exam scheduling** | Once application is accepted CSLB issues a Notice to Appear. You schedule both exams through PSI (psiexams.com). Sites statewide; reschedule penalty was eliminated in 2025. |
| 7 | **Take the two exams** | Law & Business (~115 questions, 3.5 hours) and the Class B trade exam (~115 questions, 3.5 hours). Multiple choice. Closed book. Passing score ~72% (Angoff-calibrated, varies). |
| 8 | **Asbestos open-book exam** | Mandatory for all license types. Takes ~30 minutes. |
| 9 | **Post bond, file workers' comp / exemption** | $25,000 license bond from a CA-admitted surety, plus workers' comp certificate (or exemption — see SB 216 transition below). Bond of Qualifying Individual ($25,000) also required if the qualifier is an RME or a non-owner RMO. |
| 10 | **License issued** | Plastic card mailed. License number assigned. **Total elapsed time: 3–6 months** typical, longer if experience documentation triggers questions. |

### 1.4 Exam Content Breakdown

**Law & Business exam (~40% of overall study time)**:
- Business organization (forms, registration, taxes)
- Employment regulations (workers' comp, unemployment, payroll, AB5)
- Bonds and insurance
- Contracts (HIC requirements, change orders, mechanic's liens)
- Public works (prevailing wage, DIR)
- Safety (Cal/OSHA, IIPP, asbestos, lead)
- Project management, financial management, recordkeeping
- Licensing law (B&P Code §7000 et seq.)

**Class B Trade exam (~60% of study time)**:
- Plan reading and estimating
- Sitework / excavation / grading
- Foundations and concrete
- Framing (wood, light-gauge steel)
- Roofing assemblies
- Building envelope, finishes
- MEP coordination (overview, not deep trade work)
- Code basics (CBC, CRC)
- Safety on the jobsite

**Recommended study time**: 80–120 hours total over 2–3 months. CSLB publishes free study guides at cslb.ca.gov; commercial prep courses (Contractors State License Service, RocketCert, Contractor Training Center) cost $300–$900 and dramatically raise first-attempt pass rates.

### 1.5 Qualifier Options — Who Holds the License

| Role | Who | Risk Profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Sole owner-qualifier** | Owner is also the qualifying individual. No separate qualifier needed. | Cleanest. Owner controls everything. |
| **RMO — Responsible Managing Officer** | An owner/officer of a corporation or LLC who is the qualifier. Must own at least 20% (else treated like an RME). | The RMO is personally responsible for license operations. If something goes wrong, both the entity and the RMO are exposed. |
| **RME — Responsible Managing Employee** | An employee (not an owner) who qualifies the license. Must be a *bona fide* employee — 32+ hours/week or 80% of working hours, on the company's payroll. | Risky for the RME. They carry the qualifying experience but have no equity. Bond of Qualifying Individual required. |

**Warning — "renting" your license**: Acting as an RMO/RME for someone else's company while you don't actually supervise the work is a serious violation (B&P §7068.1). CSLB has aggressively pursued these arrangements: license revocation for all parties, criminal referral, and personal civil liability for the qualifier on every project. If you serve as RMO, you must actually manage construction operations — there is no passive-income version of this.

### 1.6 Bonds — License Bond vs. Other Bonds

| Bond | Amount | Required When | What It Protects |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Contractor's License Bond** | $25,000 | Every license, always | Consumers, employees (unpaid wages), and subs harmed by contractor's violation of license law. Filed with CSLB. |
| **Bond of Qualifying Individual** | $25,000 | When qualifier is an RME, or an RMO owning <10% | Same protections; covers when the qualifier is not also the owner. |
| **Disciplinary Bond** | $15,000–$150,000 | Imposed by CSLB after discipline | Higher-risk operator. |
| **LLC Employee/Worker Bond** | $100,000 | LLC license only | Protects employees from unpaid wages and fringes (in addition to license bond). |
| **Performance Bond (contract bond)** | % of contract | Required by owner on commercial / public works | Owner is paid if contractor fails to complete. |
| **Payment Bond** | % of contract | Required by owner on commercial / public works | Subs and suppliers paid if contractor doesn't pay them. |

**Cost reality**: A $25,000 license bond costs $100–$400/year for someone with good credit (personal FICO 700+). Bad credit can push it to $500–$2,500. The premium is non-refundable. The bond is **not insurance for you** — if a claim is paid, the surety will recover from you personally via the indemnity agreement you sign. Treat it like a credit line you must repay.

**Performance/payment bonds** (contract bonds) are a different animal: written one project at a time, underwritten on your financials, work-in-progress, and experience. Bonding capacity (single-project and aggregate) is built over years with a surety. Most surety relationships are introduced by a specialized bond broker, not a regular P&C agent.

### 1.7 Workers' Comp and the 2028 Transition (SB 216 / SB 1455)

- Historically: contractors with no employees could file a CSLB **certificate of exemption** from workers' comp.
- SB 216 (2022) ended this exemption for several trades in 2023 (C-8 concrete, C-20 HVAC, C-22 asbestos, D-49 tree service) and was scheduled to extend to all classes January 1, 2026.
- SB 1455 (2024) **pushed the universal mandate to January 1, 2028**.
- Effective Jan 1, 2026 (per CSLB industry bulletin): all licensees must provide proof of workers' comp at licensing/renewal regardless of employees — though the universal hard mandate is 2028.
- **Practical plan**: even if you operate solo, budget for owner workers' comp (~$1,500–$3,500/year for a B contractor with no employees, depending on payroll classification) or a joint-venture-with-no-employees exemption certificate (narrow).

### 1.8 License Renewal, Enforcement, Discipline

- **Renewal**: every 2 years. Active renewal fee $700 (2026). Inactive renewal $350. No continuing education is required in California (unlike Florida, Nevada, and many other states). Pay attention anyway — code changes annually.
- **Enforcement**: CSLB has roughly 320 enforcement staff; 15,000–20,000 complaints/year statewide. Investigations begin with a written complaint. CSLB will mediate first; if unresolved, refer to Industry Expert review; then citation, ALJ hearing, or referral to DA for criminal prosecution.
- **Discipline ladder**: warning → citation (up to $30,000) → license suspension → revocation. License history is public at the CSLB Check-A-License portal — *every* discipline appears for life of license.
- **Unlicensed contracting (B&P §7028)**: misdemeanor first offense (up to $5,000 fine + 6 months), felony on repeat offenses or above $500 threshold during a state of emergency. Civilly: an unlicensed contractor **cannot sue to collect payment** on a contract over $500 (B&P §7031), and the owner may sue to **disgorge all sums paid** to the unlicensed contractor. Brutal. Never let your license lapse mid-project — coverage must be continuous from contract through completion.

## 2. Business Structure

### 2.1 The Five Options

| Structure | Liability | Tax | CA Annual Cost | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sole proprietorship** | Unlimited personal | Schedule C on 1040 | $0 (just DBA + city) | Hobby / pre-license. Do not use for real GC work. |
| **General Partnership** | Unlimited personal, joint & several | Pass-through, K-1 | $0 state minimum | Avoid. Worst of all worlds. |
| **Limited Partnership (LP)** | LP partners limited, GP unlimited | Pass-through | $800 min franchise tax | Real estate / capital structures. Rare for GCs. |
| **LLC** | Limited (members) | Pass-through default, can elect S/C | $800 min franchise tax + LLC fee on gross receipts (see below) + $100,000 employee bond | Small/mid GC, <$250K profit, single owner. |
| **S-Corporation** | Limited (shareholders) | Pass-through, 1.5% CA tax on net income | $800 min franchise tax (waived year 1) + 1.5% net | GC with $80K+ profit who wants payroll-tax savings. |
| **C-Corporation** | Limited | Entity tax (CA 8.84%) + dividend tax | $800 min + 8.84% net | Rare. Use only for venture-backed scale. |

### 2.2 Sole Proprietorship — Why Not

The owner *is* the business. A judgment from a single bad project (slip-and-fall, defect lawsuit, subcontractor injury) attaches to the owner's house, vehicles, retirement (limited protection), and future wages. Liability insurance helps, but coverage gaps and umbrella limits are real. For a construction business — which exists to take on physical risk — sole prop is malpractice toward yourself. Use it only for the first license-prep months while you set up the entity.

### 2.3 LLC vs. S-Corp — The Real Decision

For most CA residential GCs the choice is between an **LLC taxed as a partnership / disregarded entity** and an **LLC or corporation that has elected S-corp taxation**. Both give liability protection. The split is tax.

| Factor | LLC (default taxation) | S-Corp election |
|---|---|---|
| Self-employment tax | All net income subject to 15.3% SE tax up to SS wage base | Only the W-2 salary portion is subject to FICA |
| Owner compensation | Owner's draw (untaxed when taken) | Reasonable W-2 salary required (else IRS reclassifies distributions) |
| Payroll complexity | None for owner | Quarterly 941s, W-2, EDD, payroll software |
| State franchise tax | $800 min | $800 min (waived year 1) |
| State gross-receipts fee | LLC fee: $0–$11,790 sliding scale | None |
| State net income tax | None (pass-through to owner) | 1.5% of CA net income |
| CSLB bond requirements | $100K LLC employee bond + $25K license bond | $25K license bond only |
| Best at revenue level | Under ~$250K net | Above ~$80–100K net |

**Rough rule**: Once your business is netting **$80,000–$100,000 of profit** after a reasonable owner salary, the S-corp's payroll-tax savings (avoiding 15.3% SE tax on distributions) typically exceed the cost of payroll administration. Below that level the LLC's simplicity usually wins. Many CA GCs start as LLCs and elect S-corp status (Form 2553) in their second or third year.

**Reasonable salary rule**: An S-corp owner-employee must take a "reasonable" salary before taking distributions. For a working CA GC operating their own jobs, that's typically $70,000–$120,000/year depending on revenue. Underpaying yourself ($30K salary, $200K distributions) is an IRS audit trigger.

### 2.4 California LLC Gross Receipts Fee (a Gotcha)

The $800 minimum tax is on top of an **LLC fee** based on California gross receipts:

| CA Gross Receipts | Annual LLC Fee |
|---|---|
| < $250,000 | $0 |
| $250,000 – $499,999 | $900 |
| $500,000 – $999,999 | $2,500 |
| $1,000,000 – $4,999,999 | $6,000 |
| $5,000,000+ | $11,790 |

For a GC, **gross receipts** are typically the entire contract revenue including pass-through subs and materials, not just your fee. A $1M revenue GC with 10% margin pays $6,000 of LLC fee on $100K of profit — meaningful. S-corps don't have this fee, which is part of why GCs converting at scale.

### 2.5 Setup Checklist (One-Time)

1. **Pick a name** — check CA Secretary of State business search and CSLB license name search and (informally) USPTO TESS. Avoid generic names like "Quality Construction" — they get confused with other companies' liens and complaints.
2. **File formation** — CA Secretary of State: Articles of Organization (LLC, Form LLC-1) or Articles of Incorporation (corp). $70 filing fee, online.
3. **Statement of Information** — within 90 days. $20 LLC / $25 corp. Renew biennially (LLC) or annually (corp).
4. **Operating Agreement / Bylaws** — internal document. Even single-member LLCs need one; banks and courts ask. Templates from RocketLawyer / LegalZoom are acceptable starting points; have an attorney customize if you have partners.
5. **EIN** — IRS, free, online, 10 minutes. Never use your SSN for the business. Required for bank account, payroll, contracts.
6. **S-corp election** — Form 2553 within 75 days of formation or by March 15 of the year you want it effective.
7. **California EDD registration** — required when first employee hired (e-Services for Business).
8. **City business license / tax certificate** — every city you do business in. LA city, SF, Oakland have gross-receipts-based fees. Most other cities have flat fees.
9. **DBA / Fictitious Business Name** — only if operating under a name different from the legal entity. County-level filing + newspaper publication (4 weeks).
10. **Update CSLB** — file an Application for Original Contractor's License *under the new entity* or a Change of Personnel form if migrating an existing license. The license belongs to the legal entity, not transferable across entities.

## 3. Insurance — What You Need and Why

### 3.1 The Core Stack

| Coverage | Typical Limits | Annual Premium (small GC) | Required By |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commercial General Liability (CGL) | $1M occurrence / $2M aggregate | $2,500–$8,000 | Most owners/GCs by contract; not by CSLB |
| Workers' Compensation | Statutory | $1,500 (solo) – 6–15% of payroll | CSLB (transitioning), Labor Code §3700 if employees |
| Commercial Auto | $1M CSL | $1,200–$3,500/vehicle | Any vehicle used for business |
| Tools & Equipment (Inland Marine) | Replacement cost | $400–$1,500 | Lender on financed equipment |
| Umbrella / Excess Liability | $1M–$5M | $800–$3,000/M | Owner contracts often $5M+ |
| Professional Liability (E&O) | $1M occurrence | $1,500–$5,000 | Design-build, consulting |
| Builder's Risk | Project value | 1–4% of construction cost | Lender/owner |
| Installation Floater | Per project | Included or $300–$800 | Self-protection on stored materials |

### 3.2 Commercial General Liability (CGL) — Read the Form

The standard ISO CG 00 01 form covers:
- Bodily injury and property damage to third parties from your operations or products
- Personal & advertising injury
- Premises liability (your office/shop)
- **Completed operations**: damage caused after a project is "put to its intended use" — critical for construction

Key endorsements to negotiate:
- **CG 20 10** — additional insured for ongoing operations (owner/GC asks for this)
- **CG 20 37** — additional insured for completed operations (owner/GC asks for this; many sub policies don't include it — check)
- **CG 24 04** — waiver of subrogation
- **Per-project aggregate** — without this, one ugly claim eats your entire policy's $2M aggregate for the year

Common **exclusions** that surprise new GCs:
- Damage to **your own work** (subs may cover via additional insured, but you can't insure yourself against poor workmanship — that's why you need a quality program)
- Subsidence / earth movement
- Mold (separate sub-limited endorsement at best)
- Wildfire/smoke in some CA zip codes
- Residential exclusions (some carriers exclude single-family or condo work — verify before you write that policy)
- **Designed for / Action over** — injury to a sub's employee suing the GC (action-over coverage must be confirmed not excluded)

Construction-friendly carriers in CA: Travelers, Zurich, Liberty Mutual, USLI, Tokio Marine HCC, BTIS (wholesale), and California-specialty brokers like Tradesman Program Managers and Heffernan. Don't use State Farm or Allstate for a real GC — they will non-renew at the first claim.

### 3.3 Workers' Compensation

- **State Fund** (statefundca.com) is the insurer of last resort but writes most small-contractor policies in California.
- Premium = (Payroll / $100) × Class Rate × Experience Modifier (Mod) × Carrier Factors
- Class rates for B-GC work in CA (2025): roughly $5–$15 per $100 of payroll for office (8810) classes, $25–$60+ per $100 for jobsite labor (5403, 5645, 5474, etc.).
- **Experience Modification Rate (Mod)**: starts at 1.0 (industry average). One serious claim can drive it to 1.3+, which adds 30% to your annual premium for 3 years. Two claims and you're effectively uninsurable except through State Fund.
- **Cost control playbook**:
  1. Written **Injury & Illness Prevention Program (IIPP)** — Cal/OSHA requires this anyway
  2. Pre-employment **physicals** for labor positions
  3. **Drug screening** (post-accident at minimum)
  4. **Light-duty / return-to-work** policy — get injured employees back on payroll fast so claims don't develop into lost-time
  5. **Report immediately** (within 5 days; serious injuries within 8 hours to Cal/OSHA, within 24 hours to insurer)
  6. **Audit your payroll** at year-end — correct classification matters; carpenters mis-coded as laborers cost you money

### 3.4 AB5 / Labor Code §2750.3 — The ABC Test

For California wage, tax, and workers' comp purposes, a worker is an **employee** unless the hiring entity can prove all three:

| Prong | Test |
|---|---|
| **A** | The worker is **free from the control and direction** of the hirer, in contract and in fact. |
| **B** | The worker performs work **outside the usual course** of the hirer's business. (For a GC, framing is *not* outside your usual course — this prong almost never passes for trades a GC normally does.) |
| **C** | The worker is **customarily engaged in an independently established trade**, occupation, or business of the same nature. (License, business cards, EIN, other clients, advertising.) |

**Construction industry exception (Labor Code §2781)**: The older multi-factor *Borello* test (not the ABC test) applies between a **licensed contractor** and a **licensed subcontractor** if all of the following are met:
1. The sub is licensed by CSLB and the work is within their license scope.
2. The sub has a CA business license/tax registration where required.
3. The sub has a written contract.
4. The sub has the required CA business licenses, permits, and insurance.
5. The sub maintains a business location separate from the GC.
6. The sub is customarily engaged in an independently established business.
7. The sub has authority to hire and fire and set rates.
8. The sub holds out to the public as available to perform similar services.

**Practical implication**: You can subcontract legitimately to other **licensed** contractors — that's normal construction. You cannot subcontract to an unlicensed "1099 framer" or "1099 helper" — they are your employee under AB5 and must be on workers' comp and payroll.

**Penalties for misclassification**: $5,000–$15,000 per violation, $10,000–$25,000 if "willful pattern" (Labor Code §226.8). Plus back wages, unpaid overtime, unpaid taxes, penalties, and personal liability for the owner under Labor Code §558.1.

### 3.5 Commercial Auto

Personal auto policies **exclude** business use. Driving your truck to a job site with tools and materials is business use. A claim on a personal policy will be denied and the carrier will non-renew you.

Get a **commercial auto policy** covering:
- Owned vehicles (your truck, company vans)
- **Hired auto** (rented trucks, employee rentals)
- **Non-owned auto** (employee driving their own car for company errands — this is the gap that bites GCs)

$1M combined single limit (CSL) is standard. Add umbrella for higher.

### 3.6 Builder's Risk (Course of Construction)

- Covers the project itself (materials and work-in-progress) against fire, theft, vandalism, weather damage during construction.
- Activates at the start of construction, ends at substantial completion or occupancy (90 days after, typically).
- **Who buys it**: On residential remodels, usually the homeowner's existing dwelling policy + a rider, OR the GC buys a separate policy. On new construction, the GC typically buys (and bills the owner). On commercial, the owner's lender requires it and the owner buys.
- **Get the named-insured language right**: owner + GC + lender + key subs, all as named insureds or additional insureds.

### 3.7 Umbrella / Excess

Stacks on top of CGL, commercial auto, and (sometimes) employer's liability. A $5M umbrella for a small GC runs $2,500–$5,000/year. Owners on $500K+ projects often require $5M total limits; without an umbrella you don't qualify. Add it before you need it.

### 3.8 Installation Floater

A type of inland marine policy that covers materials in transit from supplier and stored on-site before installation. CGL does **not** cover stolen lumber from your jobsite or a truck of cabinets totaled on the freeway. Installation floater fills this gap.

### 3.9 Certificates of Insurance (COIs) — Give and Receive

**To give** (owner or GC asks you for):
- Limits matching contract requirement
- Additional insured (ongoing AND completed ops)
- Waiver of subrogation
- **Primary and non-contributory** wording
- 30-day notice of cancellation

**To receive** (from every sub, every project, every renewal — no exceptions):
- Valid CGL ($1M minimum, $2M aggregate)
- Workers' comp (or owner's exemption if a true sole-licensed sub)
- Commercial auto
- **You** named as additional insured
- Valid CSLB license verified at the same time

**The COI tracking failure** is one of the biggest preventable losses in small GCs. A sub's GL lapses mid-project; the sub's helper falls; the sub has no coverage; the injured worker sues the GC; the GC's CGL excludes sub-injuries; everyone loses. Use a spreadsheet, a CRM field, or software (Insurance Tracker, MyCOI) — track every sub's expiration date and require renewal certs before re-mobilizing.

## 4. Financial Infrastructure

### 4.1 Banking & Cards

- **Separate business checking** — non-negotiable. Co-mingling personal and business funds can pierce the LLC veil and lose your liability protection.
- **Business credit card** with at least 30 days of float. American Express Business Platinum, Chase Ink, or Bank of America Business — pick one that integrates with your accounting software.
- **Line of credit** — apply when you don't need it, draw when you do. A $50K–$100K business LOC at your bank is the difference between making payroll and missing it when a draw is late. Banks underwrite based on 2 years of tax returns and your personal credit; SBA-backed LOCs are an option once you have history.

### 4.2 Accounting System Setup

**QuickBooks Online** with the **Plus** or **Advanced** plan is the industry default. Why:
- Job-costing by Customer:Job and class
- Sub-contractor 1099 tracking
- Time tracking integration (TSheets/QB Time)
- Tax-line mapping for CPA hand-off
- Bank/CC sync

**Chart of accounts for a residential GC** (minimal version):

| Account Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Income | Construction Revenue, Change Order Revenue, Reimbursable Income |
| Cost of Goods Sold (Job Costs) | Subcontractors, Materials, Equipment Rental, Permits, Job Site Labor, Disposal, Job Insurance Allocation |
| Expenses (Overhead) | Office Rent, Office Salaries, Vehicle Expense (allocated), Software, Insurance (Office portion), Marketing, Professional Fees, Bank/Loan Fees |
| Other | Owner's Draw / Distributions, Salaries Officer (S-corp), Loan Repayment, Tax Payments |

Set up **Items** that map to job-cost categories. Track **WIP (Work In Progress)** monthly — total contract value, costs incurred to date, billings to date, costs to complete. Overbillings and underbillings reconcile here.

### 4.3 Cash Flow — The Hardest Part of Running a GC

A typical residential GC project cycles:
- Day 0: Sign contract, receive $1,000 down (HIC cap).
- Day 1: Order long-lead materials, pay 50% deposit to cabinet maker.
- Day 14: First progress billing — get paid 2 weeks later.
- Day 30: Pay subs and suppliers, payroll.
- Day 60: Second draw paid, but cabinet balance now due.
- Day 90: Substantial completion. Owner withholds 10% retention.
- Day 120: Final punch-list cleared, retention released.

**You float costs for 30–60 days**. On a $200K kitchen remodel that's $40K–$80K of working capital tied up. Multiply by every concurrent job. **This is why GCs go broke during growth, not during recession** — every new job consumes cash before producing margin.

**Rules of thumb**:
1. Keep **3 months of overhead** in reserve at all times (own salaries + rent + insurance + minimum operating).
2. **Bill weekly or biweekly**, never monthly. Time-and-materials with weekly billing dramatically improves cash flow vs. monthly progress draws.
3. **Front-load the schedule of values** where defensible (mobilization, demo, layout) so early billings cover early outflows. Don't game it; owners and lenders catch this and trust dies.
4. **Pay subs on net-30 from receipt of owner payment** — "paid when paid" clauses are enforceable in CA for cash flow timing but not for ultimately denying payment.

### 4.4 Accounting Method & Tax

| Method | When Income Recognized | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash basis** | When cash received | Small GCs (<$25M average revenue). Simple, defers tax. |
| **Accrual basis** | When earned (invoiced) | Larger GCs, GAAP financial statements for sureties. |
| **Percentage-of-Completion (PCM)** | Pro rata over project as costs are incurred | **Mandatory for long-term contracts (>1 year) for tax purposes**; required for sureties. Tied to WIP schedule. |
| **Completed-Contract Method (CCM)** | When project finishes | Limited to small contractors, short contracts. Defers tax until completion. |

For a small residential GC doing 3–9 month projects: cash basis for tax, accrual + WIP for management reporting. Your CPA will reconcile.

**Quarterly estimated taxes** — federal (Form 1040-ES) and California (Form 540-ES). Safe-harbor rule: pay the lesser of (a) 90% of current year tax or (b) 110% of prior year tax (100% if AGI <$150K). Miss a quarter and you owe underpayment penalty (currently ~8% APR). Set up auto-transfer of ~25% of every distribution to a "tax reserve" account; pay quarterly from there.

### 4.5 California-Specific Tax Items

- **CA state income tax** for pass-through to owner: 1%–13.3% progressive
- **CA franchise tax**: $800 LLC, $800 corp min (waived first year for corps only)
- **CA LLC fee**: see §2.4
- **CA S-corp tax**: 1.5% of net income
- **PTE (Pass-Through Entity Elective Tax)** — California's SALT-cap workaround. The entity pays 9.3% on the owner's share of CA-source income; the owner gets a credit on their personal return. Net benefit: $40K+/year in federal tax savings for owners netting >$300K. Talk to your CPA about whether to elect.
- **SDI** (employee-paid, you withhold): 1.1% of wages, no cap as of 2024
- **SUI** (employer-paid): new employers 3.4% on first $7,000 of wages per employee (in 2025-2026); experience-rated after 3 years
- **ETT** (employer-paid): 0.1% on first $7,000

## 5. Hiring and HR

### 5.1 Employee vs. Sub — The Practical Line

The construction-industry §2781 exception (see §3.4) lets you treat a **licensed sub** as a subcontractor. Everyone else who works for you in the field is an employee.

| Scenario | Status |
|---|---|
| Licensed C-10 electrical sub with their own LLC | Subcontractor |
| Roofing crew that "subs" from another GC's payroll | Their employer's employees; you're using them as a sub through their employer |
| "1099 carpenter" with no license, works on your projects | **Employee under AB5** — must be W-2'd |
| Day labor from Home Depot parking lot | **Your employee for the day** — must have workers' comp |
| Architect designing a project for your client | Independent professional, exempt from AB5 |
| Office bookkeeper working 10 hours/week | Employee (or licensed accounting firm) |

### 5.2 Onboarding Checklist (Day 1 of First Employee)

| Form / Action | What | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| **I-9** | Employment eligibility verification (federal) | First day; documents within 3 business days |
| **E-Verify** | Optional in CA but required for federal contractors and some local ordinances | First 3 days |
| **W-4** | Federal income tax withholding | Day 1 |
| **DE 4** | California state income tax withholding | Day 1 |
| **DE 34** | New Hire Report to EDD | Within 20 days |
| **Wage Theft Prevention Notice (Labor Code §2810.5)** | Written notice of rate, OT, paydays, employer info, workers' comp carrier | Day 1 |
| **IIPP acknowledgment** | Receipt of written Injury & Illness Prevention Program | Day 1 |
| **Heat illness training (Cal/OSHA T8 §3395)** | For any outdoor work | Before outdoor work begins |
| **Workers' comp pamphlet** | DWC Form-1A "Time of Hire" pamphlet | Day 1 |
| **Sexual harassment training** | 1 hour non-supervisory, 2 hours supervisory; within 6 months of hire; every 2 years thereafter | Within 6 months |

### 5.3 Payroll Options

| Option | Cost | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DIY (QuickBooks Payroll)** | $50–$150/month | Integrated with books; cheap | You're responsible for tax filings, end-of-year W-2s, errors |
| **Gusto** | $40 + $6/employee | Modern UI, auto-tax filings, benefits integration | Limited on certified payroll |
| **ADP / Paychex** | $80 + $4–$8/employee | Full-service, HR support, handles certified payroll well | Pricey, sales-y, slower customer service |
| **Construction-specific (Foundation, Sage 100, Vista)** | $300+/month | True job-cost integration, certified payroll, union compliance | Overkill until you're $5M+ revenue |

### 5.4 Prevailing Wage / Public Works

If you ever bid public works (school, city, state, federal-funded private):
- **Threshold**: prevailing wage applies to public works projects >$1,000.
- **DIR registration** required for both prime and subs on public works contracts ≥$25,000 (construction) or ≥$15,000 (maintenance). $400/year. Register at dir.ca.gov before bidding.
- **Prevailing wage rates** published by DIR by craft, county, and project type. Includes basic hourly rate + fringe benefits (health, pension, vacation, training).
- **Certified payroll records** (CPR / Form A-1-131, federal form WH-347 if federal-funded) submitted weekly to the awarding body and to DIR's online eCPR system.
- **Apprentice ratios** — must request dispatch from an approved apprenticeship program; minimum 1 apprentice hour for every 5 journeyman hours on most crafts.
- Penalties for non-compliance: $200/day per worker; debarment 1–3 years from public works.

Public works is its own discipline. Don't dabble — either commit to a public-works compliance system or stay private.

### 5.5 California Labor Law — The Big Rocks

- **Minimum wage**: $16.50/hr statewide 2025 (rises annually). Many cities are higher (SF $18.67, LA $17.28, etc.). Construction workers almost always exceed minimum wage anyway.
- **Overtime**: 1.5× after 8 hours/day or 40 hours/week; 1.5× first 8 hours of 7th consecutive workday; 2× over 12 hours/day or over 8 hours on the 7th day. **No "8/80" exemption for construction in California** — that's a federal hospital rule. CA construction follows the standard 8-hour daily threshold.
- **Alternative Workweek Schedule (AWS)** — 4/10s allowed only if adopted by 2/3 secret-ballot of affected employees and registered with DLSE. Without an AWS, four 10-hour days = 8 hours of daily OT.
- **Meal breaks**: 30 min unpaid by end of 5th hour worked (1st meal); 2nd meal by end of 10th hour. Premium of 1 hour's pay per missed meal break.
- **Rest breaks**: 10 min paid for every 4 hours worked or major fraction thereof.
- **Final paycheck timing**:
  - Quit with no notice: within 72 hours
  - Quit with 72-hr notice: last day worked
  - Fired/laid off: **immediately**, at termination, including accrued vacation. Late = waiting-time penalties of 1 day's wages per day late, up to 30 days. Brutal — don't be late.
- **Pay stubs**: 9 specific items required (Labor Code §226). Wrong pay stub = $50 penalty first violation, $100 subsequent, up to $4,000 per employee. PAGA claims multiply this.
- **Sick leave**: 5 days / 40 hours per year (CA Healthy Workplaces, Healthy Families Act expanded 2024).
- **Heat illness standard** (T8 §3395): water, shade, rest, training, emergency procedures. Cal/OSHA inspects.

### 5.6 Workers' Comp Claim Management

When an injury happens:
1. **First aid only?** Treat on-site. Record on the OSHA 300 log if appropriate.
2. **Beyond first aid or any indication of medical care?** Give the employee the **DWC-1 Claim Form** within 1 working day. Send it back to the carrier within 1 working day of receipt.
3. **Serious injury, illness, or death?** Report to **Cal/OSHA within 8 hours** (legal requirement, $5,000+ penalty if missed).
4. **Notify carrier** within 5 days (Labor Code §3760).
5. **Investigate** — photos, witness statements, equipment inspection. Document immediately while memories are fresh.
6. **Return-to-work**: get the employee back in a light-duty capacity ASAP. Every day they're out increases claim cost and your future mod. Have a written modified-duty job menu ready (filing, inventory, training, photo documentation).

## 6. Contracts and Legal

### 6.1 Home Improvement Contract (HIC) — B&P §7159

Every contract for residential remodel, repair, addition, or alteration must contain (in 12-point or larger type, signed by owner before any work):

| Required Element | Notes |
|---|---|
| Name, business address, license number of contractor | Plus salesperson registration # if applicable |
| Date of contract execution | The date owner signs |
| Approximate start date and approximate completion date | "Approximate" is allowed but must be in good faith |
| Description of work and materials | Specific, not vague — "kitchen remodel per plans dated X" |
| Total contract price | The fixed price or cost-plus formula |
| Down payment amount | If any, capped at lesser of $1,000 or 10% (§7159.5) |
| Schedule of progress payments | Each tied to a defined milestone; cannot exceed value of work completed |
| List of documents incorporated | Plans, specs, allowances, exclusions |
| Note about extra work / change orders | Written change orders required |
| **Notice of Cancellation / 3-day right to cancel** | In specific statutory format; for in-home solicitations, certain seniors get 5 days |
| **Mechanic's Lien Warning Notice** | Statutory language; given separately or in contract |
| Insurance and workers' comp disclosures | License bond, workers' comp carrier (or exemption) |
| Information About Contractors State License Board | CSLB contact for complaints |

Failure to comply: contract is voidable by the buyer, contractor cannot enforce payment, CSLB citation, possible misdemeanor.

### 6.2 The Down Payment Cap (B&P §7159.5)

For residential home improvement contracts:

> **The down payment may not exceed $1,000 or 10 percent of the contract price, whichever is less.**

A $1,000 cap on a $500K addition is brutal for cash flow. The workaround is **lawful progress payments tied to defined milestones**. You cannot front-load the schedule of values to circumvent the cap — progress payments must be roughly proportional to value of work completed.

**Exception — swimming pool contracts**: down payment cap is $200 or 2% of the contract, whichever is less, AND progress payments are restricted (cannot exceed % of work performed plus % of materials delivered). Pools are highly regulated.

### 6.3 Change Orders (B&P §7159.6)

Every change to scope, price, or schedule must be:
1. **In writing**
2. **Signed by the owner before the work begins**
3. **Describe the work, the price, and the time impact**

Verbal change orders are unenforceable against owners. The CSLB has cited contractors hundreds of times for trying to bill verbal changes. Use a printed Change Order form, get signature, attach to contract. No exceptions.

### 6.4 The 3-Day Cancellation Right (and the 2026 Update)

For most home solicitation sales (including HICs signed at the buyer's home), the buyer has **3 business days** to cancel without penalty. Some senior citizens (65+) and disaster-area contracts get **5 days**.

**2026 change (AB 1327)**: cancellation may now be delivered by **email** as well as mail/fax/in-person. The contract must include an email address and phone number specifically designated to receive cancellations.

### 6.5 Written Contract Requirement (B&P §7164, §7159)

In California:
- **Any home improvement contract over $500** must be in writing.
- **Any contract requiring a permit** must be in writing regardless of price.
- Cost-plus contracts must specify the formula and a "not to exceed" maximum.

Verbal contracts are technically valid in some cases but if you have to sue to collect and the contract should have been in writing, you may lose by default.

### 6.6 Mechanic's Lien Warning Notice

Required separately or within the HIC. Statutory language warns owner that subs/suppliers can lien their property if not paid. Owner signs/acknowledges. Missing it is a §7159 violation.

### 6.7 Attorney — When to Engage

| Situation | Need |
|---|---|
| Entity formation, operating agreement | Business attorney, one-time $1,500–$3,500 |
| Standard contract template (HIC) | **Construction attorney** — generic templates miss CA-specific requirements |
| Contract review before signing a $500K+ owner contract | Construction attorney, $1,500–$5,000 |
| Sub dispute, lien, mechanic's claim | Construction litigator |
| CSLB complaint or investigation | License defense attorney (different specialty) |
| Employment claim (wage, harassment, discrimination) | Employment defense attorney |
| Cal/OSHA citation | Safety / Cal-OSHA defense attorney |

**Construction attorney vs. general business attorney**: California construction law (B&P, Civil Code §8000s, Public Contract Code, Labor Code prevailing wage) is its own specialty. A general business attorney will draft you a generic services agreement that lacks the §7159 notices, fails the down-payment cap test, and skips the lien warning. Pay the extra hundred dollars per hour for a construction specialist. Membership in the **AGC, ABC, BIA, or CALCC** typically gives access to vetted construction attorney panels and template libraries.

## 7. Risk Management

### 7.1 Job Profitability — Catch Bad Jobs Early

Set up monthly WIP review:

| Metric | Calculation | Action Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **% complete by cost** | Actual costs / Total budgeted cost | Should match billing % within 5% |
| **Cost-to-complete (CTC)** | Realistic estimate of remaining cost | Update monthly, not "budget minus actual" |
| **Estimated gross profit at completion** | Contract value − total expected cost | Trending below 10%? Investigate |
| **Overbilling / Underbilling** | Billings to date − % complete × contract | Underbilling means you're financing the owner |
| **Schedule variance** | Days behind schedule | Each day late = labor cost overrun + delay liability |

Bad jobs almost always show their pattern in months 1–2. If costs are tracking high or the owner is slow to pay, **renegotiate or terminate early** rather than feeding the loss for 6 months.

### 7.2 Pre-Qualifying Owners

Before signing a $100K+ contract, ask:

| Question | Why |
|---|---|
| How are you funding the project? Cash, HELOC, construction loan, refinance? | Cash > HELOC > construction loan (which has its own headaches) |
| If construction loan: which lender? Have they approved the budget? | A lender that hasn't pre-approved your contract amount = future fight over draws |
| Have you done a project this size before? | First-time owners require 2× the hand-holding |
| Spouse on board? | Splits within married couples kill projects |
| Any prior litigation with contractors? | Pacer / county court search; serial litigants exist |
| References from prior contractors? | Same as you give them — works in reverse |

**Red flags**: pays for plans personally but won't show source of construction funds; haggles aggressively on initial price; bad-mouths every prior contractor; wants you to start "right away" without a signed contract.

### 7.3 Pre-Qualifying Subs

Every sub, every project:

| Item | Source |
|---|---|
| Active CSLB license, no discipline | cslb.ca.gov/OnlineServices/CheckLicenseII |
| Classification matches scope | Don't let a C-39 roofer do C-43 sheet metal |
| Valid CGL with you as additional insured | COI with §3.9 endorsements |
| Workers' comp current | COI |
| References from 3 recent GCs | Call them |
| Financial stability (for larger subs) | Trade references, D&B if available |
| Master Subcontract Agreement signed | Annual blanket, with project-specific work orders |

### 7.4 Lien Rights Protection

California Civil Code §§8000–8848 governs construction liens (the "Works of Improvement Act"). Critical deadlines:

| Action | Who | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| **20-Day Preliminary Notice** (Civ. Code §8200) | Every claimant **except** direct contractors with the owner | Within 20 days of first labor/materials. Late notice = lien limited to work done in 20 days preceding service. |
| **Stop Payment Notice** | Sub/supplier without direct contract with owner | Before final notice of completion is recorded |
| **Mechanic's Lien** | Anyone who served preliminary notice (if required) | Within 90 days of completion or cessation of work; 60 days if Notice of Completion is recorded by owner |
| **Foreclosure on Lien** | Lien claimant | Within **90 days** of recording the lien — else lien expires |
| **Release Bond (Civ. Code §8424)** | Owner | Owner can post bond at 125% of lien amount to clear title; suit then proceeds against the bond |

As a **prime contractor** you don't need to serve preliminary notice on a *private* job (you have a direct contract with the owner). You **do** need to serve preliminary notice on the construction lender if there is one. On public works, you serve preliminary notice on the public agency.

As a GC you also receive notices from your subs and suppliers — track them. Unconditional waivers (Civ. Code §8132, §8136) are how subs release their lien rights upon payment. Use the statutory forms; don't accept custom waivers that broaden the release.

### 7.5 Dispute Prevention Kit

1. **Complete written contract** — §7159 compliant, signed before work begins
2. **Signed change orders** — every change, no exceptions
3. **Daily logs** — weather, crew, work performed, deliveries, visitors, RFIs, problems. Buildertrend / CompanyCam / paper notebook — pick one and never miss a day
4. **Progress photos** — daily, geo-tagged. CompanyCam is the industry standard
5. **Email everything** — verbal conversations get a follow-up email summary; this is your written record
6. **Meeting minutes** — even informal owner meetings get a 3-line recap email
7. **Substantial completion letter** — date-stamped letter notifying owner of substantial completion starts the warranty clock and the retention release timeline
8. **Final waiver and release** — exchanged at final payment

### 7.6 Business Continuity

What if you get hit by a truck mid-project?

- **Key-person life insurance** ($500K–$2M term) — payable to business, funds completion of jobs and operating expenses while transitioning
- **Disability insurance** — own-occupation, replacing 60% of income for 24+ months
- **Power of attorney / successor designation** — written instruction on who can sign change orders, access bank accounts, complete jobs. CSLB allows continuation under a different qualifier for limited periods.
- **CSLB license continuation**: if the sole owner-qualifier dies, the license can be continued for up to 1 year while a successor obtains licensure; this requires advance designation and family awareness.

## 8. Scaling the Business

### 8.1 The Field vs. Office Transition

A typical solo GC running 2–3 concurrent jobs at $100K–$300K each tops out at $1M–$1.5M annual revenue. Beyond that, you cannot personally manage every detail. The first scaling decision is **when to step out of the field and hire a project manager**.

Signals to hire a PM:
- You're working 60+ hours/week and still missing details
- Client communication is your biggest complaint
- Bids are sitting because you're swinging hammers
- Margins are dropping because you can't supervise multiple jobs

PM compensation: $80K–$140K base + bonus tied to job profitability. Often a former superintendent or assistant PM from a larger GC. The PM eats your role as on-site overseer, freeing you to bid, manage finances, and run the business.

### 8.2 Overhead as % of Revenue — The Health Indicator

| Revenue Tier | Healthy Overhead % | Common Failure Point |
|---|---|---|
| Solo, $300K–$800K | 8–12% | Owner under-paid; one slow month breaks cash flow |
| Small team, $1M–$3M | 12–18% | First office, first PM hire — overhead jumps before revenue catches up |
| $3M–$10M | 14–20% | Adding admin, estimating, accounting; danger zone for "growing broke" |
| $10M+ | 16–22% | Should have stable systems; if not, structural problem |

**Danger sign**: overhead growing faster than gross profit dollars. If you add a $90K PM but only gain $400K of revenue at 12% margin = $48K of gross profit, you've made the business worse. Hire ahead of revenue only if you have signed work to backfill the new capacity within 90 days.

### 8.3 Volume Thresholds That Trigger Requirements

| Trigger | What Activates |
|---|---|
| First employee | Workers' comp policy, EDD registration, IIPP, payroll system |
| 5 employees | Sexual harassment training (supervisory) becomes mandatory for any supervisor |
| 15 employees | FEHA full coverage; CalSavers retirement plan (state-mandated if you don't offer one) |
| 20 employees | COBRA continuation coverage (federal) |
| 50 employees | FMLA / CFRA, EEO-1 reporting, ACA employer mandate |
| 100 employees | SB 1162 pay data reporting to CRD; EEO-1 with pay data |
| $1M revenue | CA gross-receipts LLC fees begin (see §2.4) |
| $5M revenue | Most sureties expect reviewed financial statements; bonding capacity scales |
| Federal contracts ≥$10K | E-Verify required (federal contractor) |

### 8.4 Sub Network vs. Self-Perform Crew

| | Sub Network | Self-Perform |
|---|---|---|
| Capital required | Low | High (tools, vehicles, payroll float) |
| Margin per hour | Lower (sub's overhead in the cost) | Higher if utilized; brutal if not |
| Scheduling flexibility | Subs have other clients — schedules slip | You control the calendar |
| Quality control | Variable per sub | Consistent if you train and retain |
| Risk | Sub insolvency, walks off, files lien | Workers' comp claims, OT, mod increases |
| Best at scale | Mid-size GCs (residential remodel, custom homes) | Production builders, repetitive work |

Most successful CA residential GCs run a **hybrid**: self-perform carpentry/PM + reliable sub network for MEP, drywall, paint, flooring, roofing. The carpentry crew is your culture and quality backbone; the subs are your capacity multiplier.

### 8.5 Estimating Tools — Stair-Step

| Stage | Tool | Cost | When to Upgrade |
|---|---|---|---|
| Starting | Excel + cost database | $0 | Until you're bidding 30+ jobs/year |
| Growing | Buildertrend / CoConstruct / JobTread | $300–$600/mo | When client communication / financial tracking are slipping |
| Serious estimating | STACK / PlanSwift / ProEst | $200–$500/mo | When takeoff time is the bottleneck |
| Full ERP | Sage 100 Contractor / Foundation / CMiC | $5K–$50K + monthly | $10M+ revenue |

### 8.6 CRM and Lead Management

Track every lead from first contact to outcome:

| Stage | Data Point |
|---|---|
| Source | Referral (whom?), Google, Houzz, Yelp, drive-by, repeat |
| First contact date | |
| Scope/budget | |
| Bid sent? Bid amount? | |
| Won/lost/no decision | |
| Reason | Price, schedule, fit, ghosted, lost to specific competitor |
| Margin actual vs. estimated | (After completion) |

A simple CRM (HubSpot Free, Pipedrive, Buildertrend's CRM module) lets you compute:
- **Win rate** by source (referrals usually 60–80%, cold leads 10–20%)
- **Cost per lead** by channel
- **Margin by lead source** — referred leads almost always have higher margins than cold leads
- **Sales cycle length** — how long from first contact to signed contract

### 8.7 Marketing for Residential GCs

The marketing hierarchy for high-margin residential work, roughly in order of ROI:

1. **Past-client referrals** — highest close rate, highest margins. Build a referral program: $500–$2,000 thank-you, hand-written notes at 6 months and 1 year.
2. **Architect / designer relationships** — they hand you pre-qualified, pre-funded clients. Coffee meetings, do excellent work on their referrals, send them photography. Expect 20–40% of revenue from this channel once established.
3. **Realtor relationships** — pre-listing renovation work, post-sale buyer projects. Slightly lower margins (realtors want speed), but a steady flow.
4. **Houzz Pro** — high-intent traffic for kitchens/baths/additions. $300–$1,000/month for premium listings. Strong portfolio is everything.
5. **Google Business Profile** — free, often overlooked. Photos, response to every review, weekly posts. Local SEO matters.
6. **Yelp** — declining but still relevant in some metros. Worth a free profile; not always worth paid.
7. **Website + SEO** — eventually necessary; start with one good page (about, portfolio, contact, service area, license #) and grow.
8. **Instagram** — works for design-forward GCs serving design-conscious buyers. Time-intensive.
9. **Paid ads** — lowest in the hierarchy; high cost per lead, lowest close rate. Skip until you have the systems to handle low-quality leads.
10. **Door-to-door / cold calls** — illegal for high-pressure home solicitation in many ways; reputation cost is high. Avoid.

## Closing Thought

Running a GC business is two jobs welded together: the construction craft you already have, and the **financial-legal-HR machine** that makes it sustainable. The craft brings in revenue. The machine keeps the revenue. New GCs almost always under-invest in the machine — they treat insurance, accounting, contracts, and HR as overhead instead of as the actual operating system. The contractors who survive year five have, almost without exception, made peace with the machine: they pay for the attorney, they refuse the verbal change order, they track every COI, they pay themselves a salary and run quarterly taxes. The work is the same. The discipline is what compounds.

Build the machine first. Then build the buildings.
