# Tier 2 Pilot Launch Readiness

**Status**: Approved for execution concurrent with Phase 1 outreach
**Pilot window**: Weeks 4–11 post-Phase-1-send
**Decision gates**: Week 6 (adoption assessment), Week 11 (pilot debrief and full Tier 2 authorization)

---

## Executive Summary

The Tier 2 pilot prepares 3–5 organizations for an 8-week engagement window (Weeks 4–11 post-Phase-1) while Phase 1 outreach completes. The pilot tests whether Tier 2 sector-specific framing generates substantive practitioner engagement rather than polite acknowledgment, and whether feedback collected during the pilot is strong enough to refine messaging before full Tier 2 rollout.

**Success metrics**:
- 60%+ adoption rate among pilot organizations (active use reported within 2 weeks of first contact)
- Feedback triggers at least 2 messaging refinements before the full Tier 2 wave
- At least 1 downstream policy ask generated (training deployment, committee briefing, downstream tool integration)
- Fewer than 5% of pilot organizations are also Tier 1 contacts (minimize double-counting and over-contact)

**Why run this in parallel with Phase 1 rather than after**: Phase 1's 2–3 week active window is dead time for Tier 2 preparation if used passively. All pilot materials, contact verification, and feedback surveys can be prepared during Weeks 1–3 without sending a single pilot email. When Phase 1 outreach completes and the gate is passed, pilot invitations go out the same week rather than 2 weeks later.

## Pilot Organization Selection

### Selection Rationale

Each pilot organization must satisfy three criteria: (1) a direct match to a ready-now scenario playbook, (2) staff with the technical or operational depth to evaluate the playbook against real-world practice, and (3) a network multiplier effect — internal or external distribution capacity that compounds the pilot's reach beyond the initial contact. A fourth criterion distinguishes preferred from acceptable candidates: existing relationship or clear bridge from Phase 1 contacts.

### Recommended Pilot Cohort

**1. Freedom of the Press Foundation (FPF)**
Maps to: Journalist Security Playbook (Playbook 5)

FPF operates SecureDrop at 65+ newsrooms and runs the only systematic journalist digital security training program in the United States. Their security team has both the technical depth to evaluate the CBP border device protocol and Signal safety number verification sections, and the distribution infrastructure to implement feedback immediately. Any gap FPF identifies in Playbook 5 is a gap affecting the largest segment of the journalist pilot audience.

Playbook status: Playbook 5 ready to deploy.
Network multiplier: 65+ newsrooms through SecureDrop network. A single FPF adoption decision cascades into the largest journalist security training pipeline in the country.
Pilot ask: 45-minute technical review session with the FPF security team, followed by structured feedback on three specific sections — CBP device protocol, Signal safety number verification, and SecureDrop deployment guidance for small newsrooms.
Contact: security@freedom.press

**2. National Lawyers Guild Mass Defense Committee (NLG)**
Maps to: Activist Organizing Playbook (Playbook 3)

NLG Mass Defense is already embedded in labor union networks through its Legal Observer and protest legal support work. The 5-tier role-specific guidance (frontline participant through organizational leadership) and the ALPR documentation are exactly the content Mass Defense coordinators evaluate for their protest legal support work. The escalation matrix (Level 1/2/3) is immediately integrable into Legal Observer training.

Playbook status: Playbook 3 ready to deploy.
Network multiplier: NLG operates in overlapping spaces with labor union organizers; adoption by Mass Defense introduces the activist organizing playbook into the labor legal support pipeline.
Pilot ask: Assessment of the escalation matrix for use in Legal Observer training, and feedback on whether frontline participant guidance conflicts with any NLG advice currently given to protest participants.
Contact: massdef@nlg.org

**3. Community Legal Services Philadelphia (CLS)**
Maps to: Immigration Surveillance Evasion Playbook (Playbook 1)

CLS runs immigration legal clinics and works directly with undocumented clients navigating enforcement risk. This makes CLS one of the few organizations that can validate the immigration playbook's threat model against real client intake experience — specifically Part 0 (preparedness protocol) and the data broker opt-out sequence, which were designed for the legal aid context that CLS operates in.

Playbook status: Playbook 1 ready for validation (CLS pilot provides the practitioner confirmation that the threat model is accurate before wider distribution).
Network multiplier: CLS's client base and referral network extend into the Philadelphia-area immigrant community, creating a direct pathway from organizational adoption to individual end-user reach.
Pilot ask: Practitioner review of the Part 0 protocol and data broker opt-out sequence, specifically whether the threat model reflects the enforcement risk patterns CLS attorneys currently advise clients on.
Contact: Standard organizational intake (verify current contact during Weeks 1–3 preparation window)

**4. Democratic GAIN (Election Worker Security Network) — DEFERRED**
Maps to: Election Worker Scenario — no dedicated playbook currently available.

GAIN bridges election security professionals and local election administrators. The audience is real and the threat model is documented in `election-worker-opSec-supplement.md`, but the corpus gap — no dedicated election worker playbook — makes GAIN premature as a pilot partner. Including GAIN in the pilot before the playbook exists would generate feedback that cannot be acted on.

**Decision**: Defer the GAIN track pending Domain 37 corpus research feedback. When a dedicated election worker playbook is ready, GAIN becomes the lead pilot contact for that sector. Do not send the `election-worker-opSec-supplement.md` as a standalone playbook substitute — it is a gap document, not a finished resource.

If the gate is met at end of Week 3: send pilot invitations Monday–Wednesday of Week 4, one per day maximum, in priority order (FPF first, then NLG, then CLS).

If the gate is not met: delay pilot launch by 1 week. Pilot organizations are more sophisticated audiences than Phase 1 legal aid contacts; an unrefined or premature approach reflects poorly on the corpus.

### Weeks 4–6 (Days 22–36): Initial Feedback Collection and Usage Assessment

Initial responses from these organizations typically arrive within 5–10 business days. Positive responses generate scheduling conversations; acknowledgment responses generate a follow-up with the playbook attached.

| Task | Timing |
|------|--------|
| Follow up on non-responses | Day 10 after each send (1 follow-up only per org) |
| Schedule orientation sessions for positive responders | Within 5 business days of positive reply |
| Deliver first sessions (if scheduled in Week 5) | Week 6 |
| Track engagement score for each pilot org | Ongoing, weekly update |

**Decision Gate — Week 6**: Adoption assessment. Count the number of pilot organizations that have (a) responded substantively (not just acknowledged receipt) or (b) scheduled a session.

- **If 60%+ adoption**: proceed to full engagement sessions and full Tier 2 wave preparation on schedule.
- **If 40–59% adoption**: proceed but flag messaging for refinement. Run subject line diagnostic before the full Tier 2 wave.
- **If under 40% adoption**: pivot messaging before full Tier 2 launch. Run 1-on-1 calls with non-responders; diagnose whether the problem is contact quality (wrong staff function reached), framing (subject line too generic), or timing. Do not launch the full Tier 2 wave until at least 2 of 3 pilot orgs show engagement.

### What "Substantive Feedback" Means

A substantive feedback item is any of: (a) a specific claim the reviewer can or cannot verify against their practice; (b) a countermeasure they have tested and found accurate or inaccurate; (c) a use case the playbook does not address that they regularly encounter; (d) a question the playbook raises but does not answer. Positive-only feedback — "this is great, very thorough" — counts as acknowledgment, not substantive feedback.

## Contingency Plans

### Low Adoption (Fewer Than 2 of 3 Pilot Organizations Respond by Week 6)

**Diagnosis first**. Before changing the approach, run a contact quality and framing diagnostic:
- Are the email subject lines specific? ("Journalist security playbook — review request" is specific; "New security resource" is not.)
- Are pilot invitations under 200 words? These are not the standard Tier 2 templates.
- Are you reaching technical or practice staff, not communications generalists? FPF's security@freedom.press reaches their digital security team; press@freedom.press reaches their press team. Wrong contact function means wrong audience.

**Response if diagnostic confirms framing problem**: Revise pilot invitations and re-send to non-responding organizations with a different subject line. Allow 7 more days.

**Response if fewer than 2 organizations respond after correction**: Launch the full Tier 2 wave on the standard Week 11 schedule without waiting for pilot debrief. The pilot has not produced enough data to be worth further delay. Document the attempt in the tracking spreadsheet for the quarterly review.

**Intensive 1-on-1 calls for non-responders**: If any pilot organization acknowledged receipt but has not engaged further, schedule a 20-minute call rather than sending additional email. These organizations have the technical staff to engage — the barrier is usually prioritization, not interest. A direct ask ("Can we schedule 20 minutes this week?") converts at higher rates than a follow-up email for this audience.

### High-Risk Feedback (Security Gap Identified)

If any pilot organization reports that the playbook contains guidance that could increase rather than decrease risk for their clients or population:

1. Do not dismiss or minimize the concern.
2. Pause distribution of the relevant playbook section to any new organizations until the concern is assessed.
3. Escalate to the user for threat assessment before wider distribution proceeds.
4. Update the Gist with a correction or a "under review" note on the affected section.
5. Return to the reporting organization with a specific response to their concern within 5 business days.

This contingency exists because the immigration and DV survivor playbooks carry the highest risk of harmful guidance if the threat model is wrong. Pilot organizations with direct client contact (CLS, in particular) are the most reliable early-warning source for this failure mode.

### Contact Turnover

Organizations in these sectors experience significant staff turnover. Pre-identify substitute contacts at each pilot organization before Week 4:

- **FPF**: If security@freedom.press bounces or goes silent, the FPF communications team (info@freedom.press) can route to the correct technical staff member.
- **NLG Mass Defense**: If massdef@nlg.org is unresponsive, the NLG national office can identify the current Mass Defense coordinator.
- **CLS Philadelphia**: Verify the correct program contact during Weeks 1–3. If the initial contact has left, the CLS intake line can identify the correct immigration program staff member.

Contact turnover is expected, not exceptional, in legal aid and advocacy organizations. Pre-identifying the fallback saves a week of delay.

### Full Wave Delay (Pilot Surfaces Systematic Problem)

If pilot feedback reveals a systematic error in a playbook — legal inaccuracy, outdated protocol, threat model mismatch with current enforcement practice — delay the full Tier 2 wave until the correction is made and the pilot organization confirms it is adequate.

A 2-week delay for a substantive correction is acceptable. A 4-week delay is the maximum before the cost of delay exceeds the benefit of a corrected document.
