# Tier 2 Distribution Sequencing & Organizational Outreach Strategy

**Bottom line**: Phase 1 (Tier 1 individual-focused distribution to 45 organizations) is complete and awaiting approval. This document designs Phase 2 from first principles so that organizational outreach can begin within 48 hours of Phase 1 launch — not 2–3 weeks into discovery. The document answers five questions in sequence: which Tier 1 organizations graduate to Tier 2, how do you message each sector compellingly, what materials do you need to build, when does each wave happen, and what metrics unlock a public Phase 2 launch.

---

## Executive Summary

Phase 2 shifts from individual awareness to institutional embedding. Where Phase 1 asked individuals to read, understand, and apply a framework, Phase 2 asks organizations to adopt it into governance, operations, and distribution to their own networks. This is a harder ask with a higher payoff: a single organizational adoption at CLINIC (400+ affiliated programs) or Freedom of the Press Foundation multiplies reach more than dozens of individual recipients.

Three design choices drive the strategy:

**First**, Tier 2 candidates are not pre-selected — they emerge from Phase 1 engagement data. The three-factor readiness framework (engagement depth + integration signal + network multiplier) produces a scored list at the Day 28 checkpoint. Organizations that score 3/3 get personal pre-contact invitations; 2/3 get inclusion in the standard wave; below 2/3 are excluded. This eliminates guesswork and ensures Tier 2 outreach lands on organizations already oriented toward the framework.

**Second**, the messaging to organizational audiences differs structurally from Phase 1. Phase 1 framing: "here is a framework for your security." Phase 2 framing: "here is how to integrate this into your governance and operations." Each sector gets a distinct angle — law schools hear curriculum and clinic integration, think tanks hear research partnership and institutional vocabulary adoption, unions hear steward training and internal communications architecture, NGOs and legal aid organizations hear governance documentation and board-level risk assessment.

**Third**, the public Phase 2 launch is gated on confirmed organizational commitments, not calendar dates. The trigger is 5 organizations at Integration Maturity Level 2 or above (defined below). This creates a credibility foundation before any public announcement and prevents premature signal amplification.

## Section 1: Tier 1 to Tier 2 Transition Matrix

### The Three-Factor Readiness Framework

The scoring system from `tier-1-success-metrics.md` Section 5 assigns each organization a 0–3 readiness score at the Day 28 checkpoint:

- **Factor 1 — Engagement depth** (0 or 1 point): Score 3–5 on the 0–5 engagement scale = 1 point. Confirmed click-through or substantive reply required.
- **Factor 2 — Integration signal** (0 or 1 point): Score 4 or 5 (organization routes the framework internally, requests customization, or confirms deployment) = 1 point.
- **Factor 3 — Network multiplier** (0 or 1 point): National or cross-sector reach, or documented ability to make warm referrals to 10+ organizations = 1 point. Automatic for CLINIC, NILC, NLG, EFF, FPF, IRE, and any national network with 50+ affiliates.

**Invite priority by score**:

| Score | Action | Expected count from 45-org cohort |
|---|---|---|
| 3/3 | Pre-contact before Wave 1 send (personalized, reference their specific engagement) | 3–8 orgs |
| 2/3 | Standard Wave 1 inclusion | 6–12 orgs |
| 1/3 | Conditional — only if specific integration signal emerges post-Day 28 | 5–10 orgs |
| 0/3 | Exclude from Tier 2 targeting | Remainder |

## Section 2: Phase 2 Messaging Strategy — Sector-Specific Angles

The core distinction between Phase 1 and Phase 2 messaging:

- **Phase 1**: "Here is a framework for your organization's personal and operational security."
- **Phase 2**: "Here is how to integrate this framework into your governance, training programs, and institutional operations."

Phase 2 invitations reference the organization's specific Phase 1 engagement. The first sentence of every Phase 2 outreach email names what the organization did — which section they engaged with, what question they asked, what they shared internally. Generic Phase 2 invitations fail. Personalization is not a nicety; it is the mechanism that converts.

### Messaging Angle 1: Law Schools

**Core frame**: Curriculum integration, clinic adoption, and law review engagement.

**Why it lands**: Law schools operate on two tracks — academic research (law review, faculty publications) and applied practice (clinical programs with active caseloads). The framework serves both. For the research track, the primary-source documentation (FOIA-sourced contracts, federal court filings) is citable in legal scholarship on surveillance law, immigration law, and Fourth Amendment jurisprudence. For the clinical track, clinic directors can adopt specific framework sections as intake tools for clients facing immigration enforcement.

**Institutional vocabulary**: "Integration into existing curriculum," "primary-source documentation for supervised legal work," "clinic resource adoption," "law review symposium contribution."

**Template — Law School Clinic Director**:

> Subject: OpSec framework for immigration/civil rights clinic work — follow-up on [their engagement action]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for [specific engagement action from Phase 1 — e.g., "your question about the DROP platform ID requirement" or "routing this to your clinic team"]. That tells me this is relevant to your active caseload, not just background reading.
>
> I want to offer something more specific for your clinic context. The immigration surveillance evasion playbook — which extends the framework you reviewed — is designed to be walked through with clients who are evaluating their exposure to the ELITE targeting system. It requires no technical expertise from the client and includes specific pathways for California residents without government-issued ID.
>
> What institutional adoption would look like for your clinic: adding the Part 0 data broker opt-out checklist as a standing intake item for clients who meet the threat criteria, and having the playbook available as a standing clinic resource alongside your KYR materials. I can deliver a 60–90 minute staff orientation session for your clinic team to walk through the threat model and how to use the playbook with clients.
>
> If your law review has an upcoming symposium on surveillance law, immigration enforcement technology, or Fourth Amendment implications of commercial data purchases, the primary-source documentation in the threat model section is structured specifically to support academic citation. I'd be glad to discuss how that might fit.
>
> Would a staff orientation session be useful? I can work around your clinic schedule.
>

### Messaging Angle 2: Think Tanks

**Core frame**: Research partnership, institutional vocabulary adoption, and policy briefing integration.

**Why it lands**: Think tanks produce the framing language that policymakers use. If a think tank's research program adopts the framework's conceptual vocabulary — "fourth-party data exposure," "commercial data broker as government enforcement infrastructure," "confidence-scored targeting" — those concepts travel into policy debates, congressional briefings, and media framing that the framework itself cannot directly reach.

**Institutional vocabulary**: "Policy research integration," "empirical grounding for policy arguments," "briefing material for congressional staff," "institutional position development."

**Template — Think Tank Program Director**:

> Subject: Research partnership on commercial surveillance policy — following up on [their engagement action]
>

>

The framework's documentation of how deregulated commercial data markets become government enforcement infrastructure — with primary-source procurement documents and court filings behind every claim — provides empirical grounding for policy arguments your team may already be making analytically.

> Specifically, the data broker loophole provision that exempts commercially purchased data from Fourth Amendment protection is documented in enough primary-source depth to support a policy brief, congressional testimony, or a formal position paper. If your team is working on any of the following — data broker regulation, FISA reauthorization, immigration enforcement accountability, or election security infrastructure — the framework extends your existing research base.

Time commitment on your end: a 30-minute briefing and whatever engagement your team finds useful.
>
> Is there a specific policy question your research program is currently working on where this documentation might be useful? That is the most efficient starting point.

### Messaging Angle 3: Unions

**Core frame**: Steward training, internal communications security architecture, and member protection.

**Why it lands**: Unions have two distinct security concerns that the framework addresses. First, member protection — undocumented members or members in high-enforcement-risk categories face direct threat from the ELITE targeting system, and the framework's Part 0 is actionable for them immediately. Second, organizational security — union organizing staff and communications infrastructure are increasingly subject to surveillance, and the framework's communications security and device hardening sections apply directly.

**Institutional vocabulary**: "Steward training program," "member protection resources," "secure internal communications," "organizing department security protocols."

**Template — Union Organizing Director or Communications Security Lead**:

> Subject: Member protection and steward training resources — following up on [their engagement action]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> [Reference specific Phase 1 engagement.] I want to follow up on that specifically because your organizational context is a strong fit for what the framework actually enables.
>
> For unions with members in immigration enforcement risk categories, the framework provides two things your stewards can use immediately: (1) the Part 0 data broker opt-out guide, which walks members through reducing their data exposure to the ELITE targeting system without requiring any technical expertise, and (2) the threat assessment section, which helps stewards understand which members are in highest-risk categories and why.
>
> For your organizing department specifically, the communications security section addresses the operational security of internal communications during organizing campaigns — Signal setup, encrypted file sharing, device hardening for staff and volunteer organizers. This is separate from the member protection material and is designed for the organizing team rather than the general membership.
>
> What institutional integration would look like: a 90-minute steward training session that covers the threat model, walks through Part 0 with the members present, and equips stewards to deliver this to their own shop floor contacts. The training does not require technical expertise from the facilitator; I can deliver it live or provide the materials for your own training team to deliver.
>
> If your federation has a working group on member digital safety, I would like to speak with them about integrating this into the federation's training calendar. Which is the right person to connect with?

### Messaging Angle 4: NGOs and Nonprofit Legal Services

**Core frame**: Governance documentation, board-level risk assessment, and operational security for at-risk programs.

**Why it lands**: NGOs operating in immigration, civil rights, or high-risk advocacy contexts face board-level fiduciary responsibility for organizational security. If an ICE operation compromises client data held by a nonprofit legal services organization, the board bears legal and reputational exposure. The framework provides the documentation infrastructure for a board-level risk assessment — something that can be presented to a board audit committee as evidence that staff have conducted a systematic threat assessment and implemented countermeasures.

**Institutional vocabulary**: "Board-level risk assessment," "organizational governance documentation," "client data protection protocols," "fiduciary duty to at-risk populations."

**Template — NGO Executive Director or Compliance Lead**:

> Subject: Governance documentation for client data protection — following up on [their engagement action]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> [Reference specific Phase 1 engagement.] I want to follow up with something that might be useful at a different level of your organization.
>
> The threat your clients face from the ELITE surveillance system is not only an operational security problem — it is a governance problem. Your board has a fiduciary responsibility to ensure that your organization has conducted a systematic assessment of the surveillance threats to client data and implemented documented countermeasures. If a breach or enforcement action exposes client data, the question a board will ask is: "What did we know about this threat, when did we know it, and what did we do about it?"
>
> The framework provides the documentation architecture for answering that question credibly. Specifically: a board-level risk briefing (30 minutes, no technical expertise required), a written threat assessment that can be attached to board minutes as evidence of due diligence, and a staff implementation guide that documents what countermeasures your team has taken and why.
>
> What organizational adoption would look like: (1) a board risk briefing, (2) a written threat assessment incorporated into your governance documentation, (3) staff implementation of the relevant framework sections, and (4) a designated governance lead who receives quarterly threat updates and can report to the board on implementation status.
>
> I can provide a board briefing slide deck customized for your organization's specific client population and threat profile. Would it be useful to schedule a 30-minute call to understand your organization's current governance documentation and identify where the framework fits?
>
> [Sender]

Enables organizations to self-report adoption status for the framework's aggregate measurement.

**5. Steward Training Module** (2 hours) [Wave 2 delivery, union sector only]
Stand-alone 45-minute training module for union stewards to deliver to their own shop floor contacts without the framework author present. Includes: facilitator guide (no technical expertise required), member handout (Part 0 opt-out steps in plain language, one page), discussion prompts for shop floor conversations. Designed to enable train-the-trainer distribution.

### Response Rate Expectations

**Wave 1 pre-contact (3/3 list)**: 60–75% positive response expected. These organizations are already screened for engagement — the invitation is not cold.

**Wave 1 standard (2/3 list)**: 35–50% positive response expected. Lower because Factor 2 (integration signal) may not have emerged for all of these contacts.

**"Positive response" defined**: Organization agrees to an orientation session OR assigns a named internal contact to evaluate the framework for adoption. A reply expressing interest but no follow-through is not a positive response — it is a conditional response requiring a follow-up call within 5 business days.

**"Confirmed adopter" defined**: An organization is confirmed when it meets at least one of the following: (a) completes the staff orientation session, (b) assigns a named governance lead and adds the framework to a governance document, (c) signs an NDA if the organization requires one before integrating into internal systems, (d) formally commits to a board review within 30 days.

## Section 5: Success Criteria and Phase 2 Public Launch Decision Framework

### Integration Maturity Levels

Organizations are assigned an Integration Maturity Level based on observable adoption behaviors, not self-report alone. Level is tracked in the Organizational Adoption Metrics Template.

**Level 1 — Passive Awareness**
Observable signal: Organization downloaded materials and shared internally (email forward, Slack share, resource list addition). No structured implementation or governance action.
Tracking: Bitly referrer data showing secondary clicks from the organization's IP range or geographic cluster. Self-report from contact ("I shared this with my team").
This level is the Phase 1 output. It is not an organizational adoption. Do not count Level 1 toward Phase 2 public launch criteria.

**Level 2 — Governance Review**
Observable signal: A governance committee, leadership team, or board has formally reviewed the framework. A named individual has been designated to lead implementation or evaluation.
Tracking: Contact confirms governance review via email or call. Organization assigns a named implementation lead (name and role on file).
This level represents the minimum threshold for counting an organization as a Phase 2 adopter. The public launch trigger requires 5 organizations at Level 2 or above.

**Level 3 — Formal Adoption**
Observable signal: (a) Staff orientation session delivered, (b) framework section integrated into an operational workflow (intake checklist, training calendar, governance documentation, resource list), (c) institutional messaging uses framework vocabulary or cites framework documentation in published work.
Tracking: Orientation session confirmed (date, attendee count). Organization self-reports workflow integration on 30-day feedback survey. Monitoring for framework citations in published materials.
This level is the primary target for the six-month window. Three organizations at Level 3 is a strong outcome; five is exceptional.

**Level 4 — Secondary Distribution and Customization**
Observable signal: (a) Organization distributes the framework to secondary audiences without prompting from the sender (members, client networks, affiliated organizations, conference presentations), (b) organization requests customization of framework materials for their specific context, (c) organization serves as a warm referral source for new organizations entering the pipeline.
Tracking: Inbound contacts citing the Level 4 organization as their source. Customization requests received. Published materials that cite the framework and are attributable to the organization's distribution.
This level is the self-sustaining distribution signal from the November 2026 checkpoint. Three or more Level 4 organizations means Phase 2 is operating as designed.

### Metrics That Unlock Phase 2 Public Launch

**Trigger threshold (minimum)**: 5 organizations at Level 2 or above, with at least 2 at Level 3.

**Rationale**: A public Phase 2 launch with fewer than 5 confirmed organizational commitments signals premature announcement. The announcement's credibility depends on being able to say "these organizations have formally reviewed and integrated the framework" — not "we have outreached to these organizations and hope they will."

**Additional conditions before public launch**:
- At least one organization from a direct-service sector (immigration legal aid, mutual aid, or NGO) at Level 3 or above. The framework's value proposition is grounded in use by the populations most at risk. A Phase 2 launch anchored entirely in digital rights or academic organizations is not yet validated for its primary purpose.
- At least one piece of published external documentation (law review note, blog post, policy brief, resource listing) citing the framework, produced by a Tier 2 adopter without direct prompting.
- No outstanding factual errors or methodological critiques from Level 3+ adopters that have not been addressed in a framework update.

**What "public launch" means**: A public-facing announcement describing the organizational adoption program, listing adopting organizations (with their permission), and opening a structured application process for new organizations to join the Tier 2 partnership. This is distinct from the current approach of individual, personalized outreach — the public launch creates a visible institutional program.

**If the threshold is not met by November 2026**: Do not force a public launch. Extend the organizational adoption window for 60 days and investigate whether the bottleneck is in messaging, materials, or organization capacity. The public launch should mark real adoption, not aspirational adoption.

## Section 6: Integration Maturity Roadmap — Tracking and Measurement

### Tracking Infrastructure

**Primary tracking tool**: `engagement-scoring-template.csv` — update columns `tier2_readiness_score` and `tier2_candidate` at the Day 28 checkpoint. Add a separate organizational tracking tab for confirmed Tier 2 partners with columns: org_name, sector, integration_maturity_level, orientation_date, governance_lead, workflow_integrations, last_contact, notes.

**Secondary monitoring**: Google Scholar alerts for framework citations. Monitoring for Gist URL referrers from institutional domains. Quarterly search of Tier 2 partner websites for framework mentions or resource listings.

**30-day feedback survey** (sent to confirmed adopters): Four questions. (1) Which sections has your staff used with clients or members? (2) What friction or gaps did you encounter? (3) Has your organization cited the framework in any published work? (4) Have you referred the framework to another organization? Responses feed directly into the Level assessment and the Phase 2 demand queue.

### Decision Framework for Maturity Escalation

| Current Level | Upgrade Trigger | Required Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 → Level 2 | Organization assigns a named governance lead or schedules a governance review | Email confirmation of assignment or review date |
| Level 2 → Level 3 | Orientation session completed AND workflow integration confirmed | Orientation date logged; self-report of specific integration in 30-day survey |
| Level 3 → Level 4 | Inbound contact cites this organization as source, OR organization publishes framework citation, OR customization request received | Inbound contact log; published URL; customization request in email |

**Downgrade logic**: If an organization at Level 2 has no governance action within 60 days of assignment, downgrade to Level 1 and send a single re-engagement contact. "We noticed there hasn't been movement on the governance review — is there anything I can do to support that?" If no response within two weeks, maintain Level 1 and move to passive monitoring.

### Sector-Specific Outcome Targets (Six-Month Window)

**Law schools**: By November 2026, at least one clinic director has added the framework to an active clinic resource list, confirmed a client walkthrough using the material, or submitted a law review piece citing the framework documentation. One integration is a meaningful outcome given academic institutional timelines.

**Think tanks**: By November 2026, at least one policy brief, congressional testimony, or published research paper cites the framework as a primary source. The test is external publication, not internal use.

**Unions**: By November 2026, at least one steward training session has been delivered using the framework materials, with self-reported attendance count. The steward training module enables this without requiring the framework author to be present.

**NGOs and nonprofit legal services**: By November 2026, at least two organizations have completed the board adoption playbook workflow (board briefing + governance document update + named governance lead). At least three organizations report client walkthroughs of Part 0.

### November 2026 Checkpoint Questions

1. Are three or more Level 2+ organizations generating new inbound contacts via referral or citation?
2. Are any Level 4 organizations training their own affiliates or federation members without direct support from the framework author?
