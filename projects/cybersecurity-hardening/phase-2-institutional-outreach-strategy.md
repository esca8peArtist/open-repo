# Phase 2 Institutional Outreach Strategy: Organizational Leadership Networks

**Bottom line**: Phase 1 reaches 50–60 individuals with the authority to integrate the corpus into their own workflows. Phase 2 reaches the organizational bodies and leadership networks that govern how entire sectors respond to digital security threats — bar associations, AG coalitions, law school associations, journalist membership organizations, and union security committees. The audience shifts from decision-makers-who-can-act-alone to decision-makers-who-move-institutions. Messaging, timing, and success metrics must change accordingly.

**Coordination note**: The existing Tier 2 documentation (TIER2_DISTRIBUTION_PREP.md, tier-2-organizational-strategy.md, TIER_2_DISTRIBUTION_STRATEGY.md) covers outreach to specific organizations in four sectors: digital rights orgs, academic cybersecurity programs, security researcher communities, and journalist organizations. This document covers the institutional network layer above those organizations — the membership bodies, coalitions, and leadership associations that can adopt the corpus as a sector-wide resource rather than a single-organization resource. Do not duplicate Tier 2 individual-organization outreach with this strategy; run them in parallel once Phase 1 gates are met.

## Part 1: Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition Gates

### The Core Transition Question

The move to institutional outreach requires a different kind of evidence than the move from Tier 1 to Tier 2. Organizational leadership networks — AG coalitions, bar associations, journalist membership bodies — will not adopt a resource that has not already demonstrated traction with their member organizations. The transition gate is therefore not just "has Phase 1 worked well enough?" but "do we have at least one named adoption signal within a sector that we can reference when contacting that sector's institutional leadership?"

Without a named adoption signal, institutional outreach becomes a cold pitch from an unknown entity asking a membership organization to recommend a resource none of their members have reviewed. That pitch fails reliably. With one or two named member adoptions — even informal ones — the pitch becomes "organizations your membership already works with are using this; here is how to make it available sector-wide."

### Minimum Gates (All Three Must Be True Before Any Phase 2 Institutional Outreach)

**Gate 1 — Phase 1 volume**: At least 40 of the targeted 50–60 Phase 1 individuals have been contacted, with tracking spreadsheet rows complete for each.

**Gate 2 — Sectoral traction signal**: At least one of the following, specifically from a sector you intend to approach at the institutional level:
- One Score 4–5 (integration or adoption) response from a named Phase 1 organization in that sector
- Three Score 3 (confirmed read) responses from organizations within the same sector
- One published citation or public mention of the corpus by any Phase 1 organization

**Gate 3 — Infrastructure**: Gist publicly accessible, no Gmail deliverability block, Bitly active and tracking. Response templates ready for institutional (slower, more formal) communication cadence.

### Sector-Specific Transition Criteria

Different sectors have different minimum evidence requirements before institutional bodies will engage:

| Sector | Minimum Phase 1 Signal Required | Why |
|---|---|---|
| State AGs / AG coalitions | One AG office acknowledgment (even "received, will review") | AG offices will not recommend to colleagues without prior review by their own staff |
| Law school associations | One clinic director adoption OR one faculty citation | Association membership expects peer validation before association-level endorsement |
| Journalist membership bodies (CPJ, ICFJ, SPJ) | One journalist or journalism-adjacent org engagement | These bodies recommend only resources vetted by working journalists |
| Union security committees | One union education director engagement | Security committee chairs need shop-floor testimony that the resource works |
| Think tank networks | One published citation or mention by any peer organization | Think tanks grant authority based on prior publication/citation track record |

### Phase 1 Checkpoint Timing (Relative to Phase 1 Day 1)

- **Day 20 (May 28 if Phase 1 launches May 8)**: Score all contacts. Identify 3/3 readiness candidates (per PHASE_2_ORGANIZATIONAL_LAUNCH_STRATEGY.md). Generate sectoral traction inventory.
- **Day 28**: Apply sector-specific transition criteria above. Identify which sectors have met the minimum signal threshold for institutional outreach.
- **Day 35**: Begin Phase 2 institutional outreach for any sector that met Day 28 threshold. Sectors without sufficient signal: hold until Day 42 reassessment.
- **Day 42**: Final reassessment. Any sector still below threshold: do not begin institutional outreach for that sector. Wait for Phase 1 follow-up loop (Weeks 4–7) to generate additional signal, then re-evaluate.

### Contingency: Low Response Scenario (<5% Phase 1 Response Rate After Week 2)

Do not launch any Phase 2 institutional outreach if Phase 1 has fewer than 3 responses of any type from 50 sends after 2 full weeks. The institutional outreach message depends on Phase 1 being able to answer "who is already using this?" — if the answer is "no one yet," the institutional pitch collapses.

**Adjustment**: Delay Phase 2 institutional outreach by 4 weeks. Use the additional time to:
1. Diagnose the Phase 1 low-response cause (per PHASE_2_SEQUENCING.md Section 3, Scenario A)
2. Correct the framing issue before it carries forward into institutional messaging
3. Run an adjusted Phase 1 Week 3–4 with corrected subject lines before measuring transition readiness again

### Contingency: High Response Scenario (>25% Phase 1 Response Rate)

If Phase 1 generates 12+ responses or 3+ Score 4–5 contacts by end of Week 2:

1. Begin Phase 2 institutional prep 1 week early (start of Week 3 instead of Week 4)
2. Identify which sectors the high-engagement organizations belong to — these are the sectors with the strongest institutional pitch
3. Draft the institutional outreach emails for those sectors first, incorporating anonymized case study language from the high-engagement contacts
4. Do not launch institutional outreach before Day 28 regardless of response quality — you need the Day 28 scoring to know who to name

## Part 2: Tier 2 Audience Targeting — Institutional Networks

The following 18 organizations represent the institutional network layer across five sectors. Selection criteria: organizational reach (must touch 20+ member organizations or 500+ individual professionals), decision-making authority (must have the organizational authority to recommend resources sector-wide), and sector fit (must work on issues directly connected to the corpus's documented threat model).

### Sector A: State Attorneys General Networks

**Why this sector**: State AGs have civil rights enforcement authority, subpoena power, and public accountability in ways that federal agencies currently do not. Several state AGs are actively litigating against federal immigration enforcement practices. An AG network adoption means the corpus reaches all legal staff in the AG's office, plus creates a policy-citation pathway to legislative hearings and amicus filings.

**What makes "institutional adoption" compelling to this sector**: AG offices adopt resources when they provide primary-source documentation of federal conduct that their own investigators cannot obtain through normal channels. The corpus's FOIA-sourced Palantir ELITE procurement documents and data broker contract terms are exactly this. An AG investigating ICE practices in their state can use these documents as the factual predicate for civil rights enforcement actions. The value proposition is not "here is a guide for immigrants" — it is "here is the primary-source documentation of the federal surveillance system that is operating in your state, which your office can use in enforcement investigations and amicus filings."

**What these organizations specifically want**: Evidence that will survive judicial scrutiny (primary source density), data that supports state-level enforcement actions (especially in states with mini-FISA laws or state privacy statutes), and resources that help them brief non-technical staff attorneys on the technical architecture of the surveillance system they are investigating.

**Target organizations**:

**A-01: National Association of Attorneys General (NAAG)**
- Decision-maker: Executive Director (currently Chris Toth); Policy staff
- Contact: staff@naag.org / naag.org/contact
- Network reach: All 52 state and territory AGs and their offices
- Phase 2 pitch: Present corpus as a technical reference resource for AG offices investigating ICE commercial data purchases in their jurisdictions. NAAG's annual meeting and working group structure means a single adoption creates a distribution channel to all member offices.
- Adoption pathway: NAAG Resource Library listing → AG staff attorney education → Brief or investigation citation

**A-02: Democratic Attorneys General Association (DAGA)**
- Decision-maker: Executive Director; State AG liaisons
- Contact: info@democraticags.com / democraticags.com
- Network reach: 22+ Democratic AGs actively litigating against federal immigration enforcement overreach
- Phase 2 pitch: Several DAGA member AGs have filed amicus briefs on ELITE data practices. The corpus provides the technical documentation layer their legal teams need for the next round of challenges. Frame as a litigation research resource, not advocacy.
- Adoption pathway: AG staff attorney network → amicus brief citation → public AG report

**A-03: State AG Multi-Division Task Force Networks**
This is not a single organization but a pattern of informal multi-state AG coalitions that form around specific enforcement issues. The 32 state AGs who signed the SAFER Banking letter are an example. For the corpus, the relevant coalition is the group of state AGs who have filed joint comments or letters on ICE data broker practices. Target: identify the lead AG on any joint immigration enforcement letter from 2025–2026 and approach their office directly as the coalition anchor contact.
- Decision-maker: Chief Deputy AG or Senior Policy Counsel at the lead state's office
- Adoption pathway: Lead AG office integration → circulation to co-signatory AG offices via existing coalition communication channel

## Part 3: Phase 2 Messaging Templates

### Messaging Shift: Individual to Organizational Leadership

The core message shift from Phase 1 to Phase 2 institutional outreach:

| Dimension | Phase 1 (Individual) | Phase 2 Institutional |
|---|---|---|
| Voice | Peer-to-peer; direct; resource-sharing | Institutional partnership; research collaboration |
| Evidence required | "This exists and is well-sourced" | "Others in your sector are already using this" |
| CTA | "Read Part 0; forward to clients" | "Review for sector-wide adoption; recommend to members" |
| Timeline expectation | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |
| Follow-up cadence | 10-day initial follow-up | 3-week follow-up; monthly thereafter |
| Success signal | Individual engagement; adoption | Membership recommendation; resource library inclusion; training integration |

### Staggered Send Rate

Send 3–4 institutional emails per day maximum during active phases. Institutional recipients (AG offices, professional associations, large union organizations) have more formal intake processes and longer response timelines than individual Phase 1 contacts. A slower send rate (compared to Phase 1's 5/day) is appropriate here because:

1. Each institutional email requires more personalization research (30–45 min per send vs. 8–12 min for Phase 1 contacts)
2. Institutional follow-up cadence is 3 weeks, not 10 days, meaning the response management load per send is higher
3. A deliverability issue with a large institutional domain (e.g., naag.org) could flag your send-from address for every subsequent institutional contact in that domain

### Sector Sequencing Logic

**Wave 1 (Week 6): Sectors with strongest Phase 1 signal**
Contact whichever sector generated the most Phase 1 engagement first. Typically: if immigration legal aid (Tier 1A) generated Score 4–5 contacts, start with AG networks and law school associations, since Phase 1 signal from legal aid organizations is the most compelling credentialing evidence for both. If digital rights organizations (Tier 2 early contacts) generated strong engagement, start with journalist membership bodies and think tanks.

**Wave 2 (Week 7): Second sector priority**
After Wave 1 sends are out, move to the next sector. Do not wait for Wave 1 responses before sending Wave 2 — institutional response timelines are 3–6 weeks, so sequential sector waves would extend the campaign unnecessarily.

**Wave 3 (Weeks 11–14): Slow-signal sectors**
Sectors where Phase 1 generated weak or no signal receive institutional outreach only after Waves 1 and 2 have had time to generate institutional-level engagement that can be referenced. If Wave 1 generates no institutional response by Week 10, reassess messaging and domain framing before Wave 3.

### Contingency Paths

**Low institutional response (<10% acknowledgment rate after 3 weeks)**

Diagnostic steps:
1. Check that emails are reaching correct decision-makers (not communications/fundraising inboxes)
2. Verify that sector-specific Phase 1 signal is accurate — did you actually have adoption in this sector?
3. Test an alternate subject line variant that leads with domain specificity rather than corpus description

Adjustment: pivot from association-level contacts to named individual contacts within member organizations, using the individual outreach templates from TIER2_DISTRIBUTION_PREP.md. Association-level contacts are harder to convert without a warm referral; if cold association outreach is not working, work from the member up to the association.

**High institutional response (>25% acknowledgment rate in first 2 weeks)**

This is a positive signal that the institutional message is landing. Management:
1. Respond to all engagement responses within 48 hours using institutional-appropriate templates (longer, more formal than Phase 1 response templates)
2. Prioritize organizations asking for additional documentation — provide the sector-specific threat briefings as companion materials
3. Update Tier 2 individual outreach emails (TIER2_MESSAGING_TEMPLATES.md) to reference any institutional engagement: "The corpus is currently under review by [organization type, not name] for inclusion in their [resource library / training curriculum]"

**Sector-specific contingency: AG offices request verification of claims**

AG staff attorneys may ask for documentation of specific claims before forwarding to their investigators. This is a high-quality engagement signal, not a problem. Response protocol:
1. Provide the specific FOIA document numbers and court filing citations for the claims they question
2. Offer to create a customized technical summary focused on the enforcement practices in their jurisdiction
3. Do not rush — AG offices move on their own timeline; a 6-week response window is normal
