# Tier 1 Success Metrics Framework

**Lead finding**: The most important number to watch in the first two weeks is not reply rate — it is the ratio of Stage 1+ responses (someone read beyond the subject line) to Stage 0 routing responses ("I'll pass this along"). A campaign with 20% reply rate but 80% Stage 0 routing responses has almost no adoption signal. A campaign with 12% reply rate but 70% Stage 1+ responses is actively converting. Track reply quality before reply quantity.

---

## 1. Metric Hierarchy

The framework uses a three-tier metric hierarchy: primary engagement metrics that are immediately measurable, secondary distribution metrics that emerge over 4–12 weeks, and institutional impact metrics that develop over 6–18 months.

### Tier 1 Metrics: Primary Engagement (Days 1–30)

These metrics are measurable from the tracking spreadsheet alone. They require no external monitoring infrastructure.

| Metric | Definition | Measurement Method |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Send completion rate | % of 12 named organizations contacted within execution window | Tracking spreadsheet: `date_sent` populated |
| Bitly click rate | % of sends where recipient clicked the corpus link | Bitly analytics: clicks / sends |
| Acknowledgment rate | % of sends receiving any reply within 14 days | Tracking spreadsheet: `reply` = Y |
| Stage 1+ response rate | % of replies showing evidence of reading beyond subject line | Tracking spreadsheet: `reply_type` = Positive, Routing, MoreInfo, or Conversion |
| First response latency | Calendar days from send to first reply | `reply_date` minus `date_sent` |
| Referral generation | Net new contacts generated by recipient forwarding | `referral_count` column in tracker |
| Declination rate | % of sends receiving explicit "not for us" responses | `reply_type` = Negative |

**Rationale for reply quality over reply rate**: The existing `post-distribution-impact-tracker.md` notes that nonprofits and mission-driven orgs see 10–16% reply rates on targeted personalized outreach (consistent with data showing nonprofits exceed 16.5% cold email reply rates against a B2B average of 3–5%). The Tier 1 outreach is higher-fit than typical cold outreach — named contacts, specific threat landscape alignment, immediate client relevance. The expected ceiling is 25–35%. Quantity of replies is easy to measure; quality takes a classification step but is the only metric that predicts adoption.

### Tier 2 Metrics: Secondary Distribution (Weeks 2–12)

These metrics require passive monitoring infrastructure (Bitly, Google Alerts, Gist view logs) plus active follow-up tracking.

| Metric | Definition | Measurement Method |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Organic link sharing | Gist URL appearing in contexts not directly from your sends | Google Alerts, Bitly referrer sources |
| Institution-to-network forwarding | Named Tier 1 org sharing corpus to affiliated programs or membership | Reply content or direct referral follow-up |
| Curriculum adoption signal | Any indication corpus entered an org's training or resource toolkit | 30-day follow-up reply; org newsletter search |
| Warm referral count | New organizational contacts generated via "who else should have this?" | 30-day follow-up question 3 response |
| Gist view trajectory | View count growth rate month-over-month | Monthly Gist traffic log |
| Network multiplier | Estimated total reach per Tier 1 contact (using known network sizes) | Contact documentation (NILC: 50+ affiliated programs; CLINIC: 400+ affiliates) |

**Network multiplier note**: The 12 named Tier 1 organizations have vastly different inherent reach. A single successful contact with CLINIC (400+ affiliated programs nationally) represents a fundamentally different distribution outcome than a successful contact with a local mutual aid chapter. Track both absolute engagement and estimated network reach separately. Do not aggregate them — a CLINIC adoption converts as 1 organization but reaches the resource infrastructure of 400.

### Tier 3 Metrics: Institutional Impact (Months 3–18)

These metrics require external monitoring and long reporting lags. Do not measure at the 30-day mark.

| Metric | Definition | Measurement Method | Expected Timing |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Resource list inclusion | Corpus appears in an org's published client resources or practitioner toolkit | Web mention monitoring; org website searches | 2–6 months |
| Policy or training citation | Corpus referenced in a policy document, brief, training curriculum, or amicus filing | Overton.io quarterly search; CourtListener alert | 6–18 months |
| Hiring or intake protocol change | Organization modifies client intake security practices citing the corpus | 180-day case study interview | 6–12 months |
| Media amplification | Journalist or outlet cites corpus following referral from a Tier 1 organization | Google Alert; direct press inquiry | 3–12 months |
| Cited in legal filing | Corpus referenced in court filing, regulatory comment, or legislative testimony | CourtListener PACER search; direct notification | 12–24 months |

## 3. Realistic Success Rate Benchmarks

The task brief provides three precedent timelines from large-scale policy reform adoption. These are long-run structural benchmarks calibrated to legislative or regulatory adoption — not email reply rates. Translate carefully.

### Legislative/Regulatory Adoption Baselines (Long-Run)

| Framework | Introduced | Full Adoption | Rate | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Model Penal Code | 1962 | 36 states by late 1970s | ~2.4 states/year over 15 years | Academic drafting → state legislative adoption wave |
| ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct | 1983 | All 50 states + DC by ~2020 | ~2.5 states/year over 37 years | Professional bar adoption → mandatory ethics binding |
| Brennan Center Automatic Voter Registration | 2015 (OR first) | 24 states + DC by Nov 2025 | ~2.4 states/year over 10 years | Advocacy → state legislative adoption |

**Key translation note**: These precedents measure the rate at which a fully institutionalized reform travels through 50 state-level legislative or regulatory bodies. They are not directly comparable to non-governmental adoption of a security guidance document. The relevant analog is not "how fast does a policy get enacted" but "how fast does a practitioner resource get integrated into field practice by organizations with direct-service delivery roles."

For that analog, the evidence from civil society digital security guidance distribution is thinner, but the Access Now Digital Security Helpline reached 10,000 cases over its first several years of operation, and SAFETAG adoption in the civil society security space shows uptake concentrated in organizations already engaged in security audits — a far narrower deployment path than a resource designed for direct distribution.

### Realistic Short-Run Benchmarks for This Campaign

| Phase | Timeframe | Realistic Target | Strong Performance | Below-Threshold Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| First reply | Days 3–7 | At least 1 reply within 7 days | 3+ replies within 7 days | Zero replies by Day 10 → deliverability or contact issue |
| 14-day acknowledgment rate | Days 1–14 | 25–35% of 12 orgs acknowledge receipt | 45%+ | Below 15% → systemic issue (subject line, spam) |
| Stage 1+ response rate (within replies) | By Day 21 | 50% of replies show Stage 1+ engagement | 70%+ | Below 30% → message not reaching right contact |
| Referral generation by Day 30 | Month 1 | 2–3 new warm contacts | 5+ | Zero referrals by Day 45 → referral prompt missing from follow-up |
| Adoption signal emergence | Weeks 4–12 | 1 documented adoption signal (resource list, forwarding to clients) | 3+ adoption signals | Zero signals at 90 days → consider revised corpus format (PDF, standalone Part 0) |
| Institutional impact citation | Months 6–18 | 1 published citation (policy brief, training curriculum, filing) | 3+ citations | 12-month mark with no citations → reassess Tier 2/3 pipeline |

**Sector-specific response latency expectations** (from cold email research on mission-driven organizations and known sector patterns):

| Sector | Expected Response Window | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Immigration legal aid (1A) | 5–14 days | High volume email, but mission-aligned; named contacts expedite |
| Community-based orgs (1B) | 7–21 days | Smaller staff, higher individual responsiveness, but Signal preferred |
| Mutual aid networks (1C) | 2–10 days for Signal; 7–21 days for email | Security-culture audiences are responsive to technical content |
| Think tanks and digital rights (Tier 2, for comparison) | 7–21 days | Named contacts perform significantly better than generic forms |
| Academic programs (Tier 2, for comparison) | 21–60 days | Semester cycle dependency; email volume high |

## 4. Attribution Methodology

Measuring the framework's marginal contribution to institutional decisions is the hardest measurement problem in this campaign. Three approaches are available, with different confidence levels.

### Attribution Approach 1: Self-Reported Connection (Medium Confidence)

Ask directly in the 90-day follow-up: "Did this corpus contribute to any decisions your organization made in the past three months?" This is not rigorous attribution but it is the only scalable approach. Self-reported connection captures the organization's perception of the corpus's contribution, which is what matters for understanding adoption patterns.

**Confidence limitation**: Confirmation bias. Organizations that implemented guidance and recall reading the corpus will affirm the connection. Organizations that would have implemented similar guidance anyway may still attribute change to the corpus. This over-reports causation. Treat self-reported connection as "consistent with influence" rather than "proof of causation."

### Attribution Approach 2: Differentiation by Timing (Medium Confidence)

If an organization changes a protocol, publishes a resource, or cites relevant material within 60 days of receiving the corpus, and no other major catalyst (enforcement action, media story, internal incident) preceded the change, the timing is consistent with corpus influence. Log the adoption date and search for concurrent external events (ICE enforcement news, ProPublica/Intercept reporting on ELITE, major civil society security incident) that could explain the timing independently.

**Concurrent initiatives that reduce attributable signal**: EFF, ACLU, and Access Now all publish immigration security guidance. Any organization that cites "digital security" resources may have drawn on multiple sources simultaneously. The corpus's unique contribution markers (California DELETE Act DROP platform path, Palantir ELITE primary-source documentation) are distinctive enough that specific reference to either is a strong attribution signal. Generic references to "data broker opt-outs" or "Signal use" are not.

### Attribution Approach 3: Unique Content Markers (Highest Confidence)

The corpus contains several claims and procedural paths that are either unique to it or are primary-source-documented in a way that distinguishes it from generic guidance:

1. The California AB 60/AB 1766 state ID → DELETE Act DROP platform path for undocumented residents without government ID
2. The Palantir ELITE "address confidence score" mechanism documented from specific FOIA and contract sources
3. The verification checkpoint structure in the implementation guide (the practice of confirming a countermeasure actually worked rather than assuming it did)
4. The threat tier structure (Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 graduated response to risk level)

If an organization's published material, follow-up reply, or training content references any of these four elements by name or description, treat this as strong attribution evidence. These are not industry-standard terms from any pre-existing guidance document.

### Attribution Accounting Protocol

For each adoption signal logged in the tracker, complete:
- Unique content marker present? (Y/N/Partial)
- Concurrent external catalyst? (Y/N — if Y, describe)
- Self-reported connection? (Y/N/Not asked)
- Attribution confidence: High (unique marker, no concurrent catalyst) / Medium (timing-consistent, no unique marker) / Low (general topic adoption, multiple possible sources)

Only High confidence attributions should be cited in any Phase 2 proposal or public report. Medium confidence attributions inform internal iteration but not external claims.

## 5. Time-Horizon Expectations

### Short-Term (Weeks 1–4): Engagement Signals

**Primary task**: Confirm the corpus reached real people who can act on it. Success at this stage does not require organizational change — it requires evidence that the right contacts are reading it.

- Week 1: First replies expected from Tier 1A organizations (legal aid). Reply latency from named contacts with direct client relevance should be 5–14 days.
- Week 2: First Bitly click spikes from forwarding (if recipients shared the link before replying). A click trajectory that shows multiple views per organization suggests internal forwarding.
- Week 3: Follow-up wave for non-responders. By this point, non-response is not failure — it is data. Organizations with zero engagement after 21 days require a different approach (see Section 6 of `TIER1_EXECUTION_RUNBOOK.md`).
- Week 4: First adoption signals from the fastest-moving sector. Community-based organizations (Tier 1B) with community education infrastructure may post the corpus to Signal groups or include it in a newsletter before formal engagement begins.

### Medium-Term (Months 2–3): Adoption Signal Emergence

**Primary task**: Observe which organizations are moving from review to implementation. The 30-day follow-up email (per `post-distribution-impact-tracker.md` Section 2) should be sent at this stage.

- Month 2: Organizations that showed Stage 1+ engagement at Week 2–3 may now be in internal circulation (Stage 2). The "following up" email creates a low-friction checkpoint.
- Month 3: The 90-day follow-up produces the first structured feedback. Question 3 ("Is there someone else who should have this?") is the referral activation step. Expect 2–5 new warm contacts from this wave.
- First adoption signal threshold: One organization that modifies a client intake protocol, adds the corpus to a resource page, or references it in a staff training counts as the adoption signal gate. If this has not occurred by Day 90, the Tier 2 launch should wait until after the 90-day follow-up results are analyzed.

### Long-Term (Months 6–18): Measurable Policy and Practice Adoption

**Primary task**: Document and preserve evidence of institutional practice change, policy citation, and community reach.

- Month 6 (90-day reassessment gate): Organizations that showed Stage 2+ adoption may now be completing Stage 3 (partial implementation). Case study interview window opens for any Stage 3+ organizations.
- Month 12: Tier 3 institutional impact metrics become meaningful. If the corpus was distributed to academic and legal networks via Tier 2, the 12-month Google Scholar and Overton searches may produce first results.
- Month 18: The full institutional impact picture is visible. ABA Model Rules analogy suggests that practitioner resources that achieve credibility in legal aid networks tend to persist for years in field practice long after the originator's active outreach ends.

**Calibrated expectation against legislative benchmarks**: The Model Penal Code / ABA Model Rules / Brennan AVR adoption rates (2.4–2.5 units/year) measure state-by-state legislative enactment — a fundamentally more resource-intensive and politically contested adoption pathway than organizational policy adoption. For this corpus, the equivalent of "state adoption" is "organization integrates the corpus into sustained practice." Given the population of 12 named Tier 1 contacts plus regional expansion contacts, achieving 3–5 documented sustained adoptions within 18 months is the realistic analog to the 2–3 state/year legislative adoption benchmark. This is not a discouraging comparison — it reflects that non-governmental organizational adoption, when the resource is directly applicable to active operations, proceeds faster than legislative reform cycles.

## 6. Phase 2 Feedback Integration

Tier 1 metrics directly inform Phase 2 (Tier 2 distribution) in three ways:

**Message framing update**: If the 30-day follow-up data shows Tier 1 organizations engage primarily with Part 0 (data broker opt-outs) rather than the technical countermeasures, lead the Tier 2 digital rights outreach with the threat model angle rather than the implementation guide.

**Format update**: If Tier 1 feedback shows PDFs are more useful than the Gist URL (common for organizations that need to share resources internally without reliable internet access), produce a PDF before Tier 2 sends. Digital rights organizations are more likely to circulate a linked PDF.

**Contact list expansion**: If Question 3 ("Who else should have this?") referrals from Tier 1 repeatedly point to an organization type not on the Tier 2 list (domestic violence shelter networks serving mixed-status families, labor union legal departments, university pro bono clinics), add those categories to the Tier 2 target list before the next wave.

The adoption signals from Tier 1 also function as social proof for Tier 2 outreach. "NILC shared this with their affiliated legal programs" (if accurate and permissioned) is a stronger opening for a Tier 2 email to the EFF or CDT than a cold introduction.
