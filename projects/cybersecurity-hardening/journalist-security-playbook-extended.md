# Journalist Security Playbook — Extended Edition

**This playbook is not legal advice. No portion substitutes for counsel to a news organization or individual journalist. The legal landscape — shield law scope, Section 702 authorization status, DOJ media subpoena policy — is in active flux as of May 2026. All legal references require verification with qualified press freedom counsel before reliance.**

## Before You Read: The Single Most Important Update Since the Base Playbook

The base `journalist-security-playbook.md` was written before two developments that materially change the journalist threat model:

**First: ICE confirmed on April 1, 2026 that it is actively using Paragon Graphite zero-click spyware.** Graphite is a device-level compromise tool that reads Signal, WhatsApp, and Telegram messages after decryption — it bypasses end-to-end encryption by compromising the endpoint. WhatsApp identified approximately 90 journalists and civil society members targeted with Graphite in early 2025. The Citizen Lab confirmed two Italian journalists at Fanpage.it were infected through an iMessage zero-click exploit (CVE-2025-43200, patched in iOS 18.3.1). ICE's confirmed deployment means a U.S. immigration enforcement agency now operates a tool designed to defeat the encrypted communications architecture this entire corpus is built around.

**Second: On January 15, 2026, FBI agents executed a search warrant at Washington Post reporter Hannah Natanson's home** — the first time in U.S. history a reporter's home was searched in a national security leak investigation. Agents seized her phone, two laptops, a Garmin watch, a portable hard drive, and a recording device. Attorney General Bondi had rescinded Biden-era protections against journalist subpoenas in April 2025. Courts have since blocked the DOJ from searching the seized devices directly, with a magistrate judge ordering independent review — but the seizure happened first.

These two developments mean the threat model has moved from theoretical to operational: domestic enforcement agencies are now deploying spyware capable of reading encrypted communications, and the DOJ has demonstrated willingness to physically search a reporter's home. The countermeasures in this playbook are designed for this environment.

## Part 1: The 2026 Journalist Threat Model — What Has Changed

### 1.1 The Pre-Contact Data Problem: Sources Are Already in the System

The conventional source protection model focuses on securing the communication channel: use Signal, use SecureDrop, verify safety numbers. This remains correct but is now insufficient. The threat model for 2026 has a pre-contact layer that this framework addresses for the first time.

ICE's Palantir ELITE system builds "address confidence scores" for targeting by purchasing location data from smartphone app SDK networks. This is commercial data generated by apps on a source's phone — location pings that the app sold to a data broker that sold to a government contractor — before the source ever contacted a journalist. A source who follows every security protocol a journalist recommends may still be locatable through months of pre-contact commercial data.

The implication for journalists: **source protection now includes sharing data broker opt-out guidance at the initial contact point.** The California DELETE Act's DROP platform provides a pathway for individuals without government-issued ID. For sources in non-California states, privacy-focused tools (Optery, DeleteMe) address a meaningful subset of the broker ecosystem. This is not optional housekeeping — for a source who is already in a Palantir targeting queue, the commercial location data is the exposure vector, not the Signal conversation.

See the companion `implementation-guide.md` Part 0 and `opsec-playbook.md` Part 1 for the full data broker opt-out protocol to share with sources.

Sources: [ICE acknowledges it is using powerful spyware — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/07/nx-s1-5776799/ice-spyware-privacy), [ICE Just Admitted It Uses Spyware That Reads Encrypted Messages — State of Surveillance](https://stateofsurveillance.org/news/draft-ice-confirms-graphite-spyware-active-use-congressional-letter-2026/)

### 1.2 Zero-Click Spyware: The Endpoint Compromise Threat

Paragon Graphite is the specific zero-click spyware now confirmed in U.S. government (ICE) deployment. Its architecture is important to understand because it changes the countermeasure calculus:

- Graphite exploits zero-click vulnerabilities — attack vectors that require no action by the target. The Fanpage.it journalists were infected through an iMessage zero-click (CVE-2025-43200) while running iOS 18.2.1. They received no message they could avoid opening.
- Once installed, Graphite reads messages **after decryption on the device** — it operates at the OS layer below the encryption layer. This means it can extract Signal, WhatsApp, Telegram, and iMessage content from devices where those apps are properly configured and have verified safety numbers. Encryption protects the transit channel; Graphite attacks the device endpoint.
- The zero-click exploit used against the Fanpage journalists was patched in iOS 18.3.1 (February 2025). New zero-clicks are discovered continuously — Graphite's operator model depends on maintaining an exploit inventory.

**What this changes operationally:**

First, keeping devices updated is now a first-line security measure against zero-click exploitation, not a routine maintenance task. iOS updates and GrapheneOS updates that patch kernel and messaging vulnerabilities close zero-click windows. Delayed updates extend exposure windows. This should be an organizational policy, not an individual choice.

Second, Apple's Lockdown Mode significantly reduces the zero-click attack surface for iPhones. Lockdown Mode disables iMessage link previews, blocks most message attachment types, disables JIT JavaScript, and restricts several features that zero-click exploits use as entry points. For journalists under active threat or covering high-sensitivity beats, enabling Lockdown Mode is a meaningful countermeasure against the class of attacks Graphite uses — not a guarantee, but a documented reduction in attack surface.

Third, GrapheneOS's security architecture (hardware memory tagging on Pixel 8+, hardened memory allocator, kernel lockdown) makes it meaningfully harder to maintain persistent spyware after initial compromise. A device that reboots regularly (the 18-hour auto-reboot setting recommended in the base playbook) and runs GrapheneOS on Pixel 8 hardware is substantially harder to maintain persistent zero-click spyware on than a standard iOS or Android device.

Fourth, **the three-device architecture matters most in the zero-click environment.** If a journalist's personal phone is compromised by Graphite, a dedicated source communication device that never appears at the same location (and is thus not a targeting priority for the same operator) is not compromised. The compartmentalization that was previously justified by metadata separation is now also justified by the zero-click threat.

Sources: [Graphite Caught: First Forensic Confirmation — Citizen Lab](https://citizenlab.ca/research/first-forensic-confirmation-of-paragons-ios-mercenary-spyware-finds-journalists-targeted/), [Paragon Graphite Spyware — SecurityWeek](https://www.securityweek.com/paragon-graphite-spyware-linked-to-zero-click-hacks-on-newest-iphones/), [Access Now: U.S. Has Reactivated Its Paragon Contract](https://www.accessnow.org/paragon-contract-spyware/)

### 1.3 DOJ Bondi Policy: The Legal Framework Has Reversed

In April 2025, Attorney General Pam Bondi rescinded the Biden-era DOJ media guidelines that prohibited the use of compulsory process against journalists except under limited, specified circumstances. The new policy permits the DOJ to seek subpoenas, court orders, and search warrants for journalist records and testimony in leak investigations. Advance notice to the journalist is required, and actions must be approved by DOJ leadership — but these are procedural constraints, not substantive prohibitions.

The practical consequence was demonstrated in January 2026: FBI agents searched Hannah Natanson's home pursuant to a search warrant in a leak investigation targeting a government contractor who had communicated with her. Natanson was not the target of the investigation — her devices were seized because they contained communications with the person who was. Courts have blocked the DOJ from directly searching the seized devices, with Magistrate Judge William Porter conducting an independent review. The litigation continues as of May 2026.

**What this means for the journalist threat model:**

The seizure of a journalist's devices now requires only a judge-issued search warrant, not the higher bar previously required by DOJ policy. The first-ever home search of a reporter in a national security context has been executed. Natanson's devices were seized regardless of the ongoing court fight about what DOJ can subsequently do with them — the chilling effect operates independent of the litigation outcome.

The countermeasure architecture is unchanged but its urgency is elevated: the scenario that was theoretical in prior guidance has occurred. Devices that contain source communications when seized yield those communications unless they are encrypted such that the journalist cannot be compelled to produce the key (which is contested legal ground) or unless the communications never existed on the device.

The operational implication: disappearing messages on the dedicated source communication device are not just a privacy preference — they are a functional countermeasure against device seizure. A device seized from a journalist on which source communications have already expired yields nothing about those specific source relationships.

Sources: [Justice Department revokes Biden-era protections — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/04/25/nx-s1-5377624/pam-bondi-reporters-subpoena-leaks), [Washington Post demands return of devices — CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/21/media/washington-post-hannah-natanson-fbi-doj-devices), [RCFP Analysis: DOJ Rescinds News Media Guidelines](https://www.rcfp.org/doj-rescinds-news-media-guidelines-analysis/)

### 1.4 Visa and Residency Threats to Journalist Sources and Foreign Correspondents

The visa threat to journalists themselves — and to journalist sources — has operationalized in 2025–2026 in ways that the base playbook did not address:

**Foreign journalists in the U.S.:** In August 2025, DHS proposed a rule that would significantly restrict the duration of I-visa (media visa) stays, potentially forcing hundreds of foreign journalists to leave the United States. RSF and 17 press freedom organizations filed formal opposition. The proposal is not yet final as of May 2026, but it represents the specific threat vector: foreign-based journalists covering the U.S. who receive government or institutional funding face visa revocation risk as a leverage mechanism.

**RFA, VOA, and RFE/RL journalists:** Following the termination of USAGM grants in 2025, approximately 30 Radio Free Asia employees faced potential visa revocation. Journalists working for organizations that received now-terminated federal funding are in a documented legal limbo regarding their I-visa status.

**Source protection in a visa-threat environment:** A source who is a visa holder faces adverse action risk that extends beyond criminal prosecution — visa revocation, detainer, and removal are administrative actions, not criminal prosecutions, and they operate on a lower evidentiary threshold. A source who has communicated with a journalist about government misconduct and is also a non-citizen has an adversarial action risk profile that requires the journalist to treat source protection as protecting against administrative as well as criminal consequences. The standard Signal + SecureDrop architecture addresses criminal prosecution risk; it must also be evaluated for administrative action risk, which is driven more by commercial data and Palantir ELITE targeting than by communications intelligence.

Sources: [RSF: Proposed journalist visa restrictions — catastrophic consequences](https://rsf.org/en/usa-proposed-journalist-visa-restrictions-would-have-catastrophic-consequences-press-freedom), [ICIJ: Trump cuts threaten RFA reporters](https://www.icij.org/news/2025/03/unable-to-stay-in-the-country-unable-to-return-home-reporters-face-grim-prospects-after-latest-trump-cuts/), [CPJ Safety Advisory: Traveling to the US](https://cpj.org/2025/04/cpj-safety-advisory-traveling-to-the-us/)

## Part 2: Device Security for Source Contact — Extended Protocol

### 2.1 The Three-Device Architecture: Why Compartmentalization Is Not Excessive

The three-device architecture — personal device, professional device, dedicated source communication device — is a common question in security training: "Is this really necessary?" In the 2026 threat environment, the answer is yes, and the reasons are now specific rather than precautionary.

**Personal device:** Carries your personal identity, carrier metadata, location history, personal contacts, financial apps, and social media. Subject to NSL via your carrier; subject to CBP search at borders; subject to subpoena as a personal communication device. Appropriate for personal communications, published research, public social media.

**Professional device:** Organizational-issued or organizational-managed device. Subject to organizational IT oversight, discoverable in civil litigation against the organization, and in some jurisdictions subject to organizational legal compliance obligations that differ from personal device protections. Appropriate for published work, organizational email, public-record newsgathering. **Not appropriate for source communications** — organizational IT management is a legal discovery vector.

**Dedicated source device:** GrapheneOS on Pixel hardware, registered with a VoIP number unlinked to your carrier identity, used exclusively for source communications. Never co-located with personal device. Never brought to your home or office (both are documented in carrier location history and would create an entity-resolution link). This device's metadata trace should not be linkable to your personal identity.

The three-device architecture is not paranoia. It is the minimum separation required to ensure that CBP border seizure of your travel device does not yield source communications, that organizational IT management does not yield source communications, and that device forensics on any single device does not yield the complete source relationship picture.

### 2.2 GrapheneOS Setup: Production Configuration Checklist

Beyond the base playbook's summary, here is the production configuration sequence for the dedicated source device:

**Hardware selection:** Pixel 8 or Pixel 8 Pro is the current recommended platform (hardware memory tagging via ARM MTE, available from the $499 Pixel 8 base model). Pixel 7 is acceptable. Do not use Pixel 6a or below — hardware memory tagging is absent, which limits one of GrapheneOS's strongest defenses against zero-click memory corruption exploits.

**Installation:** grapheneos.org/install — follow the web installer method; it is straightforward and does not require command-line tools. Verify the installation's cryptographic integrity via the checksum on the download page before flashing.

**Post-installation configuration:**

1. Network: Do not log into any Google account. Under Settings > Network & Internet > Private DNS, set to "dns.quad9.net" (Quad9 is a privacy-respecting DNS resolver that blocks known malicious domains without logging queries by IP).

2. Auto-reboot: Settings > Security > Auto-reboot Timer > 18 hours. This returns the device to Before First Unlock (BFU) state after 18 hours without active use. In BFU state, the full-disk encryption key is not loaded in memory, and Cellebrite UFED extractions are severely limited to unencrypted metadata.

3. PIN/passphrase: Use a 6-digit PIN minimum; a 10-digit PIN or alphanumeric passphrase is strongly preferred. The Fifth Amendment's protection of memorized passcodes is more robust than fingerprint or face unlock in the current legal landscape — a court can compel your finger, but compelled production of a memorized passphrase is contested.

4. App sandbox: GrapheneOS isolates each app in a separate sandbox by default. Install Signal from the GrapheneOS App Store (which mirrors the official Signal APK with verified signatures) rather than side-loading.

5. Microphone and camera indicators: GrapheneOS shows hardware-level indicators when the microphone or camera are actively accessed — this is a real-time alert to any app (including a potential Graphite implant) activating sensors without your knowledge.

6. Network permission: GrapheneOS allows per-app network permission controls. For Signal specifically, network access is required. For any other app on the dedicated device, deny network access by default — the dedicated device should run Signal and nothing else.

**Phone number:** Purchase a MySudo number (getsudo.com) or JMP.chat number on a different device, on a different network, with a payment method not linked to your primary identity. Register Signal with this number. The registration process is straightforward: install Signal, enter the number, enter the SMS/voice verification code.

### 2.3 Signal Configuration: The Full Production Setup

Beyond the base playbook, these additional Signal settings are specifically relevant for the 2026 threat environment:

**Note-to-Self sync:** If enabled, Signal syncs messages across linked devices. The dedicated source communication device should have Signal registered on one device only — not linked to Signal Desktop. Linking to a desktop installation means a subpoena to your laptop yields the same messages as a subpoena to the dedicated phone.

**Incognito keyboard:** Settings > Privacy > Advanced > Incognito Keyboard: On. This prevents the system keyboard from learning and potentially transmitting typing patterns from Signal conversations.

**Screen security:** Settings > Privacy > Screen Security: On. This prevents Signal from appearing in the device's app switcher (the preview visible when you switch between apps), which matters if the device is ever briefly visible to someone else's camera.

**Call relay:** Settings > Privacy > Advanced > Always Relay Calls: On. This routes Signal calls through Signal's servers rather than establishing a direct connection between your device and the caller's device. Without this, a caller can potentially learn your IP address from the direct WebRTC connection. With it, Signal's server is the relay — the caller sees Signal's server IP, not yours.

**Sealed sender:** Signal uses sealed sender by default in current versions. This hides the sender's identity from Signal's servers when delivering messages — the server can decrypt the envelope to find the recipient but not the sender. Ensure you are running a current version (check signal.org for the current release; GrapheneOS App Store updates should be checked weekly).

**Disappearing messages default:** For the dedicated source device, set the global default to 7 days via Settings > Privacy > Default Timer. For individual source conversations where you assess higher risk, reduce to 24 hours or 1 hour within the specific conversation. The timer runs from when each message is sent, not from a fixed clock — conversations that go quiet for weeks will retain old messages until the timer expires from their send date.

## Part 3: Source Encryption and Anonymization — Full Architecture

### 3.1 SecureDrop vs. Custom Intake: Decision Framework

The base playbook covers SecureDrop's architecture and deployment path. This section addresses the decision framework for news organizations evaluating whether SecureDrop is the right intake tool, and for individual journalists who cannot operate organizational SecureDrop.

**SecureDrop is the correct answer for:**
- News organizations with dedicated IT staff or ability to contract setup support from FPF
- Organizations that receive regular document submissions from confidential sources
- Organizations where multiple journalists work with the same source intake infrastructure
- Organizations that need the strongest possible defense against server-side legal process (SecureDrop's air-gapped workstation design means a warrant to the Application Server yields only GPG-encrypted data that the server cannot decrypt)

**OnionShare is the correct alternative for:**
- Individual journalists or small outlets that cannot maintain SecureDrop infrastructure
- Single-story intake where a persistent installation is not justified
- Sources who need a one-time secure submission channel for a specific document

OnionShare (onionshare.org) creates a temporary Tor onion service directly from the journalist's computer. The journalist generates an onion address, provides it to the source, and the source uploads documents through Tor Browser. There is no central server — the journalist's computer is the server, running only while the sharing session is active. After the session ends, the onion address is gone. The trade-off: OnionShare requires the journalist's computer to be online during the upload window, which creates a timing correlation risk (an adversary who knows the journalist is receiving a document can correlate that window with the source's network activity). For the highest-sensitivity ongoing source relationships, SecureDrop's asynchronous design is superior.

**The Tor hidden service intake option for custom deployments:**
Smaller news organizations that want SecureDrop's anonymity properties without the full infrastructure can deploy a minimal onion service intake form using GlobaLeaks (globaleaks.org), a FOSS whistleblowing platform that is lighter to deploy than SecureDrop and supports Tor onion service access by default. GlobaLeaks lacks SecureDrop's air-gapped journalist workstation architecture but is meaningfully more anonymous than a clearnet submission form or standard email.

The critical requirement for any intake channel: **communicate the intake address to potential sources through verified channels.** A source who reaches your intake through a search engine result may be reaching a spoofed lookalike site. The FPF directory (securedrop.org/directory) is the authoritative source for SecureDrop addresses. For OnionShare and custom onion service deployments, the onion address should be published on your organization's HTTPS website (not just in a third-party directory) and verifiable via your organization's official public identity.

Sources: [SecureDrop Documentation](https://docs.securedrop.org/en/stable/what_is_securedrop.html), [Tor at the Heart: SecureDrop — Tor Project Blog](https://blog.torproject.org/tor-heart-securedrop/), [FPF: SecureDrop Priority Support](https://securedrop.org/priority-support/)

### 3.2 Off-Grid Data Transfer: Physical Isolation for the Most Sensitive Documents

For documents of exceptional sensitivity — particularly documents that have arrived via SecureDrop and must be processed by a journalist — the correct handling environment is air-gapped. This is not theory; it is what the most security-conscious newsrooms already do.

**The workflow:**

1. **Receipt on the air-gapped SecureDrop Workstation:** The SecureDrop Workstation runs Qubes OS and Tails on a machine that is never connected to the newsroom network or the internet. Journalist reads and downloads the document on this machine.

2. **Transfer via encrypted USB to processing environment:** If the document must be moved from the air-gapped workstation to a working environment, transfer via a USB drive that is used exclusively for this purpose. The USB drive should be wiped before and after each transfer. Consider using VeraCrypt to create an encrypted container on the USB for the transfer.

3. **Document processing in Tails OS:** Open and work with the document inside Tails OS (tails.net), booted from a separate USB drive on a machine that is not connected to the internet. Tails routes all internet traffic through Tor and leaves no trace on the host computer after shutdown. Documents opened in Tails are processed in an environment that generates no OS-level logs, no browser history, and no cached files that persist after the session ends.

4. **Metadata scrubbing before documents enter the working environment:** Use mat2 (available in Tails) to strip metadata from documents before they enter your normal workflow. `mat2 document.pdf` — this removes author name, creation date, modification date, revision history, organization, device identifier, and software version from the file. A document submitted by a source and forwarded to an editor with metadata intact can expose the source's identity through the document properties.

5. **Physical security for the air-gapped machine:** The air-gapped machine should be stored in a physically secure location. It should never be connected to any network — not Wi-Fi, not Ethernet, not Bluetooth. If the machine needs an OS update, the update process must be conducted offline (Tails self-updates from the Tails USB itself).

**For individual journalists without access to organizational SecureDrop infrastructure:** A personal Tails OS installation provides the document handling environment. The receipt channel is OnionShare or direct source delivery. The trade-off is that you are operating the intake infrastructure yourself, without FPF's operational security review — the architecture is sound but the implementation is your responsibility.

### 3.3 Hardware Security Keys for Account Protection

The base playbook and the `journalist-implementation-guide.md` recommend YubiKey hardware security keys for high-value accounts. The May 2026 threat update in `tier2-journalists-threat-briefing.md` upgrades this recommendation from "recommended" to mandatory for journalists whose account compromise would expose source information.

**Why hardware keys in 2026:**

Voice cloning (documented in the threat briefing) defeats voice-based verification. Deepfake video (ProKYC at $629/year as documented in Part I of the threat briefing) defeats video-based verification. SMS-based 2FA is defeated by SIM-swapping (a known attack against journalists). TOTP authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Authy) are defeated by real-time phishing proxies that relay your TOTP code to the attacker before it expires.

Hardware security keys using FIDO2/WebAuthn cannot be phished. The key authenticates to the exact domain it was registered on — a phishing proxy that redirects you from proton.me to pr0ton.me receives a response that the hardware key refuses to generate because the domain does not match the registered origin. This is a cryptographic property, not a policy or software check.

**Setup protocol:**

1. Purchase two hardware security keys — a primary (carried daily, YubiKey 5C NFC at approximately $55) and a backup (stored in a physically secure location, same model). Never carry both at the same time.

2. Register both keys on every high-value account: ProtonMail, Signal account registration lock, your encrypted cloud storage (ProtonDrive), your password manager (1Password, KeePassXC with FIDO2 support), and organizational accounts.

3. For accounts that do not support FIDO2: use a TOTP authenticator (Ente Auth or Raivo OTP — open source, locally stored, no cloud sync) as a second factor. The YubiKey 5 series also supports TOTP generation directly on the hardware key (using Yubico Authenticator), which is preferable to a phone-based authenticator because the TOTP codes are generated on a hardware device that is not itself a smartphone attack surface.

4. Store the backup key's registration in your news organization's physical security infrastructure — not in a cloud document or email — because the backup key is the recovery mechanism if the primary is lost.

Sources: [FPF 2026 Journalist Digital Security Checklist](https://freedom.press/digisec/blog/journalists-digital-security-checklist/), [Yubico Best Practices PDF](https://docs.yubico.com/hardware/yubikey-guidance/best-practices/webdocs.pdf), [FIDO2 Hardware Security Keys 2026 — Corbado](https://www.corbado.com/blog/best-fido2-hardware-security-keys)

## Part 4: Travel Security for International Reporting

### 4.1 Border Device Seizures: The Complete Protocol

The base playbook covers the core travel device protocol. This section extends it with the complete implementation checklist and the specific risk profile for international correspondents.

**The 72-hour rule:** A device that has been unlocked (in After First Unlock / AFU state) within the preceding 72 hours is substantially more vulnerable to Cellebrite UFED extraction than one that has been powered off for a period that exceeds the iOS Secure Enclave's key derivation protection. Powering off the device (not just screen-locking or airplane mode) before reaching the border checkpoint is the minimum required. For maximum protection, power off and leave off for at least an hour before the crossing.

**For returning international correspondents with source material from the field:** The correct protocol is to complete data transfer of field materials to an encrypted cloud storage account (ProtonDrive, accessed from the field after clearing foreign customs) before the return crossing. The travel device that crosses back into the U.S. should contain no field materials — they were already transmitted to cloud storage encrypted with your key. CBP's advanced search of a device with no source materials yields nothing. Cloud storage accessed from a different device after clearing U.S. customs is not within CBP's border search authority.

**If your device is seized at the border:**

1. Request Form 6051D — this is the CBP Receipt for Detained/Seized Property. Agents are required to provide it.
2. Do not voluntarily unlock. You may be detained and the device may be seized. Neither carries criminal penalties for the refusal itself in most circumstances — seizure and detention are administrative actions, not arrests.
3. If the device is retained for a "forensic hold" and returned: treat it as compromised. Factory-reset before returning it to any use that touches source communications. The forensic hold process may have extracted data, installed monitoring software, or both.
4. Contact RCFP legal hotline (1-800-336-4243) or your organization's press freedom counsel immediately after any border device seizure or search.

**The Journalist Assistance Network:** CPJ and four other press freedom organizations launched the Journalist Assistance Network in May 2025, providing free legal, safety, and immigration resources for journalists facing government-related threats. This is a resource the base playbook did not have available — it is a multi-organization clearinghouse for journalists facing exactly the kinds of threats this playbook addresses. cpj.org/journalist-assistance-network.

**Country-specific risk assessment:** CBP's U.S. border authority is the relevant threat on entry to the United States. For international trips, the equivalent threat profile varies by destination country. The CPJ Journalist Safety Index (cpj.org) provides country-specific safety assessments. For high-risk country assignments, consult CPJ's country-specific guides before travel. The Committee to Protect Journalists and RSF both publish destination-specific safety guidance that should be part of any international assignment briefing.

Sources: [EFF Journalist Security Checklist for Border Travel](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/06/journalist-security-checklist-preparing-devices-travel-through-us-border), [CPJ Safety Advisory: Traveling to the US (April 2025)](https://cpj.org/2025/04/cpj-safety-advisory-traveling-to-the-us/), [EFF to Third Circuit: CBP Needs a Warrant — State of Surveillance](https://stateofsurveillance.org/news/eff-third-circuit-border-device-search-warrant-2026/)

### 4.2 Hotel Network Compromise and Field Network Security

Hotel networks are a documented threat vector for corporate espionage and government intelligence collection. For journalists covering international beats, hotel Wi-Fi is an active threat environment:

**The hotel network threat profile:** Hotel networks in jurisdictions with aggressive press freedom environments are frequently monitored at the network level. Network-level interception captures unencrypted traffic, DNS queries (which reveal which sites you visit even if you use HTTPS), and in some configurations performs TLS interception with hotel-issued certificates. For journalists operating in China, Russia, the Gulf states, or other jurisdictions with documented journalist surveillance programs, assume the hotel network is monitored.

**Countermeasures:**

1. **VPN before any hotel network use:** Connect to Mullvad VPN (mullvad.net — $5/month, accepts cash, no-log policy verified in court proceedings) before any network activity on hotel Wi-Fi. The VPN encrypts all traffic between your device and the Mullvad server before it touches the hotel network. The hotel network sees only encrypted traffic to a VPN endpoint.

2. **Cellular data over Wi-Fi for sensitive work:** For the dedicated source communication device, use cellular data rather than Wi-Fi for all Signal communications. Hotel Wi-Fi is a shared network; your cellular connection is a separate SIM channel. In jurisdictions where both are monitored, the cellular channel is harder to monitor at the packet level without carrier-level access.

3. **Local SIM strategy for high-risk assignments:** Purchase a local prepaid SIM in cash for data connectivity during the reporting trip. This provides: (a) a data channel not linked to your U.S. carrier identity; (b) an IP address associated with a local carrier rather than your personal carrier; (c) a phone number that can receive Signal verification for a temporary field Signal registration. After the trip, deregister Signal from the local SIM before returning to the U.S., and re-register on your usual channel after crossing.

4. **Do not use hotel business centers:** Hotel business center computers are shared, potentially keystroke-logged, and have unknown security configurations. Never access source communications, encrypted email, or organizational systems from a hotel business center computer.

## Part 5: Organizational Protocols

### 5.1 Coordinating With Legal Counsel Pre-Publication

The base playbook addresses the subpoena response protocol. This section addresses the pre-publication legal coordination that reduces the likelihood a subpoena yields source-identifying materials.

**The pre-publication privilege review:** For investigations where the news organization anticipates a government response (classified information, ongoing criminal investigations, immigration enforcement operations), a pre-publication legal review with press freedom counsel should address:

1. **What materials contain source-identifying information?** Draft documents, source communications, research notes, and source-provided documents should be inventoried. The lawyer needs to know what exists before advising on how to protect it.

2. **Which state shield law applies?** Shield law protection depends on the jurisdiction of the publication and, in some cases, the jurisdiction where newsgathering occurred. RCFP's state-by-state shield law guide (rcfp.org/reporters-privilege/) is the reference — but the specific application to a specific investigation requires counsel review, not a guide.

3. **What materials can be destroyed lawfully before publication?** Journalists who complete an investigation are not legally required to retain draft notes, source-identifying materials, or intermediate research documents beyond what they need for their editorial process. Materials destroyed before a subpoena is served are not subject to the subpoena. The ethical obligation to sources can be served by destroying source-identifying data after publication — this requires a specific decision, not a general assumption that notes should be retained indefinitely.

4. **What is the publication's response protocol if a pre-publication subpoena is attempted?** In some leak investigations, the DOJ has sought prior restraint orders or emergency subpoenas before publication. The news organization's counsel should have a response brief template ready.

**Attorney-client privilege for these communications:** The pre-publication legal review should occur on Signal with verified safety numbers or in person. Email communications with counsel (even privileged communications) are on PRISM-reachable servers if the lawyer uses Gmail or similar. Attorney-client privilege protects the content from compelled production in a proceeding; it does not prevent PRISM collection of the communications before any proceeding begins.

### 5.2 Backup Protocols for Seized Devices

The Hannah Natanson case is the template: when a device is seized, the journalist loses access to everything on that device — contacts, documents, ongoing communications. For an investigative journalist, a seized device can represent months of work and ongoing source relationships.

**The backup architecture:**

1. **Investigation documents:** All investigation documents should exist in encrypted cloud storage (ProtonDrive) accessible from a second device. If the primary device is seized, you can access your documents from a different device after clearing any custody event. The backup is not a convenience — it is a business continuity measure.

2. **Source contacts:** Source contacts on the dedicated source communication device should not exist in a single-point form. Consider maintaining a secure, encrypted offline record (KeePassXC database on an encrypted USB drive stored physically secure) of active source contacts and their preferred communication channels. This record should not be on the device that is used for source communications — it is a backup that allows you to reestablish contact from a different device if the source device is seized.

3. **Published article records:** Maintaining a personal encrypted archive of published work — not just relying on the publication's servers — provides continuity if organizational IT access is interrupted.

4. **Post-seizure device reconstitution:** After any device seizure, assume the returned device is compromised (whether or not a forensic examination was conducted). Factory-reset before any use. Re-register Signal with a new number. Re-verify safety numbers with all sources before resuming sensitive communications.

### 5.3 Newsroom-Level Compartmentalization

**Need-to-know principle for source identities:** Within a newsroom, knowledge of a source's identity should be limited to the minimum number of people required for editorial decision-making. In practice: the reporter, the direct editor, and in cases where the source's credibility is being evaluated for legal review, legal counsel. A source whose identity is known to the full editorial team is a source exposed to every security risk in the team's security posture.

**Written compartmentalization policy:** The news organization should have a written policy specifying who knows source identities and under what circumstances additional disclosure is appropriate. This policy matters for two reasons: first, it creates accountability within the organization; second, in a legal proceeding where the organization is arguing shield law protection, a documented compartmentalization policy is evidence that the organization took the confidentiality obligation seriously.

**Separate communication channels by sensitivity:** The organizational communication infrastructure should distinguish:
- Public-facing communications: organizational website, public social media, press release channels
- Internal general communications: Slack, Teams, organizational email — appropriate for published work coordination, not for source-related discussion
- Sensitive editorial communications: Signal group (verified safety numbers, disappearing messages) or ProtonMail (Swiss jurisdiction, encrypted Proton-to-Proton) — appropriate for source-related discussion, investigation strategy, and legal consultation
- Source communications: the dedicated device architecture, individual to each journalist, not on organizational infrastructure

This separation is not implemented by most newsrooms today. It is the correct architecture for any newsroom where journalists may cover investigations that attract government legal attention.

**Team Signal group configuration:** For a reporting team with shared investigation knowledge, a Signal group with disappearing messages (7-day default) and verified safety numbers for all members provides secure internal coordination. The group membership should be limited to active team members — adding general editorial management to the group is a compartmentalization failure.

## Part 6: Case Studies

### 6.1 The Hannah Natanson / Washington Post Case (2026) — Active Seizure Template

**Timeline and facts:** January 15, 2026 — FBI agents execute a search warrant at Washington Post reporter Hannah Natanson's home. Agents seize her phone, two computers, a Garmin watch, a portable hard drive, and a recording device. The search warrant is related to a government contractor's disclosure of classified information; Natanson is not herself under investigation.

January 21, 2026 — A federal judge in the Eastern District of Virginia blocks the DOJ from searching the seized devices, ordering an independent judicial review instead.

February 20, 2026 — The judge who authorized the original warrant publicly criticizes the DOJ's conduct in court.

May 4, 2026 — A second judge upholds the block on DOJ searching the devices; independent review ongoing as of May 2026.

**Security lessons:**

This case illustrates the gap between legal protection (ongoing court fights) and operational protection (the data was already seized). Courts have blocked the DOJ from directly accessing the devices — but the devices are not in Natanson's possession. The legal fight is about what the DOJ can do with data already extracted; the legal fight cannot restore the journalist's access to her ongoing work.

The countermeasure this case argues for: **encrypted cloud backup as a business continuity tool, not just a security tool.** If Natanson's investigation documents were in ProtonDrive with a key the government cannot access, a device seizure would be disruptive (loss of device use) but not a data access event. The legal fight about the seized devices continues; her investigation continues in parallel.

The second lesson: **disappearing messages for source communications limits seizure exposure.** If source communications expired before the seizure, the seized device yields no source communication content for the time period before the disappearing message timer. The Garmin watch was seized — an unexpected device category. Any device that might be seized (including peripherals, smartwatches, tablets) should be treated as within the seizure scope.

Sources: [Second judge maintains DOJ can't search data from Post reporter — WaPo](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national-security/2026/05/04/post-reporter-justice-department-search/), [FBI can't examine devices seized from reporter, judge rules — NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/fbi-cannot-examine-devices-took-washington-post-reporter-judge-rules-rcna255286), [Project 2025 laid groundwork for raid on WaPo reporter — Nieman Lab](https://www.niemanlab.org/2026/01/project-2025-laid-the-groundwork-for-wednesdays-raid-on-a-washington-post-reporters-home/)

### 6.2 Paragon Graphite / Fanpage.it (2025) — Zero-Click Spyware Template

**Timeline and facts:** January–February 2025 — Two journalists at Fanpage.it (Italian investigative news site), including head of the Naples newsroom Ciro Pellegrino, have their iPhones infected with Paragon Graphite spyware through an iMessage zero-click exploit. The journalists were not aware of the infection during the infection period. April 29, 2025 — Apple notifies both journalists of the targeting. The Citizen Lab conducts forensic analysis, confirms infection with Paragon fingerprints, and identifies CVE-2025-43200 as the zero-click vulnerability exploited. February 2025 — Apple ships iOS 18.3.1, patching CVE-2025-43200. Italian prosecutors confirm Pellegrino's compromise; investigation ongoing.

**ICE context:** ICE confirmed it is actively using Paragon Graphite on April 1, 2026. WhatsApp separately disclosed 90 journalists and civil society members targeted with Graphite in early 2025. The same operator model (nation-state or agency customer of Paragon) is now confirmed operational in U.S. domestic immigration enforcement.

**Security lessons:**

The Fanpage.it case demonstrates that even a sophisticated Italian investigative newsroom with experienced journalists did not detect the Graphite infection during the infection period. Detection came from Apple's proactive notification system (Threat Notifications), not from the journalists' own security practices.

**Countermeasures specific to zero-click threats:**

First, iOS updates are not routine maintenance — they are the primary defense against known zero-click exploits. CVE-2025-43200 was exploited against Fanpage.it journalists while they ran iOS 18.2.1; it was patched in iOS 18.3.1. The window between patch release and update application is an active risk window. Enable automatic updates: Settings > General > Software Update > Automatic Updates > On for all toggles.

Second, Apple's Lockdown Mode reduces the zero-click attack surface by disabling iMessage link previews, blocking most message attachment types, and disabling the JIT compiler that is often used in browser-based zero-click chains. For journalists under active threat, Lockdown Mode is a concrete defense, not a precautionary one.

Third, the dedicated source communication device using GrapheneOS provides a separate attack surface. A Graphite operator who compromises a journalist's personal iPhone has access to whatever is on that iPhone — not to a GrapheneOS device that has never been at the same location and is not associated with the journalist's personal identity.

Fourth, Apple Threat Notifications are the detection mechanism that worked in the Fanpage.it case. Ensure your Apple ID contact details (email and phone) are current — Apple's notification system is how you would learn of a confirmed targeting. The notification does not appear in the device itself if the device is compromised; it is sent to your Apple ID contact address.

Sources: [Graphite Caught: First Forensic Confirmation — Citizen Lab](https://citizenlab.ca/research/first-forensic-confirmation-of-paragons-ios-mercenary-spyware-finds-journalists-targeted/), [ICE acknowledges it is using powerful spyware — NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/04/07/nx-s1-5776799/ice-spyware-privacy), [Paragon Graphite Spyware Used a Zero-Day Exploit — SecurityAffairs](https://securityaffairs.com/178940/mobile-2/paragon-graphite-spyware-used-a-zero-day-exploit.html)

### 6.3 Pegasus / Eastern European Journalists (2024–2025) — Foreign-Source Spyware Template

**Pattern across confirmed cases:** In March 2025, Amnesty International's Security Lab confirmed that two journalists at the Serbia-based Balkan Investigative Reporting Network (BIRN) were targeted with NSO Group's Pegasus spyware in February 2025. In May 2024, Access Now and Citizen Lab reported Pegasus targeting of at least five journalists with connections to exiled press communities from Russia, Latvia, and Belarus. In February 2024, a separate Access Now report documented Pegasus infections on 30 individuals in Jordan, including journalists.

**Common thread across cases:** The journalists targeted were covering stories that attracted state-level surveillance interest. The Serbian BIRN journalists were covering corruption and government accountability in a country where intelligence agencies have confirmed NSO Group access. The exiled Russian, Latvian, and Belarusian journalists were covering their countries of origin from diaspora positions — a context where the originating country's intelligence services have confirmed motivation and, in some cases, confirmed NSO access.

**The relevance for U.S.-based journalists covering international beats:**

Foreign sources under Pegasus or Graphite surveillance may have already-compromised devices. When such a source contacts a journalist through Signal, the Signal encryption on the transit channel is intact — but if the source's device is a Pegasus-compromised endpoint, the attacker reads the messages from the device after decryption. The journalist's end of the conversation is secure (assuming the journalist's device is not separately compromised). But the source's side of the conversation is not.

Signal safety number verification is not a countermeasure against Pegasus on the source's device. It is a countermeasure against a man-in-the-middle replacing the source's keys. Safety number verification confirms the keys are the source's — it does not confirm the source's device is uncompromised.

The practical implication: for sources in high-risk country contexts (Serbia, Jordan, Russia, Belarus, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Mexico — all documented Pegasus/Graphite deployment countries), the security model must assume the source's device may be compromised at the OS level. This means: minimize what is communicated over digital channels, maximize in-person verification for sensitive content, and use SecureDrop for document receipt rather than Signal attachment (SecureDrop protects the source's network identity even if the source's device is compromised — the attacker on the source's device can see the document was uploaded, but the journalist's identity and the SecureDrop server address are protected by Tor at the network layer).

Sources: [Serbia: Journalists Targeted with Pegasus Spyware — Amnesty Security Lab](https://securitylab.amnesty.org/latest/2025/03/journalists-targeted-with-pegasus-spyware/), [Pegasus Spyware Targets Exiled Journalists — CPJ](https://cpj.org/2024/05/report-pegasus-spyware-targets-exiled-journalists-from-russia-latvia-belarus/), [Journalists and Activists Targeted in Jordan — Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/2/1/journalists-activists-targeted-in-jordan-with-israeli-made-pegasus-spyware)

### 6.4 Financial Journalists and Adverse-Action Risk — Structural Case Study

This case study is structural rather than incident-based, because the specific threat to financial journalists covering bank prosecutions or regulatory investigations is an underaddressed area in most journalist security guidance.

**The threat model for financial journalists:**

Financial journalists covering bank fraud, regulatory violations, or white-collar criminal investigations are subject to a threat model from non-state actors (the subjects of their investigation) in addition to the government surveillance threats the rest of this playbook addresses. The relevant actors are corporate security teams, law firms with investigative resources, and private intelligence firms retained by investigation subjects.

The documented capabilities of corporate adversaries relevant to financial journalists: litigation-targeted OSINT (building dossiers on the journalist from public sources, professional directories, and data broker purchases), subpoenas for journalist records in civil litigation (civil subpoenas do not require the DOJ Bondi policy threshold; any civil plaintiff can subpoena a journalist's records), and in extreme cases, retention of private intelligence firms with OSINT and technical capabilities that overlap with the government capabilities in this playbook.

**Source protection in financial reporting:**

Sources in financial regulation contexts — whistleblowers at banks, SEC or CFTC employees with knowledge of ongoing investigations, traders or analysts with knowledge of unreported misconduct — face specific adverse action risks. Financial institutions are required by SEC whistleblower program rules to maintain confidentiality of whistleblowers who submit through official channels. But a source who speaks with a journalist outside official channels is not protected by the SEC program and is instead exposed to potential employer retaliation, civil suit for breach of confidentiality agreements, and in some cases criminal exposure for disclosing information covered by regulatory confidentiality requirements.

The implication for the journalist: **source contact through SecureDrop or anonymous encrypted intake, not through any channel that could be identified via corporate discovery or NSL, is specifically important in the financial reporting context.** A corporate plaintiff can subpoena a journalist's phone records through civil discovery without the DOJ threshold. A call record showing repeated communication with an anonymous VoIP number is more protective than a call record showing communication with a financial institution employee's personal cell.

**Pre-publication coordination in financial investigations:** The RCFP's pre-publication review resources (rcfp.org/resources/pre-publication-review-guide/) address the specific considerations for financial reporting, including the "market manipulation" risk from pre-publication disclosure of a story that could move markets. For stories about ongoing SEC or CFTC investigations, pre-publication legal review is not just about source protection — it is also about avoiding inadvertent securities violations and managing the publication timeline in coordination with regulatory processes.

Sources: [RCFP Pre-Publication Review Guide](https://www.rcfp.org/resources/pre-publication-review-guide/), [CPJ: Digital and Physical Safety — Protecting Confidential Sources](https://cpj.org/2021/11/digital-physical-safety-protecting-confidential-sources/)

## Part 7: The Journalist Assistance Network — 2026 Institutional Resources

Five U.S. press freedom organizations formally launched the Journalist Assistance Network in May 2025 to provide coordinated legal, safety, and immigration resources for journalists. This represents a meaningful change from the fragmented landscape the base playbook described. The relevant resources:

**Journalist Assistance Network (JAN):** A multi-organization coordination mechanism connecting journalists to legal support (RCFP), safety support (CPJ), and immigration resources. cpj.org/journalist-assistance-network

**EFF Digital Security Helpline:** Free, confidential technical assistance for journalists facing digital threats, including spyware, device compromise, and data broker exposure. eff.org/pages/digital-security-helpline

**FPF Digital Security Helpline:** 24/7 security assistance for journalists and news organizations. freedom.press/digisec/programs/ — includes emergency support for active security incidents.

**Access Now Digital Security Helpline:** security@accessnow.org — provides support for journalists outside the U.S. and for journalists facing nation-state-level spyware threats including Pegasus and Graphite. Access Now documented the Jordan Pegasus cases and the exiled journalist targeting cases cited in Part 6.3.

**Reporters Committee Legal Defense Hotline:** 1-800-336-4243 — for any legal question including subpoena response, shield law analysis, search warrant challenge, and pre-publication review.
