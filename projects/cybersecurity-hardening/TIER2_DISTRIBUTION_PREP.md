# Tier 2 Distribution Prep: Ready for Outreach

This document consolidates the Tier 2 distribution strategy, provides ready-to-use email templates, and outlines exact next steps. Tier 2 targets amplifier networks: organizations that don't work directly with the at-risk population but have the reach, credibility, and technical fluency to amplify and validate the corpus.

**Status**: Materials prepared. Awaiting user approval to execute. Tier 1 outreach should be launched or underway before beginning Tier 2.

---

## Why Tier 2 Second

Tier 1 organizations work directly with the people targeted by ICE's ELITE system. Tier 2 organizations amplify to wider, more technically sophisticated audiences:

- **Digital rights organizations** publish policy analyses, legal threat assessments, and tool guides that shape how both individuals and institutions respond to surveillance threats
- **Academic cybersecurity programs** can integrate the corpus into curricula, cite the threat model in research, and lend institutional credibility through peer engagement
- **Security researcher communities** can audit, extend, and critique the technical claims — their validation matters to technical audiences
- **Journalist organizations** can train their own members on operational security and may cover the corpus as a story (source protection angle) or as a resource

The Tier 2 audience is already somewhat aware of surveillance risks. What the corpus adds is a specific, sourced threat model for a current active system (ELITE/Palantir), with actionable countermeasures verified against that specific threat.

I'm sharing this because [Organization]'s work in [surveillance/privacy/digital

## Outreach Execution Plan

### **Step 1: Sequence Matters**

Do not send Tier 2 outreach simultaneously with Tier 1. The strategic sequence:

1. Tier 1 outreach sent (or at minimum drafted and in progress)
2. Allow 1–2 weeks for Tier 1 organizations to acknowledge
3. Begin Tier 2 outreach, starting with digital rights organizations (2A) because:
   - They have the fastest response cycles
   - Endorsement from EFF, CDT, or Access Now lends credibility to academic and journalist outreach

Suggested sequencing within Tier 2:
- Week 1–2: Digital rights organizations (2A)
- Week 3–4: Journalist organizations (2D) — they have fast news cycles and will publish or not quickly
- Week 5–8: Academic programs (2B) — slower response cycles, semester-driven
- Ongoing: Security researcher community channels (2C), via conference submissions and individual researcher contact

### **Step 2: Research Current Contacts**

Before sending, verify:
- Who is currently leading relevant programs at each organization (leadership changes frequently)
- Any recent publications by the organization that directly relate to the corpus topic (ICE surveillance, data brokers, Palantir)
- Whether the organization has taken a public position on any issue the corpus addresses

Use: LinkedIn, organization "About" pages, recent press releases, conference speaker lists.

### **Step 3: Customize Each Email**

The templates above are starting points. For Tier 2 organizations:
- Reference their specific work (a recent publication, a campaign, a program name)
- Explain why their organization specifically is the right fit
- Adjust the technical depth to match the audience:
  - Digital rights orgs: full technical detail is welcome
  - Journalist training programs: emphasize practical utility over technical depth
  - Academic programs: emphasize research value and citation potential

### **Step 4: Follow Up Strategically**

Tier 2 organizations are high-volume recipients of outreach. One follow-up is appropriate; two is a maximum.

- **First follow-up**: 2 weeks after initial email. Subject: "Following up: [original subject]". Keep it to 2 sentences.
- **Alternative channel**: If you have a mutual contact at the organization, use that relationship for an introduction rather than a cold follow-up.
- **Conference opportunities**: If any Tier 2 target organization is presenting at a conference you attend, personal introduction is more effective than email.

### **Step 5: Track and Build**

Every positive response creates an opportunity to ask: "Who else in your network should see this?" Digital rights organizations in particular have adjacent networks — an introduction from EFF to CDT or Access Now compresses multiple cold outreach steps into one warm handoff.

## FAQ: Tier 2 Concerns

**1. "We have a policy of not promoting external resources."**
Response: Understood. The corpus is not seeking endorsement — I'm sharing it for your awareness in case it's relevant to your own research or policy work. If there's anything in the threat model that informs your work, that's sufficient.

**2. "Can you share this under an open license so we can adapt it?"**
Response: Yes. The corpus is designed to be cited and adapted. If you adapt sections, note what was changed from the original and cite the Gist URL. The threat model in particular is intended as primary-source-citable.

**3. "Have you had this reviewed by security researchers?"**
Response: The corpus is published openly and I am actively seeking technical review. If your team identifies any errors in the countermeasures or threat model, I would welcome the feedback directly.

**4. "This is relevant to a story we're working on."**
Response: The threat model section is documented entirely from public sources (FOIA, government contracts, court filings) and is designed for citation in journalism. Happy to discuss further.

**5. "Can you speak at our conference / training program?"**
Response: Happy to discuss. Frame any participation offer around the threat model and countermeasures rather than personal background — the corpus stands on its sourcing.

**6. "How current is this? Does it cover [recent development]?"**
Response: The threat model was documented in 2025-2026 and draws from public sources through [current date of publication]. The ELITE system continues to evolve — any developments not covered by the corpus would benefit from additional sourcing if your team has it.

**7. "Is this specific to California?" (re: DROP platform)**
Response: The California DELETE Act DROP platform is specifically documented because it provides a pathway for residents without government-issued ID. The general data broker opt-out framework applies nationally. Individual state opt-out processes vary and are documented in the implementation guide.

## Success Metrics for Tier 2

You'll know Tier 2 outreach is working when:

1. **EFF, CDT, or Access Now** acknowledges the corpus and shares it internally or externally — these organizations have high credibility signal for subsequent Tier 3 contacts
2. **An academic program** incorporates the threat model as a course reading or cites it in research — indicates the sourcing holds up under scrutiny
3. **Freedom of the Press Foundation or IRE** integrates the countermeasures into journalist security training — indicates the content is practical enough for real workflow use
4. **A security researcher** provides technical feedback on the countermeasures — either validation or corrections, both strengthen the corpus
5. **Journalists** contact you about the ELITE system story — indicates the threat model is compelling enough to drive original reporting
6. **Gist access metrics increase** after sending each wave — indirect indicator of distribution depth (GitHub does not provide per-referrer stats, but total view count is visible)
