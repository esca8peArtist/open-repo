## Part I: Strategic Sequencing & Institution Prioritization

### Tier 1 Institution Hierarchy (Why This Order Matters)

**Distribution velocity hierarchy**: Direct-threat networks → Amplifier networks → Researcher/policy networks

The following sequencing maximizes impact while accounting for institutional response times and amplification potential.

---

### Wave 1: Tier 1A — Immigration Legal Aid (Days 1–7)

**Rationale**: Direct client relationships with undocumented populations facing ELITE targeting. Legal aid organizations have established channels for client resources and can integrate Part 0 (data broker opt-outs) into client intake and community education workflows immediately.

**Outreach goal**: 25 contacts across 5 national + regional immigration legal aid organizations

**Sequencing strategy**:
1. Start with 5 high-profile national organizations (Day 1) — they have verified contact info and rapid internal amplification networks.
2. Proceed to regional immigration legal aid organizations (Days 2–5) using consistent research methodology.
3. Expand to law school immigration clinics and public defender immigration units (Days 6–7) — these are secondary distribution channels with good contact info availability.

**Personalization framework for 1A (Legal Aid Organizations)**:

| Theme | What They Care About | How to Frame the Corpus |
|-------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Primary**: Client protection | Their clients are under active threat | Lead with Part 0 (actionable, no-tech-expertise opt-outs) and the legal-citability of the threat model |
| **Secondary**: Litigation & impact | Sourcing and primary documentation | Emphasize FOIA/court filing structure; offer to discuss specific sections for use in advocacy |
| **Tertiary**: Staff education | How to talk to clients about surveillance | Frame as a resource for internal staff training on emerging threat landscape |
| **Avoid**: Heavy technical language. Use "the databases ICE queries" not "ELITE address confidence scoring pipeline" |

**Key talking points**:
- Part 0 is immediately actionable (2–4 hours, no expertise needed)
- California residents without gov ID have a documented path (AB 60/AB 1766 → DROP platform)
- Threat model is designed to be citable in legal briefs and advocacy
- Implementation guide includes verification checkpoints (fraud prevention + security validation)

---

### Wave 2: Tier 1B — Community-Based Organizations (Days 8–14)

**Rationale**: Ground-level organizations with direct trust relationships in immigrant communities. Faster distribution velocity than formal legal organizations; easier to adapt resources for workshops and community education.

**Outreach goal**: 25 contacts across 5 national + regional community organizations and local sanctuary networks

**Sequencing strategy**:
1. Start with 5 national community organizations with established communications infrastructure (CASA, Make the Road, United We Dream, Centro CDM, local sanctuary network entry point).
2. Proceed to regional sanctuary networks using standardized search patterns ("[city] sanctuary network").
3. Expand to community legal education programs embedded in churches, community centers, and immigrant service agencies.

**Personalization framework for 1B (Community-Based Organizations)**:

| Theme | What They Care About | How to Frame the Corpus |
|-------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Primary**: Accessibility & workshoppability | Can our volunteers/staff teach this? | Lead with "no technical expertise required," "2–4 hours," "immediately actionable" |
| **Secondary**: Community education infrastructure | Do we have materials to distribute? | Frame as a handout/reference guide; offer one-page summaries |
| **Tertiary**: Language accessibility | Do you serve Spanish speakers? | Acknowledge Spanish-language version is on July 2026 roadmap |
| **Avoid**: Corporate framing. Use practical language; lead with impact not process |

**Key talking points**:
- Part 0 is workshoppable — a 2-hour community session can walk through opt-outs together
- No technical background required for Tier 1 checklist
- Corpus emphasizes harm reduction, not perfect security
- Community organizations can adapt the resource for their specific population

---

### Wave 3: Tier 1C — Mutual Aid Networks (Days 15–21)

**Rationale**: Mutual aid networks reach people not accessible through formal legal or community organizations. Lower barrier to entry; security-conscious audiences who can understand and share Tier 2/Tier 3 material.

**Outreach goal**: 15–20 contacts across national bail funds, local mutual aid networks, Food Not Bombs chapters, Anarchist Black Cross affiliates

**Sequencing strategy**:
1. Start with 5 national mutual aid + bail fund networks with established infrastructure (National Bail Fund, Community Justice Exchange).
2. Proceed to local/regional mutual aid networks using geographic targeting and Signal/Telegram group access.
3. Template shifts from formal email (1A/1B) to Signal-native format (shorter, direct, security-culture appropriate).

**Personalization framework for 1C (Mutual Aid Networks)**:

| Theme | What They Care About | How to Frame the Corpus |
|-------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Primary**: Movement security & harm reduction | What can we actually implement? | Emphasize Part 0 as highest-ROI action for broadest population; Tier 2/3 for specific investigation risk |
| **Secondary**: Avoiding false confidence | Won't this give people false security? | Explicitly address: targets bulk surveillance infrastructure, not targeted investigation with warrant |
| **Tertiary**: Direct action impact | Does this help our members now? | Frame as immediate risk reduction, not comprehensive protection |
| **Avoid**: Institutional/corporate framing. These audiences are skeptical of formal language; keep it direct and practical |

**Key talking points**:
- Part 0 (data broker opt-outs) is the highest-leverage action for the broadest population
- Tier 2 and Tier 3 are for people with specific reason to believe they are under active investigation
- No false confidence — explicitly addresses what this does and doesn't protect against
- Security culture audiences can understand Tor, Signal, GrapheneOS without explanation

## Part III: Contact Research & Personalization Framework

### How to Research Each Contact (8–12 minutes per organization)

Work through this methodology in order. Stop when you have 2–3 sentences of genuine personalization (not name-insertion).

#### 1. Organization Website (2 minutes)
- Read "About" and "Programs" sections
- Note: primary geographic focus, client population, current campaigns
- Look for keywords: digital security, data privacy, enforcement response, community education

#### 2. Recent News & Press Releases (2 minutes)
- Search: `[organization name] site:[organizationsite.org]`
- Search: `[organization name] press release 2025 2026`
- Note any recent work related to: ELITE targeting, data broker issues, digital security, immigration enforcement

#### 3. Social Media (1–2 minutes) [If applicable]
- Twitter/X or Bluesky: `[organization handle]`
- Look for recent posts about: enforcement, ICE, data privacy, surveillance
- Recent relevant post = natural opening hook

#### 4. LinkedIn (2 minutes, if you have access) [Optional]
- Search for specific contact person
- Note: title, tenure at organization, any recent articles or posts
- Useful for personalizing to named contact vs. general inbox

#### 5. Publications & Reports (1–2 minutes) [Optional for clinics/think tanks]
- Google Scholar: `[organization name] immigration enforcement`
- Recent papers or practice guides = evidence of field expertise

### Personalization Template (Apply to Every Email)

```
Opening hook [1–2 sentences, personalized]:

  OR
```

### Response Classification & Handling

**Class 1: Engagement**
- Signals: Questions about corpus, intent to share, requests for call/meeting, says forwarded to colleagues
- Action: Reply within 24 hours using Template R1 (answer specific questions, offer further discussion)
- Escalation path: Call/meeting if requested; Spanish-language version roadmap; staff training opportunity

**Class 2: Acknowledgment**
- Signals: "Thank you," "we'll review," "passed along to team," no questions
- Action: Log it. Send Template R2 follow-up after 10 days if no further engagement
- Escalation path: Close as "Acknowledged, no further engagement" after 10-day follow-up with no reply

**Class 3: Declination**
- Signals: "Not for us," "no capacity," "remove us from future outreach"
- Action: Send Template R3. Mark as "Declined — closed"
- Escalation path: Do not follow up further. Note if pattern emerges (adjust messaging for that category)

**Class 4: Out-of-Office (OOO)**
- Signals: Automated reply with return date
- Action: Log return date. Set calendar reminder for 2 business days after return
- Escalation path: Send follow-up after return, not during OOO period

**Class 5: Bounce**
- Signals: Mailer-daemon bounce, "user not found," "address does not exist"
- Action: Research alternate contact immediately (check org website, search email pattern, use web form)
- Escalation path: If no alternate found, mark "Unreachable — closed"

## Part IX: Expected Response Types & Handling

### Positive Response Pathway

**What you hope to see**:
1. Engagement: Contact asks questions, expresses interest, says they will share
2. Acknowledgment: Contact thanks you, says they'll review or pass along
3. Amplification signal: Contact forwards to their network (most valuable)

**What to do**:
- **Engagement**: Reply within 24 hours with specific answers (Template R1)
- **Acknowledgment**: Follow up after 10 days if no further engagement (Template R2)
- **Amplification signal**: Log it, ask about reach/impact, offer follow-up support

---

### Neutral/Declination Response Pathway

**What you may see**:
1. Silence (no response after 10 days)
2. Out-of-office (auto-responder)
3. Declination (not for us, no capacity, remove)
4. Bounce (bad email address)

**What to do**:
- **Silence**: Follow up once (Template R2) after 10 days. Close after second attempt.
- **OOO**: Note return date, follow up 2 business days after return
- **Declination**: Send acknowledgment (Template R3), do not follow up further
- **Bounce**: Research alternate contact immediately, reroute if found
