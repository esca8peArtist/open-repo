# Tier 2 Organizational Strategy: Institutional Adoption Infrastructure for 7 Sectors

**Bottom line up front**: Existing Tier 2 documentation (TIER_2_DISTRIBUTION_STRATEGY.md, TIER2_DISTRIBUTION_PREP.md, tier-2-sector-contact-lists.md) covers digital rights organizations, academic cybersecurity programs, security researchers, journalist organizations, think tanks, and some union/AG coverage. This document covers the 7 sectors where institutional decision-making, adoption pathways, and contact specifics are either absent or underspecified in existing files: (1) law school deans/clinics, (2) civil rights nonprofits, (3) legal services nonprofits, (4) labor unions, (5) immigrant rights organizations, (6) faith-based organizing networks, and (7) academic criminology departments. Sector-level analysis from TIER_2_DISTRIBUTION_STRATEGY.md is not repeated here; this document goes deeper on the contacts, decision-maker structures, adoption mechanisms, and pitch templates for each sector.

## Part I: Institutional Decision-Maker Discovery

### Sector 1: Law School Deans and Clinical Programs

**Who decides**: The operative distinction is between law school deans and clinical faculty. A dean who receives this corpus will forward it to whoever directs clinical programming — a dean does not adopt curriculum. The decision-maker is the clinical program director or the director of a specific clinic (immigration, civil rights, privacy/technology) who has both the subject matter fit and the authority to assign readings, develop case materials, or initiate a clinical placement.

**Org structure**: Typical law school structure has a Dean of Clinical Education (or similar title) who oversees all clinics, and then individual clinic directors who manage specific subject-matter clinics. In schools with a privacy/technology law clinic, the director of that clinic is the highest-priority contact. In schools without a dedicated privacy clinic, the immigration law clinic director or the civil rights clinic director is the appropriate first contact.

**Decision trigger for law schools**: The most reliable adoption trigger is a live legal question that the corpus provides the factual record for. The Carpenter v. United States gap (does the Fourth Amendment extend to commercially-purchased broker-aggregated location data?) is unresolved in every federal circuit. A clinic director who sees a case opportunity — or a faculty member who sees a student note or comment topic — will move from curiosity to action. Frame corpus outreach not as "here is a resource" but as "here is the factual predicate for a case or scholarship project in an area where the law is currently unsettled."

**Adoption pathway**:
```
Corpus received by wrong contact (communications, dean's office)

Clinic director reads threat model section

  → OR identifies case possibility and raises with senior clinic student
  → OR uses as research foundation for faculty scholarship (longer path)
Minimum adoption artifact: course reading assignment confirmed
Strong adoption artifact: student note or clinic case citing corpus documentation
```

## Part II: Tier 2 Pilot Site Prioritization

### Selection Criteria

A good pilot site has three properties: (1) high receptiveness — a decision-maker who has already demonstrated interest in surveillance/digital rights/immigration technology; (2) network multiplier — the organization's adoption creates downstream adoption in 5+ other organizations through their networks; and (3) low friction — adoption does not require a lengthy institutional approval process. Organizations that are small enough to move fast but connected enough to cascade are the best pilots.

### Recommended Pilot Sequence: 8 Organizations

**Phase 2 Wave 1 (Month 1, Weeks 5–6): 2 pilots**

**Pilot 1: University at Buffalo Law — Civil Rights and Housing Clinic**
Justification: Already representing Privacy International and ACLU in a federal surveillance lawsuit — the corpus is directly usable for their active case, not a future possibility. Receptiveness is highest because they are operating in the same legal space the corpus documents. Network multiplier: UB Law has a clinic exchange relationship with other NY-region law schools; adoption here becomes a case study for broader law school outreach. Friction: low — a single clinic director can add corpus to their reading list and case materials without committee approval.
Contact: law.buffalo.edu/beyond/clinics/civil-rights.html
Ask: "We understand your clinic is currently involved in surveillance litigation. Would the primary-source documentation in this corpus be useful for your case team? We'd like to offer a briefing session."

**Pilot 2: ILRC (Immigrant Legal Resource Center)**
Justification: ILRC has an existing Director of Technology (Kat Kimmons), publishes practitioner guides used nationally, and trains legal practitioners in all 50 states. ## Part IV: Success Metrics by Institutional Type

### Measurement Framework

Metrics are tiered by adoption depth: (1) reach (corpus delivered to decision-maker), (2) acknowledgment (reply or confirmed receipt), (3) integration (corpus embedded in a workflow), and (4) downstream (corpus appears in external output — published citation, intake protocol, training slide deck).

| Sector | Minimum (Reach + Ack) | Integration Target | Downstream Target | Measurement Method |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Law school clinics | 5 clinic directors contacted; 2 acknowledgments | 1 clinic director assigns corpus sections in course | 1 student note, published brief, or clinic case citing corpus | Faculty email confirming course integration; published citation search |
| Civil rights nonprofits | 5 organizations contacted; 2 replies from program (not press) contacts | 1 organization adds corpus to internal research database | 1 published report, amicus brief, or litigation filing citing corpus | Published document citation; direct confirmation from attorney |
| Legal services nonprofits | 5 organizations contacted; 2 replies from managing attorney or program director | 1 organization adds Part 0 to intake protocol | 1 organization's intake form includes corpus opt-out steps; or ILRC/CLINIC training materials updated | Intake form shared; training materials cited corpus |
| Labor unions | 5 organizing departments contacted; 2 replies from organizing (not press) staff | 1 union adds device hygiene + Signal guidance to organizer orientation | Training module shared; 100+ organizers trained in corpus countermeasures | Training coordinator confirms; slide deck shared |
| Immigrant rights orgs | 5 organizations contacted; 3 replies from KYR coordinators | 1 org integrates Part 0 into KYR workshop | 1 org creates community-facing one-page summary referencing corpus | Community handout shared; direct confirmation |
| Faith networks | 3 networks contacted (regional level); 2 replies from program coordinators | 1 network adds corpus digital safety steps to sanctuary preparation protocol | 1 congregation-level digital safety session delivered to 20+ community members | Program coordinator confirms; attendance confirmed |
| Criminology academia | 5 faculty contacted; 2 substantive replies (not social acknowledgment) | 1 faculty member assigns corpus in graduate seminar | 1 peer-reviewed publication or conference presentation citing corpus | Published citation search; conference program reference |
