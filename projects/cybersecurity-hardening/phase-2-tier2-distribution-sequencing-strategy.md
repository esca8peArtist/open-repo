# Phase 2 Tier 2 Distribution Sequencing & Organizational Outreach Strategy

**Bottom line**: Tier 1 individual-focused distribution is production-ready and awaiting launch approval. This document designs the transition from Phase 1 individual awareness to Phase 2 organizational adoption. The June–July 2026 Tier 2 launch targets the sector organizations best positioned to embed the corpus into governance, operations, and their own distribution networks. A single organizational adoption at CLINIC (400+ affiliated programs) or Freedom of the Press Foundation generates more impact than 50 additional individual recipients. This document answers six questions in sequence: what "Phase 1 success" looks like, which Tier 1 organizations graduate to Tier 2, how to pitch organizational adoption sector by sector, what new materials need to be built, when each wave executes, and what metrics define Tier 2 success.

---

## Section 1: Phase 1 Success Metrics and Tier 1→2 Transition Criteria

### What Phase 1 Success Looks Like

Phase 1 outreach targets approximately 45 Tier 1 organizations across immigration legal aid, civil rights nonprofits, academic programs, activist networks, journalist organizations, and digital rights organizations. Success is measured on three dimensions.

**Engagement reach** (minimum threshold to proceed to Tier 2): 30% of contacted organizations confirm read or click-through on the main Gist corpus link within 21 days. A 30% engagement rate against a cold send to professional organizations is achievable given the specificity of the subject line strategy in `TIER2_MESSAGING_TEMPLATES.md`. Below 20% signals subject line or contact routing problems that need diagnosis before Tier 2 outreach begins.

**Integration signals** (the quality indicator): At least 5 organizations reply with substantive engagement — a question about specific sections, a request for additional materials, or confirmation that they have forwarded the corpus internally. This number is more important than raw engagement rate. Five substantive replies from 45 contacts is an 11% substantive engagement rate, which is realistic for a first cold outreach to institutional contacts. These 5 organizations form the pre-contact list for Tier 2 pre-wave outreach.

**Qualitative signal patterns** indicating readiness to advance:

- **Law schools**: At least one law school clinic (Harvard Berkman, Georgetown Center on Privacy and Technology, NYU Immigrant Rights Clinic) acknowledges the corpus and routes it to their clinical supervisors or faculty research leads. This signals that the ELITE threat model's FOIA-sourced methodology is being treated as academically credible, which is the threshold for curriculum or case development integration.
- **Immigration legal aid organizations**: At least one CLINIC-affiliated program or AILA member organization requests the corpus with the explicit intent of sharing with clients in the intake process. This signals Part 0 (the accessible data broker opt-out section) is landing as a practical tool, not just an informational resource.
- **Media coverage**: Any mainstream, trade, or legal-sector coverage of the ELITE threat model — whether directly citing the corpus or citing primary sources the corpus documents — validates that the sourcing holds up to editorial scrutiny. Coverage in immigration trade press (American Immigration Lawyer, Immigration Daily), legal press (National Law Journal, Above the Law), or security press (The Record, Wired) satisfies this criterion; general press coverage exceeds it.
- **Secondary distribution**: At least 3 organizations that received Phase 1 outreach forward the corpus to an organization the sender did not contact. This is the earliest signal of organic propagation — the condition that makes Phase 2 organizational outreach self-reinforcing rather than purely sender-driven.

### The Phase 1→2 Transition Gate

The decision to launch Tier 2 organizational outreach is not solely calendar-driven. The trigger is the intersection of two conditions: (1) Phase 1 outreach has been live for at least 28 days, and (2) at least one of the integration signal patterns above is confirmed. If both conditions are met at the 28-day mark, proceed to Tier 2 pre-contact outreach in Week 1 of Phase 2. If only the calendar condition is met with no integration signals confirmed, add a 14-day diagnostic window to identify whether the problem is content, routing, subject lines, or contact targeting — then remediate before Tier 2 launch.

## Section 2: Tier 2 Candidate Pre-Identification Framework

### The Three-Factor Scoring Model

Each Tier 1 organization is scored at the Day 28 checkpoint on three factors. The score is binary per factor: a point is either earned or it is not. Maximum score is 3/3. This is not a soft preference ranking — it determines invitation timing and investment level.

**Factor A — Engagement Depth (0 or 1 point)**

Scoring threshold: a Day 7 or Day 14 engagement score of 3 or higher on a 0–5 scale, where 5 = substantive reply with implementation question. Score 3 requires at minimum: confirmed open (tracking pixel, delivery receipt, or explicit acknowledgment), click-through to the Gist corpus, and no bounce or decline. Score 4–5 requires a substantive reply. Factor A = 1 only at Score 3 or higher.

Proxies when tracking is unavailable: a reply that quotes or references specific sections indicates deep engagement regardless of whether open/click data exists.

**Factor B — Integration Signal (0 or 1 point)**

Scoring threshold: the organization has explicitly or behaviorally indicated intent to integrate the corpus into operations. Factor B = 1 requires one of: (a) explicit statement of intent to share with staff, clients, or members; (b) request for additional materials tailored to their context (e.g., "do you have a version formatted for client intake?"); (c) question about Part 0 implementation that implies active client or member use rather than abstract interest. A contact that replied "this is useful, will share" earns Factor B = 1. A contact that replied "interesting, thank you" without specificity does not.

**Factor C — Network Multiplier (0 or 1 point)**

Scoring threshold: the organization has documented national reach or a formal affiliate/member network that would receive the corpus through the adopting organization's internal channels, without additional direct outreach from the sender. Factor C = 1 if the organization has either (a) 10+ formally affiliated member or partner organizations in the same sector, or (b) a documented internal communications channel — newsletter, training infrastructure, listserv — that reaches 50+ persons in the target population.

Factor C = 1 is automatic for: CLINIC (400+ affiliates), NILC (national network), NLG (national chapter structure), EFF (amplification reach to technical civil society), FPF (training infrastructure for journalist organizations), IRE (NICAR conference pipeline to investigative journalists), and any national association with documented multi-state membership.

## Section 3: Tier 2 Messaging Strategy and Customization

### The Core Shift: Individual Use to Organizational Adoption

Phase 1 messaging is calibrated for individuals who will read, understand, and apply the framework personally. Phase 2 messaging is calibrated for organizational decision-makers who are evaluating whether to commit institutional resources — staff time, training slots, communications channels, and workflow modifications — to a new resource. The question these contacts are implicitly asking is not "is this useful?" but "does adopting this make my organization more effective at its existing mission, and does the commitment it requires fit our current capacity?"

Each sector gets a distinct primary angle. Secondary variants serve different entry points within the same organization (e.g., IT lead vs. program director vs. executive director).

### Law Schools and Universities

**Primary angle**: Curriculum integration and clinic case development.

The ELITE threat model is built on FOIA-obtained government contracts, federal court filings, and published procurement documents — the same sourcing standard required for law review articles and federal court submissions. The Carpenter v. United States decision (2018) left commercially-purchased broker-aggregated location data outside its holding; the ELITE system operates directly in that constitutional gap. This is live, unsettled doctrine with a documented operational system as the case study.

**Variant 1 (clinical faculty / clinic director)**: "The ELITE threat model documents an active system operating in the Carpenter gap — commercially purchased GPS data converted into deportation targeting scores without a warrant. Every claim is sourced to government contracts and FOIA disclosures, structured for federal court citation. If your clinic is working on Fourth Amendment or administrative law cases involving immigration enforcement, this is the factual predicate. I'm offering a 60-minute orientation session for clinical faculty."

**Variant 2 (curriculum/academic program director)**: "We have a documented threat model for a current active surveillance system — ICE/Palantir ELITE — built from primary sources at methodological standards appropriate for course assignment or research citation. The countermeasures section is calibrated to the specific documented attack surface, not generic hygiene. This is suitable for privacy law, cybersecurity policy, or immigration enforcement courses. Happy to provide a formatted teaching package."

**Variant 3 (research center director)**: "The ICE/Palantir ELITE documentation represents a case study in how commercial data broker deregulation becomes operational enforcement infrastructure. Every data flow in the system is traced to its commercial source with published procurement documentation. If your center funds applied security research with policy implications, this is a primary-source dataset available for secondary analysis and publication."

### Immigration Legal Aid Networks

**Primary angle**: Toolkit development and client briefing materials at intake.

The critical value proposition for immigration legal aid is that the threat model documents the specific data flows ICE uses to find clients before they walk into an attorney's office. Part 0 (data broker opt-outs) is designed to be walkable with a client in 20 minutes — no technical expertise required. An organization that integrates Part 0 into its standard intake checklist reaches every qualifying client automatically, not just the ones whose attorney happens to remember to discuss it.

**Variant 1 (executive director / program director)**: "Your clients are in the ELITE threat model before they walk in the door. ICE purchases their location history from smartphone app data brokers — without a warrant — and converts it into address confidence scores used to prioritize enforcement. Part 0 of our countermeasures guide takes 20 minutes with a client and documents the specific opt-out pathways, including the California DROP platform for residents without government-issued ID. If this belongs in your standard intake process, I'd like to show you how it integrates."

**Variant 2 (intake paralegal coordinator / training director)**: "We have a client-facing data broker opt-out guide built for individuals without technical expertise. It's been designed to work in a 20-minute intake conversation and covers the California DELETE Act DROP platform — which is the only documented opt-out pathway for residents without government-issued ID. I can provide a formatted version suitable for staff training and a facilitation guide for paralegals to walk clients through it."

**Variant 3 (network coordinator / CLINIC affiliate director)**: "The DocketWise breach in October 2025 exposed 116,000 immigration clients' complete case files — names, addresses, case details, law firm relationships. Our organizational security playbook covers the file security architecture that limits this exposure at the intake, storage, and destruction level. If your affiliate network needs a standardized file security protocol, this is a starting point for a CLINIC-wide policy."

### Activist Organizing Networks

**Primary angle**: Operational security protocols, steward training, and affinity group risk assessment.

Activist organizations face a dual threat: government surveillance of their activities and employer/institutional surveillance of their members. The three-layer organizing communication architecture in Playbook 2 (public messaging, closed coordination, leadership security) is not abstract — it is the specific operational structure that separates campaigns that maintain operational security from those that leak. The risk for organizers who conflate layers is concrete: employers use keystroke logging and email monitoring to detect organizing before legal protections attach.

**Variant 1 (organizing director)**: "The organizing security risk in 2026 is not just government surveillance — it is employer surveillance software detecting organizing activity before NLRA protections attach. ActivTrak and Teramind can flag organizing-related communications in real time. The three-layer communication architecture in our labor union playbook is the operational standard for campaigns that need to maintain security through the card-check window. I'm offering a 90-minute steward training module your lead organizers can deliver internally."

**Variant 2 (digital security trainer / communications director)**: "We have a three-layer communication architecture guide for organizing campaigns: public messaging (Signal groups, social media with privacy controls), closed coordination (Signal groups with 20–30 verified members, in-person meetings for sensitive decisions), and leadership security (encrypted phones, face-to-face for escalation decisions). The critical gap most organizations have is layer conflation — organizers posting about their role on personal social media, then discussing strategy in the same channels. This guide covers the compartmentalization protocols and the training module to get your members implementing them."

**Variant 3 (risk assessment / legal team)**: "The 2026 USDA Palantir workforce surveillance contract includes a $75M workforce monitoring component for real-time analytics. Federal contractors with union organizing drives are at elevated risk of biometric identification and immigration-specific targeting of immigrant workers. Before your next major campaign, a 2-hour risk assessment session using our organizational threat profile tool will identify which members are at elevated risk and what additional security measures are appropriate."

### Think Tanks and Policy Organizations

**Primary angle**: Framework adoption for policy briefs, institutional vocabulary adoption, and public positioning.

Think tanks do not need to be convinced that commercial surveillance infrastructure is a policy problem — they publish on it. What the corpus provides is a primary-source documented case study at sufficient granularity to serve as the factual foundation for a policy brief without requiring additional research. The ELITE threat model's FOIA-sourced procurement architecture maps directly onto active policy debates: the Government Surveillance Reform Act (June 12 deadline), state election protection legislation (7 states active), and ICE ICM biometric integration (September 2026 deadline).

**Variant 1 (research director / program director)**: "The ELITE system documentation provides a factual case study for commercial data broker deregulation at scale — sufficient for a policy brief without additional FOIA work. Every data flow (Medicaid records, DMV data, app SDK location data) is traced to its procurement source in government contracts. This maps directly onto the Government Surveillance Reform Act data broker loophole provision, which has a June 12 deadline. If your program is publishing on surveillance policy this cycle, this is the primary-source record."

**Variant 2 (communications director / senior fellow)**: "The corpus includes a framing on commercial data as enforcement infrastructure that may be useful for public positioning: ICE does not surveil communities — it purchases commercial data that communities have already been sold into, without their meaningful consent. This reframes the policy question from 'law enforcement access' to 'commercial data broker deregulation.' Your organization's language choices in this debate matter for coalition-building with immigrant rights, labor, and data privacy advocacy communities. Happy to discuss the framing in more detail."

## Section 4: New Materials and Playbooks Needed

### What Already Exists

The four organizational playbooks in `ORGANIZATIONAL_OPSEC_PLAYBOOKS.md` (NGO/nonprofit infrastructure security; labor union organizing and encrypted coordination; legal service providers and immigration attorneys; academic institutions and faculty protection) are complete, sector-specific, and production-ready from Session 847. These are substantive documents covering governance-level protections, staff safety, data architecture, subpoena response, and financial compartmentalization. They do not need to be rebuilt — they need to be supplemented with materials targeted at the organizational decision-making context (not the IT implementation context).

### Gap 1: Think Tank Integration Guidance

**What is missing**: A brief (500-word) guide on how policy organizations integrate the corpus framing into policy briefs without reproducing the entire threat model. The existing TIER2_MESSAGING_TEMPLATES.md covers how to pitch think tanks, but does not provide a tool for the organization's own research staff once they have accepted the corpus.

**Scope**: The guide covers (1) how to cite FOIA-sourced procurement documents in policy brief footnote style; (2) three sentence-level framing options for the "commercial data as enforcement infrastructure" argument; (3) which sections of the threat model map to active policy windows (June 12 GSRA deadline, September 2026 ICM biometric integration). This is not a full research brief — it is an in-house reference for researchers who will write their own briefs drawing on the corpus.

**Priority**: Medium. Needed before the first think tank or policy organization orientation session, not before the wave send. Estimated production time: 3–4 hours.

### Gap 2: Academic Institution Research Security Playbook

**What is missing**: Playbook 4 in `ORGANIZATIONAL_OPSEC_PLAYBOOKS.md` covers academic institution faculty protection at a high level. What it does not cover is the specific threat profile of university research groups handling sensitive human subjects data — the combination of NSPM-33 compliance requirements (mandatory for institutions receiving $50M+ in federal research funding), IRB data security obligations, and the new threat vector of federal defunding pressure on politically sensitive research.

**Scope** (~800 words): (1) NSPM-33 compliance as a security floor — what the federal mandate requires and what it does not cover; (2) data compartmentalization for human subjects research (pseudonymization, separate encryption for name-ID mapping, off-site backup for raw data); (3) government request response for research data — what is protected under IRB protocols, what is not, and how institutional counsel should be positioned before a request arrives; (4) a specific protocol for the scenario where a federal agency defunds research and requests data as a condition of defunding — the threat vector documented in the Stanford NIH case and the DARPA facial recognition suppression case. Playbook 4's existing faculty protection sections remain; this is an addendum for the research group context specifically.

**Priority**: High. Academic institutions are a priority Tier 2 target, and the research security angle is the entry point that distinguishes this corpus from generic cybersecurity guidance for universities. Estimated production time: 5–6 hours.

### Gap 3: Attorney and Legal Service Provider Client Protection Protocol

**What is missing**: Playbook 3 in `ORGANIZATIONAL_OPSEC_PLAYBOOKS.md` covers legal service providers comprehensively but at an organizational level. Individual attorneys — particularly solo practitioners and small-firm immigration lawyers — need a shorter, practitioner-level guide focused on client-facing data protection rather than organizational governance.

**Scope** (~600 words): (1) Client intake data minimization — what information to collect, how to store it, and what to destroy when a case closes; (2) the DocketWise breach as a case study — 116,000 clients exposed in October 2025 because immigration case management software was inadequately secured; what a solo practitioner can do differently (on-premise vs. cloud practice management, encryption at rest requirements); (3) client briefing language for the subpoena risk conversation — what the attorney is obligated to disclose about file subpoena risk and how to frame it without creating client panic; (4) the parallel construction risk — why privilege assertion does not prevent enforcement and what attorneys should tell clients about this limitation. This is written for a solo or small-firm practitioner, not an institutional legal director.

**Priority**: High. The DocketWise breach makes this newly urgent — attorneys receiving this guide in June 2026 are receiving it six months after a 116,000-client breach in their specific practice area. The timeliness makes adoption more likely. Estimated production time: 4–5 hours.

## Section 5: Timeline and Sequencing

### Week 1: Candidate Identification and Invitation (Week After Phase 1 Launch)

**Day 1**: Run the three-factor readiness scoring for all Tier 1 contacts using Day 28 engagement data. Generate four sub-lists: pre-contact (3/3), standard wave (2/3), conditional (1/3), exclude (0/3). This takes approximately 2 hours given 45 contacts. Update the provisional scoring table in Section 2 with actual Factor A scores.

**Days 2–5**: Draft and send personalized pre-contact invitations to the 3/3 list. No more than 2–3 invitations per day to preserve response management capacity. Each invitation: (a) references the specific Phase 1 engagement — name the section they asked about or the material they forwarded; (b) identifies the specific organizational playbook relevant to their sector; (c) offers a 60–90 minute orientation session (bounded time commitment, specific dates proposed); (d) names one concrete organizational outcome — "we can walk through how Part 0 integrates into your intake process" rather than "would love to discuss."

**Day 7 (Decision gate)**: Is at least one orientation session confirmed in the weeks-2–3 window? If yes, proceed with standard Tier 2 wave send. If no confirmed sessions: delay standard wave by one week and re-contact pre-list contacts who have not replied, using an alternative subject line referencing the June 12 GSRA deadline as a time anchor.

**Parallel to outreach**: Begin production of the academic research security addendum and the attorney client protection protocol (both high priority, needed before sector-specific orientation sessions).

### Weeks 2–3: Pilot Sessions, Feedback, and Messaging Refinement

**Week 2 start**: Send standard Tier 2 wave to 2/3 candidates. Message includes a social proof element — reference to at least one pre-contact organization that has confirmed adoption (name the sector, not the organization, until explicit permission is received).

**Weeks 2–3 (Orientation Window)**: Conduct organizational orientation sessions for pre-contact 3/3 organizations at a rate of no more than 3 sessions per week. Session structure: 15 minutes threat model overview calibrated to sector, 30 minutes countermeasures walkthrough, 20 minutes on how the organization will deliver this to its own population, 15 minutes Q&A. Send a written summary within 48 hours. Schedule 30-day check-in.

**Week 2 checkpoint (mid-wave)**: Tally response rates from the standard wave send. If below 15% acknowledgment: diagnose subject lines, personalization depth, and sector routing before proceeding to Week 3 follow-up. If above 15%: flag any 4–5 score contacts for expedited pre-contact follow-up.

**Qualitative feedback**: Collect specific objections, framing questions, and integration challenges from orientation sessions. These feed directly into messaging refinement for the Week 4 soft launch preparation.

### Week 4: Finalize Materials, Confirm Soft Launch Readiness

**Week 4**: Complete all high-priority material gaps (academic research security addendum, attorney client protection protocol, board-level briefing template if not already complete). Publish v1.1 of the relevant organizational playbook sections incorporating feedback from pilot orientation sessions.

**Week 4 soft launch confirmation**: Verify that at least 3 organizations have completed orientation sessions and at least 1 has confirmed intent to modify an operational workflow (intake checklist, training curriculum, communications protocol) to include corpus content. This is the minimum credibility threshold for a public Phase 2 announcement.

**Confirm co-promotion willingness**: For the 3+ confirmed organizational partners, request explicit permission to name them as partners in the public Phase 2 launch. This is the difference between a sender-credentialed launch and an institutionally-credentialed one. Even one named partner from a recognized organization (Georgetown CPT, EFF, FPF) transforms the public launch signal.

### Week 5+: Full Tier 2 Launch with Organizational Partnerships

Full Tier 2 launch proceeds once the Week 4 conditions are met. The launch is not a single send — it is a public announcement (Gist update, any associated social media or press outreach) that references confirmed organizational adoption, followed by a second wave of Tier 2 outreach to organizations not yet contacted, with social proof from confirmed partnerships.

The second wave targets: faith-based organizing networks (PICO, Evangelical Immigration Table, RAICES faith partnerships), labor legal networks (NLG Labor Committee, ILG national), and academic criminology departments (for secondary research use of the threat model).

## Section 6: Success Metrics and Contingency

### Tier 2 Success Criteria

**Adoption rate by organization type** (primary metric):

- **Minimum threshold** (proceed to Tier 3 planning): 3 organizations complete orientation sessions and confirm that they will use at least one playbook component in operations. Distribution across at least 2 different sectors is required — a single-sector success does not validate the cross-sector strategy.
- **Target** (activate secondary wave): 6 organizations with confirmed adoption, across 3 sectors.
- **Strong outcome** (public launch with named partners): 9 organizations with confirmed adoption, including at least 1 national network (CLINIC, NLG, AFL-CIO) and at least 1 academic institution.

**Secondary distribution** (network effect metric):

Secondary distribution occurs when a Tier 2 organization shares the corpus with organizations the sender did not contact, without prompting. Minimum signal for self-sustaining distribution: 1 inbound contact citing a Tier 2 organization as the source within 90 days of the first orientation session cohort. Strong signal: 3 or more such inbound contacts. If zero at the 90-day mark, diagnose whether organizations are using materials internally without amplifying (adoption without propagation) or not using materials at all (adoption failure). The two have different remedies.

**Media and institutional positioning** (visibility metric):

Coverage of the corpus — or of the ELITE threat model it documents — in any of the following constitutes a visibility signal: legal press (National Law Journal, Legal Aid community publications), security press (The Record, Wired, Ars Technica), immigration trade press (AILA publications, Immigration Daily), or academic publication citing the threat model as a primary source. Coverage is not a goal in itself; it is a byproduct of organizational adoption by credentialed institutions and a signal that the sourcing has held up to editorial scrutiny.

### Contingency: Lower-Than-Expected Engagement

**If pre-contact (3/3) organizations do not schedule orientation sessions within 2 weeks of invitation**: Switch from session-first to document-first approach. Send the sector-specific playbook directly without requiring a session commitment, and make the 30-day check-in the follow-up action. Some organizations will adopt materials without ever scheduling a call.

**If standard wave engagement falls below 10% acknowledgment**: The subject line or personalization is the likely problem. Conduct a diagnostic split test with the next 10 contacts: test a subject line anchored to the June 12 GSRA deadline vs. a subject line anchored to the DocketWise breach (for legal aid contacts) vs. a subject line anchored to the FISA 702 no-warrant-protection confirmation (for digital rights contacts). Use the highest-performing variant for the remainder of the wave.

**If no organizational workflow modification is confirmed at Week 4**: Do not publicly launch Phase 2. Extend the pilot window to 8 weeks. Use the additional time to deepen engagement with the 3/3 pre-contact list through secondary touchpoints: share the quarterly threat model update (identifying new developments since Phase 1 launch), offer to co-author a short case study with one organization on how they implemented Part 0 at intake. A co-authored case study is a stronger credibility signal than any number of internal adoptions, and it creates a co-promotion relationship the organization has a stake in.

**If a confirmed partner retracts**: Document the reason without pressure. Retraction is most commonly organizational bandwidth (not skepticism about the content). Maintain the relationship by sending the quarterly threat update without any ask. Organizations that retract due to capacity constraints frequently re-engage when capacity frees up — which typically happens after a major campaign, a funding cycle close, or a leadership change. The relationship is an asset regardless of the timing of adoption.
