# Decision Tree Prototype: Which Security Guide Do You Need?

**How to use this**: Answer the first question. Follow the path that matches your situation. Each path ends at a specific, printable one-page guide you can use immediately. You do not need to read any other document unless you want more depth.

**If you are in immediate danger**: If you are afraid someone will find you right now, call 911 or text "START" to 88788 (National Domestic Violence Hotline). Do not read this guide first.

---

## START HERE

**Question 1: Which of these best describes why you are reading this?**

- A. I am worried about immigration enforcement — ICE, deportation, or surveillance of my community. **Go to Branch A.**
- B. I think my ex-partner, spouse, or someone I live with is tracking or monitoring me. **Go to Branch B.**
- C. I organize workers, lead a union campaign, or participate in protests or political organizing. **Go to Branch C.**
- D. I am a journalist, filmmaker, or researcher who works with confidential sources. **Go to Branch D.**
- E. I work in elections — I run an election office, I'm a poll worker, or I work for a state or county election authority. **Go to Branch E.**
- F. None of these, but I want to protect my privacy. **Go to Branch F.**

## Branch A: Immigration Enforcement Concern

**Question A1: Is anyone in your household currently facing a deportation case, an ICE encounter, or an active immigration enforcement action?**

- Yes, there is an active enforcement situation. **Go to A2.**
- No, I want to take precautions before anything happens. **Go to A3.**

**A2 — Active enforcement situation**

This is urgent. Your highest-priority actions right now:

1. Do not open the door to anyone who does not present a warrant signed by a federal judge. An ICE administrative order of removal is not a warrant. Know your rights.
2. Power off your phone or put it in airplane mode if you are near an area where ICE is operating. Location data from your phone can be accessed without a warrant.
3. Contact an immigration attorney or legal aid organization immediately. The ILRC (Immigrant Legal Resource Center) can connect you with local legal help: ilrc.org.

**End state**: Use the **Immigrant Worker Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-immigrant-worker.md`) for full actions. If you have a facilitator available, the Phase 2 Immigration Surveillance Evasion Playbook (`phase-2-immigration-surveillance-evasion-playbook.md`) has detailed guidance.

**A3 — Precautionary (no active enforcement)**

You have time. The most valuable precautionary actions are:

1. Data broker opt-outs — removing your address from LexisNexis, Spokeo, and Whitepages reduces your "address confidence score" in ICE's targeting system.
2. Turning off location sharing and removing your home address from Google Maps and Apple Maps.
3. Reviewing what is visible on your social media profiles.

**End state**: Use the **Immigrant Worker Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-immigrant-worker.md`). Spanish version available: `quick-start-immigrant-worker-es.pdf`.

**Accessibility note**: This guide is available in Spanish. If you need a different language, contact your local immigrant rights organization, which may have facilitators who can walk through it with you.

## Branch B: Partner Surveillance / Domestic Violence

**SAFETY WARNING — READ THIS FIRST**: If someone is monitoring your devices, reading this guide on a device they have access to may alert them. Before continuing: if possible, use a device they do not know about (a library computer, a friend's phone, a new device). If that is not possible, you can read this, but delete your browser history when you are done.

**Question B1: Are you currently living with the person who may be monitoring you?**

- Yes, I still live with them. **Go to B2.**
- No, I have left or am in a safe location. **Go to B3.**

**B2 — Still living with the person**

Do not make any changes to your device or accounts right now. Changes that cut off their surveillance access may escalate danger. Your first step is to contact a domestic violence advocate who can help you plan your next steps safely.

**Immediate action**: Call or text the National DV Hotline — 1-800-799-7233 or text "START" to 88788. You can also chat silently at thehotline.org. Ask specifically about technology safety planning.

**End state**: After speaking with an advocate, use the **DV Survivor Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-dv-survivor.md`) together with them, in the order they recommend.

**B3 — In a safe location**

You are in a safer position to take action. The most important steps now are:

1. Change all your passwords on a device the person does not have access to.
2. Sign out of all Apple ID or Google account sessions, then change those passwords.
3. Check whether you are still on a shared phone plan — the account holder can see call records for all lines.

**End state**: Use the **DV Survivor Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-dv-survivor.md`) for the full sequence of actions.

**Accessibility note**: The National DV Hotline (1-800-799-7233) has Spanish-speaking advocates available 24/7.

## Branch C: Organizing and Protest Security

**Question C1: Which of these best describes your situation?**

- C-i. I am organizing a union campaign at my workplace or leading a worker organizing drive.
- C-ii. I organize political protests, rallies, or community actions.
- C-iii. Both — I do union organizing and I participate in protests.

**C-i — Union organizing**

Your primary concern is employer surveillance during an organizing campaign and protecting your committee members before a petition is filed. The employer's anti-union consultants may be monitoring company email, Slack, and social media for organizing activity.

**End state**: Use the **Labor Organizer Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-labor-organizer.md`). For more depth: Phase 2 Activist Organizing Security Playbook (`phase-2-activist-organizing-security-playbook.md`), Section 4 (communications security).

**C-ii — Political protest organizing**

Your primary concerns are social media OSINT, drone surveillance at protests, vehicle plate tracking, and account unmasking via DHS administrative subpoenas.

**End state**: Use the general principles in the **Labor Organizer Quick-Start Guide** for communications, and the **General Public Quick-Start Guide** for device baseline. For much more depth: Phase 2 Activist Organizing Security Playbook, Sections 1–6.

**C-iii — Both**

Start with the **Labor Organizer Quick-Start Guide** for your organizing communications, then add the protest-specific countermeasures from the Phase 2 Activist Organizing Security Playbook (Section 4.2 for drone countermeasures, Section 5 for vehicle tracking).

## Branch D: Journalist / Source Protection

**Question D1: Do you currently have sources whose identity you are protecting, or are you planning to report on government, law enforcement, or politically sensitive topics?**

- Yes — I have active or potential sources. **Go to D2.**
- No — I am a journalism student or I want to understand the general journalist threat model. **Go to D3.**

**D2 — Active source protection**

Your primary risks: CBP device search at borders (no warrant required), FISA Section 702 warrantless access to your communications with foreign sources, National Security Letters compelled disclosure to your communications provider, and administrative subpoenas to social media platforms seeking to unmask anonymous sources.

**End state**: Use the **Journalist Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-journalist.md`) for immediate actions, then read the Phase 2 Journalist Security Playbook (`phase-2-journalist-security-playbook.md`) in full — particularly Section 3 (clean-device border crossing protocol) and Section 4 (SecureDrop and source communication security).

**D3 — Learning / general journalist security**

**End state**: Start with the **Journalist Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-journalist.md`) for baseline actions. The Phase 2 Journalist Security Playbook has full depth.

## Branch E: Election Worker

**Question E1: Are you a full-time election administrator (county or state election office staff), or a poll worker who works only on election days?**

- Full-time election administrator. **Go to E2.**
- Poll worker / temporary staff. **Go to E3.**

**E2 — Election administrator**

Your primary risks are phishing targeting election management system credentials, ransomware, and credential theft. The CISA workforce reductions have reduced federal support; your office now carries more of this responsibility independently.

**End state**: Use the **Election Worker Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-election-worker.md`) for office-wide baseline, and contact the EAC (eac.gov) for their cybersecurity training library.

**E3 — Poll worker**

Your primary responsibilities are not connecting personal devices to election networks, not discussing sensitive election logistics on personal communications apps, and knowing who to contact if you observe suspicious activity on election day.

**End state**: Use the **Election Worker Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-election-worker.md`), specifically the Poll Worker section (Page 1, bottom half).

## Branch F: General Public / Privacy Concerns

You do not need to be under active surveillance to benefit from basic privacy practices. The three highest-impact actions for the general public are:

1. **Data broker opt-outs**: Removing your home address from Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, and similar sites reduces your exposure to commercial surveillance. It takes about 45 minutes and costs nothing. Start at OptOutPrescreen.com.
2. **Signal for sensitive conversations**: If you discuss anything you wouldn't want read in court — health matters, finances, legal matters, relationship problems — use Signal instead of SMS or regular phone calls.
3. **Disappearing messages**: Set Signal to delete messages after 1 week by default. This protects you if your phone is ever seized or accessed without your permission.

**End state**: Use the **General Public Quick-Start Guide** (`quick-start-general-public.md`) for step-by-step instructions on all three actions.

## Accessibility Notes (For Facilitators Distributing This Tree)

**Non-technical audiences**: When printing this tree for community distribution, consider using the simplified flowchart version (PDF) rather than the full text version. The flowchart uses icons and single-sentence questions.

**Spanish language**: Spanish versions of all six quick-start guides are available at the same distribution URL as English versions. Ask for the `-es.pdf` version of any guide.

**Low-literacy audiences**: For communities where written guides will not be effective, this tree works best as a verbal script for a facilitator, walking one person through the questions in conversation. The end state for each branch is a one-page printable guide that the facilitator can leave with the individual.

**Screen reader / accessibility**: This linear text version is screen-reader compatible. The interactive web version (when published) will include full ARIA labels and keyboard navigation. The PDF flowchart version is visual only — use this text version for screen reader access.

**Languages other than Spanish**: The corpus does not currently have translations beyond English and Spanish. Organizations that serve communities with other primary languages (Haitian Creole, Mandarin, Vietnamese, indigenous languages) should contact a trusted community translator to adapt the quick-start guides for their specific population.
