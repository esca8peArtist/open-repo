# Phase 2 Organizational Launch Strategy
## Tier 1 → Tier 2 Transition, Organizational Adoption, and Playbook Sequencing

**Bottom line**: Phase 2 launch is not a separate campaign — it is a natural extension of Tier 1 relationships already forming. The transition decision happens at the May 28 checkpoint: any Tier 1 organization that has scored 3/3 on the three-factor readiness framework becomes a Tier 2 organizational candidate, contacted individually before the Tier 2 wave send. The first two Tier 2 playbooks (immigration surveillance evasion and activist organizing security) must be ready before May 28 — they already are. The value proposition to organizational partners is not "distribute our materials" but "we will customize for your specific sector context and support your integration process." Time commitment per organization is bounded: 2–4 hours/month for the first six months, then 1–2 hours/month. The November 2026 checkpoint determines whether the distribution is self-sustaining.

---

## Part 1: Tier 1 → Tier 2 Transition Logic

### The Three-Factor Readiness Score (Applied)

The transition framework from `tier-1-success-metrics.md` Section 5 gives each organization a 0–3 readiness score at the Day 28 checkpoint. The scoring:

- **Factor 1 — Engagement depth** (0 or 1): Score 3–5 on the 0–5 engagement scale = 1 point; Score 0–2 = 0 points
- **Factor 2 — Integration signal** (0 or 1): Score 4 or 5 (integration signal confirmed or adoption confirmed) = 1 point; Score 3 only = 0 points
- **Factor 3 — Network multiplier** (0 or 1): National or cross-sector reach, or documented ability to make warm referrals to 10+ organizations = 1 point; local/single-city reach = 0 points

A 3/3 score means the organization has confirmed reading, confirmed integration interest or adoption, and reach beyond its own immediate network. These are the pre-contact candidates — the organizations contacted personally, before the Tier 2 wave, with a request to serve as early reviewers and adoption partners. Expect 3–8 organizations in this category from a 45-organization Tier 1 cohort at realistic engagement rates.

A 2/3 score means the organization is confirmed reading with either integration signal or network multiplier, but not both. These organizations receive Tier 2 materials as part of the standard wave send, not a personalized pre-contact.

### The May 28 Checkpoint

The May 28 checkpoint replaces the Day 28 readout described in `tier-1-success-metrics.md` — it is the same event, specific to the Phase 1 launch date of May 8. At May 28, twenty days into outreach:

**What to run**:
1. Score all 45 contacts at their current state (some will not yet be at Day 14 — use best available data, noting trajectory)
2. Apply the three-factor readiness score to all contacts at Score 3 or higher
3. Generate the four sub-lists: pre-contact (3/3), standard Tier 2 (2/3), conditional (1/3), exclude (0/3 + declines + bounces)
4. Extract the social proof inventory: which Score 4–5 organizations, with what adoption signal, can be referenced in Tier 2 outreach to give it credibility?
5. Identify sector diversity in the pre-contact list — aim for at least one direct-service organization (1A/1B/1C), one digital rights organization, and one journalist or academic contact

**Important caveat**: Academic program contacts should not be finalized as non-responsive at Day 20. Per the sector-specific guidance in `tier-1-success-metrics.md`, academic timelines extend to Day 28 and beyond. Mark these as "Hold — academic latency" and exclude from the pre-contact list unless a reply has already arrived.

### What "Organizational Adoption" Looks Like by Sector

These definitions distinguish organizational adoption from individual engagement. An individual reading and using the corpus is a Tier 1 success. Organizational adoption means the institution has embedded the corpus into its operational infrastructure in a way that will persist beyond the initial contact.

**Immigration legal aid organizations (Tier 1A)**:

Adoption has two valid forms. The clinic model means the organization incorporates specific corpus sections into client intake — Part 0 data broker opt-outs become a standard intake checklist item delivered by intake staff to every client meeting the threat criteria, not a reference linked in a newsletter. The practitioner toolkit model means the organization distributes the corpus to its legal staff as a standing resource alongside its Know Your Rights materials, with at least one attorney or paralegal who has read it and can answer client questions. The strongest adoption signal is a named staff person who has walked at least three clients through Part 0 and can report what worked and what needed translation. This is measurable, not just aspirational.

**Community-based organizations and mutual aid networks (Tier 1B/1C)**:

Adoption means the corpus is incorporated into an existing operational workflow — a Know Your Rights training session, a newcomer orientation, a community security night. The distinguishing signal from individual sharing is that the organization has made a distribution decision, not just a forwarding decision: someone with institutional authority decided this resource belongs in a program that will use it repeatedly. Translation of Part 0 into Spanish or another community language is always Score 5 and always qualifies as organizational adoption regardless of other signals.

**Digital rights organizations (Tier 2, amplifier cohort)**:

Adoption at this level does not mean they distribute to clients — they do not have clients in the Tier 1 sense. Adoption means the corpus is cited in published policy work, incorporated into advocacy campaign materials, or referenced in a litigation filing. The secondary adoption signal is that a named staff member (not a communications generalist) has engaged substantively and is treating the corpus as a source, not just a document. Ask directly at the pre-contact stage: "Would it be useful for your [specific campaign/working paper/litigation team] to reference this?" A "yes" with a named use case is organizational adoption.

**Law schools and academic programs (Tier 2, amplifier cohort)**:

Two paths. The law review model is a student note or faculty article citing the corpus — this requires no ongoing relationship and happens on academic timelines (6–18 months). The clinic model is faster and higher-impact: a clinic director identifies a Fourth Amendment, HIPAA, or state privacy law question raised by the ELITE documentation and takes it as a clinic case or research project. The clinic model requires an ongoing relationship because clinic projects need source access. Priority: the clinic model, because it creates a durable institutional connection.

**Union organizing (Tier 3, pre-distribution)**:

Organizational adoption means the union's organizing department or communications security working group incorporates relevant corpus sections into a member training session or internal communications protocol. The practical signal is a staff person who attended the corpus orientation session and subsequently presented it to their own team. This is not likely to happen within the Tier 1 window; it is the target state for Phase 2 labor outreach.

## Part 2: Organizational Outreach Strategy

### The Value Proposition

Why should an organization adopt the corpus rather than simply sharing it? The honest answer is that sharing it is fine, but organizational adoption produces better outcomes for the organization's own constituents. The value proposition is not altruistic — it is operational:

1. **Customized sector materials**: The six Phase 2 scenario playbooks address specific sector threat profiles. An immigration legal aid organization that adopts the corpus gets the immigration surveillance evasion playbook (v1.1, already production-ready) customized further for their state and client population, not the generic corpus. A journalist organization gets the journalist security playbook. Customization is meaningful — it means the organization can hand a client or member something that speaks to their exact situation without the client having to navigate a 2,000-line general-purpose document.

2. **Staff orientation session**: A 60–90 minute live session for relevant staff covering the threat model, the most applicable countermeasures, and how to walk a client or member through the key steps. This is not available to organizations that only share the link.

3. **Integration support**: For the first 90 days after organizational adoption, the organization can bring specific questions — "a client said the DROP platform wouldn't accept their document type, what do we tell them?" — and get a timely response. This closes the gap between distributing a resource and actually using it under field conditions.

4. **Feedback incorporation**: Organizations in active adoption generate ground-truth feedback about what works and what needs clarification. That feedback directly improves the corpus for the next version. Organizations that participate in this feedback loop are credited in the quarterly update revision notes (if they consent). This creates a minor reputational incentive and, more practically, ensures the next version is more useful for exactly their context.

### Offer Structure

The organizational partnership offer has four components:

**a. Sector-specific playbook** (pre-existing or custom). The organization receives the relevant Phase 2 scenario playbook as the primary deliverable. For Tier 1A organizations, this is the immigration surveillance evasion playbook. For digital rights organizations, this is framed as the full corpus plus a briefing on the most policy-relevant sections. The playbook is a concrete artifact they can put to immediate use.

**b. Staff orientation session** (60–90 minutes). Delivered via video call. Covers: the threat model in 15 minutes, the most applicable countermeasures in 30 minutes, how to deliver this to clients/members in 30 minutes, and Q&A. Maximum 10–15 staff participants. The session is not recorded (security practice, not just preference — recording creates a document that could be subpoenaed).

**c. 90-day integration support** (async, via Signal). A direct Signal contact for questions during the integration period. Not a helpdesk — responses within 24–48 hours for non-urgent questions, same-day for urgent ones (e.g., a client presenting for intake in one hour has questions about Part 0). Time commitment: 1–2 hours/month.

**d. Feedback loop** (structured, quarterly). A short survey sent 30 days after the orientation session and at the quarterly review checkpoint. Questions: what worked with clients/members, what needed clarification, what was missing. The feedback is incorporated into the quarterly update if it identifies a genuine gap or error.

### Channel and Personalization

Outreach to Tier 2 organizational candidates is individual direct contact — not a mass send, not a newsletter, not a form email. The message references the specific Tier 1 engagement that qualifies them: "You asked about Part 0 and the DROP platform's ID requirement — based on that conversation, I think your clients would benefit from the full immigration surveillance evasion playbook, and I'd like to offer a staff orientation session."

This personal reference is the core differentiator from the initial Tier 1 outreach. It signals that the invitation is based on their demonstrated engagement, not a spray of invitations to anyone who clicked a link.

**Timing**: Contact the 3/3 pre-contact list between May 29 and June 4. This is a 7-day window. Do not contact more than 2–3 organizations per day — the session scheduling and follow-up needs to be managed, and compressing too much outreach into too short a window creates capacity strain.

**Volume ceiling**: 5–10 organizational partnerships in Phase 2 is the right scale. Fewer than 5 does not generate enough feedback diversity to meaningfully improve the corpus. More than 10 in the first six months creates unsustainable support burden. The hard ceiling is 10 active partnerships at any given time.

## Part 4: Organizational Partnership Model

### What the Partnership Looks Like Operationally

**Onboarding** (Weeks 1–2 of partnership):
- Pre-session: send the relevant playbook(s) 5 days before the orientation session for staff to read
- Orientation session (60–90 minutes via video call): threat model overview, countermeasures walkthrough, client/member delivery practice, Q&A
- Post-session: send a written summary of key points from the Q&A, including any corrections or clarifications raised during the session. This summary is the primary reference document for staff who could not attend.
- Feedback survey at 30 days

**Active support period** (Months 1–6):
- Signal contact for integration questions (1–2 hours/month per organization)
- Quarterly update notification when corpus is revised (brief summary of what changed and why)
- One check-in call at the 90-day mark: 20–30 minutes, covering what's working, what's not, and any changes in the organization's threat context

**Steady-state** (Month 7 onward):
- Quarterly update notification
- Signal contact available for urgent questions, but proactive check-ins reduce to once per six months
- Annual: invite to contribute to the annual corpus review (15–20 minute written survey covering their sector's evolving threat landscape)
- Time commitment: 1–2 hours/month across all active partnerships at steady state

### Resource Commitment Estimate

| Phase | Per-Organization | Total (10 orgs) |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding (Weeks 1–2) | 3–5 hours | 30–50 hours (spread over 8 weeks) |
| Active support (Months 1–6) | 2–4 hours/month | 20–40 hours/month |
| Steady-state (Month 7+) | 1–2 hours/month | 10–20 hours/month |

The active support phase (20–40 hours/month for 10 organizations) is the binding constraint. This is why the volume ceiling of 10 organizations is firm. Exceeding it means either degrading support quality for existing partners or burning out capacity for the Tier 3 outreach track that needs to begin in June–August.

**Capacity management rule**: Do not onboard more than 3 new organizational partners in any given week. Onboarding compression creates a queue of orientation sessions and post-session summaries that cannot be completed at quality.

## Part 5: Success Metrics for Tier 2

### Organizational Adoption Metrics

|---|---|---|---|
| Organizations with staff orientation complete | 3 | 6 | 9 |
| Organizations distributing to members/clients | 2 | 4 | 7 |
| Organizations reporting implementation | 1 | 3 | 5 |
| Feedback items received | 5 | 15 | 30 |
| Feedback items incorporated into corpus | 1 | 4 | 8 |

### Sector-Specific Outcome Targets

**Law schools / academic programs**: By November 2026, at least one clinic director has either (a) identified an ELITE-related case or research project using the corpus as a primary source, or (b) confirmed that the corpus appears on a course syllabus or clinic resource list. Academic timelines are long — one integration in six months is a meaningful outcome given semester cycles.

**Immigration legal aid**: By November 2026, at least two organizations report walking more than 10 clients through Part 0. At least one organization has modified their standard intake checklist to include a data broker opt-out step. These are the signals that distinguish deep organizational embedding from one-time distribution.

**Mutual aid networks**: By November 2026, at least one network reports incorporating the corpus into an operations security protocol — meaning new members receive a security orientation that includes corpus content, not just a link. This is the difference between the corpus as a reference resource and the corpus as part of institutional culture.

**Unions and labor organizing**: By November 2026, at least one union's organizing department has delivered a member training session using corpus content. This is a longer-horizon target given the Tier 3 timeline; include it in the November readout as a forward indicator rather than a primary metric.

### The November 2026 Self-Sustaining Distribution Checkpoint

The November 2026 checkpoint asks one question: are Tier 2 organizations propagating the corpus through their own networks without ongoing support from the sender?

Evidence of self-sustaining distribution:
- A Tier 2 organization has referred the corpus to a new organization that contacts the sender directly, citing the Tier 2 organization as the source
- A Tier 2 organization has cited the corpus in published work that is then cited by a third organization not in the existing distribution pipeline
- A Tier 2 organization has trained its own staff who are now training other organizations in their network (train-the-trainer model)
- Gist view analytics show traffic originating from domains or regions not covered by the direct distribution effort

If the November checkpoint shows that three or more Tier 2 organizations are generating new inbound contacts via referral or publication, Phase 2 distribution is operating at the self-sustaining threshold and Tier 3 outreach planning can proceed with confidence.

If the November checkpoint shows zero self-sustaining distribution after six months of organizational partnerships, the diagnosis question is: are the materials being used but not being amplified (adoption without propagation), or are the materials not being used at all (adoption failure)? The distinction determines the response: adoption without propagation calls for adjusting the offer to include referral facilitation; adoption failure calls for investigating whether the sector-specific customization was sufficient or whether the onboarding model needs structural changes.

## Part 6: Risk Mitigation

### Org Fatigue

The primary fatigue risk is not receiving too many outreach messages — it is making commitments that cannot be fulfilled at quality when multiple organizations onboard simultaneously. The mitigation is the volume ceiling and the staggered onboarding schedule: no more than 3 organizations per week in the June 5–30 onboarding window, with orientation sessions scheduled at least 3 days apart.

A secondary fatigue risk is over-communicating with partner organizations after onboarding. The steady-state model (quarterly updates + on-demand Signal contact) is intentionally light. Do not send unprompted updates more than once per quarter. Organizations that have embedded the corpus into their operations do not need frequent reminders; they need to know where to find updated materials when the corpus changes.

### Quality Control

The risk is that organizations in active distribution misrepresent the corpus — claiming it covers situations it does not, overstating the certainty of risk claims, or using it in contexts for which it was not designed.

The mitigation is documentation, not enforcement. The corpus contains a confidence annotation system (Section 1 of the threat model flags confidence levels for each claim). The orientation session explicitly covers scope boundaries: "This is documented from public sources; it covers the ELITE threat model and these specific countermeasures; it does not provide legal advice; it does not cover all threat vectors." This scope boundary language is in the orientation session template and should be repeated in the post-session summary.

For organizations that are clearly misrepresenting the corpus (claiming, for instance, that following Part 0 guarantees immunity from enforcement), the response is a direct Signal message to the contact: "I want to flag something I noticed — it would help to add a note that Part 0 reduces targeting risk but does not eliminate it." This is a correction, not a retraction; the goal is accuracy, not control.

### Feedback Scope

Not all feedback is within scope to incorporate. The scope rule is:

**In scope for incorporation**:
- Factual errors in the threat model (a cited contract has different terms than stated, a FOIA document has been updated)
- Procedural gaps in the implementation guide (a step is missing, an interface has changed)
- Sector-specific context that is missing (a Texas-specific DROP alternative, a California Medi-Cal enrollment angle)
- Accessibility failures (a step requires a capability the target population does not have)

**Out of scope for the current corpus architecture**:
- Requests to add threat categories not related to immigration enforcement or civil society surveillance (e.g., requests to add a corporate espionage section)
- Requests to change the tiered countermeasure architecture to a single-tier "do everything" format
- Requests to add political advocacy recommendations or calls to action
- Legal advice or case-specific guidance for individual situations

The out-of-scope feedback is documented and addressed honestly: "That's outside the scope of this corpus, but [name of appropriate resource] addresses it." The corpus stays focused on its documented purpose.

### Timeline Risk: Late Phase 1 Launch

If Phase 1 launches later than May 8, the downstream dates shift accordingly. The critical path is:

- Tier 2 pre-contact candidates need 20 days of Tier 1 engagement before the readiness scoring can run. The May 28 checkpoint assumes May 8 launch. A May 15 launch shifts the checkpoint to June 4.
- The June 5–30 organizational onboarding window assumes pre-contact invitations sent by June 4. With a shifted checkpoint, the onboarding window becomes June 11 – July 7.
- The November 2026 self-sustaining distribution checkpoint is based on 5–6 months of organizational partnership. It can absorb a 2-week Phase 1 delay without changing — the checkpoint remains in November and the evidence threshold remains the same; it just reflects slightly less time.

The playbook readiness and the offer structure are not dependent on Phase 1 timing. They are ready to deploy whenever the readiness scoring identifies the right organizations.
