# Phase 2 Tier 2 Organizational Outreach Strategy

**Bottom line**: Phase 2 launch is conditioned on Phase 1 execution completing and producing at least one confirmed Score 4+ signal from any Tier 1 organization. Once that condition is met, this document provides the complete operational plan to move from individual-focused distribution into durable organizational adoption across four constituency types. The May 28 checkpoint is the key decision gate. Everything in this document is executable the moment that gate opens.

---

## Section 1: Tier 1 to Tier 2 Transition Criteria

### The Three-Factor Readiness Score

Each of the seven current organizational pipeline sectors is scored on three factors at the May 28 checkpoint (Day 20 of Phase 1 outreach). The scoring is binary on each factor — a point is either earned or it is not — and a total of 3/3 qualifies an organization for personalized pre-contact invitation before the main Tier 2 wave send.

**Factor A — Engagement Depth (0 or 1 point)**: Has the contact reviewed the Phase 1 materials substantively? Scoring threshold: a Day 7 or Day 14 score of 3 or higher on the 0–5 engagement scale. Score 3 requires at minimum: email confirmed read (open tracking or explicit acknowledgment), partial or full Bitly click-through to the Gist, and no bounce or decline. Score 4–5 requires a substantive reply, question about specific sections, or forwarding confirmation. Factor A = 1 only at Score 3 or higher. Contacts that opened and did not click score Factor A = 0.

**Factor B — Integration Signal (0 or 1 point)**: Has the contact indicated, explicitly or through behavior, that they would integrate the corpus into their operations? Scoring threshold: Score 4 or 5 on the engagement scale, specifically requiring one of — (a) explicit statement that they intend to share with staff, clients, or members; (b) request for additional materials tailored to their context; (c) question about Part 0 implementation that implies active client use. Score 3 contacts (read + clicked, no reply) do not earn Factor B. A contact that replied "this is useful, will share" earns Score 4 and Factor B = 1. A contact that replied with a specific implementation question earns Score 5 and Factor B = 1.

**Factor C — Network Multiplier (0 or 1 point)**: How many other organizations would this contact influence if they adopt the corpus? Scoring threshold: the organization has documented national reach or a formal affiliate/member network that would receive the corpus through the adopting organization's internal channels, without additional direct outreach from the sender. Specifically: Factor C = 1 if the organization has either (a) 10+ formally affiliated member or partner organizations in the same sector, or (b) a documented internal communications channel (newsletter, training infrastructure, listserv) that reaches 50+ persons in the target population. Local organizations with single-city reach score Factor C = 0. National associations, network organizations, and coalitions with multi-state reach score Factor C = 1.

**Readiness Score Interpretation**:

- **3/3**: Pre-contact candidate. Reach out individually, before the standard Tier 2 wave, with a personalized invitation referencing their specific Phase 1 engagement. This is not a form letter.
- **2/3**: Standard Tier 2 wave candidate. Include in the main send with sector-appropriate personalization, but without the intensive pre-contact investment.
- **1/3**: Conditional — monitor through Day 28. If engagement deepens, move to standard wave. If static, exclude from Tier 2 wave and revisit in October second-round outreach.
- **0/3**: Exclude. Bounce, decline, or zero engagement at Day 20.

1. **Legal aid organizations (immigration focus)** — Georgetown CPT and Harvard Cyberlaw Clinic are highest-probability 3/3 scorers given documented institutional alignment with the ELITE documentation methodology. Expected: 2–3 pre-contact candidates from this sector.

2. **Civil rights nonprofits** — ACLU Immigrants' Rights Project and NAACP Legal Defense Fund Digital Justice Program. Both already cite Palantir publicly; the corpus provides primary-source depth. Expected: 1–2 pre-contact candidates.

NDWA's overlap with the undocumented immigrant population is the strongest integration signal; AFL-CIO Tech Institute provides network multiplier. Expected: 1 pre-contact candidate (NDWA), 1 standard wave candidate (AFL-CIO).

4. **Academic programs** — CMU CyLab, UC Berkeley CLTC, Harvard Berkman. Academic latency applies: do not finalize readiness scoring for these contacts at Day 20. Hold all academic contacts as "pending — academic latency" unless a substantive reply has already arrived. Revisit at Day 28.

## Section 2: Tier 2 Messaging and Value Proposition

### The Shift from Individual to Organizational Framing

Phase 1 messaging is built around individual defensibility: one person, one threat model, a specific set of actions they can take to reduce their targeting risk. That framing works for direct-service contacts who will walk individual clients through Part 0. It does not work for organizational decision-makers who are evaluating whether to commit institutional resources — staff time, training slots, communications channels — to a new resource.

Organizational adoption requires a different value proposition. The question a program director or executive director is asking is not "is this useful for a person?" but "does adopting this make my organization more effective at its existing mission, and does the commitment it requires fit our current capacity?" The messaging must answer both halves of that question.

The organizational value proposition rests on three pillars:

**Pillar 1 — Your clients/members are already in the threat model, whether you address it or not.** Organizations serving undocumented immigrants, activists, workers in organizing drives, and domestic violence survivors do not get to opt out of the surveillance landscape documented in the corpus. The ELITE threat model is operational. The IRS LCA social-graph mapping is operational. The Palantir workforce surveillance contracts are operational. The choice is not whether the organization's population is targeted; it is whether the organization gives its population the tools to reduce their exposure. Framing adoption as "we now have an answer" rather than "we need to add something" reduces resistance from staff who are already overwhelmed.

**Pillar 2 — Organizational adoption multiplies individual impact.** A legal aid attorney who reads the corpus once and discusses it with a client is a Tier 1 outcome. An organization that integrates Part 0 into its standard intake checklist walks every qualifying client through data broker opt-outs, without requiring the attorney to remember to do it. An organization that delivers an annual security training session using corpus materials reaches its entire staff, not just the ones who happen to encounter the materials. The multiplier argument is concrete: one organization with 40 staff who each serve 30 clients annually represents 1,200 client touchpoints. That is the case for organizational investment.

**Pillar 3 — Sector-specific playbooks are already built; adoption requires integration, not development.** The four organizational playbooks in `ORGANIZATIONAL_OPSEC_PLAYBOOKS.md` were built to match the specific threat profiles of each constituency. A nonprofit executive director does not need to read the labor union playbook. A union organizing director does not need to read the legal services playbook. The sector specificity reduces the cognitive load of adoption and makes the resource immediately actionable rather than requiring adaptation.

### Sector-Specific Messaging

**Nonprofit Coalitions and NGOs**: The primary risk narrative is the IRS LCA social-graph mapping that treats financial relationships between organizations as investigation leads. A nonprofit that receives regular transfers from another organization under investigation enters the investigation graph automatically. This is not hypothetical — it is documented in published SPLC litigation and confirmed in FOIA releases. The value prop for nonprofit leadership: the governance framework in Playbook 1 (board-level cybersecurity committee, data classification policy, subpoena response protocol) is what good nonprofit governance looks like in 2026, not just security hygiene. Frame adoption as board accountability, not technical overhead. The message to a nonprofit executive director: "Your board has governance responsibility for client data security and government request response. We have a four-week implementation roadmap for getting the governance infrastructure in place. The two-hour orientation session covers what your board chair and IT lead need to know."

**Labor Unions**: The primary risk narrative is the convergence of employer surveillance and government financial targeting. Employer surveillance software (ActivTrak, Teramind) can detect organizing activity before the union has legal protection. IRS LCA financial mapping has documented acceleration of investigations into "left-leaning groups," with union financial accounts — especially strike funds — explicitly in scope. The 2026 USDA Palantir workforce surveillance contract adds a new vector: federal contractors with union organizing drives are at elevated risk of biometric identification and immigration-specific targeting of immigrant workers. The value prop for a union organizing director: the three-layer communication architecture in Playbook 2 is the operational standard for organizing security in 2026, not an optional enhancement. The message: "Three-layer communication structure — public messaging, closed coordination, leadership security — is what separates organizing campaigns that maintain operational security from those that leak. This is the implementation guide for that structure."

**Legal Service Networks**: The primary risk narrative is that legal files are the target. Subpoenas to immigration attorneys seeking client files are a recurring and documented pressure point. The parallel construction risk — where ICE initiates enforcement action using information obtained through other means and conceals the actual intelligence source — means that attorney-client privilege does not protect clients from enforcement consequences, only from having attorney files used as direct evidence. The value prop for a legal services director or clinic director: Playbook 3's file security and subpoena response protocol is the infrastructure that separates a reactive organization (one that receives a subpoena and has no response plan) from a resilient one (one that has a written protocol, counsel engaged, and privilege claims prepared). The message: "The question is not whether your organization will receive a government information request — it is whether you will have a protocol in place when you do. This is the protocol."

**Academic Institutions**: The primary risk narrative is the convergence of faculty targeting, foreign student surveillance, and research suppression. FBI field offices maintain files on academic researchers who work on government accountability topics — this is documented in FOIA releases, not inferred. International students and scholars face intensive surveillance under expanded SCOOP-successor programs. Research findings that contradict government policy have triggered defunding and file subpoenas. The value prop for an academic institution is institutional autonomy and research integrity. The message for a provost or general counsel: "Academic freedom policy is not operationally meaningful without the infrastructure to assert it. This is the governance framework, data security protocol, and government request response process that makes the policy operational." The message for a clinic director: "The ELITE documentation provides the factual predicate for cases or scholarship in an area where the constitutional law is currently unsettled. Carpenter v. United States left the commercially-purchased broker-aggregated location data question unresolved. This is the record."

## Section 3: New Playbooks and Materials Needed

### What Exists

The four organizational playbooks in `ORGANIZATIONAL_OPSEC_PLAYBOOKS.md` (NGO/nonprofit infrastructure security; labor union organizing and encrypted coordination; legal services and immigration attorneys; academic institutions and faculty protection) are substantive, sector-specific, and production-ready. The six individual-focused Phase 2 scenario playbooks (immigration surveillance evasion, activist organizing security, financial resistance, institutional whistleblowing, journalist security, DV survivor safety) are all complete at v1.0 or v1.1.

### Gaps for Tier 2 Organizational Adoption

The existing organizational playbooks are written for implementation by technically capable staff or leadership who will read and act on them. They are not written for the organizational decision-making context: they do not address how leadership presents the case to their board, how IT staff sequence implementation across competing priorities, or how organizations measure whether adoption has taken hold. Three specific material gaps need to be filled before Tier 2 organizational orientation sessions begin.

**Gap 1 — Board-Level Briefing Template (Priority: High, needed by June 5)**: A 1–2 page executive summary written for non-technical board members and executive directors, framing the threat landscape at the governance level and specifying the three immediate governance actions each organization type should take. The current playbooks are written for IT staff and department heads; board members need a different document. The briefing should: (a) state the threat in terms of organizational legal liability and governance risk, not technical detail; (b) specify the three immediate actions in plain language (establish board cybersecurity committee, adopt data classification policy, implement subpoena response protocol); (c) include a one-paragraph "why this, why now" framing tied to documented 2026 enforcement escalation. Estimated production time: 4–6 hours. References: `palantir-threat-model.md`, `2026-threat-landscape-q2-update.md`, Playbook 1 Section 1.2.

**Gap 2 — Staff Training Logistics Guide (Priority: High, needed by June 5)**: A structured guide for HR and operations staff on how to deliver the corpus content to their teams without requiring IT expertise. Current materials assume a technically capable reader. Organizations need a guide that covers: (a) a 90-minute training module outline with facilitator notes; (b) a pre-training device security checklist staff complete before the session; (c) a post-training action log (what each staff member commits to implementing within 30 days); (d) a follow-up assessment at 60 days. This converts the playbooks from reference documents into a trainable curriculum. Estimated production time: 6–8 hours.

**Gap 3 — Integration Checklist by Sector (Priority: Medium, needed by July 1)**: A two-page sector-specific integration checklist for each of the four organizational playbook types. The checklist converts the playbook's Implementation Roadmap (Weeks 1–2, 3–8, 9–16, Months 4–12) into a trackable action list with owners and completion criteria. Organizations can use it to verify implementation progress and provide feedback on what did and did not work. This is also the primary feedback collection tool for the 30-day and 90-day check-in calls. Estimated production time: 3–4 hours per sector (12–16 hours total).

### Domain Documents from Resistance-Research That Strengthen the Case

The following documents from the broader project corpus provide the evidence base that makes the organizational playbooks credible, and should be referenced in orientation sessions and the board-level briefing:

- `palantir-threat-model.md` — Primary documentation of ELITE system architecture; the foundation for the risk narrative in all four organizational sectors.
- `2026-threat-landscape-q2-update.md` — Current threat assessment; provides the "why now" framing.
- `ai-surveillance-threat-analysis.md` — AI-specific surveillance vectors including biometric identification and predictive scoring.
- `regional-threat-model-analysis.md` — Geographic variation in enforcement intensity; supports state-specific framing for organizations in high-enforcement zones (Texas, Florida, Arizona, New York).
- `may-2026-advanced-threats.md` — Supply chain and firmware threat vectors; relevant for the technology section of the nonprofit and academic playbooks.

## Section 4: Phase 2 Timeline and Sequencing

### Week 1: Candidate Identification and Invitation (May 28 – June 4)

**Day 1 (May 28)**: Run the three-factor readiness scoring for all Tier 1 contacts. Generate four sub-lists: pre-contact (3/3), standard wave (2/3), conditional (1/3), exclude (0/3). This takes approximately 2 hours given 45 contacts.

**Days 2–7 (May 29 – June 3)**: Draft and send personalized pre-contact invitations to the 3/3 list. No more than 2–3 invitations per day. Each invitation: references the specific Phase 1 engagement ("you asked about X / you forwarded to Y"), names the specific organizational playbook relevant to their sector, specifies the orientation session offer (60–90 minutes, bounded time commitment), and proposes two or three specific session dates in the June 5–30 window. Expect 40–60% response rate from this list given the warm context; schedule sessions as responses arrive.

**Day 7 (June 4)**: Decision gate — is at least one orientation session confirmed for the June 5–30 window? If yes, proceed with standard Tier 2 wave send on June 5. If no confirmed sessions: delay standard wave send by one week and re-contact pre-list contacts who have not replied.

### Weeks 2–3: Feedback Gathering and Value Prop Refinement (June 5 – June 21)

**June 5**: Send standard Tier 2 wave to 2/3 candidates. Message includes social proof element: reference to at least one pre-contact organization that has confirmed adoption (name the sector, not the organization, until explicit permission is received).

**June 5–30 (Orientation Window)**: Conduct organizational orientation sessions at no more than 3 per week. Each session: 15-minute threat model overview, 30-minute countermeasures walkthrough calibrated to sector, 30-minute delivery practice (how the organization will present this to its clients or members), 15-minute Q&A. Send written summary within 48 hours. Collect 30-day feedback survey scheduling.

**June 15 checkpoint (mid-wave)**: Tally response rates from the standard wave send. If below 15% acknowledgment: review subject lines, personalization depth, and sector routing. If above 15%: flag any 4–5 score contacts for expedited pre-contact follow-up.

**June 5–21**: Systematically gather qualitative feedback from the pre-contact orientation sessions. What did the sector-specific framing miss? What questions came up that the playbooks do not answer? This feedback feeds directly into the board-level briefing and staff training guide development (which should be completed by June 30 at latest).

### Month 2: Tier 2 Launch — Week-by-Week Execution (July 2026)

**Week 1 (July 1–7)**: 30-day feedback surveys arrive from June orientation cohort. Triage and respond within 72 hours. Begin threat model quarterly update research (Mobile Fortify, HART biometrics, post-SCOTUS DOGE/SSA status). Identify Tier 3 outreach contacts (labor organizations, faith-based networks) — verify staff contacts and draft preliminary outreach emails, but do not send until Tier 2 active support is stable.

**Week 2 (July 8–14)**: Process feedback from the 30-day surveys. Incorporate confirmed factual errors or procedural gaps into v1.1 of the organizational playbooks. Begin drafting the integration checklists (Gap 3 from Section 3). Reach out to the 1/3 conditional contacts from May 28 scoring — some will have moved to Score 3+ with more time. Offer standard Tier 2 wave materials without the orientation session pre-commitment.

**Week 3 (July 15–21)**: Complete integration checklist drafts for at least two of the four organizational sectors. Test the staff training logistics guide with one organization in the active partnership cohort — request a brief debrief on how the guide performed in an actual training context. Begin Spanish translation scoping for Part 0 and the nonprofit organizational playbook (highest-priority combination given the NDWA and CLINIC network demographics).

**Week 4 (July 22–28)**: Publish v1.1 of all organizational playbooks incorporating July feedback. Distribute quarterly threat model update to all active Tier 2 partners. Confirm Tier 3 outreach preparation is complete. Conduct 90-day check-in calls for the June onboarding cohort (if sessions ran in early June).

### Success Metrics

**Organizations**: Minimum threshold is 3 organizations completing orientation sessions within 6 months of Phase 2 launch. Target is 6. Strong outcome is 9. Hard ceiling is 10 active partnerships simultaneously — above that, support quality degrades.

**Staff Reached**: Minimum threshold is 30 organizational staff reached through orientation sessions (averaging 5 staff per 6 sessions). Target is 75 staff (averaging 12 per 6 sessions). Strong outcome is 150 staff across 9 organizations.

**Integration Signals**: By November 2026, minimum target is 2 organizations that have modified an operational workflow (intake checklist, training curriculum, communications protocol) to include corpus content. Strong target is 5 organizations with documented workflow modification.

**Client and Member Touchpoints**: Minimum target is 2 organizations reporting that they have walked 10+ clients or members through Part 0. Strong target is 5 organizations with confirmed client-facing use.

**Self-Sustaining Distribution**: The November 2026 checkpoint question: are Tier 2 organizations generating inbound referrals from organizations they contacted, without further direct outreach from the sender? Minimum signal for self-sustaining distribution: 1 new inbound contact citing a Tier 2 organization as the source. Strong signal: 3 or more such inbound contacts. If zero at November checkpoint: diagnose whether adoption without propagation (organizations using but not amplifying) or adoption failure (organizations not using at all).

**Feedback Quality**: Minimum target is 5 substantive feedback items received in 6 months. Target is 15. Strong outcome is 30. Feedback is measured by items that are either incorporated into the corpus (factual error corrections, procedural gaps, sector-specific additions) or documented as out-of-scope with a rationale.
