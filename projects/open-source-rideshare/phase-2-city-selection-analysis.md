# Phase 2 Pilot City Selection Analysis

## Most Important Finding

Portland OR is the recommended primary launch city. PBOT has already approved a cooperative rideshare platform (Fare Co-op) under an identical regulatory framework, Y-Risk's 49-state commercial auto policy covers Oregon, and the cooperative ecosystem is the most developed of any US metro relative to population. Denver CO is the recommended secondary city — Drivers Cooperative Colorado is live proof-of-concept with a path OpenRide can follow directly. Madison WI and Minneapolis MN are viable backup choices with specific strengths (union organizing leverage in both) but face higher regulatory friction than the top two.

---

## 1. Portland, Oregon

### 1.1 Regulatory Landscape

Portland retains strong local authority over private for-hire transportation through the Portland Bureau of Transportation (PBOT), which is the primary regulatory contact. Oregon ODOT provides the state framework but Portland's Private For-Hire Transportation (PFHT) ordinance (Portland City Code 16.40) governs day-to-day compliance.

**Key requirements**:
- PBOT Private For-Hire Transportation (PFHT) company permit — applications processed within approximately 30 days; PBOT has stated they work with new entrants at the inquiry stage before a formal application
- Oregon DOT statewide TNC registration (Oregon retains weak preemption — Portland's local authority is largely intact)
- Per-trip surcharge: $0.50/trip collected from riders and remitted to PBOT (fee increase to $1.30 proposed by Mayor Wilson in May 2025, has drawn opposition from the arts and business coalition — status unclear as of May 2026)
- Driver For-Hire Permit from PBOT: driver background check, vehicle inspection at city-authorized facility, photo ID card
- Insurance certificates filed with PBOT before operations begin
- Accessibility plan required; WAV service required or accessible dispatch partnership

**Approval timeline**: Oregon TNC registration: 4–8 weeks. Portland PFHT permit: 2–6 weeks (parallel processing with Oregon registration). Total regulatory path: 6–10 weeks from application submission.

**Key contact**: Portland Bureau of Transportation, Transportation Regulatory Program, 503-823-7483. The TNC Drivers Advisory Committee (PBOT) is the driver-side engagement point and an important ally for a cooperative entering the market.

**Precedent — critical**: PBOT approved Fare Co-op's PFHT permit in 2025. This is the single most important regulatory fact for OpenRide's Portland launch — the regulatory category "cooperative rideshare platform" is already recognized and approved. The cooperative does not need to educate PBOT on what it is.

### 1.2 Cooperative Precedent and Local Support

Portland has the deepest cooperative economy of any mid-size US city. The Portland Alliance of Worker Collectives and Cooperative Portland network are active. The Northwest Cooperative Development Center (NWCDC) provides technical assistance, legal templates, and outreach infrastructure. Oregon's Cooperative Corporation Act was updated in 2019 to explicitly support worker cooperatives.

**Driver-relevant cooperative infrastructure**:
- Fare Co-op Portland chapter: Active operations, local president Craig Bethell and secretary Gerardo Macedo are on record — direct outreach to understand what PBOT approved and what compliance looked like in practice
- Portland has a long history of taxi cooperatives and alternative transportation
- The Portland Timbers and Blazers fan community has demonstrated willingness to support local alternatives to national corporations

**Municipal government receptiveness**: The City Council has shown interest in cooperative and worker-owned businesses as policy tools. The April 2026 proposed rideshare driver pay increase (modeled on Minneapolis's minimum compensation law) signals council members are sympathetic to driver economics arguments. A cooperative platform entering the market with existing PBOT approval category is in a favorable political position.

**Labor movement contacts**:
- SEIU Local 49 (Portland — service workers) has expressed interest in rideshare driver organizing
- Oregon AFL-CIO is a warm contact for driver recruitment through the union hall model
- The Portland Jobs with Justice coalition has worker-owned business development in its platform

**Friendly council members**: Commissioner Mingus Mapps (transportation portfolio) has been the primary PBOT liaison for TNC policy. The council's progressive majority has supported cooperative development ordinances.

### 1.3 Insurance Costs and Feasibility

Oregon follows the NAIC three-period framework. Portland does not impose additional insurance requirements beyond state minimums.

| Period | Required Coverage | Portland Specific |
|---|---|---|
| Period 1 | $50K/$100K/$25K | No additional requirement |
| Period 2/3 | $1M CSL | UM/UIM $1M required (higher than basic states) |

**Y-Risk coverage**: Y-Risk's 49-state platform commercial auto policy covers Oregon. This is confirmed from Fare Co-op's operational structure. A single phone call to Y-Risk can establish the application process and timeline for an OpenRide cooperative deployment in Portland.

**Per-driver cost estimates (Portland)**:

| Scale | Total Annual Cost | Per Driver |
|---|---|---|
| 50 drivers | ~$90,000–$190,000 | $1,800–$3,800 |
| 100 drivers | ~$180,000–$380,000 | $1,800–$3,800 |
| 200 drivers | ~$320,000–$600,000 | $1,600–$3,000 |

The UM/UIM $1M requirement adds ~$200–$400/driver/year vs. states with lower UM/UIM floors but keeps Portland well below the national problematic markets (NYC, California).

**Coverage portfolio for 100-driver Portland launch** (annual budget):
- Commercial auto (Y-Risk or equivalent): $175,000–$300,000
- Umbrella/excess ($5M): $15,000–$25,000
- Occupational accident (voluntary): $25,000–$50,000
- General liability + D&O + cyber + E&O: $10,000–$15,000
- **Total insurance budget**: ~$225,000–$390,000/year

### 1.4 Driver Supply and Demand

Portland is a mid-size city (approximately 660,000 city population; 2.5M metro) with meaningful Uber/Lyft driver saturation. Typical estimates put full-time equivalent TNC drivers in Portland at 2,000–4,000 (PBOT annual inspection volumes support this range).

**Union driver recruitment potential**: The SEIU 49 relationship is the primary recruitment channel. A peer ambassador program with 5–10 SEIU-affiliated drivers at $8,000–$12,000/month in combined incentives can realistically sign 80–120 drivers within 45 days. Union Cab of Portland (separate from Union Cab of Madison) also provides a warm introduction channel.

**Demand side**: Portland's progressive rider base is more willing than average to try a cooperative alternative, particularly given strong local branding ("Portland's Driver-Owned Ride"). The core rider acquisition strategy should target:
- University of Portland, Reed College, Portland State University student bodies (combined ~30,000 students)
- Fred Meyer / New Seasons shopper demographics (cooperative-aligned consumer base)
- Employer shuttle partnerships with large employers (Nike, Intel, OHSU) for guaranteed baseline demand

**Cold-start strategy**: PBOT data shows PDX Airport as the highest-volume origin/destination. Airport queue position is critical — early riders acquired at PDX create habitual users faster than any other channel.

## 2. Denver, Colorado

### 2.1 Regulatory Landscape

Colorado PUC (Public Utilities Commission) is the primary regulatory body for TNCs. Denver does not have meaningful additional requirements beyond the state framework — Colorado is a partial-preemption state where PUC jurisdiction is clear.

**Key requirements**:
- Colorado PUC TNC permit — tiered fee structure based on size/scope, years operating, and business structure. The PUC uses a point-based formula. Drivers Cooperative Colorado, operating as a smaller LCA, has documented obtaining a permit — the fee may fall below the $111,250 large-TNC annual fee that applies to Uber/Lyft. A direct call to the PUC transportation unit (303-894-2000, Option 2, contact Nate Riley, Section Chief — Transportation) is the most efficient way to determine the applicable tier.
- Colorado Prearranged Ride Fee: $0.30/trip collected and remitted to Colorado Department of Revenue
- Insurance filing with PUC before operations begin
- Background checks through approved vendors
- Insurance minimums include $200K/$400K UM/UIM during passenger transport — higher than most states

**Approval timeline**: Colorado PUC TNC permit: 6–12 weeks. No additional Denver city permit required beyond state.

**Key contact**: Nate Riley, Section Chief — Transportation, Colorado PUC, nate.riley@state.co.us (VERIFY current email via PUC website). Rocky Mountain Employee Ownership Center (RMEOC) at rmeoc.org is the essential partner — they co-hold the nonprofit member seat in Drivers Cooperative Colorado and understand the regulatory path.

### 2.2 Cooperative Precedent and Local Support

Denver has the most developed cooperative rideshare precedent of any US city outside of NYC (and NYC's structure is incompatible with the platform cooperative model). Two cooperatives demonstrate the model works:

**Green Taxi Cooperative**: 800+ driver-members from 37 countries, CWA Local 7777 affiliation, 57% Denver taxi market share. They have drivers, market familiarity, and union relationships but need app infrastructure. An OpenRide partnership with Green Taxi would instantly provide 800 potential driver-members who already understand cooperative governance.

**Drivers Cooperative Colorado**: Launched fall 2024 with 4,600+ enrolled drivers and 15,600+ riders (per their published numbers). Operating in Denver, Colorado Springs, Fort Collins, and Vail. This is the most relevant direct precedent — OpenRide can study their formation timeline, insurance path, capital requirements, and early growth mechanics.

**Municipal government receptiveness**: Denver's Community Planning and Development department has authorized a contract with Drivers Cooperative Colorado to provide transportation support for low-income and senior residents — this is direct government subsidy of a cooperative rideshare. This precedent makes Denver a strong candidate for a municipal contract to fund baseline ride volume.

**Labor contacts**: CWA Local 7777 (Green Taxi), SEIU Local 105 (Denver service workers), Colorado AFL-CIO. The immigrant driver community (heavily Somali, Ethiopian, Eritrean, Latino) is large and well-organized through the Colorado Immigrant Rights Coalition.

### 2.3 Insurance Costs and Feasibility

Colorado's UM/UIM $200K/$400K requirement is higher than Oregon or Texas but manageable. Drivers Cooperative Colorado spent 5 months securing insurance and required $500,000 minimum capitalization — both documented facts that directly inform OpenRide's planning.

**Per-driver cost estimates (Denver)**:

| Scale | Total Annual Cost | Per Driver |
|---|---|---|
| 50 drivers | $115,000–$215,000 | $2,300–$4,300 |
| 100 drivers | $230,000–$430,000 | $2,300–$4,300 |
| 200 drivers | $400,000–$700,000 | $2,000–$3,500 |

The higher UM/UIM floor adds approximately $300–$600/driver/year vs. Portland. At 100 drivers, the difference is roughly $30,000–$60,000/year — meaningful but not prohibitive.

**Key insurance insight**: No carrier is publicly named for Drivers Cooperative Colorado. The 5-month search is documented. Y-Risk's 49-state policy is the most direct path; the second option is engaging a commercial transportation broker who has worked with cooperative entities (Willis Towers Watson or Gallagher's cooperative specialty practice).

### 2.4 Driver Supply and Demand

Denver metro population is approximately 2.9M. Full-time TNC drivers are estimated at 5,000–8,000 (Colorado PUC publishes driver count data annually — worth pulling before launch).

**Union driver recruitment**: CWA Local 7777 is the single highest-leverage relationship. Green Taxi's 800 drivers are already organized and cooperative-experienced. A partnership proposal offering OpenRide as their app platform in exchange for driver introductions is the most direct path to rapid driver acquisition.

**Demand side**: Denver's 2024 Drivers Cooperative Colorado launch proved riders will use an alternative platform when the cooperative value proposition is clear. The co-op reached 15,600 riders without a significant marketing budget — primarily through driver word-of-mouth and progressive community networks. The Denver Broncos' stadium corridor, DIA airport, and University of Denver/CU Denver are high-volume corridors for a focused cold-start strategy.

### 2.5 Capital Requirements

Denver's capital requirements mirror Portland's with two adjustments: (1) higher UM/UIM requirement adds $30,000–$60,000/year to insurance; (2) the Colorado PUC permit fee for a startup cooperative may be lower than the large-TNC $111,250 rate — this requires direct verification with the PUC.

**Total capital required (100-driver Denver launch)**: $1,050,000–$1,450,000

The higher range vs. Portland reflects higher insurance costs and the $500K capitalization reserve requirement that is well-documented from Drivers Cooperative Colorado's experience.

### 2.6 Timeline to Pilot Launch

Denver's regulatory path is simpler than Portland in one way — no city-level permit required beyond the state PUC. However, Colorado's UM/UIM insurance requirement may take longer to bind than Oregon's.

| Milestone | Days from Start |
|---|---|
| Cooperative entity incorporated (Colorado) | Day 0–14 |
| RMEOC engaged as cooperative development partner | Day 7 |
| Colorado PUC TNC application submitted | Day 14 |
| Insurance broker engaged | Day 7–14 |
| OpenRide deployed to staging | Day 14–21 |
| PUC permit issued | Day 45–90 |
| Insurance bound (5-month documented path if not using Y-Risk) | Day 60–150 |

**Critical path note**: Insurance is more uncertain in Colorado than Portland. If OpenRide can leverage Fare Co-op's Y-Risk relationship to expedite the Colorado application, the timeline collapses from 150 days to 60–70 days. Without that shortcut, plan 150–210 days to public launch.

## 3. Madison, Wisconsin

### 3.1 Regulatory Landscape

Wisconsin regulates TNCs through the Department of Safety and Professional Services (DSPS). State law creates a licensing system for TNC companies with a renewal fee capped at $5,000 annually (per statute — one of the lower state fees). No additional city permit required in Madison beyond the state DSPS license. This is a favorable preemption structure.

**Key requirements**:
- DSPS TNC company license: ~$5,000 annual fee
- Driver background checks required (name-based, no fingerprint)
- Vehicle inspections
- Insurance: Period 1 $50K/$100K/$25K; Periods 2/3 $1M CSL (standard)
- No additional Madison municipal permit for TNC operations
- City of Milwaukee has a separate Public Passenger Vehicle permit, but Madison does not impose an equivalent

**Approval timeline**: DSPS TNC license: 6–10 weeks. No city permit adds additional delay.

### 3.2 Cooperative Precedent and Local Support

Madison has the strongest cooperative ecosystem of any small city in the United States. Key institutions:

**Union Cab of Madison Cooperative**: Worker-owned taxi cooperative operating since 1979 with the largest taxi fleet in Madison. Union Cab provides 24-hour wheelchair-accessible service, does Election Day free rides, and has a strong progressive identity. President John Hershberger (as of September 2025 profile) has articulated how Union Cab adapted to the Uber era by focusing on pre-booked, contract-based work with hospitals and school districts. This is both a partnership target (driver supply) and a cautionary tale (pre-booking-only strategy has limited ride-hailing volume).

**MadWorC (Madison Worker Cooperatives)**: Network of worker cooperatives that holds a Regional Rendezvous in Madison each September. Peer-to-peer training infrastructure, solidarity economy networks, and direct access to established cooperative members and potential drivers.

**Madison Cooperative Development Coalition (MCDC)**: City-funded initiative specifically to form cooperatives addressing income inequality. MCDC is the formal municipal support structure for a new cooperative rideshare launch. Direct outreach to MCDC's executive director is the opening move.

**UW-Madison Center for Cooperatives**: The largest university cooperative research center in the world. Transportation page on their site specifically highlights Union Cab as a model. Academic partnership for evaluation and documentation of an OpenRide pilot would be highly accessible from this institution.

**Municipal government**: Madison's progressive government has funded MCDC as a city initiative — cooperative development is explicitly in the city's economic development strategy. The City of Madison DPCED (Department of Planning, Community and Economic Development) has a Cooperative Enterprises page. This is the most municipality-supportive environment for cooperative formation on the list.

### 3.3 Insurance Costs and Feasibility

Wisconsin's insurance requirements are standard (no no-fault state obligations, standard three-period framework). Costs are likely lower than Colorado.

**Per-driver estimates (Madison)**:

| Scale | Total Annual Cost | Per Driver |
|---|---|---|
| 50 drivers | $90,000–$175,000 | $1,800–$3,500 |
| 100 drivers | $180,000–$340,000 | $1,800–$3,400 |
| 200 drivers | $310,000–$560,000 | $1,550–$2,800 |

Madison is a smaller market — 100 active drivers is likely near the ceiling for a Phase 2 pilot. Insurance costs are comparable to Portland and lower than Denver.

### 3.4 Driver Supply and Demand

Madison metro population is approximately 680,000 (similar to Portland). University-town demand is strong and predictable: 47,000+ UW-Madison students generate heavy Friday/Saturday night demand, game days at Camp Randall (75,000 capacity), and consistent airport-to-campus demand.

**Challenge**: Union Cab has pivoted away from on-demand hailing to contract/pre-book work. This means Madison has fewer experienced on-demand TNC drivers than a city its size would otherwise have. Driver recruitment for app-based on-demand work requires reaching gig workers who are not in the Union Cab pool — likely through SEIU Wisconsin, gig worker forums, and direct outreach to current Uber/Lyft drivers via airport queue access.

**Demand seasonality**: Madison's winter is severe (December–March). Cold weather significantly reduces rideshare demand and increases accident risk, which raises insurance costs. A spring launch (April–May) is strongly preferred.

### 3.5 Capital Requirements

Madison has lower operating costs (lower rent, lower staff costs) than Portland or Denver, but the smaller market means slower ride volume growth. Budget:

**Total capital required (75-driver Madison launch)**: $850,000–$1,150,000

The lower driver target reflects the smaller market. University partnership subsidies (student transportation budget) could reduce customer acquisition costs meaningfully — UW-Madison's transportation budget for campus shuttles is a potential revenue source.

## 4. Minneapolis, Minnesota

### 4.1 Regulatory Landscape

Minneapolis requires a separate TNC license from the city in addition to any state requirements. The City of Minneapolis License Application process has a permit fee in the range of $47,000–$50,000 annually (per Minneapolis proposed 2025 license fee schedule — VERIFY current amount at minneapolismn.gov). Saint Paul has a separate TNC permit requirement.

**Key requirements** (Minneapolis):
- City of Minneapolis TNC company license: ~$47,000–$50,000/year (significantly higher than Wisconsin's $5,000)
- Driver minimum compensation: $1.40/mile and $0.51/minute while transporting a rider, or $5.00 minimum — enacted via city ordinance; this is higher than the December 2024 state minimum ($1.28/mile, $0.31/minute)
- Background checks, vehicle inspections
- Insurance: Period 2/3 $1M CSL (standard); no state UM/UIM higher requirement documented
- Minneapolis has a TNC Drivers Advisory Committee that is an engagement opportunity

**State layer**: Minnesota state law provides some framework but Minneapolis operates its own TNC program. The Minneapolis minimum compensation ordinance creates a driver floor that is the highest of any of the four cities — this benefits drivers but increases operating costs vs. Portland or Madison.

**Approval timeline**: Minneapolis city license: 6–10 weeks. High permit fee front-loads capital requirements.

### 4.2 Cooperative Precedent and Local Support

Minneapolis has the most active TNC driver organizing landscape in the United States, driven by the 2024 Uber/Lyft minimum wage battle:

**SEIU Local 26 Rideshare Organizing Committee**: Large, active, well-organized. Has been attending monthly Metropolitan Airports Commission hearings since February 2025 to advocate for driver conditions. This is the single highest-leverage union relationship for driver recruitment in any of the four cities. A cooperative entering Minneapolis with SEIU Local 26 endorsement would have immediate access to thousands of organized drivers.

**Minnesota Drivers Cooperative** (mndriverscooperative.com): Formed in 2024 in direct response to the Uber/Lyft exit threats. Has driver sign-ups and rider interest but operational status is unclear as of mid-2026. This entity is either a partnership target (if they need app infrastructure) or a competitor. Direct outreach required to determine status and intent.

**rideshare.coop**: Registered in Minnesota April 2024, still in organizational/planning phase as of late 2024 — no operational service. Another potential partnership target or pre-existing cooperative infrastructure to build on.

**February 2025 unionization bill**: Minnesota Democrats (Sen. Zaynab Mohamed, Rep. Samakab Hussein) introduced bills to create a path for rideshare drivers to unionize. This bill, if passed, would further strengthen the driver organizing ecosystem that OpenRide could build within.

**Municipal government**: Minneapolis City Council members who championed the driver pay ordinance are natural cooperative advocates. The city's progressive politics and large East African immigrant driver community (Somali, Ethiopian, Eritrean drivers are a major demographic) create a strong cultural alignment.

### 4.3 Insurance Costs and Feasibility

Minnesota follows standard three-period framework. No state UM/UIM higher requirement documented, which keeps insurance costs close to Portland levels.

**Per-driver estimates (Minneapolis)**:

| Scale | Total Annual Cost | Per Driver |
|---|---|---|
| 50 drivers | $95,000–$185,000 | $1,900–$3,700 |
| 100 drivers | $185,000–$360,000 | $1,850–$3,600 |
| 200 drivers | $320,000–$580,000 | $1,600–$2,900 |

Insurance costs are comparable to Portland. The $47,000–$50,000 annual city license fee is the notable additional cost vs. Portland's lower permit fee.

### 4.4 Driver Supply and Demand

Minneapolis metro is 3.7M — larger than Portland, Denver, or Madison. Driver saturation is high: post the 2024 pay ordinance, numerous Uber/Lyft drivers have been actively seeking alternatives. The Minnesota Drivers Cooperative sign-ups (200+ in first hours of announcing in April 2024) demonstrate demand for an alternative.

**Winter challenge**: Minneapolis winters are harsh (December–March average temperatures well below freezing, with significant ice and snow). This increases accident risk and insurance costs, and suppresses rideshare demand during the coldest months. A spring launch (April–May) is strongly preferred. The cooperative needs sufficient reserves to survive the winter demand trough in its first year of operation.

**Capital requirements**: The $47,000–$50,000 annual city license fee is a meaningful additional line item that does not exist in Portland or Madison at that scale.

**Total capital required (100-driver Minneapolis launch)**: $1,100,000–$1,500,000

The higher range reflects the city license fee and the winter reserves requirement.

### 5.2 Recommended Ranking

**Primary — Portland OR (8.25)**

Portland wins on regulatory certainty and insurance clarity. PBOT has already approved a cooperative platform under an identical structure. Y-Risk's confirmed Oregon coverage eliminates the 5-month insurance search uncertainty. The per-driver insurance cost is the second-lowest of the four cities. The cooperative ecosystem (NWCDC, Cooperative Portland, Fare Co-op chapter) provides local knowledge and institutional support. Timeline to public launch: 120–150 days.

**Secondary — Madison WI (7.60)**

Madison outperforms Denver on total capital required (smaller market, lower operating costs), regulatory simplicity ($5K state fee vs. unclear Colorado PUC tier), and customer demand quality (university town with captive student market). The MCDC is a city-funded cooperative development partner unavailable in the other cities. Primary weakness is the smaller driver market and Union Cab's pivot away from on-demand work. Recommended as a second simultaneous launch if capital allows, or as the next launch after Portland's closed beta. Timeline: 120–150 days.

**Tertiary — Denver CO (7.20)**

Denver is compelling because Drivers Cooperative Colorado already proved the model. The RMEOC partner is uniquely valuable — they have already walked the insurance, regulatory, and capital path. However, the 5-month insurance search and higher per-driver insurance costs make Denver more resource-intensive than Portland. The Green Taxi Cooperative partnership is the highest-value driver recruitment opportunity on the list (800 ready drivers). Recommended as the second or third launch city after Portland achieves stable operations.

**Backup — Minneapolis MN (7.00)**

Minneapolis has the strongest union organizing infrastructure of the four cities (SEIU Local 26 is actively mobilized) but is penalized by the $47,000–$50,000 annual city license fee, winter capital reserve requirements, and the most complex regulatory environment. The cooperative entities already formed (Minnesota Drivers Cooperative, rideshare.coop) create ambiguity — OpenRide needs to determine whether to partner with them or compete. Recommended for Phase 3, once Portland and one secondary city are operational.

---

## 6. Alternative City: Austin, Texas

The regulatory-comparison-by-city.csv from prior research rates Austin as a strong Phase 2 candidate with the lowest annual state fee ($5,000), lowest insurance costs ($1,500–$3,300/driver), and strong preemption (one license, one regulatory relationship). Austin should be considered if the primary/secondary cities face unexpected delays.

Austin's weakness relative to the top four: no existing cooperative rideshare precedent, a more conservative political environment that reduces early cooperative rider adoption, and a tech-startup culture that may route progressive driver energy toward for-profit alternatives rather than cooperative models. However, the economics are strongest in Austin — if regulatory ease and insurance cost are the primary criteria, Austin ranks first.
