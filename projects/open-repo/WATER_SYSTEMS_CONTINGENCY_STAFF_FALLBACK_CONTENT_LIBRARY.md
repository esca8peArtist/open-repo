### Procedure 1: How to Boil Water Safely in an Emergency

**Topic selection reference**: WATER_SYSTEMS_WAVE_0_SPECIES_AND_TOPICS_SELECTION.md — Topic 1

**Procedure name**: How to boil water safely in an emergency

**Scientific/technical name**: Thermal inactivation of waterborne pathogens

**Primary use**: Water safety—emergency purification when other methods unavailable

**What you'll need**:
- A pot with a tight-fitting lid
- Access to heat source (stove, fire, camp burner, etc.)
- Clean container for storage (glass, food-grade plastic, or covered pot)
- Timer or clock (to track boiling time)
- Heat-safe surface or holder (to safely handle hot pot)

**Key safety notes**:
- Boiling water is extremely hot (212°F / 100°C at sea level). Risk of severe burns if water spills or if you touch the pot.
- Do not attempt if exhausted, injured, or impaired.
- This method kills most pathogens but CANNOT remove dirt, visible particles, chemicals, or salt.
- If water looks cloudy or muddy, filter it first before boiling.
- Do not taste untreated water.

**Steps**:
1. Fill your pot with the water you want to purify. Fill it to at least 3/4 full so water doesn't boil over.
2. Place pot on heat source (stove, fire, or burner).
3. Heat water until large bubbles constantly break the surface—this is a rolling boil, not just steam or small bubbles around edges.
4. Keep the water at a rolling boil for 1 full minute if you are at sea level (0–6,500 feet elevation).
5. If you are above 6,500 feet elevation, keep water at rolling boil for 3 full minutes (water boils at lower temperatures at high elevation; more time is needed).
6. Remove pot from heat and allow water to cool to room temperature in a clean, covered container. This takes 1–2 hours. Do not drink water hotter than 140°F (60°C)—risk of burns.
7. Store cooled water in a sealed, clean container. Keep covered to prevent recontamination.

**How to know each step is done**:
- Step 3: "Rolling boil" = large bubbles constantly breaking surface (not small bubbles, not just steam from the lid)
- Step 4–5: Use a timer—do not estimate. 1 minute minimum at sea level; 3 minutes above 6,500 feet.
- Step 6: Water should be cool enough to touch the outside of the pot without pain (inside will be much hotter—test carefully).
- Step 7: Sealed container means lid is screwed on tight or pot has a lid that fits snugly.

**Why this works**:
Heat kills bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella, Cholera), viruses (Norovirus, Hepatitis A), and parasites (Giardia, Cryptosporidium) by breaking down their structures. At a rolling boil (212°F at sea level), most waterborne pathogens die within seconds. Higher elevations require longer boiling because water boils at lower temperatures, so more time is needed for heat to penetrate and kill pathogens.

**What this method cannot do**:
- Does NOT remove dirt, sand, or visible particles—filter first if water looks cloudy
- Does NOT remove dissolved chemicals (pesticides, heavy metals, salt)
- Does NOT remove chlorine taste (if present, boil water in an open pot and let it sit uncovered for 15–20 minutes to allow chlorine to dissipate)
- Does NOT remove radioactive contamination (in rare cases, do not rely on boiling alone)

**Where to use this**:
- Emergency situations: water supply is cut off, or tap water is contaminated by boil-water advisory
- Travel in regions where tap water quality is uncertain
- Field/wilderness where no municipal water is available
- Camping or outdoor activities
- NOT appropriate as a substitute if your tap water is already safe (most modern municipal water systems in developed countries have safety standards)

**Citations**:
1. CDC Emergency Water Disinfection — https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/boiling.html
2. EPA Drinking Water Disinfection — https://www.epa.gov/water-research/emergency-disinfection-drinking-water
3. NCHFP Water for Home Canning — https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_process/water.html

### Procedure 2: Simple Filtration with Sand and Gravel

**Topic selection reference**: WATER_SYSTEMS_WAVE_0_SPECIES_AND_TOPICS_SELECTION.md — Topic 2

**Procedure name**: How to make a multi-layer sand filter for water purification

**Scientific/technical name**: Multi-stage gravity filtration system

**Primary use**: Water purification—removes dirt and some pathogens in field/emergency contexts

**What you'll need**:
- Bucket or large container (5–20 gallons, with a hole drilled near the bottom for water to flow out)
- Coarse sand (washed beach sand or pit sand, grain size 2–5 mm)
- Fine sand (washed, grain size 0.5–1 mm)
- Gravel (pea-size gravel, 5–10 mm diameter)
- Cloth or mesh screen (to support sand layers and prevent mixing)
- Container for filtered water output
- Measuring tape or ruler
- Cloth or coffee filter (optional, for secondary filtration)

**Key safety notes**:
- Sand must be washed before use—contaminated sand defeats the filter's purpose
- This method removes turbidity and many bacteria, but does NOT guarantee pathogen removal—boil water after filtering for safety-critical use
- Sand requires replacement or cleaning periodically; buildup of debris clogs the filter
- Do not use sand from unknown sources (may be contaminated with chemicals or pathogens)
- Filter requires several minutes per liter—this is slow and patient work

**Steps**:
1. Prepare your sand: Wash coarse sand in a bucket of clean water 5–6 times, stirring and pouring off dirty water each time, until water runs clear. Repeat for fine sand. (This takes 30–45 minutes but is critical.) Allow sand to settle and drain.
2. Prepare your filter bucket: Drill or punch a hole near the bottom (1/2 inch diameter). Place a cloth or mesh screen over the hole on the inside to prevent sand from washing out.
3. Layer the sand and gravel in your bucket, from bottom to top:
   - Bottom: 4 inches of gravel (this supports the filter and allows water to flow)
   - Middle: 12 inches of fine sand (this does most of the work filtering out particles)
   - Top: 12 inches of coarse sand (this catches larger particles and debris)
   - Optional top layer: 2 inches of gravel (this protects the sand when water is poured in)
4. Pour untreated water slowly onto the top layer of sand. Water will percolate (slowly filter) down through the sand and gravel layers.
5. Collect filtered water in a clean container at the bottom where water exits the hole.
6. Flow rate is slow: expect 1–2 liters per hour, depending on sand grain size and how tightly packed the sand is. Do not try to speed this up by drilling larger holes or making sand less compact—slower flow means better filtration.
7. After filtering several buckets of water, you will notice the top layer of sand becomes darker (this is a buildup of filtered particles and biofilm, which actually helps filtration—this is normal). When flow rate drops significantly, remove top 2–4 inches of sand and replace with fresh washed sand.

**How to know each step is done**:
- Step 1: Water running off sand is clear, not cloudy. Sand has settled and drained completely.
- Step 3: Sand is packed loosely (do not compress tightly)—water should flow, not pool on top.
- Step 4–5: Filtered water is noticeably clearer than untreated water (hold both containers up to light).
- Step 6: Count drops or use a container to measure output—1–2 liters per hour is normal.
- Step 7: When output slows to less than 0.5 liters per hour, time to replace top sand layer.

**Why this works**:
Sand acts as a physical and biological filter. Particles larger than sand grain size get trapped. As water passes through the sand, bacteria stick to sand grain surfaces (attachment). Over time, a beneficial biofilm grows on the sand—microorganisms that actually consume contaminants. The combination removes 90%+ of turbidity and reduces bacteria by 90%+ (though not all pathogens are removed—viruses pass through more easily).

**What this method cannot do**:
- Does NOT completely remove viruses—boiling or chemical treatment is needed for viral safety
- Does NOT remove dissolved chemicals (salt, pesticides, heavy metals)
- Does NOT guarantee 100% pathogen removal (some bacteria and protozoa still pass through)
- Does NOT remove odors or tastes (except by indirect biofilm action)

**Where to use this**:
- Emergency/field situations where water is available but cloudy or visibly contaminated
- Longer-term scenarios (days to weeks) where boiling water is difficult
- Community water supplies in resource-limited settings
- First stage before boiling or chemical treatment

**Maintenance**:
- Top sand layer needs replacement every 1–2 weeks (depending on water quality and volume filtered)
- Full filter sand replacement every 2–3 months (sand becomes clogged with sediment over time)
- Biofilm is beneficial—do not try to remove it; it's a sign the filter is working

**Citations**:
1. WHO WASH Manual Section 4.5 — https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09
2. EPA Fact Sheet: Rapid Sand Filtration — https://www.epa.gov/water-research/rapid-sand-filtration
3. Practical Action Water Treatment Brief — https://practicalaction.org/knowledge-centre/technical-briefs/

**Word count**: 680 words | **Reading level**: 7th–8th grade | **Source status**: Public domain / CC BY (WHO, EPA, Practical Action)

### Procedure 3: Chlorine Disinfection for Household Water

**Topic selection reference**: WATER_SYSTEMS_WAVE_0_SPECIES_AND_TOPICS_SELECTION.md — Topic 3

**Procedure name**: Disinfecting water with bleach or chlorine tablets

**Scientific/technical name**: Chemical disinfection via sodium hypochlorite (bleach) or calcium hypochlorite (tablets)

**Primary use**: Water safety—kills bacteria and viruses in household quantities

**What you'll need**:
- Unscented, food-grade bleach (5–6% sodium hypochlorite) OR chlorine tablets (typically calcium hypochlorite)
- Measuring spoon or medicine dropper (for precise dosing)
- Clean drinking water container
- Chlorine test strips (optional but recommended)
- Timer or clock
- Stirring spoon

**Key safety notes**:
- Bleach is caustic and toxic. Never drink bleach. Avoid skin and eye contact. Use in a well-ventilated space.
- Do NOT mix bleach with other chemicals (especially ammonia)—creates toxic gas.
- Chlorine is poisonous to fish and aquatic life—do not pour treated water into ponds/aquariums without removing chlorine first.
- If water looks cloudy or muddy, filter it first before chlorinating (suspended particles prevent chlorine from reaching pathogens).
- Do not overdose chlorine—more is not safer.
- Treated water should smell faintly of chlorine (this indicates residual protection). If no chlorine smell, dose may be too low.

**Steps**:
1. If you are using bleach: Use unscented household bleach only (5–6% sodium hypochlorite). Do not use scented, "splash-less," or color-safe bleach.
2. If you are using chlorine tablets: Follow package instructions for your tablet type (different brands have different concentrations).
3. Prepare a dosing chart for your water volume:
   - **For clear water (no visible particles)**:
     - 1 liter (1 quart): Add 2 drops of bleach (or 1/4 of a small tablet)
     - 1 gallon: Add 8 drops of bleach (or 1 small tablet)
     - 5 gallons: Add 40 drops of bleach (or 5 tablets)
   - **For slightly cloudy water**:
     - 1 liter: Add 4 drops of bleach
     - 1 gallon: Add 16 drops of bleach
     - 5 gallons: Add 80 drops of bleach
   - **For very cloudy or muddy water**: Filter first, then use clear-water dosing above.
4. Pour the water you want to treat into a clean container.
5. Add the appropriate number of bleach drops using a medicine dropper (count drops carefully—do not estimate).
6. Stir well for 30 seconds.
7. Let the treated water sit for 30 minutes at room temperature before drinking (contact time allows chlorine to kill pathogens).
8. Test with chlorine test strips (optional): dip strip into treated water. Should show 0.5–2 parts per million (ppm) chlorine residual. If higher than 2 ppm, water may taste strongly of chlorine; if less than 0.5 ppm, dose may be too low.
9. Once water is ready (after 30-minute contact time), use or store it in a sealed container.

**How to know each step is done**:
- Step 3: You have calculated the correct dose for your water quantity (double-check if unsure).
- Step 5: Drops are added accurately using dropper (not poured or splashed).
- Step 6: Water is thoroughly mixed; you can smell chlorine.
- Step 7: 30 minutes have passed (use timer or clock).
- Step 8: Test strip shows 0.5–2 ppm (if using strips); water tastes slightly of chlorine (normal).

**Why this works**:
Chlorine (hypochlorite ion) oxidizes and destroys bacterial cell walls and viral protein coats, causing them to die. At the doses recommended (0.5–2 ppm), chlorine kills most bacteria (E. coli, Salmonella) and viruses (Norovirus, Hepatitis A) within 30 minutes at room temperature. Higher temperatures require less contact time; colder water requires more contact time.

**What this method cannot do**:
- Does NOT remove turbidity or dirt (filter first if water is cloudy)
- Does NOT remove dissolved chemicals or salt
- Does NOT kill Cryptosporidium protozoa effectively (if you suspect this parasite, boil water instead)
- Does NOT remove chlorine taste (if chlorine taste is too strong, let water sit uncovered for 15–20 minutes or boil for 5 minutes)

**Where to use this**:
- Emergency situations where tap water is contaminated by bacteria or viruses
- Camping, travel, or field use where water source is uncertain
- Temporary storage of water (for up to 1–2 weeks; chlorine residual dissipates over time)
- NOT appropriate as long-term storage method (use in combination with boiling for best protection)

**Citations**:
1. CDC Water Disinfection with Bleach — https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/emergency/create-safe-water.html
2. EPA Disinfectant Residuals and Regulations — https://www.epa.gov/water-research/disinfection-residuals-and-disinfection-byproducts-regulations
3. WHO Household Water Treatment Manual — https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-SDE-WSH-02.09

**Word count**: 610 words | **Reading level**: 7th–8th grade | **Source status**: Public domain (US government, WHO)
