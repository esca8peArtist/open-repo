# Nutrition 01 — Macro & Micronutrient Framework

**Reading order:** Start here, then proceed to Nutrition 02 (meal timing), Nutrition 03 (templates), and so on. This document sets the target numbers; everything downstream uses them.

---

## The Priority Stack

Before any specific target, understand the hierarchy of nutritional importance:

1. **Total daily calories** — the master variable governing weight change and energy availability
2. **Protein** — the non-negotiable; every other macro is flexible around it
3. **Carbohydrates** — the primary performance fuel; adjust to training volume and goal
4. **Fats** — hormonal substrate and micronutrient carrier; maintain a floor, don't slash
5. **Micronutrients** — the silent infrastructure; deficiency caps everything above
6. **Meal timing and supplements** — amplifiers, not foundations

If you get #1–3 right 80% of days, you will progress. Everything else is marginal gain territory.

## Part 1 — Protein

### Evidence Base

The landmark 2018 meta-analysis by Morton et al. (42 study arms, 723 participants) established the statistical plateau in resistance-training-induced fat-free mass gains at **1.62 g protein/kg/day** (95% CI: 1.03–2.20 g/kg). Updated 2024–2025 network meta-analyses confirm that gains plateau near **2.0 g/kg/day**, with marginal but still measurable returns up to approximately 2.4 g/kg/day in some populations.

For strength-focused athletes in a caloric deficit, the upper bound shifts upward: protein at **2.2–2.4 g/kg** preserves lean mass more effectively than lower intakes when calories are restricted.

### Targets by Goal

| Goal | Protein Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Hypertrophy (building) | **1.8–2.2 g/kg/day** | Maximizes muscle protein synthesis; upper end for hard gainers or older athletes |
| Strength (powerlifting emphasis) | **1.6–2.0 g/kg/day** | Slightly lower range acceptable when calories are generous; muscle gain is secondary |
| Conditioning / Endurance emphasis | **1.4–1.8 g/kg/day** | Sufficient for tissue repair; very high intakes confer no aerobic performance benefit |
| Body recomposition (deficit + heavy RT) | **2.2–2.6 g/kg/day** | Counter-intuitively high; necessary to preserve LBM when energy is restricted |
| Injury recovery | **2.0–2.5 g/kg/day** | Attenuates disuse atrophy; collagen synthesis also protein-dependent |

**Practical floor:** For any training goal, never drop below **1.6 g/kg/day**. Below this threshold, the body begins catabolizing muscle protein to meet systemic demands regardless of training stimulus.

### Protein Quality and Distribution

Not all protein sources are equal. Three factors drive quality:

- **Leucine content per serving:** Leucine is the primary anabolic trigger for muscle protein synthesis. The leucine threshold per meal is approximately **2–3 g**. Whey, eggs, chicken, beef, and casein all meet this threshold at standard serving sizes (25–35 g protein). Most plant proteins require combining sources or higher absolute quantities.
- **Digestibility (DIAAS score):** Whey and egg achieve DIAAS > 1.0 (superior). Soy scores ~0.9 (adequate). Wheat, rice, and pea score lower but are competitive when combined or supplemented.
- **Distribution across meals:** A 2024 review confirms that spreading protein across 4–6 daily meals (targeting **30–40 g protein per meal** for a 180 lb person) produces superior muscle protein synthesis compared to front-loading or back-loading the same daily total.

**Minimum effective dose per meal:** 0.4 g/kg (approximately 30–35 g for an 82 kg athlete) to reliably trigger muscle protein synthesis above baseline.

### Vegetarian and Vegan Adaptations

Omnivore targets apply equally. To compensate for lower leucine density in plant proteins:
- Add 10–15% more total protein (e.g., target 2.0 g/kg where an omnivore targets 1.8 g/kg)
- Prioritize soy, pea+rice blends, hemp, and seitan as primary sources
- Include a leucine supplement (2–3 g leucine added to low-leucine meals) if total soy-based intake is insufficient
- Creatine supplementation becomes **more important** in vegans because dietary creatine is essentially zero

## Part 2 — Carbohydrates

### Evidence Base

Carbohydrates are the primary substrate for high-intensity anaerobic work — the kind that dominates resistance training, HIIT, and sport-specific conditioning. Glycogen depletion directly impairs strength output, power, and work capacity. The principle of "fueling for the work required" (established in ISSN Position Stand, 2017; reinforced in Morton et al. 2025) underpins all carbohydrate recommendations: intake should scale with training volume and intensity, not be applied uniformly.

### Targets by Goal and Training Volume

| Goal | Carbohydrate Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hypertrophy (moderate volume) | **5–7 g/kg/day** | Replenishes glycogen between daily sessions; upper end for 5–6 day training |
| Strength (lower rep, lower volume) | **3–5 g/kg/day** | Lower volume means less glycogen demand; adequate for 3–4 day programs |
| Conditioning / Sport-specific | **4–6 g/kg/day** | Moderate intensity × high volume; prioritize around session windows |
| Body recomposition | **3–4 g/kg/day** | Reduced to create energy deficit; preserve peri-workout carbs |
| Endurance-heavy (>10 hrs/week) | **6–10 g/kg/day** | Maximum glycogen synthesis requirement |

**Adjust carbs — not fats — when changing calories.** Fat intake has a hormonal floor; carbohydrates are the lever for energy manipulation.

### Carbohydrate Source Quality

**Performance context (peri-workout):** High-glycaemic index sources — white rice, white bread, banana, sports drinks, honey, glucose — are optimal for rapid glycogen repletion. Fibre and fat slow gastric emptying and should be minimized in the 90-minute window around training.

**Base meals (away from training):** Moderate-GI whole-food sources — oats, sweet potato, legumes, whole grain bread, fruit — provide micronutrient density alongside carbohydrate. These are the backbone of non-training meal carbohydrates.

**Timing split (practical rule):** Consume approximately **60% of daily carbohydrates in the 3-hour peri-workout window** (pre + intra + post training). Distribute the remaining 40% across breakfast and the final meal of the day.

## Part 3 — Fats

### Minimum and Optimal Ranges

| Goal | Fat Target | Floor |
|---|---|---|
| All goals | **0.8–1.2 g/kg/day** | Never below **0.6 g/kg/day** (hormonal floor) |

Testosterone and other anabolic hormones are synthesized from dietary cholesterol and fatty acids. Studies consistently show that diets providing less than 15% of calories from fat impair testosterone production in male athletes. Chronic low-fat dieting — below 0.6 g/kg/day — carries measurable hormonal and immune cost.

**Fat quality matters more than quantity:**
- **Saturated fats (animal products, coconut oil):** 20–30% of fat intake; necessary for testosterone synthesis
- **Monounsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, almonds):** 40–50% of fat intake; heart-protective
- **Polyunsaturated fats — especially omega-3 (fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed):** Target 1–2 g combined EPA+DHA daily for anti-inflammatory effect on muscle recovery

**Omega-3 supplementation case:** Athletes who do not eat fatty fish 2–3×/week should supplement 1–2 g combined EPA+DHA daily (fish oil or algae-based). Evidence from Frontiers in Nutrition (2025) confirms omega-3s reduce exercise-induced muscle damage markers (CK, DOMS) and modestly improve protein synthesis.

## Part 4 — Micronutrients for Athletes

Most micronutrient literature uses sedentary reference values. Athletes have meaningfully higher requirements — particularly for minerals lost in sweat and B-vitamins consumed as cofactors in energy metabolism.

### Priority Micronutrients

#### Iron
- **Why critical:** Oxygen transport via haemoglobin and myoglobin; iron deficiency without anaemia still impairs VO2max and endurance capacity
- **Prevalence of deficiency:** 3–11% of male athletes; 20–40% of female athletes (higher menstrual losses)
- **Daily target:** 8 mg (males), 18 mg (pre-menopausal females), up to 32 mg for athletes in heavy training
- **Food sources:** Lean red meat (haem iron, 2–3× more bioavailable), chicken, oysters, spinach, fortified cereals; combine non-haem sources with vitamin C to enhance absorption
- **Supplementation threshold:** Supplement only if serum ferritin < 30 ng/mL (confirmed by blood test); ferrous sulfate 325 mg/day is the standard protocol; avoid co-ingesting with calcium or coffee (absorption blockers)

#### Zinc
- **Why critical:** Protein synthesis, IGF-1 signalling, immune function, testosterone production; sweat losses are significant in high-volume training
- **Daily target:** 8–11 mg (baseline); athletes may need 12–16 mg in heavy training
- **Food sources:** Beef, lamb, oysters, pumpkin seeds, cashews, chickpeas
- **Supplementation threshold:** Zinc glycinate or zinc citrate 15–25 mg/day if dietary intake is inadequate (common in vegetarians and high-endurance athletes); do not exceed 40 mg/day (upper limit; higher doses impair copper absorption)

#### Magnesium
- **Why critical:** ATP synthesis, muscle contraction and relaxation, nervous system function, sleep quality (GABA agonist and melatonin enhancer); athletes consistently underconsume
- **Daily target:** 310–420 mg (sex-dependent baseline); athletes often need 400–600 mg
- **Food sources:** Almonds, pumpkin seeds, spinach, dark chocolate (70%+), oats, black beans
- **Supplementation protocol:** Magnesium glycinate 250–400 mg taken 30–60 min before sleep (best tolerated form; avoids the laxative effect of oxide form)
- **Performance evidence:** 8-week supplementation studies show improved sleep efficiency, reduced nighttime cortisol, and modest improvements in strength endurance

#### Vitamin D
- **Why critical:** Calcium absorption, bone health, muscle contractile function, immune modulation, testosterone; deficiency is endemic at northern latitudes and in indoor athletes
- **Daily target:** 1,000–4,000 IU/day dietary + supplement to maintain serum 25(OH)D > 40 ng/mL (100 nmol/L) for athletes
- **Food sources:** Fatty fish (salmon: 570 IU/100 g), egg yolks, fortified dairy; sunlight (10–30 min/day of direct skin exposure is roughly 10,000 IU synthesized)
- **Supplementation protocol:** Vitamin D3 (not D2) 2,000–4,000 IU/day with a fat-containing meal (fat-soluble); more effective when paired with vitamin K2 (100–200 mcg) to direct calcium to bone rather than arteries
- **Performance evidence:** Meta-analysis (MDPI Nutrients, 2024) confirms vitamin D3 supplementation improves lower-limb muscle strength, particularly in athletes deficient at baseline

#### B-Vitamins
- **Why critical:** B1, B2, B3, B6, B12, and folate are cofactors in energy metabolism pathways that fuel exercise; requirements scale with caloric intake and training volume
- **Highest-risk groups:** Vegans and vegetarians (B12 deficiency virtually certain without supplementation); athletes eating low-calorie diets
- **Protocol:** A B-complex supplement covering 100% DV for all B-vitamins is adequate for most athletes; vegans should supplement B12 separately at 250–500 mcg/day (methylcobalamin form preferred)

### Calcium
- **Daily target:** 1,000 mg (adults < 50), 1,200 mg (athletes with high sweat losses or restricted dairy)
- **Food sources:** Dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese), fortified plant milks, sardines, tofu, kale
- **Note:** Calcium supplementation above dietary needs shows no performance benefit in athletes with adequate dietary intake. Only supplement if below 800 mg/day from food.

### Electrolytes During Training
For sessions > 60 minutes or in hot/humid conditions:
- **Sodium:** 500–1,000 mg/hour of sweating (roughly 1/4 tsp salt per liter of water)
- **Potassium:** 200–400 mg/hour (from food or electrolyte drinks)
- **Goal:** Maintain plasma osmolality; prevent hyponatremia in ultra-endurance athletes

## Part 5 — Peri-Workout Nutrient Timing

### Pre-Workout (90–120 Minutes Before)

**Goal:** Enter the session with adequate blood glucose and glycogen, without GI distress.

| Component | Target | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Carbohydrates | 40–60 g moderate-GI | Oatmeal + banana, white rice + honey, sweet potato, whole grain toast + jam |
| Protein | 20–30 g | Chicken, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, protein shake |
| Fats | < 10 g | Avoid — slows gastric emptying |
| Fibre | < 5 g | Avoid pre-workout — bloating risk |

**Hypertrophy goal:** 50 g carbs + 25 g protein (e.g., chicken breast + white rice)
**Strength goal:** 40 g carbs + 20 g protein (e.g., oatmeal + Greek yogurt)
**Conditioning goal:** 60 g carbs + 15 g protein (e.g., banana + honey + small protein shake)

**Fasted training exception:** If training more than 3 hours after last meal, consume 20–30 g fast carbs (banana, glucose tablet, raisins) 15–20 minutes before session. Even a small carbohydrate dose reduces catabolism and maintains glycogen availability for skill work.

### Intra-Workout Fueling (Sessions > 60 Minutes)

For sessions under 60 minutes at moderate intensity: water only is sufficient.

For sessions **60–90+ minutes** or high-intensity intervals:
- **Simple carbohydrates:** 30–60 g/hour (sports drink, glucose gel, raisins, banana pieces)
- **Electrolytes:** Sodium 300–600 mg/hour; potassium 100–200 mg/hour
- **Hydration:** 200–300 ml water every 15–20 minutes

For Tier 3 (full gym) athletes training in long sessions with high compound volume, intra-workout carbohydrate is not optional — it directly prevents the performance decline that occurs when glycogen drops below 50% saturation.

### Post-Workout Recovery Window (0–60 Minutes)

The "30-minute anabolic window" has been revised by current evidence. The window is more accurately described as a **1–2 hour post-exercise period** during which muscle protein synthesis and glycogen resynthesis are elevated. However, if you ate a substantial pre-workout meal within 2 hours of training, post-workout urgency is reduced — total daily intake matters more than exact timing.

**Practical targets post-workout:**

| Goal | Protein | Carbohydrates | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypertrophy | 30–40 g | 60–80 g | 1:2 (protein:carbs) |
| Strength | 25–35 g | 40–60 g | 1:1.5 |
| Conditioning | 20–30 g | 60–90 g | 1:3 (carbs prioritized) |
| Body recomposition | 35–45 g | 30–50 g | Higher protein to offset restricted carbs |

**Evidence note:** A 2025 Frontiers in Nutrition systematic review on post-exercise supplementation timing confirms that protein consumed within 2 hours of resistance training increases MPS regardless of exact timing. The pre-workout meal primes the same anabolic machinery as an immediate post-workout shake — so if the pre-workout meal was substantial, a post-workout meal within 2 hours is adequate.

## Integration with Workout Program Phases

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1–4, all tiers):** Start at the lower end of protein targets. The nervous system is adapting; protein requirements are not yet maximal. Carbs at the lower bound of goal range. Build the habit first.

**Phase 2 (Weeks 5–12, hypertrophy block):** Increase protein to upper end of target. Carbs at upper bound — this is peak training volume and glycogen demand is highest. Do not diet during this phase.

**Deload weeks:** Maintain calories and protein. Do not reduce protein during reduced training volume — muscle protein breakdown remains elevated for 48–72 hours after the last hard session.

**Sports-specific extensions:** Athletes adding sport practice to the gym program (Phase 2 extensions) are adding substantial aerobic and skill-based volume. Increase carbs by 1–2 g/kg to match the additional glycolytic demand.
