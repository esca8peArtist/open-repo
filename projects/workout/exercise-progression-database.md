# Exercise Progression Database — By Movement Pattern

This database organizes exercise progressions by fundamental movement pattern rather than muscle group. Movement-pattern thinking avoids gaps and redundancies: a program that hits all seven patterns below is structurally balanced. Each section covers: what the pattern is, how to progress it (load, volume, density, range, tempo), typical progression curves, plateau-breaking protocols, regression protocols, and realistic strength timelines.

---

## Pattern 1: Squat (Knee-Dominant, Bilateral or Unilateral)

**Definition.** Vertical displacement of the center of mass through knee and hip flexion, with the torso remaining relatively upright.

**Primary muscles.** Quadriceps, glutes, adductors. Secondary: hamstrings, spinal erectors, core.

### Progression Hierarchy

From easiest to hardest, with progression triggers:

| Exercise | Prerequisite | Progress To |
|---|---|---|
| Bodyweight squat | None | Goblet squat, box squat |
| Goblet squat | Controlled depth, neutral spine | Front squat, box squat with load |
| Box squat (high box → low box) | Basic barbell comfort | Back squat, front squat |
| Back squat | Hip flexion to parallel, ankle dorsiflexion >20° | Deeper ROM, higher loads |
| Front squat | Clean grip or cross-arm, thoracic mobility | Overhead squat (advanced) |
| Bulgarian split squat (BSS) | Goblet squat with depth | Loaded BSS, barbell BSS |
| Pistol squat | BSS mastered, unilateral balance | Weighted pistol |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Load.** The primary progression driver for loaded squats. Beginners should add 5–10 lb per session on back squat (session-to-session linear progression) until that stalls; intermediates progress weekly. Target: 2–3% load increase per week on successful weeks. Never increase load after a failed set or form breakdown.

**Volume.** Add one set per session or per week before adding load. Example: 3×5 → 4×5 → 5×5 → add load, return to 3×5. This volume-first approach reduces injury risk when approaching heavier loads.

**Range of Motion (ROM).** Deliberately increase squat depth over a training block. Box squat with a progressively lower box, or elevate heels temporarily to achieve depth while ankle mobility is limiting. More ROM at a given load is mechanically harder; treat depth increases as equivalent to load increases — not both in the same session.

**Tempo.** Adding a controlled eccentric (3–4 seconds down) increases time under tension and mechanical stress without adding load. Particularly useful when form is good but load increases feel premature. Example progression: 2-0-1 (2-sec down, 0 pause, 1-sec up) → 3-1-1 → 4-2-1 (full paused squat).

**Density.** Reduce rest periods by 15–30 seconds per week to increase metabolic demand and training density. Useful for fat loss phases; less useful when maximum strength is the goal.

### Load Management Curves

**Beginner (0–6 months).** Can add 5–10 lb per session on main squat. Weekly gain: 10–20 lb per week in absolute terms. This rate slows significantly by month 3–4.

**Intermediate (6 months–2 years).** Weekly load increase on back squat: 2.5–5 lb when progress is happening. Monthly gain: 5–15 lb. Over a 12-week strength block, expect 15–40 lb of new 1RM if programming is sound.

**Advanced (2+ years).** Monthly or quarterly load gains. Annual 1RM increases of 10–30 lb are realistic. Progress is measured in mesocycles, not sessions.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Variation switch.** If back squat stalls, spend 4–6 weeks emphasizing front squat, SSB squat, or pause squat. The variation trains similar muscles from different angles and eliminates technique compensation patterns that develop under accumulated load. Return to back squat with a modest reset (-10%) and progress rapidly again.

**Volume wave.** Add 2–3 sets per week for 3 weeks, then deload. The elevated volume drives a new adaptation baseline, making subsequent heavier loads feel relatively easier.

**Tempo emphasis block.** Spend 3–4 weeks doing 3-second eccentric squats at 70–75% instead of normal tempo. This develops eccentric strength (typically undertrained) and corrects the sticking point by forcing muscular control through the range where most lifters rush.

**Pause squats.** A 2-second pause at the bottom eliminates the stretch-shortening reflex, forcing the lifter to stand the weight up from pure muscular force. Pause squat at 80% of regular squat produces equivalent or greater adaptation than free squat at 85%.

### Regression Protocols

**When to back off.** Drop load 10–15% when: (1) form degrades on more than one rep per set, (2) sharp joint pain appears (as distinct from muscular fatigue soreness), (3) bar speed slows dramatically despite adequate rest.

**Deload strategy.** Reduce volume 40–50% for one week while maintaining intensity at 70–75% of recent training max. Do not eliminate squatting entirely — maintaining the motor pattern through a deload prevents technique drift.

**Regression for acute injury.** Knee pain → switch to box squat at a height that eliminates pain; reduce load 30–40%. Rebuild depth over 4–6 weeks. Hip pain → unilateral work (BSS) at reduced load often bypasses hip impingement patterns. Lower back pain → front squat (more upright, less spinal load) or goblet squat until back resolves.

## Pattern 2: Hinge (Hip-Dominant, Posterior Chain)

**Definition.** Hip displacement backward with a near-neutral spine, loading the posterior chain through hip extension from a hinged position.

**Primary muscles.** Hamstrings, glutes. Secondary: spinal erectors, upper back, core.

### Progression Hierarchy

| Exercise | Progress To |
|---|---|
| Hip hinge bodyweight (groove) | Romanian Deadlift (RDL) |
| RDL | Trap bar deadlift, conventional deadlift |
| Trap bar deadlift | Conventional or sumo deadlift |
| Conventional deadlift | Deficit deadlift, snatch-grip deadlift |
| Good morning | Loaded good morning, barbell good morning |
| Single-leg RDL (SL-RDL) | Weighted SL-RDL |
| Kettlebell swing | Double-KB swing, barbell hip thrust |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Load.** Deadlift is the strongest human pulling movement — beginners progress 10–15 lb per session initially, settling to 5 lb per session. Intermediate progression: 5 lb per week when on track.

**Deficit.** Standing on a 1–2" deficit increases ROM and demands greater hamstring flexibility and hip mobility. Only add deficit after the conventional lift is technically solid, as deficit pulling with a rounded lower back is a reliable injury mechanism.

**Pause at knee.** A 2-second pause with the bar at mid-shin forces conscious positional awareness. Develops the portion of the pull where most mechanical breakdowns originate.

**Density and volume (for RDL specifically).** RDL is the volume-friendly hinge variant. 3–4 sets of 6–12 at 50–65% of deadlift max builds posterior chain volume without the systemic fatigue of conventional pulling. Volume increases are the primary progression driver for RDL.

### Load Management Curves

Beginner deadlift progression: 5 kg/week for the first 2–3 months is common (see *Starting Strength* LP benchmarks). Intermediate: 5–10 lb per week during a productive block. First-year lifters can expect total 1RM gains of 100–200 lb over 12 months of consistent training. After two years, 30–50 lb of annual gain is excellent.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Romanian Deadlift supplementation.** If conventional deadlift stalls, add 3×10 RDL at 40–50% of conventional 1RM each lower body session. Hamstring strength and hip hinge endurance are the most common weak links in the conventional deadlift pull.

**Accessory pulls.** Snatch-grip deadlift (wider grip), rack pulls (bar elevated 2–4"), and deficit pulls each train different portions of the pull. Rotate the primary accessory hinge every 3–4 weeks.

**Block pulling from below knee.** If the sticking point is off the floor, deficit work solves it. If it is above the knee, rack pulls just below sticking point with heavier-than-normal loads (overload principle) retrain the neural drive through that position.

### Regression Protocols

**Acute low back.** Move immediately to trap bar deadlift (more upright, lower spinal load) or RDL at 50% reduced load. Eliminate conventional pulling until pain resolves. Maintain the movement pattern; do not eliminate hinge work entirely, as deconditioning of the spinal erectors prolongs recovery.

**Hamstring strain.** Hip thrust and glute bridge (remove hamstring stretch-under-load demand), Nordic curl eccentric-only protocol at RPE 4–5 to maintain hamstring capacity without aggravating strain.

## Pattern 3: Horizontal Push (Anterior Chain, Pressing)

**Definition.** Force production away from the body in a roughly horizontal plane, primarily through chest, shoulder, and tricep function.

**Primary muscles.** Pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, triceps.

### Progression Hierarchy

| Exercise | Progress To |
|---|---|
| Push-up (standard) | Loaded push-up, ring push-up |
| Incline bench press | Flat bench press |
| Flat dumbbell press | Barbell bench press |
| Barbell bench press | Close-grip bench, pause bench, spoto press |
| Dips (bodyweight) | Weighted dips |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Load.** 2.5 lb per session (LP), settling to 2.5–5 lb per week (intermediate). Bench is the slowest-progressing major compound lift, particularly for women.

**Pause reps.** 1–2 second pause at chest eliminates the bounce reflex, demands more from the chest, and is a better technique check than touch-and-go. Pause bench at 90% of regular 1RM equals regular 1RM in difficulty.

**Tempo.** 3-second eccentric develops chest hypertrophy more effectively than fast eccentrics at equal load.

**Board/block presses (partial ROM).** Pressing from a limited ROM (3-board press) allows overload above full-ROM capacity. Used in peaking phases for neural drive through the lockout.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Close-grip bench.** When bench stalls, a 3–4 week emphasis on CGBP (grip width 12–16 inches) shifts demand to the triceps — often the weakest link in a stalled bench. Return to standard grip with a 5–8 lb reset.

**Floor press.** Eliminates leg drive and removes the stretch-shortening reflex at the bottom. A brutally honest test of pressing strength, and a useful variation when shoulder discomfort limits full-ROM work.

**Frequency increase.** Benching 3×/week produces faster bench-specific adaptation than 1–2×/week in most intermediate lifters. If you bench once per week, doubling frequency often breaks a plateau faster than any technical variation.

## Pattern 4: Vertical Push (Overhead Pressing)

**Definition.** Force production directly overhead, through shoulder flexion/abduction and elbow extension.

**Primary muscles.** Anterior and lateral deltoids, upper pectorals, triceps. Secondary: serratus anterior, upper trapezius, core for stabilization.

### Progression Hierarchy

| Exercise | Progress To |
|---|---|
| Pike push-up | Decline pike push-up, HSPU negative |
| Dumbbell overhead press | Barbell strict press |
| Barbell strict press | Push press (leg drive), jerk |
| Seated DB press | Standing barbell press |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Standing vs seated.** Standing overhead press is harder than seated due to core stability demands. Transition from seated to standing is a meaningful difficulty increase without load change.

**Strict vs push press.** Adding leg drive (push press) allows 15–30% more load overhead; primarily a power/athletic development tool rather than a shoulder strength builder.

**Load.** Overhead press is the most slowly progressing compound lift. Expect 1.25–2.5 lb increments at intermediate levels. Annual absolute gains of 10–30 lb over the first 2 years are realistic.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**DB press supplementation.** Dumbbell press with 3-second eccentric, 3×10, builds shoulder volume that feeds back into barbell press strength.

**Incline variation emphasis.** Incline bench at 60–75° is mechanically between flat bench and overhead press. A 4-week incline emphasis block often carryover to overhead.

## Pattern 5: Horizontal Pull (Posterior Chain, Rowing)

**Definition.** Force production toward the body in a roughly horizontal plane, through elbow flexion and shoulder extension/horizontal abduction.

**Primary muscles.** Latissimus dorsi, rhomboids, rear deltoids, biceps.

### Progression Hierarchy

| Exercise | Progress To |
|---|---|
| Band pull-apart / face pull | Cable row, DB row |
| Inverted row (bodyweight) | Loaded inverted row, ring row |
| DB row | Barbell row, chest-supported row |
| Barbell row (Pendlay or bent-over) | Heavy barbell row, Yates row |
| Seated cable row | Single-arm cable row |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Load.** DB rows and barbell rows should progress similarly to pressing movements — 2.5–5 lb per session or week. Pulling strength is often undertrained relative to pushing in novice programs; expect rapid initial gains.

**Unilateral emphasis.** Single-arm DB rows eliminate compensation between sides and allow greater ROM. Heavier loads are achievable unilaterally than bilaterally per arm.

**Tempo.** 2-second pause at full contraction (elbow behind torso) increases upper back activation and corrects the tendency to use momentum.

**Grip variation.** Wide-grip rows emphasize rear delts and rhomboids; narrow/supinated grip rows bias biceps and lower lats. Both are necessary for complete posterior chain development.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Chest-supported row.** Eliminates lower back fatigue from stabilizing the torso. Allows pure focus on pulling mechanics and higher volume accumulation without spinal stress.

**Face pull volume.** External rotator and rear delt weakness is the hidden limiter in most horizontal pulls. Adding 3–4 sets of 15–20 face pulls per session for 4–6 weeks frequently breaks horizontal pull plateaus.

## Pattern 6: Vertical Pull (Pull-Ups, Lat Development)

**Definition.** Force production upward (or body upward relative to a fixed bar) through elbow flexion and shoulder adduction/extension from above.

**Primary muscles.** Latissimus dorsi, biceps, lower trapezius, rhomboids.

### Progression Hierarchy

| Exercise | Progress To |
|---|---|
| Dead hang (grip/scapular) | Scapular pull-up, assisted pull-up |
| Band-assisted pull-up | Bodyweight pull-up |
| Bodyweight pull-up (3+ clean reps) | Weighted pull-up |
| Weighted pull-up | Archer pull-up, muscle-up |
| Lat pulldown | Weighted pull-up |

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Volume first.** If you cannot do 5 clean pull-ups, add pull-up volume before adding load. Accumulate 25–50 total pull-up reps per session distributed across sets (greasing the groove approach).

**Load.** Add 2.5–5 lb via a dip belt or vest. Intermediate lifters should target a weighted pull-up with 25–40% bodyweight added as a 12-month benchmark.

**Pause at top.** Chin above bar + 2-second hold eliminates the kipping habit and tests true strength.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Frequency.** Pull-ups improve faster with 4–6 shorter sets spread through the day (grease the groove) than 3–4 max-effort sets once per day. If pull-up progress has stalled, increase frequency while reducing per-session set fatigue.

**Lat pulldown volume.** 4×10–12 lat pulldown at 70% of load where pull-up is failing develops the lat under load without the bodyweight stabilization demand. Feeds back into pull-up strength within 4–6 weeks.

## Pattern 7: Carry and Core (Loaded Locomotion, Anti-Rotation/Flexion)

**Definition.** Loaded walking, carrying, or static anti-movement patterns that train core stiffness, grip strength, and whole-body force transmission.

**Primary muscles.** Core musculature (anti-flexion: erectors, QL; anti-rotation: obliques, glute med), grip/forearm, trapezius.

### Progression Hierarchy

**Carries:**
Farmer's walk → heavy farmer's walk → Zercher carry → overhead carry → Yoke carry

**Core anti-movement:**
Dead bug (bodyweight) → Pallof press (band) → Pallof press (cable) → Single-arm overhead carry → Heavy Turkish get-up

### How to Increase Difficulty

**Load.** Farmer's walk: add 5–10 lb per implement per week. Target: carry 50–75% of bodyweight per hand for 40 meters as a 12-month intermediate benchmark.

**Distance / time.** Increase carry distance by 5–10 meters per week or hold duration by 5–10 seconds per week.

**Variation complexity.** Progress from bilateral to offset loading (one heavy, one light) to single-arm to overhead. Offset and single-arm carries dramatically increase anti-lateral-flexion demand.

### Plateau-Breaking Protocols

**Grip strength supplementation.** Grip is typically the limiting factor in loaded carries. Farmer's walk progress stalls when grip fails before the torso. Add 3 sets of 30–45-second hanging dead hangs and towel pull-ups 2×/week for 4 weeks.

**Carry frequency increase.** Carries respond well to 3–4 sessions per week at moderate loads over 2 maximal sessions per week.

## Plateau-Breaking Decision Tree

When progress stalls on a lift, the root cause determines the fix. Applying the wrong intervention wastes 3–6 weeks. Work through this tree before changing anything.

```
Is bar speed slower than normal on submaximal sets?
│
├── YES → Likely CNS fatigue / accumulated fatigue
│   ├── Have you deloaded in the last 3–4 weeks?
│   │   ├── NO → Take a deload this week (40–50% volume, same intensity). Resume.
│   │   └── YES → Fatigue-resistant plateau: reduce volume 20% for 2 weeks and focus on
│   │             sleep quality. If still stalled after 2 weeks → see "Technique" branch.
│   └── Check: Are you within 5% of your body's structural ceiling for this lift?
│       → If yes: periodization model change needed (LP → WUP or WUP → DUP)
│
└── NO → Fatigue is not the cause
    │
    ├── Have you been at the same rep range for 4+ weeks?
    │   ├── YES → Accommodation (body has adapted to the specific rep/load pattern)
    │   │   └── FIX: Rep range shift. If doing 5s, move to 8s for 3 weeks. Or vice versa.
    │   └── NO → Rep range variation is not the issue
    │
    ├── Has technique been recently reviewed?
    │   ├── NO → Video a set. Most intermediate plateaus have a technical root cause:
    │   │       squat: depth or bar path / deadlift: hip position off floor / bench: leg drive
    │   │       Identify the position and drill it with tempo work for 2–3 weeks.
    │   └── YES, technique is sound → Proceed to load/volume analysis
    │
    ├── Is total weekly volume for this movement below 12 working sets?
    │   ├── YES → Add 2 sets per session for 3 weeks. Volume was below MAV.
    │   └── NO → Volume is likely not the constraint
    │
    ├── Have you tried a variation change?
    │   ├── NO → Switch primary variation for 4–6 weeks:
    │   │       Squat → front squat or SSB squat
    │   │       Deadlift → trap bar or deficit pull
    │   │       Bench → close-grip or floor press
    │   │       Pull-up → weighted lat pulldown
    │   │       Then return to main lift with 5–10% load reset.
    │   └── YES, variation cycle already done → Check accessory work
    │
    └── Is the weak link in the primary accessory muscles?
        → Back squat stall: usually hamstrings/glutes. Add 3×10 RDL per lower body session.
        → Bench stall: usually triceps. Add close-grip bench 4×6 for 3 weeks.
        → Deadlift stall: usually hamstrings or upper back. Add RDL + Pendlay row.
        → Pull-up stall: usually lats. Add 4×12 lat pulldown 2×/week.
        → Press stall: usually lateral/rear delts. Add DB lateral raise + face pulls.
```

**After running this tree, apply only one intervention at a time.** Changing variation AND volume AND accessory work simultaneously makes it impossible to identify what worked. One variable, 3–4 weeks, reassess.

**When the tree fails.** If 3 consecutive variation cycles across 12+ weeks produce no progress on a primary lift, and sleep, nutrition, and consistency are confirmed solid, you have likely approached your near-term genetic ceiling for that lift under your current body weight. Options: (1) Accept the plateau as normal advanced-stage progress and measure in 6-month increments, not weeks. (2) Add 3–5 lb of body weight over 4–6 weeks if hypertrophy is compatible with your goal — more muscle mass typically unlocks a new strength level. (3) Consult a qualified coach for in-person technique assessment.

## Volume Landmarks Per Muscle Group: Practical Ceiling Reference

The MEV/MAV/MRV framework provides a structured lens for asking "am I doing enough?" and "am I doing too much?" for each muscle group separately. Values below are drawn from the RP Strength model (Israetel & Hoffmann) and represent reasonable population averages — individual variation is ±30–50%.

| Muscle Group | MEV (sets/week) | MAV Range (sets/week) | MRV Ceiling (sets/week) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quads | 8 | 12–18 | 20–25 | Squatting 3×/week typical; includes all squat-pattern work |
| Hamstrings | 6 | 10–16 | 18–22 | RDLs, leg curls, Nordic curls combined |
| Glutes | 4 | 8–16 | 20–25 | Hip thrusts, split squats, abductors; high tolerance |
| Chest | 8 | 12–18 | 20–22 | Incline + flat; upper chest often needs separate emphasis |
| Back (lats + mid) | 10 | 14–22 | 25+ | Pulling movements have high fatigue tolerance |
| Shoulders (lateral) | 6 | 12–20 | 22–26 | High tolerance; often undertrained; face pulls count |
| Triceps | 4 | 8–14 | 16–20 | Compound pressing provides baseline; isolation adds volume |
| Biceps | 4 | 8–14 | 16–20 | Compound rows provide baseline |
| Calves | 8 | 12–16 | 20–24 | Respond to high frequency; low muscle damage per set |
| Spinal erectors | 4 | 6–10 | 12–14 | Accumulate fatigue from all compounds; monitor carefully |

**Key interpretive rules:**
1. MEV is not a target — it is a floor. Starting at MEV in a new mesocycle gives headroom to progress volume upward.
2. MRV is individual and shifts with training age, sleep, and nutrition. The numbers above are landmarks for discovery, not prescriptions.
3. Volume across exercises accumulates. A squat program already at 16 sets of quad-dominant work per week has minimal room for added leg press before hitting MRV. Count everything, not just the primary lift.
4. Above MRV, sets still stimulate protein synthesis but the fatigue they generate exceeds the adaptive signal. Performance begins to stagnate or regress — the hallmark of overreaching.

## Realistic Strength Gain Timelines

These ranges are based on Starting Strength LP benchmarks, EliteFTS intermediate progression data, and general sports science literature on novice-to-intermediate strength gains.

| Population | Squat 1RM gain / year 1 | Deadlift 1RM gain / year 1 | Bench 1RM gain / year 1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Untrained male (18–35) | +80–160 lb | +100–200 lb | +40–80 lb |
| Untrained female (18–35) | +50–100 lb | +60–120 lb | +20–50 lb |
| Returning male (trained history) | +50–100 lb | +60–120 lb | +30–60 lb |
| Intermediate male (year 2) | +20–50 lb | +25–60 lb | +10–25 lb |
| Intermediate female (year 2) | +12–30 lb | +15–40 lb | +6–15 lb |

**Key caveats.** These are population averages with high individual variance. Genetic factors (muscle fiber distribution, limb leverages, hormone levels) create a 2–3× spread within any population segment. Nutrition and sleep quality account for the difference between the low end and high end of each range more than any programming variable.

**The point of diminishing returns.** Strength gain rate slows by roughly 50% after year 1, 50% again after year 2, and continues to slow. An advanced lifter who has been training 5+ years may gain 5–15 lb on their total squat in an entire year. This is not failure — it is the mathematical reality of pushing toward genetic ceilings. The appropriate response is patience and longer planning horizons, not more complexity.
