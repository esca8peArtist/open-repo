# Nutrition 02 — Meal Timing & Nutrient Timing

**Prerequisites:** Read Nutrition 01 (macro targets) first. The timing framework here is layered on top of hitting daily macro totals — timing amplifies what daily nutrition sets up, it does not replace it.

**Key principle:** Total daily intake is the primary driver of adaptation. Timing is a secondary optimization that adds roughly 5–10% marginal benefit for most athletes. The exception is the pre/intra-workout window for sessions over 90 minutes — here timing becomes more critical because glycogen depletion directly impairs performance within the session.

---

## The Daily Meal Architecture

### Meal Frequency

**Evidence-supported range:** 4–6 meals per day maximizes muscle protein synthesis by maintaining elevated blood amino acid levels without the metabolic cost of continuous protein oxidation.

**Why 4–6 beats 2–3:** When protein is consumed in fewer, larger meals, the leucine trigger is activated but the "full" muscle protein synthesis response requires several hours to complete and then resets. Spacing 30–40 g protein doses every 3–5 hours keeps the system engaged longer across the day.

**Why 7+ meals offers diminishing returns:** Beyond 6 meals per day, practical compliance drops and no additional MPS benefit is documented in current literature.

**Practical application for each training frequency:**

| Training days/week | Meal structure | Pre-workout meal | Post-workout meal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3 days (M/W/F) | 4 meals: breakfast, lunch, post-workout, dinner | Lunch or mid-afternoon snack | Immediately post-session |
| 4 days (M/T/Th/F) | 4–5 meals | ~90–120 min before session | Within 60 min post-session |
| 5–6 days | 5–6 meals | Dedicated pre-workout meal | Dedicated post-workout meal or shake |

### Meal Timing Around the Training Window

The training session is the anchor point. Build the rest of the day around it:

**Morning trainers (6–8 AM sessions):**
- 5:30 AM: Small fast-digesting meal (banana + protein shake, 30–40 g carbs + 20 g protein)
- Or: train lightly fasted if session is under 60 min at low-moderate intensity, then eat a full breakfast immediately after
- 8–9 AM: Full post-workout breakfast (oatmeal + eggs + fruit)
- 12 PM: Lunch (protein + carbs + vegetables)
- 3:30 PM: Mid-afternoon snack (Greek yogurt + nuts or fruit)
- 7 PM: Dinner (protein + carbs + fats)

**Lunchtime trainers (12–2 PM sessions):**
- 7 AM: Breakfast (full meal — moderate carbs, moderate protein)
- 10:30 AM: Pre-workout meal (40–60 g carbs + 20–25 g protein)
- 1 PM training
- 2:30 PM: Post-workout meal (40–60 g carbs + 30–35 g protein)
- 7 PM: Dinner (protein + moderate carbs + fats)
- 9–10 PM: Optional evening snack (casein protein + slow carbs)

**Evening trainers (5–8 PM sessions):**
- 7 AM: Breakfast (full meal)
- 12 PM: Lunch (full meal — this becomes the "pre-workout" base)
- 3:30–4 PM: Pre-workout snack (banana, rice cakes, small shake)
- 6–7 PM training
- 8–9 PM: Post-workout dinner (protein + carbs)
- 10–11 PM: Optional casein snack for overnight recovery

## Pre-Workout Nutrition Protocol

### The 90–120 Minute Window

**Target:** Arrive at the first working set with blood glucose stable, glycogen partially topped up, and no GI distress.

**Composition (see Nutrition 01 for goal-specific macros):**
- 40–60 g low-to-moderate glycaemic carbohydrates
- 20–30 g protein
- Less than 10 g fat
- Less than 5 g fibre

**Goal-specific examples:**

*Hypertrophy focus (Tier 3, heavy compound day):*
- 150 g cooked white rice + 150 g chicken breast + small amount of broccoli
- Macros: 42 g carbs, 46 g protein, 3 g fat

*Strength focus (max effort day):*
- Oatmeal (60 g dry) + Greek yogurt (150 g) + honey (1 tbsp)
- Macros: 55 g carbs, 22 g protein, 5 g fat

*Conditioning / HIIT focus:*
- 2 bananas + 1 scoop protein powder + 300 ml water
- Macros: 54 g carbs, 25 g protein, 1 g fat

*Budget option (all goals):*
- 2 slices white toast + 2 eggs scrambled + 1 banana
- Macros: 50 g carbs, 18 g protein, 9 g fat

*Quick-prep (under 5 minutes):*
- Banana + protein shake (25 g) + 300 ml orange juice
- Macros: 62 g carbs, 25 g protein, 1 g fat — consume 60 min before if digestion is sensitive

### The 30-Minute Window (If You Missed the 90-Minute Meal)

If training within 30 minutes:
- Small fast-carb dose only: 20–30 g fast carbs (banana, glucose tablets, raisins, sports drink)
- Avoid protein here — it slows gastric emptying and can cause cramping during high-intensity work
- Main meal within 30 minutes of session end

### Fasted Training Protocols

Completely fasted training (3+ hours since last meal) produces:
- 10–15% reduction in high-intensity output compared to fed state
- Accelerated glycogen depletion in first 20 minutes
- Elevated cortisol response

If circumstances require fasted training:
- Consume 20 g simple carbs (glucose tablets, small sports drink, raisins) 10–15 min before session
- This is enough to blunt the cortisol spike and maintain glycogen availability for skill work
- Not ideal for heavy strength days — prioritize a fed state for max-effort work

## Intra-Workout Fueling

### When Intra-Workout Nutrition Is Necessary

| Session type | Duration | Intra-workout fuel |
|---|---|---|
| Light-moderate intensity | < 60 min | Water only |
| Moderate-high intensity | 60–90 min | Optional; 20–30 g simple carbs if performance drops |
| High intensity / heavy volume | > 90 min | Required; 30–60 g carbs/hour + electrolytes |
| Two-a-day sessions | Any duration | Required at end of first session |
| 6-day/week conditioning blocks | > 60 min | Recommended daily |

### Intra-Workout Composition

**Carbohydrate sources (fast-digesting, no fibre):**
- Sports drink (Gatorade, Powerade, generic glucose polymer drink): 30–60 g carbs/hour
- Glucose gel (25 g carbs per packet, easy to carry)
- Banana (23 g carbs, easily portioned)
- Raisins (22 g carbs per 30 g serving)
- White bread with honey (35 g carbs, simple and cheap)

**Electrolytes (essential for > 60 min at high intensity):**
- Sodium: 300–600 mg/hour (main electrolyte lost in sweat)
- Potassium: 100–200 mg/hour
- Simplest approach: commercial sports drink already contains both
- Budget approach: water + 1/4 tsp table salt + 1/4 tsp cream of tartar (potassium chloride) per liter

**What to avoid intra-workout:**
- Fats and fibre (slow digestion, GI distress risk)
- High-fibre protein bars
- Casein-heavy shakes
- Caffeine in excess (acute dehydration effect)

### Protein Intra-Workout?

Current evidence does not support consuming protein during workouts for the purpose of acutely boosting muscle protein synthesis within the session. Protein is irrelevant to ATP regeneration during training. Save protein for pre- and post-workout timing.

**Exception:** EAA (essential amino acid) drinks during 2-hour+ sessions may blunt muscle protein breakdown markers. This is relevant mainly for elite athletes doing very high volumes.

## Post-Workout Recovery Window

### Reframing the "Anabolic Window"

The concept that a 30-minute post-workout window is critical has been substantially revised. The 2013 Aragon & Schoenfeld review (PMC3577439) and the 2025 Frontiers in Nutrition meta-analysis both conclude:

1. If you ate a substantial pre-workout meal (containing protein) within **2 hours before training**, the anabolic window extends to **approximately 2 hours post-workout** — there is no rush.
2. If you trained fasted or with only minimal pre-workout food, the window is more time-sensitive — aim for **protein within 30–60 minutes** post-session.
3. **Total daily protein** remains the dominant variable for muscle protein synthesis. Timing is a secondary optimization.

**What the research does confirm:**
- Pre-workout essential amino acid + carbohydrate intake produces a **larger acute MPS elevation** than the same supplement taken post-workout
- Post-workout carbohydrate initiates glycogen resynthesis at the highest rate in the first 30–60 minutes (insulin sensitivity is transiently elevated)
- Combining protein + carbs post-workout produces superior glycogen repletion vs. carbs alone in the first 2 hours

### Post-Workout Targets by Goal

**Hypertrophy:**
- **Protein:** 30–40 g (whey ideal; whole food chicken or eggs acceptable)
- **Carbohydrates:** 60–80 g (white rice, sports drink, banana, bread)
- **Timing:** Within 2 hours of session end (immediately if trained fasted)
- **Example:** Whey shake (30 g protein) + sports drink (60 g carbs) immediately, then full meal within 2 hours

**Strength (powerlifting emphasis):**
- **Protein:** 25–35 g
- **Carbohydrates:** 40–60 g
- **Timing:** Within 2 hours
- **Example:** Chicken breast (130 g) + white rice (150 g cooked) + broccoli + 500 ml sports drink

**Conditioning / sport-specific:**
- **Protein:** 20–30 g
- **Carbohydrates:** 60–90 g (carbs prioritized for glycogen repletion when competing or training again tomorrow)
- **Timing:** Immediately post-session
- **Example:** Protein shake (25 g) + banana + 600 ml sports drink

**Body recomposition (deficit):**
- **Protein:** 35–45 g (higher to offset restricted calories)
- **Carbohydrates:** 30–50 g (below normal; save room for protein)
- **Timing:** Within 60 minutes
- **Example:** Greek yogurt (200 g) + oats (40 g dry) + 1 banana

### The Role of Liquid vs. Solid Food Post-Workout

Both work. The practical advantage of liquid nutrition (shake + sports drink) is speed of digestion — useful if you need to be somewhere within 30 minutes. Whole-food meals are equally effective if consumed within the 1–2 hour window. Choose based on logistics, not perceived superiority.

## Feeding Windows and Meal Frequency in Practice

### Structuring 4–6 Meals Per Day

**Core structure (works for all goals and tiers):**

**Meal 1 — Breakfast (on waking)**
- Priority: Protein + moderate carbs
- Breaks overnight fast; initiates daily muscle protein synthesis cycle
- Target: 30–40 g protein, 40–60 g carbs, moderate fats
- Example: Oats + eggs + milk + banana

**Meal 2 — Mid-morning or Pre-workout (2–3 hrs after breakfast)**
- Priority: Carbs + protein (pre-workout) or protein + fats (non-training day)
- Training day: This is the pre-workout meal — see pre-workout protocol above
- Non-training day: Greek yogurt + nuts + fruit (protein + fats, fewer carbs)

**Meal 3 — Post-workout meal OR Lunch**
- Priority: Protein + carbs (highest carb meal of the day on training days)
- Leverages post-workout glycogen sensitivity
- Target: 30–40 g protein, 60–80 g carbs, minimal fats

**Meal 4 — Afternoon snack (3–4 hrs after Meal 3)**
- Priority: Protein maintenance; moderate carbs or fats
- Prevents amino acid valley in the afternoon
- Example: Cottage cheese + fruit, or a protein shake + nuts

**Meal 5 — Dinner (largest whole-food meal)**
- Priority: Protein + mixed macros + micronutrient density
- Include colourful vegetables here (vitamins, antioxidants, fibre)
- Target: 35–45 g protein, mixed carbs and fats, generous vegetable portion

**Meal 6 — Evening snack (optional, 1–2 hrs before sleep)**
- Priority: Slow-digesting protein for overnight muscle protein synthesis
- See sleep nutrition section below
- Target: 20–30 g casein protein, minimal carbs

### Non-Training Day Adjustments

On rest days:
- **Reduce carbs by 20–30%** (glycogen is not being depleted; excess carbs convert to fat)
- **Maintain protein** (muscle protein breakdown continues; protein is protective)
- **Slightly increase fats** to compensate for reduced carbs and maintain total calories near target
- **Meal timing flexibility increases** — the pre/post-workout precision is not needed

**Practical rest-day rule:** Shift the carb-heavy Meal 3 (post-workout) to a protein-and-fat-focused meal instead. Redistribute carbs to breakfast and dinner.

## Sleep Nutrition: Overnight Recovery Optimization

### Casein Protein Before Sleep

**Evidence base:** Casein is a micellar protein that forms a gel-like structure in the stomach and releases amino acids steadily over 5–7 hours. Pre-sleep casein (30–40 g consumed 30 minutes before bed) produces a sustained elevation in circulating amino acids through the overnight fast, increasing whole-body protein synthesis by approximately 22% vs. placebo in multiple RCTs (Snijders et al. 2015; confirmed in 2024 pre-sleep protein reviews).

- **Dose:** 30–40 g casein protein (cottage cheese, casein powder, or Greek yogurt are all adequate sources)
- **Timing:** 30–60 minutes before sleep
- **Effect:** Increases overnight muscle protein synthesis without disrupting sleep architecture
- **Who benefits most:** Athletes in hard training blocks (5–6 sessions/week), older athletes (> 40 years, where overnight MPS is naturally blunted), and those in caloric deficit

**Practical sources by budget:**
- Budget: Cottage cheese (200 g, 25 g casein, $1.00–1.50)
- Mid-range: Greek yogurt 2% (250 g, 22 g protein, $1.50–2.00) + 1 tbsp peanut butter
- Premium: Casein powder (30–35 g serving, $1.50–2.00 per serving)
- Vegan: Soy protein concentrate (digestion is slower than whey; reasonable casein surrogate for overnight use)

### Magnesium for Sleep Quality

Magnesium acts as a GABA agonist — inhibiting the excitatory nervous system response and facilitating sleep onset. Athletes in hard training consistently show sub-optimal magnesium status from sweat losses. A 2024 scoping review of sleep and nutrition in athletes (PMC12567717) confirms magnesium supplementation improves sleep efficiency and reduces sleep latency.

- **Protocol:** 250–400 mg magnesium glycinate taken 30–60 minutes before sleep
- **Why glycinate not oxide:** Oxide form poorly absorbed and causes GI distress; glycinate is the best-tolerated form
- **Synergy with casein:** Take both together — one sleep-routine supplement and protein

### Tart Cherry Juice

The most consistently evidence-supported dietary intervention for sleep in athletes. Tart cherries are naturally high in melatonin precursors and anti-inflammatory anthocyanins. Studies show:
- +34 minutes total sleep time with 2-week supplementation
- Reduced sleep-onset latency
- Reduced DOMS markers (CK levels) 24–48 hours post-resistance training

**Protocol:** 30 ml concentrate (or 250 ml tart cherry juice) twice daily — morning and 30 min before sleep. Most effective in training blocks with high DOMS risk (new training stimulus, increased volume, deload transitions).

### Foods That Contain Melatonin-Precursor Compounds

Foods highest in tryptophan (melatonin precursor pathway):
- Turkey (250 mg tryptophan per 100 g)
- Cottage cheese (180 mg per 100 g)
- Eggs (130 mg per 100 g)
- Pumpkin seeds (576 mg per 100 g — highest plant source)
- Almonds, walnuts (also contain trace melatonin directly)

**Practical sleep meal:** Cottage cheese + pumpkin seeds + warm milk (contains tryptophan and natural melatonin). Low-tech, high-effectiveness.

### What to Avoid in the 2 Hours Before Sleep

- **Caffeine:** Half-life 5–7 hours; a 3 PM coffee still has meaningful plasma levels at 9 PM
- **Alcohol:** Reduces REM sleep and growth hormone secretion even when it aids sleep onset
- **Very large meals:** Elevate core body temperature during digestion, disrupting sleep architecture
- **High-GI carbohydrates alone:** Cause blood glucose spike then crash, increasing nighttime cortisol and potential waking

## Summary: The Daily Timeline (Training Day)

```
6:30 AM   Wake up
7:00 AM   Breakfast — Meal 1 (protein + carbs + moderate fats)
           Example: Oats, eggs, banana, milk

10:00 AM  Pre-workout meal — Meal 2 (carbs + protein, low fat)
           Example: White rice, chicken breast, broccoli

12:00 PM  Train (1–1.5 hours)

1:30 PM   Post-workout — Meal 3 (protein + carbs, immediate)
           Example: Whey shake + banana, then full meal within 90 min

3:00 PM   Full post-workout meal (if shake was used post-training)
           Example: Chicken + rice + vegetables

6:30 PM   Dinner — Meal 4–5 (full macros, add vegetables + fats)
           Example: Salmon + sweet potato + olive oil + greens

9:00 PM   Sleep snack — Meal 5–6 (casein + magnesium)
           Example: Cottage cheese + pumpkin seeds + magnesium glycinate 300 mg
```
