# Injury Prevention Protocols

This document covers the injury side of training management — the practical science of how to avoid overuse injuries, recognize early warning signs, manage volume within tolerable thresholds, and maintain productive training when a primary movement becomes unavailable. This is not medical advice; for persistent or acute pain, see a licensed sports medicine physician or physical therapist.

---

## The Biology of Overuse Injury

Most training injuries are not accidents. They are the predictable outcome of applying more load than the tissue can repair between exposures. The mechanism is well established: tissues (tendons, cartilage, bone, muscle) undergo microtrauma during training and remodel stronger during recovery. When load exceeds the repair rate — either because a single session is too high, or because accumulated sessions don't allow sufficient recovery time — microtrauma outpaces remodeling and progressive tissue degradation begins.

**The mismatch problem.** Muscle tissue adapts to new loads within 4–8 weeks. Tendons and ligaments adapt in 10–26 weeks. Bone cortex adapts in 6–12 months. This 2–3× slower adaptation rate in connective tissue is the reason people injure tendons and ligaments doing weights that their muscles can easily handle. The muscles got strong; the connective tissue has not caught up.

**Acute-to-Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR).** The most evidence-supported framework for injury risk quantification is the ACWR: the ratio of your last week's training load (acute load) to your average load over the prior 4 weeks (chronic load). A "sweet spot" ACWR of 0.8–1.3 is associated with low injury risk. An ACWR above 1.5 — a sudden spike beyond 50% of your chronic baseline — is associated with significantly elevated injury incidence. The practical implication: if your chronic weekly volume is 10 sets per muscle group, do not jump to 15 sets without a 2–3 week bridge period.

**The 10% rule.** While imprecise, the widely cited guideline of increasing weekly training volume by no more than 10% per week reflects the ACWR research at a practical level. When in doubt, increase training load slowly.

## Common Overuse Injuries by Movement Pattern

### Knee Pain (Patellar Tendinopathy, IT Band Syndrome, Medial/Lateral Compartment)

**Primary movements affected.** Squats, lunges, step-ups, running, jumping.

**Mechanism.** Patellar tendinopathy arises from accumulated tensile load on the patellar tendon (connecting quad to tibial tuberosity). The tendon degenerates when load exceeds repair capacity — most commonly from rapid increases in squat volume, jump volume, or running mileage. IT band syndrome typically presents in runners and cyclists, arising from repetitive knee flexion-extension under load, often compounded by hip abductor weakness.

**Risk factors specific to resistance training:**
- Rapid volume increases in squatting or plyometric work
- Insufficient ankle dorsiflexion causing knee valgus (knees caving in) under load
- Weak hip abductors/glute medius allowing knee valgus
- Quadriceps strength imbalance relative to hamstrings (quad dominance)
- Jumping volume increases without graduated tendon loading

**Volume threshold considerations.** Patellar tendon overuse risk increases substantially above approximately 100 high-load knee-flexion repetitions per week (combined across squats, lunges, step-ups, leg press variants). In practice, this means monitoring total squat-pattern volume carefully during any block where volume is increasing.

**Warning signs.** Pain at the inferior patellar pole (bottom of kneecap) during warm-up that resolves with activity, then returns afterward. Pain after prolonged sitting. Morning stiffness at the knee. These are early-stage tendinopathy signs — act early. The chronic stage (pain during and after activity, sleep disruption) requires longer recovery and medical evaluation.

---

### Lower Back Pain (Lumbar Disc Irritation, Erector Strain, SI Joint)

**Primary movements affected.** Deadlifts, rows, squats (lower back version), overhead press (lumbar extension compensation).

**Mechanism.** Lower back pain in lifters typically originates from one of three sources: (1) acute disc load from spinal flexion under heavy compressive load (rounded lower back deadlifts), (2) cumulative erector strain from volume exceeding recovery capacity, or (3) SI joint irritation from asymmetric loading (single-leg patterns, uneven carries). True disc herniation from resistance training is less common than is feared — most "disc herniation" symptoms in recreational lifters are temporary disc irritation that resolves with reduced load and time.

**Risk factors specific to resistance training:**
- Spinal flexion (lumbar rounding) under maximal deadlift or squat loads
- Rapid increases in deadlift or row volume
- Insufficient hip flexion mobility, causing lumbar compensation to achieve hinge depth
- Anterior pelvic tilt pattern: overactive hip flexors + weak glutes, creating chronic lumbar hyperextension

**Volume tolerance.** Spinal loading accumulates across all sessions — not just deadlifts. Rows, squats, overhead press all contribute to lumbar compressive load. Total weekly "spinal loading events" (sets of compounds with meaningful lumbar demand) should not exceed 20–25 for most recreational lifters. Above that, recovery becomes the bottleneck.

**Warning signs.** Diffuse lumbar ache 12–24 hours after heavy sessions. Tightness and reduced morning ROM. Asymmetric low back pain. Any radiating pain into the buttocks or legs is a red flag requiring medical evaluation before continuing loaded spinal work.

---

### Shoulder Pain (Rotator Cuff Impingement, Bicipital Tendinopathy, AC Joint)

**Primary movements affected.** Bench press, overhead press, pull-ups, rows.

**Mechanism.** Shoulder impingement syndrome occurs when soft tissue (typically the supraspinatus tendon or subacromial bursa) is compressed between the head of the humerus and the acromion process of the scapula. In resistance training, this is typically caused by: (1) internal rotation bias (too much pressing relative to pulling), (2) inadequate thoracic extension for overhead movements, or (3) poor scapular upward rotation mechanics under load.

**Risk factors:**
- Pressing-to-pulling volume ratio greater than 1:1 (equal or more pulling than pushing prevents most shoulder overuse)
- Rapid increase in bench or overhead press volume
- Limited thoracic extension (desk posture) → shoulder impingement risk during overhead movements
- Weak lower trapezius and serratus anterior (scapular stabilizers)
- Wide-grip overhead pressing (>1.5× shoulder width) increases impingement risk

**Volume tolerance.** Rotator cuff tendons are small and accumulate fatigue quickly. Total overhead pressing volume (overhead press + incline + any vertical pushing) above 10–14 sets per week increases impingement risk in lifters with pre-existing limited shoulder mobility. Pulling volume (rows + pull-ups/lat pulldowns) should meet or exceed pushing volume at all times.

**Warning signs.** "Catching" sensation at the top of overhead press. Anterior shoulder pain during external rotation. Pain when reaching across the body or behind the back. Pain lying on the affected side. These are early impingement signs — address them with movement modification before they become chronic.

---

### Elbow Pain (Lateral Epicondylitis, Medial Epicondylitis, Olecranon Bursitis)

**Primary movements affected.** Rows, curls, tricep work, overhead press.

**Mechanism.** Lateral epicondylitis ("tennis elbow") involves degeneration of the extensor carpi radialis brevis origin at the lateral epicondyle — typically from repeated gripping and wrist extension under load. Medial epicondylitis ("golfer's elbow") involves the flexor-pronator mass origin — more commonly from rowing and curling patterns with heavy gripping demands.

**Risk factors:**
- Rapid increase in rowing or pulling volume
- High-grip-demand exercises (barbell rows, heavy DB rows, farmer's walks) accumulated too quickly
- Lack of direct forearm conditioning: jumping into heavy rows without building grip/forearm endurance first
- Improper elbow position during tricep work (flared elbows in skull crushers)

**Volume tolerance.** Direct arm work (curls, tricep isolation) above 8–12 sets per week is rarely productive and frequently irritates elbow tendons. When doing heavy compound pulling, monitor for forearm/elbow tenderness — it often precedes lateral epicondylitis by 1–2 weeks.

## Mobility Prerequisites by Movement Pattern

These are minimum mobility benchmarks. Lifters who do not meet them are at elevated injury risk from the corresponding movement pattern and should address the deficit before loading aggressively.

### Squat — Ankle Dorsiflexion

**Prerequisite.** Approximately 15–20° of ankle dorsiflexion in weight-bearing (knee-to-wall test: 10–12 cm of clearance between toes and wall with the knee touching while heel stays flat). Below this threshold, the heel will rise during the squat or the torso will pitch forward excessively to compensate.

**Consequence of deficit.** Reduced dorsiflexion leads to increased knee valgus, reduced squat depth, and increased posterior chain compensatory loading (lower back). A 2015 PMC study (PMC4264643) confirmed that limited ankle dorsiflexion increases knee valgus angle during squatting — a documented ACL injury risk factor.

**Correction.** Daily weighted ankle mobility drills (banded ankle mobilization, kneeling ankle stretches into a wall, calf foam rolling), combined with heel elevation during squats as an interim accommodation while mobility improves. Consistent mobility work shows measurable improvement within 4–8 weeks.

### Overhead Press — Thoracic Extension

**Prerequisite.** Ability to extend the thoracic spine beyond a neutral curve to approximately 25–30° of thoracic extension without lumbar compensation. Tested by lying over a foam roller and assessing passive extension.

**Consequence of deficit.** Limited thoracic extension forces the lifter to compensate with lumbar hyperextension during overhead press — increasing disc compressive force and impingement risk. The shoulder cannot safely move into full flexion without the thoracic spine extending beneath it.

**Correction.** Thoracic extension over a foam roller (3×60 seconds daily), seated thoracic rotation, prone "Y" raises. Thoracic mobility responds to consistent daily work but requires 6–12 weeks to show meaningful change in chronic desk workers.

### Hip Hinge — Hip Flexion

**Prerequisite.** Ability to passively flex the hip to 100–110° (thigh past horizontal) with a neutral lumbar spine, tested in a supine hip flexion test. Insufficient hip flexion forces the lumbar spine to flex to compensate at the bottom of a deadlift or squat.

**Correction.** 90/90 hip stretching, pigeon pose, active hip flexion CARs (controlled articular rotations). This is typically a soft tissue restriction, not a bony one, and responds well to active mobility practice.

### Pull-Up / Overhead Hanging — Scapular Upward Rotation

**Prerequisite.** Full dead hang position without pain, and the ability to perform scapular pull-ups (elevate and depress scapulae through full range without elbow bend). Inability to control scapular position during hanging predicts shoulder impingement during vertical pulling.

**Correction.** Scapular pull-ups (3 sets of 10 depressions and elevations), face pulls with a focus on scapular retraction at end range, and band pull-aparts.

## Deload and Unload Week Protocols

### Why Deloads Are Necessary (and When They Are Not)

Fatigue accumulates during training. Fatigue masks fitness — you become fitter during a block of training, but the accumulated fatigue prevents you from expressing that fitness. A deload removes the fatigue layer, allowing the fitness gain to become visible.

Contrary to popular belief, you do not need to deload on a rigid 4-week schedule. Deload timing should be driven by performance and subjective readiness markers:

**Schedule a deload when any two of these are true:**
- Session RPE consistently runs 0.5–1 point higher than usual for the same weights
- Sleep quality is poor despite adequate time in bed
- Motivation to train is consistently low for 4–7 days
- Performance has stagnated or regressed for 2+ consecutive sessions despite adequate nutrition
- Joint tenderness is present at rest (not just during training)
- Three or more consecutive weeks of volume-increasing mesocycle completed

**You probably do not need a deload if** training feels productive, performance is progressing, and no performance regression or joint tenderness is present — even if "4 weeks have passed." Deload frequency is individual, not calendar-driven.

### Deload Structure: Practical Protocols

**Volume reduction deload (most common).** Reduce training volume by 40–50% for one week while maintaining session intensity within 5% of recent training. If you normally do 5×5 at 85%, do 3×3 at 82–85%. This preserves the neural pattern and intensity signal while giving tissues time to remodel. Research (PMC10948666) confirms this is the most commonly used and reported deload method among strength and physique athletes.

**Intensity reduction deload.** Maintain volume but reduce load to 60–65% of 1RM across all exercises. Useful when joint tenderness is the primary concern (heavy loads are the irritant) but you want to maintain movement frequency. This is the appropriate deload when you have a confirmed early-stage tendinopathy.

**Frequency reduction deload.** Reduce training days from, say, 5 to 3 per week for one week, maintaining normal volume and intensity on the sessions that do occur. Useful when overall fatigue (hormonal, CNS) is the primary concern rather than tissue-level overuse.

**Full rest week.** Rarely necessary for recreational lifters; appropriate only after very extended high-volume blocks, illness, or significant life stress. One week of no training produces no meaningful detraining in trained individuals — muscle loss does not begin in earnest for approximately 2–3 weeks of complete inactivity.

### Expected Recovery From Deload

A 2024 PeerJ study (PMC10809978) examined the effects of a one-week deload during a 9-week resistance training program and found: no negative effect on hypertrophy, no negative effect on power, no negative effect on local muscular endurance. There was a modest, temporary reduction in lower-body maximum strength during the deload week, which rebounded in the subsequent weeks. The implication: deloads do not cost you progress — they are part of the progression, not an interruption to it.

## Contingency Modifications: Maintaining Progress When Primary Movements Are Unavailable

A fundamental principle of training management: there is almost always a modification that preserves the training stimulus when a primary movement is unavailable. Stopping entirely is rarely the correct response to a minor injury or equipment limitation.

### Lower Extremity Injury: Squat or Deadlift Unavailable

| Primary Movement | Contingency Options |
|---|---|
| Back squat | Box squat at pain-free height, goblet squat, leg press, split squat |
| Conventional deadlift | Trap bar deadlift, rack pull (from knee height up), RDL if lower back is unaffected |
| Running/jumping | Seated bike, elliptical, upper body HIIT circuit |
| Both squat and hinge | Upper body hypertrophy focus; single-leg work on unaffected side (preserves some bilaterally through cross-education effect) |

### Upper Extremity Injury: Press or Pull Unavailable

| Primary Movement | Contingency Options |
|---|---|
| Bench press | Floor press (reduced ROM), incline at pain-free angle, dumbbell flyes if shoulder allows |
| Overhead press | Lateral raises, face pulls, land-mine press (more forgiving shoulder angle) |
| Pull-up / Row | Lower resistance variation (band-assisted, machine row at lower load), scapular stabilization work |
| Wrist/elbow pain during grip | Straps for pulling, neutral grip (hammer grip) reduces elbow stress, switch to machine work temporarily |

### Lower Back: Hinge and Squat Temporarily Removed

When lower back pain prevents both squatting and hinging, the contingency protocol is:
1. Maintain upper body work completely (bench, OHP, rows, pull-ups)
2. Replace squats with unweighted split squats and step-ups at pain-free depth
3. Replace deadlifts with hip thrusts and glute bridges (minimal spinal compressive load)
4. Add daily McGill Big Three protocol (McGill curl-up, side plank, bird-dog) for spine stabilization
5. Reintroduce loaded squats at 50–60% of prior training weight after 2 weeks of symptom resolution

### Shoulder: Pressing Temporarily Removed

When shoulder impingement prevents all pressing:
1. Maintain all pulling work (rows, pull-ups, face pulls) — typically pain-free
2. Replace pressing with face pulls, band pull-aparts, and external rotation exercises (corrective phase)
3. After 7–14 days of symptom reduction, reintroduce pressing from a pain-free angle (incline or floor press) at 40–50% prior load
4. Do not rush to overhead — it is the last to return. Start with incline, then flat, then overhead as pain allows

## Volume Tolerance Thresholds: When to Reduce

The injury prevention question for resistance training is not just "am I doing too much?" but "am I doing too much for this tissue, at this rate of increase, given my recovery context?" The following thresholds are derived from the ACWR research and the MEV/MRV framework.

### Absolute Volume Limits by Movement Pattern

These are conservative practical ceilings for recreational lifters training 3–5 days per week with adequate recovery. Going above these requires explicit monitoring of performance, joint tenderness, and systemic recovery markers.

| Movement Pattern / Tissue | Practical Weekly Ceiling (sets) | Tissue Most at Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Squat-pattern (all knee-dominant) | 20–25 | Patellar tendon |
| Hinge-pattern (all hip-dominant) | 16–20 | Lumbar erectors, hamstring origin |
| Horizontal push (all pressing) | 18–22 | Rotator cuff, pec minor, AC joint |
| Overhead push | 10–14 | Supraspinatus tendon, subacromial bursa |
| Horizontal pull (rows) | 20–24 | Lateral epicondyle, bicep tendon |
| Vertical pull (pull-ups/pulldowns) | 16–20 | Bicep tendon, shoulder labrum |
| Total direct arm work (curls/extensions) | 12–16 | Lateral/medial epicondyle tendons |

The overhead push ceiling (10–14 sets) is lower than other patterns because the rotator cuff tendons have the smallest cross-sectional area of any major tendon group in the body and accumulate fatigue quickly. Exceed this ceiling and impingement risk rises substantially in individuals who already have limited thoracic extension.

### Rate of Volume Increase: The 10-15% Rule in Practice

The ACWR "danger zone" above 1.5 is most commonly triggered not by absolute weekly volume but by sudden spikes. For a lifter averaging 12 sets per muscle group per week, adding 4 sets in a single week (a 33% increase) places them in the high-risk zone regardless of the absolute number. Practical examples:

- If your quadriceps training volume has been 12 sets/week for the past month, adding a new squat variation without reducing elsewhere takes that to 16–18 sets in week 1 — a sudden 33–50% spike. This is where patellar tendon overuse begins its 2–4 week silent accumulation before pain appears.
- Adding "just one more set" per exercise in a full-body program adds 4–6 sets to multiple muscle groups simultaneously — a compound spike across all tissues.

**The practical rule.** When introducing a new exercise, reduce sets elsewhere in the same movement pattern by at least as many sets as you're adding. Net volume change should be ≤15% above prior 4-week average.

### Recovery Context Modulates the Ceiling

These thresholds assume adequate sleep (7–9 hours) and adequate protein intake (1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight). Under suboptimal recovery conditions, the effective ceiling drops:

- Sleep 5–6 hours: reduce weekly volume ceiling by 20–30% (tissue remodeling rate is reduced; the same volume accumulates more residual damage)
- High life stress period: reduce by 10–20% (elevated cortisol impairs satellite cell proliferation and tendon collagen synthesis)
- Active illness or recent illness (within 1–2 weeks): treat training age as having reset by 6–12 months for ceiling purposes

## Sleep as Primary Injury Prevention

Sleep occupies a separate section here not because it is unusual advice but because the mechanism is precise enough to justify its inclusion in an injury prevention framework, not just a general wellness discussion.

### The Tissue Remodeling Window

Tendons, ligaments, bone cortex, and cartilage undergo their primary structural remodeling during sleep. The mechanism is growth hormone (GH) mediated: GH stimulates insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), which drives collagen synthesis in connective tissue. Approximately 70% of the nightly GH pulse occurs during slow-wave sleep (N3) in the first 2–3 hours of sleep. Disrupted or shortened sleep directly reduces the collagen synthesis window that tendons depend on for adaptation.

**The direct evidence.** A 2021 randomized crossover trial (PMC7785053) found that one night of total sleep deprivation reduced postprandial muscle protein synthesis by 18%, reduced testosterone AUC by 24%, and increased daytime cortisol by 21%. Cortisol at chronically elevated levels inhibits tenocyte proliferation — the cells responsible for tendon collagen production. The combination of reduced anabolic signal and elevated catabolic signal creates a net negative remodeling balance in connective tissue.

**Implication for overuse injury risk.** A lifter maintaining 18–20 sets per muscle per week during a mesocycle where sleep averages 5–6 hours is doing so with significantly reduced tendon repair capacity. They may feel muscularly recovered (muscle repair is partially redundant with multiple mechanisms) while connective tissue is progressively degenerating. Overuse injuries in this context appear to "come out of nowhere" — the lifter was not aware of the deficit because muscle function remained adequate until the tendon failed.

### Sleep Prescriptions for High-Volume Training Blocks

- During accumulation phases (highest volume): target 8–9 hours. This is when connective tissue is under the most load per week.
- During deload weeks: 8–9 hours is still appropriate — the remodeling occurs after load, not during it.
- The night before a maximum intensity test (1RM week, sport competition): sleep quality matters more than duration. Prioritize sleep onset time consistency (same time as habitual ±30 minutes) over attempting to sleep extra.
- When sleep is unavoidably compromised for 3+ consecutive days: proactively reduce training volume by 20–30% for that week. Do not rely on subjective readiness — connective tissue degradation is not felt until pain appears.

## Summary: The Minimum Viable Injury Prevention Checklist

1. Never increase weekly volume by more than 10–15% in a single week.
2. Pulling volume must equal or exceed pushing volume on a weekly basis.
3. Deload when performance markers indicate fatigue, not on a fixed calendar.
4. Address ankle and thoracic mobility deficits before loading squat and overhead patterns heavily.
5. If a movement causes joint pain (not muscular fatigue), stop the movement that session. Modify before returning.
6. Tendon adaptation takes 10–26 weeks. Be patient when working through or around tendinopathy.
7. Sleep is the highest-leverage injury prevention tool available — tissue remodeling predominantly occurs during deep sleep stages.
