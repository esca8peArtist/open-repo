# Nutrition 04 — Supplementation Guide

**Ground rule:** Supplements are the last 5% of results, not the first 50%. The hierarchy is:

1. Total daily calories and macros (Nutrition 01)
2. Whole-food diet with micronutrient diversity
3. Sleep and stress management (Nutrition 06)
4. Supplements — only then

This guide covers the **10 best-supported supplements** for athletes doing resistance training and conditioning. Each entry includes: evidence quality, specific dose, optimal timing, realistic effect size, cost, and who actually needs it.

Anything not on this list (testosterone boosters, proprietary amino acid blends, most pre-workouts) either lacks meaningful evidence or has effects that can be replicated for free through food and training.

---

## Tier 1: Foundational Supplements (Strong Evidence, Most Athletes Benefit)

### 1. Creatine Monohydrate

**Evidence quality:** Extremely strong. Most studied sports supplement in existence. 500+ studies, replicated across independent labs globally.

**What it does:** Increases intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, allowing ATP regeneration to continue longer in high-intensity (anaerobic) efforts. The practical effect is an additional 1–3 reps per set in the 1–8 rep range, and faster recovery between sets.

**Effect size from meta-analyses (2024):**
- Strength (1RM): +5–10% over 4–12 weeks vs. training alone
- Lean mass gain: +1.3–2.3 kg additional lean mass over 8 weeks vs. training alone (PMC10180745 — creatine + RT promotes small but consistent regional hypertrophy)
- High-intensity work capacity: +10–15% in repeated sprint protocols
- These effects are **additive** to training, not replacements for it

**Dose:**
- **Loading phase (optional, faster saturation):** 20 g/day divided into 4 × 5 g doses for 5–7 days
- **Maintenance phase (standard):** 3–5 g/day indefinitely
- **No loading (slower saturation):** 3–5 g/day for 28 days produces identical results by week 4–5

**Timing:** Timing does not matter significantly. Some evidence favours post-workout creatine (slightly better muscle uptake due to insulin sensitivity). In practice: take it whenever you'll be consistent — pre-workout, post-workout, or with any meal.

**Form:** Creatine monohydrate (not creatine HCl, ethyl ester, or "buffered" forms). Monohydrate has the most evidence. It is also the cheapest.

**Who needs it:**
- Everyone doing resistance training benefits
- Vegans benefit the most (dietary creatine intake is zero from plant foods)
- Older athletes (40+) have naturally declining creatine stores
- High-frequency athletes (5–6 days/week) benefit from the recovery acceleration

**Who does not need it:**
- Athletes in sport-specific phases where weight categories matter (creatine adds 0.5–1 kg water weight from intramuscular water retention)

**Side effects:** GI distress if taken all at once (split dosing prevents this). Mild water retention (+0.5–1 kg). No kidney damage in healthy individuals — this myth is definitively debunked.

**Cost:** $0.10–0.20/day (5 g/day from bulk monohydrate powder). One of the cheapest supplements per-serving. Avoid brand-name "complex" creatine products charging 5–10× more for no additional benefit.

**Monthly cost:** $3–6/month (bulk powder, 500 g container)

---

### 2. Protein Powder (Whey or Plant-Based)

**Evidence quality:** Strong. Protein supplementation combined with resistance training increases muscle mass and strength vs. training alone (confirmed in Morton et al. 2018, 49 studies, PMC5867436).

**What it does:** Provides a convenient, fast-digesting source of complete protein. Does not have magical properties beyond its amino acid content — it is food in powder form.

**Who needs it:**
- Athletes who cannot consistently hit their protein targets from whole food alone
- People with small appetites or busy schedules
- Vegans who struggle to meet leucine thresholds from whole plants alone
- Post-workout: when whole food is impractical within the 1–2 hour window

**Who does not need it:**
- Athletes already hitting 1.8+ g/kg from food consistently

**Whey protein (gold standard):**
- DIAAS score > 1.0 — highest quality protein available
- Fastest digestion of all proteins — optimal for post-workout
- High leucine content: approximately 11–12% leucine by weight
- **Dose:** 25–35 g per serving
- **Best timing:** Post-workout or as a meal supplement between whole-food meals
- **Form:** Whey concentrate (cheaper, 70–80% protein, some lactose) or whey isolate (90%+ protein, minimal lactose — better for lactose-sensitive athletes)
- **Monthly cost:** $25–40 for 30 servings (bulk/store brand); $40–70 for premium brands (same effect)

**Plant-based protein powder:**
- **Pea + rice blend:** Best plant combination; covers the amino acid gaps that each has individually. Look for 25+ g protein per serving.
- **Soy protein isolate:** DIAAS ~0.90; alone is the best single plant source. Estrogen concerns (phytoestrogens) are not clinically significant at supplement doses — the evidence for harm in healthy men does not hold at intake levels from food or standard supplement doses.
- **Hemp protein:** Lower protein density (15 g/serving), but good omega-3 profile. Use as a complement, not a primary source.
- **Dose:** 30–40 g powder (plant proteins require slightly higher doses to match whey's leucine delivery)
- **Monthly cost:** $30–50 for 30 servings

**What to look for on labels:**
- Protein content per serving > 22 g
- Leucine content per serving > 2.5 g (often not listed — rule of thumb: 10% of protein weight for whey)
- Minimal fillers, added sugars, or proprietary blends
- Third-party tested (NSF Certified or Informed Sport — particularly for drug-tested athletes)

## Tier 2: Targeted Supplements (Evidence-Based, Fill Specific Gaps)

### 3. Vitamin D3

**Evidence quality:** Strong for deficiency correction; moderate for ergogenic benefit in replete athletes.

**The deficiency problem:** Studies suggest 40–80% of indoor athletes and those at northern latitudes (> 35°N) are vitamin D deficient or insufficient (< 30 ng/mL serum 25(OH)D). Deficiency impairs:
- Muscle contractile function and force production
- Bone density and fracture resistance
- Immune function (increased upper respiratory infection risk)
- Testosterone production (correlates with 25(OH)D levels)

**Effect in deficient athletes:** A 2024 meta-analysis (MDPI Nutrients) confirms that vitamin D3 supplementation improves lower-limb muscle strength in deficient athletes. Effect size is clinically meaningful — comparable to adding a month of structured training.

**Effect in replete athletes:** Minimal additional ergogenic benefit once serum levels are > 40 ng/mL.

**Protocol:**
- **Test first:** Serum 25(OH)D test (standard blood test, covered by most insurance)
- **Deficient (< 20 ng/mL):** 4,000–5,000 IU/day for 12 weeks, then retest
- **Insufficient (20–30 ng/mL):** 2,000–3,000 IU/day
- **Maintenance (> 30 ng/mL but low sun exposure):** 1,000–2,000 IU/day
- **Form:** D3 (cholecalciferol) — not D2 (ergocalciferol, weaker and shorter-lasting)
- **Take with:** A fat-containing meal (fat-soluble; absorption drops significantly without dietary fat)
- **Pair with:** Vitamin K2 (100–200 mcg) to direct calcium to bone rather than vascular tissue

**Monthly cost:** $5–10/month (D3 2000 IU tablets are extremely cheap; combined D3+K2 capsules: $10–15/month)

---

### 4. Magnesium Glycinate

**Evidence quality:** Moderate to strong for sleep improvement and recovery; moderate for performance in deficient athletes.

**The deficiency problem:** Athletes lose magnesium through sweat. Studies consistently find below-adequate dietary magnesium in athletes eating normal diets. Athletes likely need 400–600 mg/day vs. the general population RDA of 310–420 mg.

**Effects:**
- Sleep quality improvement (reduced sleep latency, increased sleep efficiency) — confirmed in multiple RCTs
- Reduced nighttime cortisol
- Muscle relaxation between training sessions (reduces cramping)
- ATP synthesis support (magnesium is a cofactor in the Mg-ATP complex)

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** 200–400 mg elemental magnesium (glycinate form) at night
- **Timing:** 30–60 minutes before sleep (leverages GABA-agonist effect for sleep onset)
- **Form:** Glycinate or malate — best absorbed and best tolerated. Avoid oxide (poorly absorbed, causes diarrhoea at doses above 200 mg).
- **Note:** "Elemental magnesium" content differs from total weight of the compound (magnesium glycinate: 14% elemental Mg by weight; a 500 mg capsule contains approximately 70 mg elemental magnesium — check your label)

**Monthly cost:** $12–20/month for 300–400 mg/night

### 5. Omega-3 Fish Oil (EPA + DHA)

**Evidence quality:** Moderate. Anti-inflammatory effects are well-established; direct hypertrophy benefit is modest but consistent.

**What it does:**
- Reduces DOMS (delayed-onset muscle soreness) by modulating the inflammatory response to exercise
- Modest enhancement of muscle protein synthesis (independent of total protein intake)
- Cardiovascular support (EPA+DHA reduce triglycerides and support vascular health)
- Brain health and cognitive function — relevant for skill-based training

**Effect size:** 1.5–2 g combined EPA+DHA/day for 8 weeks reduces CK (muscle damage markers) and DOMS by 15–20% vs. placebo in multiple RCTs. Not large, but meaningful for athletes training 5–6 days/week.

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** 1,000–3,000 mg combined EPA+DHA daily (not total fish oil — read the label for EPA+DHA content)
- **Timing:** With meals for best absorption (fat-soluble)
- **Form:** Triglyceride form > ethyl ester form for bioavailability; or algae-based EPA+DHA for vegans (identical effect, plant-derived)
- **Fish oil freshness matters:** Rancid fish oil (oxidised) is worse than no fish oil; buy refrigerated, check expiry, taste for rancidity

**Who needs it most:** Athletes eating < 2 servings fatty fish per week; high-inflammation training phases

**Monthly cost:** $10–20/month (2 g EPA+DHA from standard gel capsules)

---

### 6. Caffeine

**Evidence quality:** Very strong. Most studied cognitive and performance enhancer. Caffeine is the one pre-workout compound that actually works and has an effect size worth discussing.

**What it does:**
- Adenosine receptor antagonist — blocks the signal that causes fatigue perception
- Increases adrenaline secretion
- Performance effects: +3–7% time trial performance, +2–5% strength output, meaningful reduction in perceived exertion

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** 3–6 mg/kg bodyweight (for an 82 kg athlete: 245–490 mg). Start at the lower end.
- **Timing:** 30–60 minutes pre-workout (peak plasma level at 60 min)
- **Cycling:** Take 1–2 days off/week or 1 week off per month to prevent tolerance build-up
- **Avoid after 2 PM** if training in evenings — half-life of 5–7 hours means 200 mg at 3 PM = still 100 mg at 8–10 PM

**Sources:** Coffee (80–100 mg/cup), pre-workout powders, caffeine tablets (cheapest and most precise dosing)

**Caution:** Anxiety, elevated heart rate, sleep disruption, GI distress at high doses. Not recommended for athletes with anxiety disorders or arrhythmias.

**Monthly cost:** Essentially free if from coffee; $5–10/month for caffeine tablets

## Tier 3: Situational Supplements (Evidence-Based for Specific Circumstances)

### 7. Carbohydrate Supplements (for Athletes > 10 hrs/week Training)

**Who needs this:** Athletes combining the Phase 2 sports-specific program with their gym work, or those running 6-day/week high-volume programs with consistent conditioning.

**Options:**
- **Maltodextrin/dextrose blend:** Fastest-digesting, cheapest, 4 cal/g. Mix into water for intra- or post-workout drink. 40–80 g per serving.
- **Cyclic dextrin (Highly Branched Cyclic Dextrin — HBCD):** Low osmolality, faster gastric emptying than maltodextrin. Notably less GI distress during training. Cost: $0.50–1.00/serving vs. $0.05 for maltodextrin.
- **Commercial sports drinks (Gatorade, Powerade):** Pre-mixed with electrolytes. Convenient; somewhat more expensive per gram of carbs.

**Dose:** 30–60 g/hour during training; 60–100 g in the post-workout window

**When NOT to use:** During fat-loss phases; during deload weeks; for athletes under 10 hours/week training (whole food carbs are sufficient)

**Monthly cost:** $5–30/month depending on form and volume

---

### 8. Collagen Peptides + Vitamin C (for Joint and Tendon Health)

**Evidence quality:** Moderate. Growing evidence from Shaw et al. and subsequent work showing that collagen + vitamin C consumed 30–60 minutes before load-bearing exercise increases collagen synthesis markers in tendons.

**Who needs it:**
- Athletes with tendinopathy history (patellar, Achilles, rotator cuff)
- Athletes starting heavier loading after a period off
- Older athletes (35+) where collagen turnover is naturally reduced

**Protocol:**
- **Collagen peptides:** 10–15 g, taken 30–60 minutes before training or physical therapy
- **Vitamin C:** 250–500 mg consumed at the same time (required cofactor for collagen synthesis)
- **Timing critical:** Must be consumed before mechanical load for the synthesis signal to peak during exercise
- **Form:** Hydrolysed collagen peptides (hydrolysis improves absorption vs. gelatin)

**Note:** Collagen is not a substitute for adequate protein from complete sources — it is low in many essential amino acids. Supplement on top of adequate protein intake.

**Monthly cost:** $15–30/month for 10 g/day

---

### 9. Beta-Alanine (for Conditioning Athletes)

**Evidence quality:** Moderate. Consistent evidence for buffering benefit in high-intensity efforts lasting **1–4 minutes**.

**What it does:** Increases intramuscular carnosine levels (a buffer against the pH drop during glycolysis — the "burn" sensation). This extends the time before acidosis-induced fatigue in repeated sprint protocols, circuit training, and conditioning rounds.

**Who benefits:**
- Athletes doing repeated high-intensity intervals (30 sec–4 min efforts)
- Combat sport athletes, team sport athletes
- Those running high-rep circuit conditioning blocks

**Who does not benefit:**
- Pure strength athletes (< 10 rep work rarely depletes the carnosine buffer system)
- Pure endurance athletes (aerobic work at > 4 min effort duration is not limited by carnosine)

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** 3.2–6.4 g/day divided into 1.6 g doses (to minimize paresthesia — the harmless tingling side effect of higher single doses)
- **Loading period:** Takes 4–6 weeks of daily supplementation to fully saturate carnosine stores; no acute effect
- **Monthly cost:** $10–20/month

---

### 10. Zinc Picolinate (for Athletes with Low Dietary Zinc)

**Evidence quality:** Moderate for deficiency correction; no benefit in replete athletes.

**Who needs it:**
- Vegetarians and vegans (phytates in plant foods block zinc absorption; plant-based athletes may need 50% higher intake)
- Athletes in prolonged caloric restriction (cutting phases)
- High-volume sweaters (zinc lost in sweat)

**Deficiency effects:** Reduced testosterone, impaired immune function, slowed protein synthesis, reduced wound healing.

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** 15–25 mg elemental zinc/day
- **Form:** Picolinate or citrate (better absorbed than oxide)
- **Timing:** With food — zinc can cause nausea when taken on an empty stomach
- **Caution:** Do not exceed 40 mg/day (upper limit) — chronic excess impairs copper absorption

**Monthly cost:** $5–10/month

## What Not To Buy

**ZMA (Zinc/Magnesium/B6 stack):** Overpriced combination of zinc and magnesium you can buy separately for a fraction of the cost. The "B6 synergy" claim is unsupported. Buy zinc and magnesium separately.

**Testosterone boosters (Tribulus, D-Aspartic Acid):** No evidence of clinically meaningful testosterone increase in healthy men. Literally zero evidence of anabolic effect in resistance-trained athletes.

**BCAAs (Branched Chain Amino Acids):** Completely redundant if you are hitting 1.6+ g/kg protein from food or whey. Leucine from whole protein stimulates the same signalling. You are paying for a fraction of what you already get from a $1 serving of chicken breast.

**Glutamine:** No benefit for muscle gain or recovery in athletes eating adequate protein. The body produces enough glutamine endogenously when protein intake is sufficient.

**HMB (Beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate):** Small positive signal in untrained or elderly populations. No meaningful benefit in resistance-trained athletes already eating adequate protein.

**"Mass gainers":** Almost always low-quality maltodextrin and cheap protein powder. Make your own: whey + oats + milk + banana costs 30–60% less and has higher food quality.

## Cost-Benefit Summary

| Supplement | Monthly cost | Evidence grade | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creatine monohydrate | $3–6 | A+ | Everyone doing RT |
| Protein powder | $25–50 | A | Athletes not hitting protein from food |
| Vitamin D3 (+ K2) | $8–15 | A (if deficient) | Test first; most indoor athletes |
| Magnesium glycinate | $12–20 | B+ | Athletes with poor sleep or high volume |
| Omega-3 fish oil | $10–20 | B | < 2 fish servings/week |
| Caffeine | $0–10 | A | Pre-workout, optional |
| Carb supplements | $5–30 | A (for high-volume) | > 10 hrs/week training only |
| Collagen + Vit C | $15–30 | B | Joint issues; older athletes |
| Beta-alanine | $10–20 | B | Conditioning-heavy athletes |
| Zinc picolinate | $5–10 | B (if deficient) | Vegetarians/vegans; deficit phases |

**Minimum effective stack (< $20/month):**
1. Creatine monohydrate 5 g/day ($5)
2. Vitamin D3 2,000 IU/day ($5)
3. Magnesium glycinate 300 mg/night ($12)

**Optimal athlete stack (< $60/month):**
All of the above + omega-3 fish oil ($15) + protein powder ($20 if needed)
