# Nutrition 05 — Nutrition Periodization

**What this is:** A structured plan for deliberately changing your calorie intake, macros, and food composition across weeks and months to match the specific demands of each training block. The principle is simple: eat more when training demands more; eat less when it demands less; never apply a fixed diet to a variable training schedule.

**The core problem with static nutrition:** A fixed 2,800 cal/day plan applied across a hypertrophy block, a deload week, a conditioning phase, and an active rest week will consistently overfeed in some periods and underfeed in others. Both conditions are sub-optimal. Nutrition periodization eliminates this mismatch.

**Integration note:** Read in conjunction with `comprehensive-plan.md` for phase structure and `phase-2-periodization-research.md` for the evidence base on training periodization.

## Part 1 — The Nutritional Periodization Model

### Three Timescales

**Macrocycle (12–24 weeks):** The full training year or goal period. Nutrition goals shift between bulking, maintaining, and cutting across seasons. A recreational athlete running a 12-week Phase 1 → 12-week Phase 2 cycle follows a clear macrocycle with different nutritional priorities in each half.

**Mesocycle (4–6 weeks):** Each training block within the macrocycle. Examples: a 4-week hypertrophy block (high carbs, moderate surplus), a 4-week strength block (lower carbs, smaller surplus), a 2-week deload (maintenance), a 4-week conditioning block (high carbs, maintenance).

**Microcycle (1 week, day-to-day):** Within each week, caloric and carbohydrate intake varies based on the training schedule. Heavy compound days get more carbs; rest days get fewer. This is sometimes called "carb cycling" — nutrition periodization subsumes it.

### The "Fuel for the Work Required" Principle

Established by the IOC consensus on athlete nutrition and confirmed in Morton et al. 2025, this principle states: carbohydrate availability should be deliberately adjusted meal-by-meal and day-by-day according to:
- The intensity of the upcoming training session
- The duration of that session
- The training goal of that session (strength, hypertrophy, conditioning, skill)

**Practical implication:** A heavy squat day requires 400–500 g carbs. A yoga/mobility rest day requires 150–200 g. The difference is justified and intentional, not a failure of consistency.

## Part 2 — Caloric Models by Training Block

### Model 1: Caloric Surplus (Building Block)

**When:** During the hypertrophy or strength-building mesocycle. Corresponds to:
- Phase 1 (Weeks 1–4, all tiers) — neural adaptation and early hypertrophy
- Phase 2 (Weeks 5–12, hypertrophy block) — peak muscle-building period
- Any training block where volume and intensity are at their highest

**Surplus size:**
| Athlete experience | Surplus | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Beginner (< 1 year training) | +300–500 cal/day | Higher muscle-building rate allows larger surplus without excess fat gain |
| Intermediate (1–3 years) | +200–350 cal/day | Rate of gain is slower; smaller surplus limits fat accumulation |
| Advanced (3+ years) | +100–200 cal/day | Marginal gains; any larger surplus produces predominantly fat |

**Duration:** 8–16 weeks maximum before reassessing. Longer than 16 weeks at a caloric surplus typically results in excessive fat gain that overshoots the lean-mass-to-fat gain target ratio.

**Macro composition in surplus:**
- Protein: 1.8–2.2 g/kg (maximum stimulus for MPS)
- Carbs: 5–7 g/kg (fuel for high-volume training)
- Fats: 0.8–1.2 g/kg (maintained at hormonal minimum)
- Extra calories come from carbs — not fat

**Expected results over a 12-week building block:**
- Beginners: 3–5 kg lean mass (with 0.5–1 kg fat)
- Intermediates: 1–2 kg lean mass (with 0.3–0.6 kg fat)
- Advanced: 0.5–1 kg lean mass (with 0.2–0.4 kg fat)

### Model 2: Caloric Maintenance (Recomposition/Transition)

**When:** During transition between phases; during deload weeks; when sport-specific skill training is the priority over mass gain; when body weight is already at goal.

**Definition:** Maintenance calories = TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). Weight is stable ±0.5 kg/week on average.

**Macro composition at maintenance:**
- Protein: 1.8–2.0 g/kg (preserved at high level)
- Carbs: 3–5 g/kg (adequate for performance, not surplus)
- Fats: 0.8–1.2 g/kg

**Who benefits from extended maintenance phases:**
- Athletes who have gained significant fat during a long surplus
- Athletes entering a competition or sport season (body weight stability preferred)
- Intermediate+ athletes who want body recomposition (see Part 4)

### Model 3: Caloric Deficit (Cutting/Fat Loss)

**When:** Intentional fat loss phase following a muscle-building block. Corresponds to:
- After a 12-week Phase 2 hypertrophy block
- Pre-competition fat loss phase
- When body fat has increased excessively during a surplus

**Deficit size:**
| Deficit | Rate of loss | Lean mass risk |
|---|---|---|
| −200 to −300 cal | 0.2–0.3 kg/week | Very low; sustainable indefinitely |
| −300 to −500 cal | 0.3–0.5 kg/week | Low; standard protocol |
| −500 to −750 cal | 0.5–0.7 kg/week | Moderate; requires very high protein to offset |
| > −750 cal | > 0.7 kg/week | High; muscle loss becomes significant |

**Macro composition in deficit:**
- Protein: 2.2–2.6 g/kg (elevated to preserve lean mass under caloric restriction — this is counter-intuitive but strongly supported)
- Carbs: 2.5–4 g/kg (reduced, but do not eliminate — performance and adherence suffer)
- Fats: 0.6–0.9 g/kg (floor maintained)

**Cut duration:** 4–12 weeks maximum. Beyond 12 weeks, hormonal adaptation (reduced T3, reduced leptin, elevated ghrelin) makes further fat loss progressively harder. Take a 2–4 week maintenance break before continuing.

**Cutting while training:** Per Data Driven Strength analysis (2024), athletes in a deficit should:
- Add training volume and intensity increases more gradually (0.5 RPE/week rather than 1 RPE/week)
- Schedule deloads more frequently (every 3 weeks rather than every 4)
- Expect 10–15% reduction in peak strength output — do not try to set PRs during a cut
- Prioritize protein and sleep above all else

## Part 3 — Macro Cycling with Training Phases

### Carbohydrate Periodization Within the Microcycle

The most practical form of nutrition periodization is adjusting daily carbohydrate intake based on training demands:

**Heavy training days (compound-focused, high volume):**
- Carbs at upper bound of goal range (5–7 g/kg for hypertrophy, 4–5 g/kg for strength)
- Consume majority of carbs peri-workout (Meal 2 pre-training + Meal 3 post-training)
- Example (82 kg athlete, hypertrophy): 410–574 g carbs, 2,800–3,000 cal

**Moderate training days (accessory work, skill work):**
- Carbs at middle of goal range
- Example: 300–380 g carbs, 2,500–2,700 cal

**Rest days:**
- Carbs at lower bound (2–3 g/kg)
- Example: 164–246 g carbs, 2,000–2,200 cal
- Increase fat slightly to compensate for reduced carb-derived calories

**Effect:** This achieves the benefits of "carb cycling" without the complexity of a named protocol. Total weekly calories remain similar; distribution shifts toward training days.

### Protein Remains Constant Across the Microcycle

Do not reduce protein on rest days. Muscle protein breakdown continues for 24–72 hours after the last training session. Protein during rest days is protective.

### Fat Stays at the Hormonal Floor

Fat is the balancing macro. When carbs go down (rest days, cutting phases), fat comes up to compensate for caloric reduction — but never below 0.6 g/kg.

## Part 4 — Body Recomposition Nutrition

Body recomposition — simultaneously reducing body fat and increasing lean mass — was long considered impossible except in beginners. A 2024 editorial in Frontiers in Nutrition and supporting RCTs now confirm it is achievable in trained populations when specific conditions are met.

**Conditions required for recomposition:**
1. **Protein at 2.2–2.6 g/kg/day** — mandatory; the elevated protein provides the substrate for MPS while creating a minimal caloric surplus at muscle level
2. **Moderate caloric deficit of −200 to −300 cal** — creates fat loss without significant lean mass catabolism
3. **Heavy resistance training 3–4 days/week** — the anabolic stimulus is what redirects energy partitioning toward muscle
4. **Sufficient training experience** — minimum 6–12 months; beginners can do this even at larger deficits

**Who recomposition works best for:**
- Athletes returning from injury or time off (muscle memory accelerates lean mass regain)
- Athletes with higher body fat (15%+ in men, 25%+ in women) — greater fat availability buffers the deficit
- Beginners and those with untapped genetic potential
- Athletes on a caloric maintenance who add structured resistance training for the first time

**Who should not attempt recomposition:**
- Advanced athletes near their genetic ceiling — gains are too slow at maintenance
- Athletes with a hard deadline for body composition change (cut is faster)

**Sample recomposition macros (82 kg athlete):**
- Target calories: 2,200–2,400 (slight deficit)
- Protein: 2.3 g/kg = 189 g/day
- Carbs: 3.5 g/kg = 287 g/day
- Fats: 0.8 g/kg = 66 g/day
- Caloric balance: Approximately −200 to −400 cal below TDEE

**Expected rate:** 0.2–0.4 kg fat loss per week while maintaining or slowly gaining lean mass. Bodyweight may be stable or drop very slowly — this is normal and correct.

**Tracking:** Body weight alone is misleading during recomposition (muscle density replaces fat). Track:
- Monthly progress photos
- Strength metrics (should be stable or slowly improving)
- Circumference measurements (waist decreasing while limbs stable = recomposition working)

## Part 5 — Deload Week Nutrition

### What Changes During a Deload

Training volume drops 30–40%. Intensity stays the same (weight on bar unchanged). The body uses the deload to:
- Clear accumulated fatigue
- Consolidate neural adaptations
- Synthesize additional contractile protein
- Restore sleep quality

Nutrition during deload should support the recovery process, not punish the reduced training.

**The deload nutrition mistake:** Athletes often eat less on deload weeks ("I'm not training as hard"). This is counterproductive — muscle protein synthesis and glycogen repletion need to continue through the deload for supercompensation to occur.

### Deload Week Protocol

**Calories:** Maintain at normal training-day level for the first 3–4 days, then drop to maintenance (or slightly below) for the final 3 days if coming off a surplus phase.

**Protein:** Maintain fully. Do not reduce protein during deload.

**Carbs:** Reduce slightly (by 10–15%) — less glycolytic work means slightly less glycogen demand. Do not slash.

**Fats:** May increase slightly to compensate for reduced carbs and maintain satiety.

**Specific deload nutrition targets:**
- Days 1–4 (peak recovery): Same macros as normal training days (protein + high carbs)
- Days 5–7 (consolidation): Reduce carbs by 15%; increase fats to compensate

**If deload follows a long surplus phase:** Use the deload as a 1-week transition to maintenance before beginning a cut. This resets hormonal markers (leptin, ghrelin) and prevents the "crash" feeling of sudden caloric reduction.

## Part 6 — Seasonal and Long-Term Periodization

### A Practical Annual Nutrition Plan

For a recreational athlete running two 12-week blocks per year (Phase 1 → Phase 2):

**Weeks 1–4 (Phase 1 — Foundation):**
- Goal: Establish consistent nutrition habits; begin progressive training
- Caloric position: Maintenance or very small surplus (+100–200 cal)
- Rationale: Neural adaptations dominate in Week 1–4; excess calories produce mostly fat, not muscle

**Weeks 5–8 (Phase 1 — Progression):**
- Goal: Fuel increasing training intensity
- Caloric position: Moderate surplus (+300–400 cal)
- Carbs: At upper bound of goal range
- Protein: 2.0 g/kg

**Week 9–10 (Phase 1 — Peak/Deload):**
- Deload week (Week 10): See deload protocol above

**Weeks 11–16 (Phase 2 — Hypertrophy/Strength Block):**
- Goal: Maximum muscle and strength development
- Caloric position: Full surplus (+400–500 cal for beginners; +200–300 for intermediates)
- Protein: 2.0–2.2 g/kg
- Carbs: 5–7 g/kg (highest of the year — matches highest training volume)

**Weeks 17–18 (Phase 2 — Deload + Assessment):**
- Deload + body composition assessment
- Transition nutrition: Begin dropping from surplus to maintenance over 2 weeks

**Weeks 19–24 (Sports-Specific or Cut Phase):**
- If sport-specific extension: Maintain calories; shift carb emphasis to peri-workout timing
- If fat loss goal: Move to deficit nutrition model (see Part 2)

### Energy Availability as the Master Variable

Sports nutrition research consistently emphasizes **energy availability (EA)** as the key metric rather than caloric balance alone:

EA = (Energy intake − Exercise energy expenditure) / Fat-free mass

**Adequate EA:** > 45 kcal/kg FFM/day  
**Reduced EA:** 30–45 kcal/kg FFM/day (reduced training adaptation, hormonal stress)  
**Low EA (RED-S risk):** < 30 kcal/kg FFM/day (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport — impairs reproduction, bone health, immune function)

**Practical implication:** An athlete burning 600 calories per session who also cuts 500 calories from diet may have chronically inadequate EA even though their stated caloric deficit seems manageable. Account for exercise energy expenditure when planning deficit phases.

**Red flags for inadequate energy availability:**
- Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep
- Declining performance across 2+ consecutive training blocks
- Frequent illness and slow injury healing
- Loss of menstrual function in female athletes
- Declining libido in male athletes
- Mood deterioration, irritability

**Response:** Increase calories by 300–500 above current level and reduce training intensity by 20% for 2 weeks. Reassess.

## Quick-Reference: Nutrition by Workout Phase

| Phase | Block | Caloric Target | Protein | Carbs | Primary Adjustment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1, Wk 1–4 | Foundation | Maintenance + 100–200 | 1.8 g/kg | 4 g/kg | Habit building; don't overcomplicate |
| Phase 1, Wk 5–8 | Progression | Maintenance + 300–400 | 2.0 g/kg | 5–6 g/kg | Increase carbs for volume |
| Phase 1, Wk 9–10 | Deload | Maintenance | 2.0 g/kg | 4 g/kg | Hold steady; don't restrict |
| Phase 2, Wk 1–4 | Hypertrophy | Surplus + 400–500 | 2.2 g/kg | 6–7 g/kg | Maximum carbs + protein |
| Phase 2, Wk 5–8 | Strength | Surplus + 200–300 | 2.0 g/kg | 4–5 g/kg | Slightly lower carbs vs hypertrophy |
| Phase 2, Wk 9–10 | Deload | Maintenance | 2.0 g/kg | 4 g/kg | Transition toward next goal |
| Sports extension | Specific | Maintenance | 1.8–2.0 g/kg | 4–6 g/kg | Time carbs around practice + gym |
| Cutting phase | Fat loss | Deficit − 300–500 | 2.2–2.6 g/kg | 2.5–4 g/kg | Protein elevated; carbs reduced |
| Recomposition | Both | Deficit − 200–300 | 2.3–2.6 g/kg | 3–4 g/kg | High protein + heavy RT |
