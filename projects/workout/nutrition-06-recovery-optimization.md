# Nutrition 06 — Recovery Optimization

Recovery is not the absence of training — it is the second half of the adaptation cycle. Training creates the stimulus; recovery is when adaptation occurs. Every specific recommendation in this document is aimed at making recovery faster, more complete, and more reliable.

**The recovery hierarchy:**
1. Sleep (non-negotiable)
2. Total calorie sufficiency
3. Protein adequacy
4. Hydration
5. Micronutrient status
6. Specific recovery nutrition strategies
7. Recovery supplements (Nutrition 04)

If #1–4 are broken, nothing in #5–7 compensates. Address the hierarchy in order.

---

## Part 1 — Sleep Quality Nutrition

### Why Sleep Is the Most Important Recovery Tool

During sleep, the body releases 70–80% of its daily growth hormone output in pulsatile bursts during slow-wave sleep (deep sleep, NREM stages 3–4). Growth hormone directly stimulates:
- Muscle protein synthesis
- Lipolysis (fat breakdown)
- Connective tissue repair
- Immune surveillance

Athletes who consistently sleep 5–6 hours vs. 8–9 hours show 20–30% reductions in testosterone, significant decreases in anabolic signalling, and 40% slower muscular recovery from equivalent training loads (PMC11195859, 2024).

**Sleep target:** 8–9 hours for athletes in heavy training blocks; 7–8 hours minimum. Quality matters as much as quantity — fragmented sleep with frequent waking produces poor GH release even at 8 hours.

### Nutrition Interventions for Sleep Quality

**1. Tryptophan-rich evening meals**

Tryptophan is the dietary precursor to serotonin (then melatonin). High-tryptophan foods consumed 2–3 hours before sleep may modestly improve sleep onset.

Foods with highest tryptophan content:
- Pumpkin seeds: 576 mg/100 g
- Turkey breast: 250 mg/100 g
- Cottage cheese: 180 mg/100 g
- Whole eggs: 130 mg/100 g
- Chicken breast: 290 mg/100 g

Practical application: Make dinner or the evening snack protein-rich from the above sources. A cottage cheese + pumpkin seed evening snack costs $1.50 and is one of the best sleep nutrition choices available.

**2. Carbohydrate co-ingestion at dinner**

Carbohydrate + protein at dinner (rather than protein alone) raises the ratio of tryptophan relative to competing large neutral amino acids in the blood, improving brain uptake of tryptophan and melatonin synthesis.

- Include a moderate carbohydrate serving at dinner (sweet potato, white rice, fruit)
- Avoid high-GI carbs alone before bed (blood glucose spike and crash disrupts sleep)
- Combination: Protein + moderate-GI carbs = optimal sleep setup

**3. Tart cherry juice**

The most consistently evidence-supported dietary sleep aid. Studies show + 34 minutes total sleep time and significantly improved sleep efficiency with 7–14 days of supplementation. Mechanism: naturally occurring melatonin precursors and anti-inflammatory anthocyanins.

- Protocol: 30 ml concentrate (or 250 ml juice) in morning and 30 min before sleep
- Particularly effective during high-soreness training blocks (also reduces DOMS markers simultaneously)
- Cost: $15–25/month for concentrate

**4. Magnesium before sleep**

See Nutrition 04 for the full protocol. Key points:
- 250–400 mg magnesium glycinate, 30–60 min before sleep
- GABA-agonist effect reduces nervous system excitability for sleep onset
- Reduces nighttime cortisol in overreached athletes

**5. Pre-sleep casein protein**

See Nutrition 02 for full protocol. 30–40 g casein (cottage cheese, casein powder, Greek yogurt) 30–60 min before sleep supports overnight muscle protein synthesis.

**6. What to avoid in the 2–3 hours before sleep**

| Item | Effect | Cutoff time |
|---|---|---|
| Caffeine (coffee, pre-workout, tea) | Extends sleep latency 30–90 min | 1–2 PM for evening trainers |
| Alcohol (even 1–2 drinks) | Suppresses REM sleep, GH release | Within 3 hours of sleep |
| Very large meals | Raises core body temperature; disrupts sleep architecture | Within 2–3 hours |
| High-sugar foods alone | Blood glucose crash causes nighttime cortisol + waking | Within 1 hour |
| Excessive water (> 500 ml) | Nocturia (nighttime bathroom urination) | Manage from 8 PM onward |

### Sleep Environment Nutrition Connection

Core body temperature must drop 1–2°C for sleep onset. Foods that generate heat (high-fat digestion, large protein meals, spicy foods) delay this drop. Keep the last meal moderate in size (400–600 cal) and avoid very high-fat meals late at night.

## Part 2 — Hydration Protocols

### Why Hydration Is a Recovery Tool, Not Just a Performance Tool

Dehydration of even 2% of body weight impairs:
- Muscle protein synthesis (cell volume is a regulatory signal for MPS — shrunken cells downregulate protein synthesis)
- Glycogen resynthesis rate
- Sleep quality (hypernatremia during mild dehydration increases arousals)
- Cognitive function (relevant for skill-based training and sports)
- Joint lubrication (synovial fluid composition)

### Daily Baseline Hydration

**Target:** 35 ml per kg bodyweight per day from all fluid sources (water, milk, sports drinks, coffee, food water content)

Example calculations:
- 82 kg athlete: 82 × 35 = 2,870 ml/day (~96 oz)
- Adjust for: hot climate (+500 ml), high-protein diet (+250 ml), heavy sweat session (+sweat losses)

**Urine colour test:** The practical field test. Target pale straw yellow. Dark yellow = dehydrated; clear = overhydrated (rare problem but reduces electrolyte balance).

**Coffee and caffeine:** Moderate caffeine (< 400 mg/day) does not cause net dehydration in habitual consumers. The diuretic effect is smaller than the fluid volume consumed. Coffee counts toward daily fluid intake.

### Pre-Training Hydration

- **2–3 hours before:** 500–600 ml water or dilute sports drink
- **15–20 minutes before:** Additional 250 ml if starting the session feeling thirsty or in hot weather
- **Goal:** Begin training euhydrated (pale urine)

### Intra-Training Hydration

**General guideline:** Drink to thirst for sessions under 60 minutes in moderate conditions. This is evidence-supported; drinking beyond thirst in shorter sessions confers no benefit and risks hyponatremia at extremes.

**Structured sessions > 60 min or in heat:**
- 200–300 ml (7–10 oz) every 15–20 minutes
- If sweating visibly: Add sodium (see electrolytes below)

**Sweat rate estimation (for athletes who want to be precise):**
1. Weigh yourself before training (in minimal clothing)
2. Train normally without drinking
3. Weigh after training
4. Each 0.5 kg lost = approximately 500 ml sweat lost
5. Sweat rate (L/hour) = weight loss (kg) / session duration (hours)

Typical sweat rates: 0.5–1.5 L/hour (moderate conditions); up to 2.5 L/hour (intense exercise in hot weather)

### Post-Training Rehydration

The body does not immediately absorb fluid at the rate you drink it. Rapid rehydration (drinking 1 L fast) results in significant excretion. Slow rehydration over 1–3 hours is more effective.

**Target:** 1.5 × sweat losses over the 2 hours post-training

Example: Lost 0.8 kg during training → drink 1,200 ml over the next 2 hours

**Electrolyte replacement post-training:**
- For sessions > 60 min at high intensity: Add 500–700 mg sodium per liter of rehydration fluid
- Sports drink, electrolyte tablets, or a pinch of salt in water
- Potassium: Banana (422 mg) or a potato covers most post-workout potassium needs

### Hydration and Alcohol

Even 1–2 drinks post-training:
- Increases urine output (alcohol suppresses anti-diuretic hormone)
- Impairs glycogen resynthesis rate (alcohol competes with glucose for hepatic uptake)
- Suppresses GH secretion (blunts overnight recovery)
- Dehydrates overnight

If alcohol is consumed post-training, drink 500 ml water per drink consumed, eat a full meal with the alcohol to slow absorption, and accept that recovery for the next 12–16 hours will be modestly impaired.

## Part 3 — Stress, Cortisol, and Nutritional Countermeasures

### Training Stress vs. Life Stress: They Are Additive

Cortisol is the primary stress hormone. It serves a necessary role in training adaptation (acute cortisol rise during exercise is normal and drives adaptation signals). The problem is chronic cortisol elevation from:
- Overtraining (volume/intensity exceeds recovery capacity)
- Caloric restriction that is too aggressive
- Poor sleep (each poor night raises next-day cortisol by 10–40%)
- Life stress (work, relationships, financial) — the nervous system cannot distinguish between stressors

When cortisol remains chronically elevated:
- Testosterone production is suppressed
- Muscle protein synthesis is inhibited
- Fat storage (particularly visceral) is increased
- Immune function is compromised
- Sleep quality deteriorates (creating a feedback loop)

### Nutritional Countermeasures for High-Stress Periods

**1. Never go below energy availability of 30 kcal/kg FFM/day**

This is the RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) threshold. Below this, cortisol rises, testosterone falls, and recovery collapses. In practical terms, do not cut calories more than 500/day, and do not cut AND increase training volume simultaneously.

**2. Carbohydrate is the primary cortisol buffer**

Low-carbohydrate diets in athletes are associated with elevated cortisol and reduced testosterone. This is dose-dependent — very low carb (<2 g/kg) during high training volume produces the clearest hormonal disruption. The easiest cortisol management tool available is eating adequate carbohydrates.

**3. High-protein diet as a secondary buffer**

Dietary protein provides amino acid substrates that buffer catabolic processes. The 2.0+ g/kg protein target during high-stress periods is protective.

**4. Anti-inflammatory foods**

Foods that reduce chronic inflammation reduce the tissue damage signals that drive cortisol response:
- Fatty fish (EPA + DHA omega-3s)
- Tart cherry concentrate (anthocyanins)
- Turmeric/curcumin (modest evidence; 500 mg standardised extract reduces CK post-exercise)
- Dark chocolate 70%+ (flavanols; anti-inflammatory and vasodilatory)
- Colourful vegetables and fruits (mixed polyphenols, vitamin C)

**5. Recognise the stress-nutrition feedback loop**

Stress suppresses appetite → undereating → increased cortisol → more stress → further appetite suppression. Break the loop by:
- Eating scheduled meals even when not hungry during high-stress periods
- Liquid calories (shakes, milk, smoothies) are easier to consume without appetite
- Prioritizing protein and carbs even when total calories are below target

## Part 4 — Injury Recovery Nutrition

### The Phases of Injury Recovery

**Phase 1: Acute Phase (Days 1–7 post-injury)**

Inflammation is the necessary first response. Do not try to suppress acute inflammation with heavy anti-inflammatory supplementation — it is an integral part of healing initiation. NSAIDs and high-dose omega-3s during acute inflammation can impair early tissue repair.

- **Primary goal:** Maintain protein intake; prevent muscle atrophy in immobilised limb
- **Protein target:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg (do not reduce protein just because training is reduced)
- **Caloric target:** Maintenance or slight reduction (−200–300 cal) — injury reduces total energy expenditure
- **Do not:** Dramatically cut calories while injured (protein + calories are needed for healing)
- **Anti-inflammatories:** Use food rather than high-dose supplements; continue fish oil at maintenance dose (1–2 g EPA+DHA)

**Phase 2: Proliferative/Repair Phase (Weeks 1–4)**

Collagen matrix is being laid down; new tissue is forming. This is the most nutrition-critical phase.

- **Protein target:** 2.0–2.5 g/kg — elevated to support collagen and muscle protein synthesis
- **Collagen peptides + Vitamin C:** 10–15 g collagen 30–60 min before physical therapy or any weight-bearing exercise (this is the timing that matters — see Nutrition 04)
- **Zinc and Vitamin C:** Both are essential cofactors in wound healing. If dietary intake is marginal, supplement (15–25 mg zinc picolinate; 250–500 mg vitamin C)
- **Vitamin D:** Maintain levels > 40 ng/mL; deficiency is associated with delayed bone and muscle healing
- **Omega-3s (EPA+DHA):** 2–3 g/day — anti-inflammatory support is now appropriate (acute phase is over)

**Phase 3: Remodelling and Return to Training (Weeks 4–12+)**

- **Protein target:** 2.0–2.5 g/kg — continue elevated intake through full return to training
- **Creatine:** Resume/begin supplementation; significantly reduces muscle mass lost during immobilisation in multiple studies
- **Caloric target:** Match to returning training volume — increase gradually as training volume increases
- **Collagen:** Continue 10 g pre-exercise, particularly for tendon/ligament injuries
- **Gradual reintroduction:** As training volume increases by 20–30% each week, increase calories proportionally

### Specific Injury Nutrition Protocols

**Muscle tear or strain:**
- Phase 1–2: High protein (2.0–2.5 g/kg), creatine (start immediately)
- Expected atrophy: 0.5–1% of muscle mass per day with immobilisation; protein + creatine reduces this substantially
- Anti-atrophy: Blood flow restriction training with light loads + high protein delays significant muscle loss

**Tendinopathy (chronic tendon pain — patellar, Achilles, rotator cuff):**
- Collagen + Vitamin C is the most specific nutritional intervention; 10–15 g collagen, 30–60 min pre-load
- Omega-3s for anti-inflammatory support
- Magnesium for tissue relaxation (reduces protective spasm)
- Adequate Vitamin D (tendon healing is D-dependent)

**Bone stress injury (stress fracture, stress reaction):**
- Calcium: Confirm dietary adequacy; 1,000–1,200 mg/day from food or supplement
- Vitamin D3 + K2: D3 4,000 IU/day until levels confirmed > 40 ng/mL
- Protein: Maintain at 1.8+ g/kg — bone matrix is partially protein-based (collagen)
- Energy availability is critical: Bone stress injuries are strongly associated with low EA (RED-S) — increase calories

## Part 5 — Monitoring Recovery Through Nutrition

### Appetite as a Recovery Signal

Appetite is regulated by the same hormonal system that governs recovery:
- **Good recovery:** Normal appetite at meal times; satisfied after eating; no extreme cravings
- **Early overreaching:** Increased appetite (the body requests more to repair)
- **Functional overreaching:** Normal to slightly suppressed appetite
- **Non-functional overreaching (overtraining threshold):** Appetite significantly suppressed; food becomes unappealing

**Implication:** If appetite has been suppressed for more than 3 consecutive days without illness, evaluate training volume and sleep as primary suspects. Force liquid nutrition to maintain protein and calories.

### Sleep Quality as the Primary Recovery Metric

Track these specific sleep indicators weekly:
- **Time to fall asleep:** < 15 minutes is good; > 30 minutes indicates elevated cortisol or pre-sleep habits issue
- **Nighttime waking:** Occasionally normal; nightly waking suggests blood glucose instability, hydration issue, or overtraining
- **Morning readiness:** Feeling rested and motivated = adequate recovery; feeling heavy, tired, or unmotivated despite 8 hours = recovery deficit

**Nutritional response to poor sleep:**
- Add tart cherry juice protocol (2-week commitment for full effect)
- Add magnesium glycinate 300–400 mg pre-sleep
- Review caffeine timing (eliminate any after 12 PM temporarily)
- Add pre-sleep casein to prevent the overnight protein deficit that may cause restless sleep

### Training Performance as a Lagging Recovery Indicator

Performance decline appears 1–2 weeks after the recovery deficit begins. Do not wait for declining strength numbers to adjust — use the earlier signals:
1. Appetite and sleep quality (earliest signal)
2. Morning resting heart rate (elevated 5+ BPM above baseline = recovery stress)
3. Subjective motivation and mood (mid-signal)
4. Training performance (lagging indicator — avoid training into this stage)

**Nutritional response to declining performance:**
1. First check: Are daily calories and protein targets being hit? If not, fix this first.
2. Second check: Is sleep 7+ hours? If not, no nutrition change will fully compensate.
3. Third check: Increase carbs by 20–30% for 1 week (the most common training plateau nutritional cause is insufficient glycogen)
4. Fourth check: Take a recovery week (unscheduled deload) — sometimes the body needs rest more than additional nutrients

### Practical Weekly Self-Assessment

Rate each on a 1–10 scale at the end of each week:

| Metric | This Week | Action if < 6 |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep quality | /10 | Add magnesium; review caffeine; add tart cherry |
| Appetite (normal = 7–8) | /10 | Too low (<5): check total calories; too high (>9): may be underfuelling |
| Morning energy | /10 | Add pre-sleep casein; increase carbs at dinner |
| Training performance (relative to last week) | /10 | Increase carbs by 50–75 g/day on training days |
| Mood and motivation | /10 | Combined low sleep + low motivation = deload immediately |
| Recovery from soreness | /10 | Add tart cherry; increase protein; check total calories |

If three or more metrics score < 6 in the same week: Take a 3–5 day reduced volume period. Do not push through — the adaptation will be lost and injury risk spikes.

## Recovery Optimization: Quick-Reference

| Goal | Primary tool | Specific protocol |
|---|---|---|
| Better sleep onset | Magnesium + tart cherry | 300 mg Mg glycinate + 30 ml tart cherry concentrate, 30 min before bed |
| Overnight muscle repair | Pre-sleep casein | 30–40 g cottage cheese or casein powder, 30–60 min before sleep |
| Faster post-workout recovery | Post-workout nutrition | 30 g protein + 60 g carbs within 60 min |
| Reduce DOMS | Omega-3 + tart cherry | 2 g EPA+DHA/day + tart cherry twice daily during high-soreness blocks |
| Injury healing | Collagen + Vit C + protein | 10 g collagen + 250 mg Vit C pre-exercise; protein at 2.2+ g/kg |
| High cortisol / overtraining | Calories + carbs + sleep | Increase carbs 20–30%; ensure EA > 30 kcal/kg FFM; prioritize sleep |
| Rehydration | Structured fluid + electrolytes | 1.5× sweat losses over 2 hours; 500 mg sodium per liter |
| Bone injury | Vitamin D + calcium + protein | D3 4,000 IU/day; calcium 1,000+ mg/day; protein 1.8+ g/kg |
