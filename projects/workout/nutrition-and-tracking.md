## Philosophy

The comprehensive workout plan is built on **progressive overload and recovery**. This guide provides the nutrition strategy and tracking system to support that. Unlike many nutrition plans, this one is deliberately minimal:

- No calorie counting apps required (pen and paper works)
- No exotic supplements
- No meal timing magic
- Evidence-based macro targets that scale across all three equipment tiers and training frequencies
- Simple, repeatable tracking that you can maintain for months without burnout

The goal is sustainable nutrition that **fuels performance**, **supports recovery**, and **enables measurable progress**. Everything here is tied back to the workout plan's intensity, frequency, and adaptation targets.

## PART 1 — NUTRITION FRAMEWORK

### Caloric Needs by Activity Level

Your baseline is your basal metabolic rate (BMR) — calories burned at rest. A practical estimate is:

**BMR ≈ 1 cal/hour/lb of bodyweight** (or roughly 10× your weight in pounds)

Example: A 180 lb person has a BMR around 1,800 cal/day.

Then multiply by activity level:

| Activity Level | Daily Multiplier | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Sedentary | 1.2 | No exercise |
| Light activity | 1.375 | 3 days/week training |
| Moderate activity | 1.55 | 4–5 days/week training |
| Heavy activity | 1.725 | 6 days/week training |

**Example calculations:**
- 180 lb person, 4 days/week: 1,800 × 1.55 = **2,790 calories/day**
- 160 lb person, 6 days/week: 1,600 × 1.725 = **2,760 calories/day**
- 200 lb person, 3 days/week: 2,000 × 1.375 = **2,750 calories/day**

### Macro Targets by Goal

Protein is the only non-negotiable macro. Carbs and fats adjust based on your training intensity and goal.

#### Strength & Hypertrophy (Building)
- **Protein:** 1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight (or 0.75–1 g/lb)
- **Carbs:** 4–7 g/kg bodyweight
- **Fats:** 0.8–1.2 g/kg bodyweight
- **Calories:** Maintenance + 300–500 (slow surplus)
- **Best for:** Plans A/B/C at all frequencies

Example (180 lb / 82 kg person):
- Protein: 82 × 1.9 = **156 g/day** (24% of calories)
- Carbs: 82 × 5.5 = **451 g/day** (55% of calories)
- Fats: 82 × 1.0 = **82 g/day** (21% of calories)
- **Total: ~2,850 cal/day**

#### Maintenance (Recomposition)
- **Protein:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg bodyweight
- **Carbs:** 3–5 g/kg bodyweight
- **Fats:** 0.8–1.2 g/kg bodyweight
- **Calories:** At maintenance level (±50 cal)
- **Best for:** Those already at goal weight, wanting to build muscle while losing fat slowly

Example (180 lb / 82 kg person):
- Protein: 82 × 1.8 = **148 g/day**
- Carbs: 82 × 4 = **328 g/day**
- Fats: 82 × 1.0 = **82 g/day**
- **Total: ~2,300 cal/day**

#### Endurance & Athletic Performance
- **Protein:** 1.4–1.8 g/kg bodyweight
- **Carbs:** 6–10 g/kg bodyweight (emphasis on peri-workout timing)
- **Fats:** 0.7–1.0 g/kg bodyweight
- **Calories:** Maintenance to slight deficit (running 6-day plans)
- **Best for:** High-frequency, conditioning-heavy training

Example (180 lb / 82 kg person):
- Protein: 82 × 1.6 = **131 g/day**
- Carbs: 82 × 8 = **656 g/day** (higher carbs, especially pre/post workout)
- Fats: 82 × 0.8 = **66 g/day**
- **Total: ~2,800 cal/day**

#### Fat Loss (Cutting)
- **Protein:** 2.0–2.4 g/kg bodyweight (preserve muscle during deficit)
- **Carbs:** 2–4 g/kg bodyweight (reduced, especially non-training days)
- **Fats:** 0.6–0.9 g/kg bodyweight
- **Calories:** Maintenance − 300–500 (slow deficit)
- **Best for:** When body composition change is the priority

Example (180 lb / 82 kg person):
- Protein: 82 × 2.2 = **180 g/day** (32% of calories — prioritized for satiety)
- Carbs: 82 × 3 = **246 g/day** (35% of calories)
- Fats: 82 × 0.75 = **62 g/day** (33% of calories)
- **Total: ~2,000 cal/day** (−750 from maintenance — aggressive but sustainable with full training)

### Aligning Nutrition with Equipment Tiers & Frequencies

The three tiers have different caloric demands:

**Tier 1 (No Equipment) — 3/4/6 Days/Week**
- Lower mechanical stress on joints (no barbell), faster recovery
- Can sustain 6-day training with lower overall calories
- Pick: Maintenance or slight surplus
- Protein priority: Essential (calisthenics demands higher tissue stress)
- Carb timing: Flexible, but still important for skill work and conditioning

**Tier 2 (Bands) — 3/4/6 Days/Week**
- Intermediate recovery demand (variable resistance = less CNS fatigue than barbells)
- Good for moderate to high frequencies
- Pick: Maintenance to slight surplus
- Protein priority: High (still training hard)
- Carb timing: Around skill work (planche, muscle-up, L-sit) and conditioning

**Tier 3 (Full Gym) — 3/4/5/6 Days/Week**
- High mechanical and neuromuscular stress (barbell loads are heavy)
- Maximum caloric demand, especially at 6 days
- Pick: Slight surplus (building strength requires energy and substrate)
- Protein priority: Highest (supporting muscle repair after heavy compounds)
- Carb timing: Critical (around compounds and skill work)

**Quick Example Table:**

| Tier | Frequency | Example Weight | Calories | Protein | Carbs | Fats | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 (BW) | 4 days | 180 lb | 2,500 | 155 g | 340 g | 75 g | Hypertrophy |
| Tier 2 (Band) | 4 days | 180 lb | 2,600 | 160 g | 370 g | 80 g | Strength |
| Tier 3 (Gym) | 4 days | 180 lb | 2,800 | 170 g | 420 g | 85 g | Strength + Size |
| Tier 1 (BW) | 6 days | 180 lb | 2,700 | 165 g | 390 g | 80 g | Endurance/Recomp |
| Tier 3 (Gym) | 6 days | 180 lb | 3,200 | 190 g | 500 g | 100 g | Building |

### Pre & Post-Workout Nutrition Timing

**"Meal timing" is not magic, but context matters.**

#### Pre-Workout (1–2 hours before)
- **Goal:** Fuel the session without gastric distress
- **Carbs:** 20–40 g (fast-digesting: fruit, rice, white bread, sports drink)
- **Protein:** 0–10 g (optional; only if heavy lifting or >90 min session)
- **Fats & Fiber:** Minimize (slow digestion, bloating)
- **Hydration:** 500 ml (17 oz) water, 2–3 hours before
- **Example:** Banana + 1 tbsp honey, or white toast + jam

**If training fasted (3+ hours since food):** Add 10 g simple carbs (glucose tablet, raisins) 15 min before to prevent glycogen depletion during skill work.

#### Post-Workout (within 2 hours)
- **Goal:** Replenish glycogen and provide amino acids for recovery
- **Carbs:** 40–80 g (depends on session intensity; 6-day plans need more)
- **Protein:** 20–40 g (from whole food or shake)
- **Fats:** Minimize in the first hour (slow digestion interferes with nutrient uptake)
- **Hydration:** 750 ml (25 oz) water per lb of bodyweight lost during session
- **Example:** Chicken + white rice + banana, or protein shake + sports drink

**Why it matters:** Glycogen repletion speeds recovery, especially on consecutive training days (Tiers 1/2/3 at 6 days/week). Amino acids blunt muscle breakdown post-training.

**If you can't eat within 2 hours:** A simple shake (protein + carbs) immediately post-workout, then full meal 1–2 hours later, is almost as effective.

## PART 2 — PRACTICAL MEAL PLANNING & SAMPLE DAYS

### Food as a Framework, Not a Prison

You don't need specific meals. You need hit daily macros consistently. Here's how:

**Step 1: Pick your target calories and macros** (from Part 1)  
**Step 2: Identify 3–4 protein sources you actually eat**  
**Step 3: Identify 3–4 carb sources**  
**Step 4: Identify 2–3 fat sources**  
**Step 5: Mix and match daily, aiming to hit macros ±50 cal, ±5 g protein**

Example framework for a 180 lb person eating 2,800 cal / 160 g protein / 380 g carbs / 85 g fat:

#### Protein Sources (160 g target)
- Chicken breast: 165 cal, 31 g protein per 100 g
- Eggs: 155 cal, 13 g protein per 100 g (2 eggs)
- Canned tuna: 100 cal, 22 g protein per 100 g
- Lean ground beef: 180 cal, 19 g protein per 100 g
- Greek yogurt: 60 cal, 10 g protein per 100 g
- Lentils/beans (cooked): 140 cal, 9 g protein per 100 g

**Daily strategy:** Chicken at lunch (100 g = 31 g protein), eggs at breakfast (3 eggs = 18 g protein), lean beef at dinner (100 g = 19 g protein), Greek yogurt snack (150 g = 15 g protein), beans with dinner (100 g = 9 g protein). **Total: ~92 g protein.**

Add a whey shake (25 g protein) post-workout + 1 more protein source at another meal = **160 g.**

#### Carb Sources (380 g target)
- White rice: 130 cal, 28 g carbs per 100 g cooked
- Oats: 150 cal, 27 g carbs per 100 g dry
- Banana: 89 cal, 23 g carbs per medium
- Sweet potato: 90 cal, 21 g carbs per 100 g cooked
- White bread: 79 cal, 14 g carbs per slice
- Pasta: 130 cal, 25 g carbs per 100 g cooked
- Whole grain bread: 80 cal, 14 g carbs per slice

**Daily strategy:** Oats at breakfast (60 g dry = 162 cal, 43 g carbs), rice with lunch (150 g cooked = 195 cal, 42 g carbs), banana pre-workout (89 cal, 23 g carbs), rice with dinner (150 g = 195 cal, 42 g carbs), sports drink post-workout (250 cal, 60 g carbs). **Total: ~891 cal, 210 g carbs.**

Add bread with meals, fruit snacks, pasta = **380 g carbs.**

#### Fat Sources (85 g target)
- Olive oil: 120 cal, 14 g fat per tbsp
- Butter: 100 cal, 11 g fat per tbsp
- Nuts (almonds): 160 cal, 14 g fat per oz
- Avocado: 240 cal, 22 g fat per fruit
- Whole eggs (yolk): 55 cal, 5 g fat per egg
- Salmon: 200 cal, 13 g fat per 100 g
- Full-fat dairy: Varies

**Daily strategy:** Olive oil cooking (3 tbsp = 360 cal, 42 g fat), whole eggs (3 = 165 cal, 15 g fat), salmon (100 g at dinner = 200 cal, 13 g fat), almonds snack (30 g = 160 cal, 14 g fat). **Total: ~885 cal, 84 g fat.**

### Essential Micronutrients Checklist

You don't need supplements if you eat whole foods — but awareness prevents deficiency.

| Micronutrient | Daily Target | Why It Matters | Food Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| Magnesium | 310–420 mg (sex-dependent) | Muscle relaxation, sleep, nervous system | Almonds, spinach, dark chocolate, pumpkin seeds, oats |
| Vitamin D | 1,000–4,000 IU | Bone health, immune function, testosterone | Fatty fish (salmon), egg yolks, sunlight exposure (10–30 min/day) |
| Iron | 8–18 mg (sex-dependent) | Oxygen transport, energy production | Red meat, chicken, beans, spinach, fortified cereals |
| Zinc | 8–11 mg (sex-dependent) | Immune function, protein synthesis, recovery | Beef, chicken, oysters, cashews, chickpeas |
| Calcium | 1,000 mg | Bone health, muscle contraction | Dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, almonds |
| Potassium | 2,600–3,400 mg | Electrolyte balance, muscle function | Bananas, sweet potato, beans, chicken, spinach |
| Sodium | 500–3,000 mg* | Electrolyte balance, performance | Salt, processed foods; generally not deficient if you eat normally |

*Most people overeat sodium, so don't stress this unless you're sweating heavily during conditioning.*

**Quick rule:** If you eat a variety of whole foods (vegetables, fruit, lean meat, dairy/alternatives, nuts, grains), you'll cover 90% of micronutrient needs. A basic multivitamin is optional insurance, not required.

### Hydration Guidelines

Dehydration kills performance faster than any other nutritional factor. Here's the framework:

**General Hydration (Non-Training)**
- **Baseline:** 35 ml/kg bodyweight/day = ~2.8 liters (94 oz) for a 180 lb person
- **Adjust for:** Climate (hot = +50%), activity (sedentary = baseline, active = baseline +500 ml), diet (high protein = +250–500 ml)
- **Simple test:** Urine color. Pale yellow = adequate. Dark yellow = drink more.

**Workout Hydration**
- **Before (2–3 hours):** 500 ml (17 oz) water or sports drink
- **During (if >60 min):** 200–300 ml (7–10 oz) every 15–20 min, OR drink to thirst
- **After:** 750 ml per 0.5 kg bodyweight lost during session (weigh yourself before and after)

**Example:**
- 180 lb person (82 kg) baseline: 2,800 ml (94 oz) daily
- Hot climate day: +500 ml = 3,300 ml
- 90-min training session, lost 0.8 kg sweat: 0.8 kg × 1.5 = 1,200 ml (drink 1–1.5 L over next 2–3 hours)

**Electrolytes (Sodium + Potassium)**
- High-intensity training >60 min or very hot climate: add sports drink with 4–8 mmol/L sodium (500–600 mg/liter) and some carbs
- Normal training (3–4 days/week, <60 min): water is fine; get electrolytes from food
- 6-day training: Sports drinks on volume/conditioning days help with sodium replacement

### What to Track & Why

You only need **three things** to assess progress:

#### 1. **Strength Metrics (Track Every Session)**

Your lifts directly reflect training quality.

| Tier/Plan | What to Track | How Often | Example Log |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plan A (BW) | Pull-ups: max reps, weighted pull-ups (backpack weight) | Each session | Mon: 10 reps / 15 lb backpack |
| Plan A (BW) | Archer push-ups: reps per side, tempo | Each session | Wed: 8 reps each side, 3s eccentric |
| Plan A (BW) | Bulgarian split squat: reps per leg, backpack weight | Each session | Fri: 10 reps, 20 lb backpack |
| Plan A (BW) | L-sit hold: seconds, progression stage | Each leg day | Mon: 20s / 25s / 30s progression |
| Plan B (Band) | Banded squat: band color, reps | Each session | Mon: Heavy band, 10 reps |
| Plan B (Band) | Banded row: band color, reps | Each session | Wed: Heavy band, 8 reps |
| Plan C (Gym) | Barbell bench: weight × reps | Each session | Mon: 225 × 5, 225 × 5, 225 × 4 |
| Plan C (Gym) | Squat: weight × reps | Each session | Tue: 315 × 4, 315 × 4, 310 × 5 |

**Why:** These directly measure progressive overload. If your numbers go up, you're making progress.

#### 2. **Bodyweight & Body Composition (Track Weekly)**

Bodyweight alone is misleading (muscle + fat + water), but trends matter.

- **Weigh yourself:** Same time daily (morning, after bathroom, before eating), same scale
- **Track:** Average weight per week (not daily fluctuations)
- **Every 4 weeks:** Take progress photos (front, side, back) and measure waist, chest, arms, thighs

| Week | Mon | Tue | Wed | Thu | Fri | Sat | Sun | **Avg** | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 180.2 | 180.5 | 179.8 | 180.1 | 180.6 | 181.2 | 180.9 | **180.6** | Normal variance, hydration fluctuation |
| 2 | 180.8 | 180.4 | 180.2 | 180.9 | 180.5 | 181.1 | 180.7 | **180.7** | +0.1 lb average — on track |
| 3 | 180.9 | 181.2 | 181.1 | 180.8 | 181.4 | 182.0 | 181.5 | **181.3** | +0.6 lb average — muscle gain + water retention |
| 4 | 181.4 | 181.1 | 180.9 | 181.2 | 180.8 | 181.0 | 180.6 | **180.9** | Deload week — small drop, expected |

**Target trends:**
- **Building (slight surplus):** +0.5 to +1.5 lb/week → sustainable lean gain
- **Recomposition (maintenance):** ±0.5 lb/week variance (no trend)
- **Cutting (deficit):** −0.5 to −1.5 lb/week → preserve muscle while losing fat
- **If trend is wrong:** Adjust calories by ±200 (takes 1–2 weeks to see effect)

#### 3. **Recovery Indicators (Track Daily)**

These predict overtraining before injury happens.

| Indicator | Log Scale | Green Flag | Yellow Flag | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sleep | 0–10 hours | 7–9 hours | 5–6 or 10+ hours | <5 hours, poor quality |
| Morning HR | BPM | 40–60 resting | 61–80 | >80 (overtraining) |
| Hunger | 1–10 | 6–8 (normal appetite) | 4–5 or 9–10 | Constantly hungry or no appetite |
| Soreness (DOMS) | 1–10 | 2–4 (mild, gone by day 3) | 5–7 (limits ROM) | 8–10 (can't train) |
| Motivation | 1–10 | 7–10 (eager to train) | 5–6 (meh) | <4 (dread) |
| Injury/Pain | None/Mild | No issues | Dull ache, improves with warmup | Sharp pain, worsens during training |

**Action based on flags:**
- **1–2 green flags:** Train as planned
- **2–3 yellow flags:** Consider dropping 1–2 conditioning circuits or reducing volume by 1 set per exercise
- **2+ red flags:** Take an extra rest day; don't force it

### Progress Beyond the Scale: What Actually Matters

Numbers on a bar don't tell the full story. Track these too:

| Metric | How to Measure | Frequency | Target Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strength endurance | Pull-ups: max unweighted reps | Monthly | +2–5 reps/month (beginner), +1 rep/month (intermediate) |
| Unilateral strength | Bulgarian split squat: reps per leg | Monthly | +2–3 reps/month |
| Movement quality | Video yourself; compare video month to month | Monthly | Less deviation from ideal form; smoother transitions |
| Work capacity | HIIT circuit: rounds completed in fixed time | Monthly | +0.5–1 round/month |
| Skill progression | Planche lean hold, L-sit hold, muscle-up reps | Monthly | Advance one progression stage per 4–8 weeks |
| Resting HR | BPM before getting out of bed | Weekly | ↓ 2–3 BPM over 12 weeks = improved fitness |
| Energy levels | Subjective 1–10 | Daily | Increase over time = better recovery |
| Confidence | Feeling strong/capable | Subjective | Should increase as lifts go up |

- **Strength:** +5–10% on main lifts (neural adaptation, form improvement)
- **Bodyweight:** +2–4 lb (muscle + glycogen + water, if building)
- **Skill:** 1–2 progression steps in calisthenics (planche, L-sit, muscle-up)
- **How you feel:** Sore first 3 days, then great energy

#### Weeks 5–8 (Hypertrophy Phase)
- **Strength:** +2–5% additional (slower gain)
- **Bodyweight:** +1–2 lb/week (good sign if staying lean)
- **Skill:** Solid holds in progression stages, confident on basic movements
- **How you feel:** Training becomes routine, recovery is stable

#### Weeks 9–12 (Plateau & Deload)
- **Strength:** Minimal gain (expected plateau)
- **Bodyweight:** Stabilizes (hitting ceiling on caloric surplus)
- **Skill:** Can now do 1–2 extra reps on skill work, or longer holds
- **Week 12:** Deload week — drop volume 30%, same intensity. Recover fully.

#### Weeks 13+ (Continuation & Periodization)
- **Strength:** Reset to earlier weight, then climb again (now aiming for higher top-end strength)
- **Bodyweight:** Slowly climbs if still in surplus, stabilizes if maintenance
- **Skill:** Advancing to harder progressions (pistol squats, planche push-ups, muscle-ups)
- **Every 12 weeks:** Assess total progress. Usually +8–15% stronger, 5–15 lb heavier (if building)

**Beginner (Months 1–6):**
- 20–50% strength gain
- 10–20 lb weight gain (depending on diet and Tier)
- Multiple skill breakthroughs (pull-ups, dips, muscle-up transitions)

**Intermediate (Months 6–18):**
- 5–15% strength gain per 12-week block
- Slow, steady 1–2 lb/month gains (or recomposition)
- Skill mastery (pistol squats, planche holds, strict muscle-ups)

**Advanced (Months 18+):**
- 2–5% strength gain per 12-week block (law of diminishing returns)
- Maintenance or slow cuts
- Specialization (pursuing elite skills like full planche, strict L-sit V-up transitions)

### When to Adjust Calories/Macros

You'll know in 2–4 weeks if your nutrition is right. Here's how to decide:

| Situation | What's Happening | Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Strength climbing, bodyweight stable | Perfect balance | Keep same |
| Strength climbing, gaining 0.5–1.5 lb/week | Building lean mass | Keep same |
| Strength flat, losing 0.5–1.5 lb/week | Cutting/recomposition working | Keep same (if goal is fat loss) |
| **Strength plateaued, bodyweight stalled for 4 weeks** | Too low calories for growth | Add +200–300 cal (mainly carbs) |
| **Strength weak, hunger suppressed, energy low** | Too much deficit | Add +300–500 cal |
| **Strength weak, gaining 2+ lb/week, getting fatter** | Too much surplus or junk food | Reduce +300 cal, check protein (aim 1.6–2.0 g/kg) |
| **Extreme hunger every day, constant cravings** | Calories/macros too restrictive | Add +200–300 cal |
| **Training 6 days/week, feeling flat, recovery poor** | Volume too high for diet | Add +300–500 cal, prioritize sleep |

### Deload Weeks & Recovery

Every 4th week, reduce training volume by 30–40% to accumulate fatigue recovery. This prevents long-term burnout and actually accelerates progress.

**Deload Week:**
- Reduce sets: 3 sets → 2 sets per exercise
- Keep weight/resistance same (don't go light)
- Skip HIIT circuits or do 2 rounds instead of 4
- Continue skill work at 50% volume
- Maintain nutrition (same calories/macros)
- Focus on sleep (8+ hours if possible)

**Why it works:** You accumulate fatigue over weeks. Deload gives your nervous system and joints recovery while maintaining strength. You'll feel fresh week 5 and often see a strength jump.

**Sign you need an unscheduled deload:**
- Resting HR elevated 10+ BPM above baseline for 3+ days
- Motivation crashed (dreading training)
- Minor aches becoming painful
- Performance declining despite good effort
- Sleep suffering despite good habits

Take 3–5 days off completely, or do easy activity (walking, yoga, stretching). You'll return stronger.

## PART 4 — COMMON TRACKING MISTAKES & SOLUTIONS

### Mistake 1: Weighing Food Obsessively

**The problem:** Digital scale, measuring every gram, eating becomes mechanical.  
**The solution:** Weigh food for 2 weeks to learn portions, then estimate by volume/visual.
- 1 palm-sized serving of protein ≈ 25–30 g
- 1 fist of carbs ≈ 50 g
- 1 thumb of fat ≈ 10 g
- This is accurate enough for 95% of people.

### Mistake 2: Only Counting What You Eat, Not Drinking

**The problem:** Juice, sports drinks, coffee with cream add 200–400 cal/day — invisible.  
**The solution:** Log everything with calories — drinks count.

### Mistake 3: Trusting Calorie Labels Blindly

**The problem:** Labels are ±20% off legally; restaurant food even more.  
**The solution:** Use labels as a guide, not gospel. Adjust calories weekly based on results (see "When to Adjust" above).

### Mistake 4: Not Eating Enough on Heavy Training Days

**The problem:** You feel like you "earned" eating less; actually, heavy days need MORE.  
**The solution:** Track honestly. On days with 4-set compounds + skill work, you *need* the carbs post-workout.

### Mistake 5: Eating Perfectly, But Not Training Hard

**The problem:** Nutrition without effort won't work. 70% effort training + 100% nutrition = slow progress.  
**The solution:** Hit your prescribed sets and reps. If you can't, volume is too high or recovery is poor (not nutrition issue).

### Mistake 6: Comparing Your Progress to Others

**The problem:** Someone online gained 20 lb in 3 months; you gained 5 lb. You "suck."  
**The problem:** They may be using drugs, eating 4,000 cal/day, or lying. You don't know.  
**The solution:** Compare yourself month-to-month. +0.5–1.5 lb/week is textbook healthy gain.

## CLOSING NOTES

**This guide is a framework, not a prescription.** Every body is different:

- Some people need 2,400 cal to gain muscle; others need 3,200
- Some digest high carbs well; others feel better on lower carbs
- Some love tracking; others thrive on intuition

**The process:**
1. Use the targets in this guide as a starting point
2. Train hard and eat consistently for 4 weeks
3. Assess: strength up? Bodyweight trend right? Sleep/energy good?
4. If yes, keep going. If no, adjust one variable by 10% and reassess.

**The magic is consistency over months, not perfection over days.** You'll gain more muscle eating 2,700 cal consistently for 12 weeks than eating 2,900 cal sporadically.

Train hard. Eat enough. Sleep. Track the important stuff. Adjust when needed. Repeat for years.

That's it.
